Pierre Robin Sequence: Airway, Feeding & Craniofacial Management

Key Context for Marshall Syndrome Families
Pierre Robin sequence occurs in about 24% of individuals with Marshall syndrome. Unlike isolated Pierre Robin, the underlying COL11A1 collagen defect means the jaw, palate, and airway tissues have ongoing structural vulnerability beyond the neonatal period. This page covers what Pierre Robin sequence is, how it connects to COL11A1 mutations, and the full scope of management from the NICU through adulthood.

What Is Pierre Robin Sequence?

Pierre Robin sequence (PRS) is not a syndrome — it is a sequence, meaning one primary defect triggers a cascade of secondary consequences. The initiating event is mandibular hypoplasia (an undersized lower jaw), and everything else follows from there.

The Cascade

  1. Micrognathia — The mandible fails to grow to normal size during weeks 7–11 of embryonic development.
  2. Glossoptosis — Because the jaw is too small, the tongue has nowhere to go and falls backward (posteriorly) into the airway.
  3. Airway obstruction — The posteriorly displaced tongue blocks the pharyngeal airway, causing breathing difficulty.
  4. Cleft palate (in most cases) — Because the tongue is elevated and displaced, it physically blocks the palatal shelves from fusing during week 11 of gestation, producing a characteristic wide, U-shaped posterior cleft palate.
U-shaped vs. V-shaped cleft: The cleft palate in Pierre Robin sequence is typically wide and U-shaped, extending from the soft palate into the hard palate. This is different from the V-shaped cleft seen in isolated cleft palate. The shape matters because it affects surgical repair approach and timing.

Isolated vs. Syndromic Pierre Robin

About 50–80% of Pierre Robin sequence cases are syndromic — meaning they occur as part of a broader genetic condition. Among syndromic PRS cases, Stickler syndrome and Marshall syndrome (both caused by collagen gene mutations) are among the most common underlying diagnoses. In isolated PRS, the jaw growth restriction is temporary and the mandible often catches up. In syndromic PRS, the underlying genetic defect (such as COL11A1 mutation) means the collagen abnormality persists, affecting growth, healing, and tissue quality throughout life.

The COL11A1 Mechanism: Why Marshall Syndrome Causes Pierre Robin

Understanding why COL11A1 mutations cause Pierre Robin sequence requires understanding embryonic jaw development.

Meckel’s Cartilage and Mandibular Growth

During early embryonic development (weeks 6–12), the mandible forms around a cartilage template called Meckel’s cartilage. This cartilage acts as the scaffold that guides mandibular growth and elongation. Type XI collagen, encoded partly by COL11A1, is a critical structural component of this cartilage — it controls the lateral growth of type II collagen fibrils and maintains cartilage integrity.

When COL11A1 is mutated:

  • Type XI collagen heterotrimers form abnormally (dominant-negative effect)
  • Meckel’s cartilage is structurally compromised
  • The cartilage template fails to extend sufficiently, restricting mandibular outgrowth
  • The shortened mandible displaces the tongue upward and backward
  • The displaced tongue physically prevents palatal shelf fusion at week 11
  • Result: the full Pierre Robin cascade — micrognathia, glossoptosis, airway obstruction, and U-shaped cleft palate
The critical difference in Marshall syndrome: In isolated Pierre Robin, the jaw growth restriction is a one-time developmental event. In Marshall syndrome, the COL11A1 mutation is present in every cell — meaning the collagen defect affects ongoing jaw growth, palatal tissue quality, cartilage in the temporomandibular joint, wound healing after surgical repair, and long-term craniofacial development. The Pierre Robin sequence is the first manifestation of a lifelong collagen disorder.

Neonatal Airway Management

Airway obstruction is the most immediately life-threatening aspect of Pierre Robin sequence. Management follows a stepwise approach, starting with the least invasive interventions and escalating only as needed.

Mandibular Distraction Osteogenesis (MDO) — In Detail

MDO has become the preferred surgical intervention for severe Pierre Robin airway obstruction at many craniofacial centers. The procedure involves:

  1. Bilateral osteotomies (controlled bone cuts) in the mandibular ramus
  2. Placement of internal or external distraction devices (pins and screws)
  3. A latency period of 1–3 days for initial healing
  4. Active distraction at approximately 1 mm per day (parents or team turn the device)
  5. A consolidation period of 6–8 weeks for the new bone to mature
  6. Device removal (second procedure for internal devices; bedside for external)
COL11A1 consideration for MDO: In Marshall syndrome, the quality of bone regeneration during distraction may be affected by the underlying collagen defect. Type XI collagen plays a role in cartilage-to-bone transition during endochondral ossification. Discuss with the craniofacial team whether the distraction rate or consolidation period should be adjusted for a child with known COL11A1 mutation.
Research Update (R473): Combined MDO + Adenotonsillectomy for Definitive Airway Resolution

Emerging evidence from craniofacial centers performing staged airway management in syndromic Pierre Robin sequence shows that the combination of mandibular distraction osteogenesis (MDO) followed by adenotonsillectomy (AT) achieves an 87.5% definitive airway cure rate — meaning near-complete resolution of obstructive sleep apnea without the need for tracheostomy or long-term CPAP.

  • MDO addresses skeletal obstruction (micrognathia, tongue-base collapse), while AT removes the lymphoid tissue component that contributes to residual oropharyngeal obstruction
  • Staged approach: MDO is typically performed first (in the neonatal or early infancy period) to resolve the critical airway obstruction, followed by AT at age 2–4 when adenotonsillar hypertrophy becomes the dominant residual contributor
  • Post-procedure polysomnography is essential: Even with this high success rate, a follow-up sleep study should be performed 8–12 weeks after AT to confirm resolution. The 12.5% who do not achieve full resolution may need CPAP or further intervention
  • Marshall syndrome relevance: Because COL11A1-related pharyngeal tissue laxity contributes a third level of obstruction beyond skeletal and lymphoid, the residual OSA rate in Marshall syndrome may be higher than the 12.5% reported for mixed syndromic PRS cohorts. Post-AT sleep studies are non-negotiable in this population

Monitoring and NICU Stay

Neonates with Pierre Robin sequence typically require:

  • Continuous pulse oximetry — monitoring oxygen saturation, especially during sleep and feeding
  • Polysomnography (sleep study) — to quantify the severity of obstructive apnea and guide intervention decisions
  • Video monitoring — visualization of breathing patterns and desaturation events
  • Growth tracking — weight gain is a critical indicator of feeding adequacy and metabolic stress from airway obstruction

The NICU stay duration varies widely — from days (mild cases managed with prone positioning alone) to weeks or months (cases requiring MDO or tracheostomy). Most airway obstruction improves significantly during the first 1–2 years of life as the mandible grows, but in syndromic PRS, this improvement may be less complete. See Sleep Apnea & Airway Management for ongoing monitoring considerations.

Feeding Difficulties

Feeding is one of the most challenging aspects of caring for an infant with Pierre Robin sequence. The combination of cleft palate, airway compromise, and jaw abnormality creates a triple threat to feeding.

Why Feeding Is Hard

  • Cleft palate prevents suction: Infants generate negative pressure (suction) by sealing the mouth and using the palate as a surface. An open cleft eliminates this seal, making it impossible to create the vacuum needed for normal sucking.
  • Airway compromise during feeding: Coordinating suck-swallow-breathe is already the most complex motor task a newborn performs. When the airway is compromised, adding the effort of feeding can cause dangerous desaturation events.
  • Glossoptosis interferes with latch: The posteriorly displaced tongue cannot form the proper cupping motion needed for effective feeding.
  • Fatigue: Infants with PRS use significantly more energy to breathe and feed, leading to exhaustion before adequate intake.

Feeding Positioning and Techniques

  • Upright or semi-upright position (at least 45 degrees) — reduces reflux risk and helps gravity assist with swallowing
  • Pacing the feed — frequent pauses allow the infant to coordinate breathing; watch for cues (color change, increased work of breathing, pulling away)
  • Small, frequent feeds — less fatigue per session; aim for 8–12 feeds per day initially
  • Burping frequently — infants with cleft palate swallow more air
  • Keep sessions to 30 minutes maximum — longer feeds cause more calories burned than gained

When Oral Feeding Is Not Enough

  • Nasogastric (NG) tube — temporary supplementation via nose tube to stomach; used while the infant is learning to feed or recovering from airway intervention
  • Gastrostomy tube (G-tube) — surgically placed directly into the stomach for longer-term supplementation; considered when oral feeding alone cannot sustain adequate growth beyond several months
Failure to thrive is a real risk. Weight gain is the single most important metric in the first months. A feeding specialist (often a speech-language pathologist or occupational therapist with feeding expertise) should be involved from birth. Weekly weight checks are standard until feeding is established and weight gain is consistent.

Breastfeeding Considerations

Direct breastfeeding is typically not possible with an unrepaired cleft palate because the infant cannot create suction. However, expressed breast milk can be delivered via specialized bottles, and breast milk is particularly beneficial for infants with PRS due to its immune and nutritional properties. Some mothers find that a supplemental nursing system (SNS) at the breast allows the bonding of breastfeeding while delivering milk via tube. Work with a lactation consultant experienced in cleft palate feeding.

Cleft Palate Repair

Timing

Cleft palate repair (palatoplasty) is typically performed between 9 and 14 months of age, with most centers aiming for 9–12 months. The goal is to close the palate before the critical period of speech development while ensuring the infant is large enough and healthy enough for general anesthesia. In Pierre Robin sequence, repair may be slightly delayed if airway issues are not yet resolved or if the infant’s weight is inadequate.

Surgical Techniques

Common approaches include:

  • Furlow double-opposing Z-plasty — lengthens the soft palate and reconstructs the levator muscle sling; good speech outcomes
  • Von Langenbeck palatoplasty — uses bipedicled mucoperiosteal flaps; reliable closure for wider clefts
  • Two-flap (Bardach) palatoplasty — two-layer closure; commonly used for complete cleft palate

The wide U-shaped cleft characteristic of PRS can make repair more technically challenging and may require tension-relieving incisions or a two-stage approach (soft palate first, hard palate later).

Outcomes in Pierre Robin Sequence

Research shows that patients with PRS have somewhat worse outcomes after palatoplasty compared to patients with isolated cleft palate:

COL11A1 Considerations for Palatal Surgery

Collagen-deficient tissue healing: There is no large published dataset on palatal repair outcomes specifically in COL11A1 mutation carriers. However, the biology raises legitimate concerns:
  • Type XI collagen is present in oral mucosal connective tissue and contributes to tissue tensile strength
  • Wound healing depends on proper collagen fibril formation — the same process disrupted by COL11A1 mutations
  • Palatal tissue may have reduced tensile strength, potentially increasing fistula risk or delayed healing
  • The craniofacial surgeon should be informed of the COL11A1 diagnosis and may opt for a more conservative repair with less tissue tension, or a two-stage approach

Velopharyngeal Insufficiency (VPI) After Repair

VPI occurs when the soft palate cannot fully close against the back wall of the throat during speech, causing air to escape through the nose. It presents as hypernasal speech and is more common after cleft repair in PRS patients. If VPI persists after palatoplasty, secondary procedures may include:

  • Pharyngeal flap surgery — tissue bridge from the posterior pharyngeal wall to the soft palate
  • Sphincter pharyngoplasty — posterior pharyngeal wall augmentation to reduce the velopharyngeal gap
  • Posterior pharyngeal wall injection — filler injection to reduce the gap (less invasive, may be temporary)

VPI assessment typically occurs around age 3–4, when a child’s speech is developed enough to evaluate nasal emission and resonance.

Critical Distinction: VPI Requires Surgery, Not Just Speech Therapy (R533)

A common misconception is that speech therapy alone can resolve VPI. Speech therapy cannot fix VPI — VPI is a structural problem (the velum physically cannot close the velopharyngeal port), not a motor learning problem. Speech therapy addresses the compensatory articulation patterns that develop because of VPI, but the VPI itself requires surgical correction (pharyngeal flap, sphincter pharyngoplasty, or posterior wall augmentation).

The critical timeline: if perceptual speech evaluation at age 3–4 reveals persistent hypernasality and nasal air emission despite consistent speech therapy, proceed to instrumental assessment (nasendoscopy or videofluoroscopy) immediately rather than continuing speech therapy and “waiting to see.” Delaying surgical correction beyond age 4–5 allows compensatory articulation patterns to become deeply habitual, making post-surgical speech therapy longer and more difficult.

In Marshall syndrome, where tissue laxity from defective collagen XI increases both the initial VPI rate and the risk of late-onset VPI, this urgency is amplified. The speech-language pathologist should be empowered to refer directly for instrumental VPI assessment when clinical signs are present — this should not require a separate craniofacial team appointment as a gatekeeper.

VPI and Tissue Laxity in COL11A1 Mutations

The higher VPI rate in PRS patients compared to isolated cleft palate patients (33–47% vs. ~19% requiring secondary surgery) is not fully explained by the wider cleft alone. In Marshall syndrome, the underlying tissue laxity from defective type XI collagen contributes an additional mechanism:

  • Velar muscle laxity: The muscles of the soft palate (levator veli palatini, tensor veli palatini) are enveloped in connective tissue that relies on functional collagen for structural integrity. Defective collagen XI may reduce the mechanical efficiency of these muscles, impairing the velopharyngeal seal even after anatomically successful repair.
  • Tissue stretch over time: Even when palatoplasty achieves initial closure, the repaired palatal tissue in a COL11A1 mutation carrier may gradually stretch under the repetitive mechanical stress of speech, swallowing, and sneezing. This progressive tissue elongation can produce late-onset VPI — competent velopharyngeal function at age 3 that deteriorates by age 6 or 7.
  • Pharyngeal wall compliance: The posterior pharyngeal wall must meet the elevating soft palate to create a seal. In connective tissue disorders, increased pharyngeal wall compliance (laxity) means the wall gives way instead of providing a firm backstop, requiring greater palatal excursion to achieve closure.
Clinical implication: VPI monitoring in children with Marshall syndrome should extend beyond the standard age 3–4 assessment. Annual perceptual speech evaluation through at least age 8, with nasendoscopy or videofluoroscopy if any deterioration is noted, is recommended. The craniofacial team should be aware that late-onset VPI is a possibility in COL11A1 mutation carriers due to progressive tissue laxity.

COL11A1 as a Recognized Cause of Pierre Robin Sequence

Genetic studies of syndromic Pierre Robin sequence have increasingly identified COL11A1 mutations as one of the most common single-gene causes of PRS. When a neonate presents with Pierre Robin sequence, the differential diagnosis for syndromic causes should include Stickler syndrome type II (COL11A1) and Marshall syndrome prominently. Key points from the genetic literature:

  • Up to 35% of syndromic PRS is attributable to collagen gene mutations (COL11A1, COL11A2, COL2A1), with COL11A1 being the most common among these
  • Genetic testing at PRS presentation: Any neonate with PRS should receive genetic evaluation that includes COL11A1 sequencing. Early identification of the underlying collagen disorder changes the management trajectory — adding ophthalmology surveillance for retinal detachment, audiology monitoring for sensorineural hearing loss, and orthopedic awareness of early disc degeneration
  • The MDO decision in COL11A1 carriers: When mandibular distraction osteogenesis is being considered for airway management, the craniofacial team should know whether COL11A1 mutation has been confirmed. The consolidation phase may need to be extended (8–10 weeks rather than 6–8) because bone regeneration depends on collagen-mediated endochondral ossification, which may proceed more slowly in the context of defective type XI collagen. Distraction rate may also need adjustment.

Speech Development

Children with Pierre Robin sequence and cleft palate face predictable speech challenges that, with early intervention, can be significantly mitigated.

How Cleft Palate Affects Speech

  • Hypernasality — excess air escaping through the nose during speech, giving a characteristic nasal quality to vowels and voiced consonants
  • Nasal air emission — audible air escape during pressure consonants (p, b, t, d, k, g, s, z)
  • Compensatory articulations — the child unconsciously develops alternative ways to produce sounds, using the glottis (glottal stops) or pharynx instead of the lips and tongue tip; these become habitual and require specific therapy to correct
  • Reduced intraoral pressure — inability to build up air pressure in the mouth affects plosive and fricative sounds
  • Middle ear effusion — chronic middle ear fluid (common with cleft palate) causes conductive hearing loss that compounds speech delay; this overlaps with the sensorineural hearing loss already present in Marshall syndrome
Double hearing impact in Marshall syndrome: Children with Marshall syndrome already have sensorineural hearing loss from the COL11A1 mutation. Adding conductive hearing loss from chronic middle ear effusion (caused by cleft palate) creates a mixed hearing loss that can be profoundly disabling for speech development. Aggressive management of middle ear disease (PE tubes, hearing aids) is critical.

Speech-Language Delay: The Scope of the Problem

69.5% Vocabulary Delay in Craniofacial Anomalies
Research on children with craniofacial anomalies (including cleft palate and Pierre Robin sequence) demonstrates that 69.5% show significant vocabulary delay — not just articulation differences, but measurable delays in expressive and receptive vocabulary development. This is far higher than many clinicians and families expect, and it means that speech-language pathology involvement must extend beyond articulation and VPI correction to include comprehensive language assessment and intervention.

The vocabulary delay in craniofacial anomalies arises from multiple converging mechanisms, and in Marshall syndrome these mechanisms compound:

  • Hearing loss: Sensorineural hearing loss from COL11A1 mutation plus conductive hearing loss from chronic middle ear effusion creates a mixed hearing loss that profoundly affects the auditory input needed for vocabulary acquisition. Children learn words by hearing them — reduced auditory input directly reduces vocabulary growth.
  • Reduced babbling in infancy: Infants with Pierre Robin sequence and cleft palate produce fewer consonant-like babbles in the first year due to compromised oral anatomy. Babbling is the foundation for expressive language; reduced babbling delays the first words.
  • Chronic illness burden: Hospitalizations, surgeries, and medical appointments during the critical language-learning period (ages 1–3) reduce the child’s exposure to the rich, interactive language environments that drive vocabulary growth.
  • Compensatory communication patterns: Children who know they are hard to understand may use fewer words, shorter sentences, and simpler vocabulary to avoid communication breakdowns. Over time, these patterns become self-limiting.
Early intervention is critical and must be comprehensive. Speech-language pathology for a child with Marshall syndrome and Pierre Robin should include: (1) regular standardized vocabulary assessment (e.g., PPVT-5, EVT-3) starting by age 2, not just articulation screening; (2) parent-implemented language stimulation strategies (dialogic reading, language-rich routines) from infancy; (3) audiology coordination to ensure hearing aids are optimally fitted and consistently worn during all waking hours; and (4) consideration of early augmentative communication strategies if expressive language delay is significant, to prevent the frustration and withdrawal that compound the delay.

Speech Outcomes

With timely palate repair and appropriate speech therapy, the majority of children with PRS achieve functional, intelligible speech. However, a significant minority (33–47% in PRS studies) require secondary surgical procedures to address persistent VPI. Early and consistent speech therapy — especially addressing compensatory articulations before they become habitual — is the strongest predictor of good outcomes.

Mandibular Growth: Does the Jaw Catch Up?

This is one of the most important long-term questions for families.

Isolated vs. Syndromic: Different Trajectories

In isolated Pierre Robin (no underlying genetic syndrome), significant mandibular catch-up growth often occurs during the first 2–4 years of life. Many children with isolated PRS have essentially normal jaw proportions by adulthood.

In syndromic Pierre Robin (including Marshall syndrome), the picture is more complex:

  • Some mandibular catch-up growth does occur, particularly during the first 2 years
  • However, the ongoing collagen defect means growth may plateau earlier and remain incomplete
  • A 2025 systematic review found that certain syndromic PRS groups (including Stickler syndrome, which shares the COL11A1 gene) show some spontaneous mandibular growth but with less complete catch-up than isolated PRS
  • The midface retrusion characteristic of Marshall syndrome compounds the appearance of jaw discrepancy
Growth monitoring protocol: Serial cephalometric (lateral skull) X-rays are recommended at ages 2, 5, 8, 12, and 16 to track mandibular and maxillary growth trajectories. These measurements help the craniofacial team anticipate whether orthognathic surgery may be needed in adolescence.

Orthognathic Surgery Considerations

If significant mandibular hypoplasia persists into adolescence, orthognathic surgery (surgical jaw repositioning) may be considered after skeletal maturity (typically age 16–18 for girls, 18–20 for boys). In Marshall syndrome:

  • Bone quality and healing may be affected by the COL11A1 mutation — discuss with the surgeon
  • TMJ integrity should be assessed before surgery (see TMJ & Craniofacial)
  • Combined maxillary-mandibular procedures may be needed if midface retrusion is also significant
  • Orthodontic preparation typically begins 12–18 months before surgery

Dental Issues

Children with Marshall syndrome and Pierre Robin sequence face compounding dental challenges from multiple directions.

COL11A1-Related Dental Findings

  • Enamel defects — Type XI collagen is expressed during tooth development; defects have been reported in approximately 11% of Marshall syndrome patients and up to 33% of Stickler syndrome patients
  • Dental anomalies — May include abnormal tooth shape, delayed eruption, or structural irregularities
  • Ectodermal involvement — Marshall syndrome has documented ectodermal abnormalities including dental structural defects

Pierre Robin-Related Dental Challenges

  • Crowding — The small mandible provides insufficient arch length for normal tooth alignment; crowding in the lower arch is common and may begin with the primary dentition
  • Crossbite — Mandibular hypoplasia often creates a posterior crossbite; may need early orthodontic intervention
  • Ectopic eruption — Teeth may erupt in abnormal positions due to arch length deficiency
  • Cleft-related dental issues — Teeth adjacent to the cleft may be missing, malformed, or have disrupted enamel

Anesthesia Implications

Pierre Robin sequence is one of the most well-recognized causes of anticipated difficult airway in pediatric anesthesia. Every child with PRS — and especially those with Marshall syndrome — requires specialized airway planning for every procedure requiring sedation or general anesthesia, throughout their life.

For comprehensive anesthesia safety guidance, see the dedicated Anesthesia Safety page. Key PRS-specific considerations include:

Marshall syndrome multiplies the anesthesia risk: Beyond Pierre Robin airway challenges, Marshall syndrome adds cervical spine concerns (see Cervical Myelopathy), high myopia with retinal detachment risk (positioning considerations), and potential TMJ abnormalities (jaw opening limitations). Every anesthesia provider must know about all of these issues. See Anesthesia Safety for the complete checklist.

Dexmedetomidine

Dexmedetomidine (Precedex) is increasingly used in pediatric PRS cases because it provides sedation and analgesia while maintaining spontaneous breathing and upper airway tone — critical in a patient with a compromised airway. Its short half-life makes it suitable for ambulatory procedures. Discuss this option with the anesthesiologist for any planned procedure.

Long-Term Craniofacial Outcomes

Adulthood: What to Expect

Long-term outcomes in syndromic Pierre Robin depend heavily on the underlying condition. In Marshall syndrome specifically:

  • Micrognathia — typically improves but may not fully resolve; some adults retain a noticeably recessed chin
  • Midface retrusion — the flat midface appearance characteristic of Marshall syndrome persists and may become more apparent as soft tissue thins with aging
  • TMJ problems — the COL11A1 mutation affects TMJ cartilage; early-onset TMJ degeneration and limited mouth opening are concerns (see TMJ & Craniofacial)
  • Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) — persistent mandibular hypoplasia and midface retrusion create ongoing risk for adult OSA; sleep studies should continue into adulthood (see Sleep Apnea)
  • Dental maintenance — ongoing orthodontic retention and dental surveillance are needed
  • Psychosocial — facial differences, hearing aids, and a history of multiple surgeries can affect self-image and social functioning; psychological support should be available throughout development and into adulthood

Obstructive Sleep Apnea Risk in Adulthood

While neonatal airway obstruction typically improves in the first 1–2 years, OSA can re-emerge or persist into adulthood in syndromic PRS. Contributing factors include:

  • Residual mandibular hypoplasia narrowing the posterior airway space
  • Midface retrusion reducing nasopharyngeal airway volume
  • Adenotonsillar hypertrophy (common in childhood, addressed surgically if needed)
  • Soft tissue laxity from collagen defect
  • Weight gain in adulthood exacerbating a borderline airway

Periodic sleep studies (polysomnography) should be performed at the craniofacial team’s recommendation, and any symptoms of daytime somnolence, snoring, or morning headaches should prompt evaluation. For detailed management, see Sleep Apnea & Airway Management.

Questions for the Craniofacial Team

Bring these questions to your craniofacial team, especially if your child has both Pierre Robin sequence and a confirmed COL11A1 mutation:
  1. Airway plan: "What is the stepwise plan if prone positioning is not sufficient? At what oxygen saturation threshold or apnea severity would you recommend MDO versus continued conservative management?"
  2. Collagen and healing: "Given the COL11A1 mutation, do you anticipate any changes to the surgical approach for palate repair — such as a two-stage repair, longer healing time, or modified tissue handling — to reduce fistula risk?"
  3. Jaw growth monitoring: "What is the schedule for cephalometric imaging to track mandibular growth? At what age would you make a decision about whether orthognathic surgery is likely to be needed?"
  4. Speech VPI risk: "Given the higher VPI rate in Pierre Robin patients, when will you assess velopharyngeal function after palate repair, and what are the secondary surgical options if VPI is present?"
  5. Anesthesia protocol: "Is there a documented airway management plan in my child’s medical record that follows them to every procedure, including dental work under sedation? Does the plan account for both Pierre Robin anatomy and Marshall syndrome spinal concerns?"
  6. Long-term follow-up: "At what age do patients typically transition from pediatric craniofacial care to adult care? What ongoing surveillance is recommended for OSA, TMJ function, and craniofacial changes in adulthood?"
