Decade-by-Decade Natural History

Understanding the typical progression helps anticipate problems before they become crises. Not every patient follows this timeline exactly, but the patterns are well-documented across the Stickler/Marshall literature.

Ages 0–5: Foundations

  • Mild spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia (abnormal vertebral/growth plate development) — may be subtle on imaging
  • Subtle vertebral flattening (platyspondyly) — often not clinically significant yet
  • Pronounced joint hypermobility — the child may seem unusually flexible, which is often mistaken for a positive trait
  • Pierre Robin sequence in approximately 24% — small jaw, possible cleft palate, neonatal airway challenges
  • Hearing and vision issues typically identified in this period

Ages 5–12: Warning Signs Emerge

  • Joint pain begins — this is frequently dismissed as "growing pains," which is a missed opportunity for early intervention
  • Scoliosis and/or kyphosis may become apparent
  • Vertebral endplate abnormalities and Schmorl's nodes (disc material herniating into vertebral bone) can appear on imaging
  • The earlier hypermobility begins to transition toward joint instability — the same lax ligaments that made the child flexible now fail to stabilize joints under load
  • This is the stage where your child likely is now — the pain is real, it has a structural explanation, and it should be taken seriously

Ages 12–18: Growth Spurt Acceleration

  • The adolescent growth spurt places enormous mechanical stress on already-compromised vertebrae
  • Schmorl's nodes may accelerate — the endplates are weakest during rapid growth
  • Scoliosis progression — curves that were mild can worsen rapidly during the growth spurt (this is why monitoring frequency increases — see Imaging Protocol)
  • Early disc degeneration visible on MRI — changes that wouldn't normally appear until age 40+ in the general population
  • Spinal ligament changes may begin (early ossification)

Ages 20s–30s: Chronic Pain Establishes

  • Chronic pain in weight-bearing joints and spine becomes the norm, not the exception
  • Radiographic osteoarthrosis — visible joint degeneration on X-rays
  • Spinal degeneration accelerates — disc space narrowing, osteophyte (bone spur) formation
  • Possible Forestier disease (diffuse idiopathic skeletal hyperostosis, or DISH) — pathological ossification of spinal ligaments
  • Activity limitations may require career and lifestyle adaptations

Ages 40s–50s: Advanced Degeneration

  • Joint replacement may be needed for hips, knees, or other severely affected joints
  • Spinal stenosis risk increases — the spinal canal narrows due to bone and ligament changes, potentially compressing nerves
  • Cumulative effects of decades of abnormal loading and matrix degradation
Important Context: This progression is not inevitable in its worst form. Early intervention — particularly physical therapy, activity modification, and proactive monitoring — can meaningfully slow progression and preserve function. The point of understanding this timeline is to act ahead of it, not to feel defeated by it.
