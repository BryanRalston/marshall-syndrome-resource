Midface Hypoplasia & Facial Features in Marshall Syndrome

Why This Page Matters
Midface hypoplasia is one of the most clinically distinctive features of Marshall syndrome — and one of the most consequential. It is not merely a cosmetic finding. The underdevelopment of the midface directly drives airway risk, dental complications, hearing dysfunction, anesthesia danger, and speech difficulty. Understanding the anatomy explains why so many organ systems are affected by what looks, on the surface, like a facial difference.

What Is Midface Hypoplasia? Established

Midface hypoplasia is underdevelopment of the maxilla and surrounding midface bones. The maxilla is the central bony structure of the middle third of the face — it contains the upper dental arch, forms the floor of the nasal cavity and orbit, contributes to the cheekbones, and anchors the soft tissue of the nose and upper lip. When the maxilla and adjacent midface structures do not grow to their full dimensions, the entire middle of the face appears flat, sunken, or retruded relative to the forehead and lower jaw.

In Marshall syndrome, midface hypoplasia results directly from the underlying COL11A1 mutation. It is present from birth, is most visually prominent in infancy and early childhood, and persists into adult life — though it typically becomes proportionally less conspicuous as facial growth progresses.

Anatomy of the Midface
The midface occupies the region between the orbital rims (eye sockets) above and the upper dental arch below. Key structures include the maxilla, nasal bones, ethmoid and sphenoid bones, and the nasal septum. The nasal septum — the cartilaginous and bony partition inside the nose — functions as a primary growth center that pushes the midface forward during fetal and early postnatal development. Disruption of this growth center is central to why COL11A1 mutations cause midface hypoplasia.

Recognizing the Marshall Facies Over Time
The Marshall facies is most pronounced in infancy and toddlerhood. As the face elongates through childhood and adolescence, the flat midface becomes proportionally less obvious. By adulthood, many individuals with Marshall syndrome do not appear strikingly dysmorphic to an untrained observer — but the underlying anatomy is unchanged, and its functional consequences remain.

Why Does COL11A1 Cause Midface Hypoplasia? Supported

The connection between a collagen gene and facial bone shape is not immediately obvious. Understanding it requires knowing where and how collagen XI functions during facial development.

The Nasal Septum as a Growth Engine

The nasal septum is not just a partition between the nostrils — it is one of the primary growth centers of the midface. During fetal development and through early childhood, the cartilaginous nasal septum acts as a thrust plate: as septal cartilage proliferates, it pushes the maxilla and surrounding midface bones forward and downward, driving the characteristic anterior projection of the human midface.

Collagen XI is a major structural component of cartilage extracellular matrix. It forms thin fibrils within the collagen II network that regulate fibril diameter and maintain cartilage stiffness and compressive resistance. In individuals with Marshall syndrome:

  • COL11A1 is expressed in nasal septal cartilage during fetal and early postnatal development.
  • Abnormal collagen XI disrupts the structural integrity of septal cartilage — reducing its mechanical stiffness and growth capacity.
  • Impaired septal cartilage growth means the thrust plate cannot push the midface forward with normal force.
  • The result is reduced anterior projection of the maxilla and surrounding bones — midface hypoplasia.
The Same Gene, Multiple Cartilages
COL11A1 is expressed wherever cartilage forms — the nasal septum, auricular cartilage, articular joint cartilage, the nucleus pulposus of spinal discs, vitreous humor of the eye, and the inner ear. This single gene's wide expression explains why Marshall syndrome affects so many organ systems: ears, eyes, joints, spine, and airways all depend on cartilage or collagen XI-rich connective tissue for their structure and function.

Mandibular Involvement

The mandible (lower jaw) is also affected in a subset of individuals with Marshall syndrome. Mandibular cartilage at the condylar growth center expresses COL11A1, and underdevelopment here produces micrognathia — a small or recessed lower jaw. When micrognathia is present at birth and accompanied by glossoptosis and airway obstruction, the resulting pattern is Pierre Robin sequence, which occurs in approximately 24% of Marshall syndrome cases.

How the Face Changes with Age

Midface hypoplasia is not static. The relationship between the underdeveloped midface and the rest of the face shifts through childhood and adolescence — but it is important to understand what actually changes and what does not.

An Important Clarification
The midface does not “grow out of it.” The absolute dimensions of the midface remain below normal throughout life. What changes is the proportion — as the lower face and neurocranium continue growing, the flat midface becomes a less dominant visual feature. The underlying bony underdevelopment, and all of its functional consequences, persist into adulthood.

Functional Consequences: Why Midface Hypoplasia Matters Medically

Midface hypoplasia is not a cosmetic concern alone. The underdevelopment of the maxilla and surrounding structures reduces space in multiple anatomical compartments, with downstream effects across several organ systems.

Airway and Breathing

A hypoplastic midface reduces the volume of the nasopharyngeal space — the corridor behind the nose and above the palate through which most breathing occurs. Consequences include:

  • Nasal obstruction: reduced nasal passage cross-section causes chronic or intermittent nasal blockage and obligate mouth breathing.
  • Sleep-disordered breathing: narrowed nasopharynx combined with low tonsillar/adenoid tissue creates conditions for obstructive sleep apnea. See Sleep-Disordered Breathing for full assessment and management details.
  • Chronic mouth breathing: secondary consequences include dry mouth, altered dental arch development, and increased upper respiratory infection frequency.
Anesthesia Risk — Every Anesthesiologist Must Know This
Midface hypoplasia combined with micrognathia (even mild) creates a predictably difficult airway for general anesthesia. Flat midface reduces the ability to form an effective mask seal for bag-mask ventilation. Micrognathia limits mouth opening and restricts laryngoscope blade angle. Together, these can make both mask ventilation AND intubation difficult — sometimes unexpectedly so, even in individuals who do not appear severely dysmorphic. This is why Marshall syndrome appears on every anesthesia risk list, and why pre-anesthetic airway assessment and specialist involvement are mandatory for any elective procedure. Never assume the airway will be straightforward.

Dental and Orthodontic

Maxillary hypoplasia directly reduces the space available for the upper dental arch. Consequences include:

  • Dental crowding: too many teeth for the available arch length, requiring extraction or expansion.
  • Malocclusion: the underprojected maxilla creates a tendency toward Class III bite (lower jaw protruding relative to upper), though this varies. Some individuals have Class II patterns if micrognathia is also significant.
  • Crossbite: narrow maxillary arch can produce posterior crossbite requiring palatal expansion.
  • Palatal arch shape: high-arched palate is frequently associated, further reducing nasal floor volume and contributing to airway narrowing.

See Dental & Orthodontic Management for treatment details.

Eustachian Tube and Hearing

The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx, equalizing pressure and draining fluid from the middle ear. Midface hypoplasia alters the geometry of the nasopharynx and the angle at which the Eustachian tube opens into it. This anatomical disruption impairs Eustachian tube function and is a major contributor to the high rates of chronic otitis media with effusion (“glue ear”) and conductive hearing loss seen in Marshall syndrome. See Hearing Management for the full picture.

Speech and Velopharyngeal Function

The palate and nasopharynx work together to separate the nasal and oral cavities during speech. Midface and palatal underdevelopment can alter this velopharyngeal mechanism, contributing to hypernasality or velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI). See VPI & Nasal Speech for details on assessment and management.

Sinus Development

The paranasal sinuses are air-filled spaces that develop within the maxillary and surrounding facial bones. In midface hypoplasia, these spaces are underdeveloped — most notably the maxillary sinuses. Underdeveloped sinuses have altered drainage pathways and reduced mucociliary clearance, contributing to:

  • Chronic sinusitis and recurrent acute sinusitis
  • Nasal congestion that does not respond to typical interventions
  • Headache patterns related to sinus pressure

Diagnostic Assessment

The diagnosis of midface hypoplasia in Marshall syndrome is primarily clinical, made by a clinical geneticist, craniofacial specialist, or experienced pediatrician who recognizes the facial gestalt in the context of other Marshall syndrome features. For surgical planning or detailed characterization, imaging is used.

Clinical Evaluation

  • Facial examination documenting the characteristic features (flat midface, nasal bridge, nares, philtrum, facial proportions)
  • Comparison of facial proportions using standardized facial thirds analysis
  • Photographic documentation at diagnosis and periodically through growth for longitudinal tracking
  • Assessment of functional consequences: airway patency, dental occlusion, hearing, speech

Cephalometric Analysis

A lateral skull X-ray with cephalometric tracing provides quantifiable data on midface position and skeletal relationships. Key measurements include:

  • SNA angle: the angle between the sella (center of the pituitary fossa), nasion (bridge of the nose), and point A (deepest point of the maxillary curve) — quantifies maxillary protrusion or retrusion relative to the skull base. Reduced in maxillary hypoplasia.
  • ANB angle: the difference between SNA and SNB (mandibular position) — quantifies the skeletal relationship between upper and lower jaws. A negative or reduced ANB suggests maxillary retrusion relative to the mandible (Class III tendency).
  • Nasal height and projection measurements: assess the degree of nasal underdevelopment.

3D CT Imaging

Three-dimensional computed tomography provides detailed bony anatomy for surgical planning when midface advancement or other craniofacial surgery is being considered. It is not routinely required for all patients with midface hypoplasia — only when surgical intervention is being evaluated.

Treatment Options

It is important to establish a baseline expectation: most individuals with Marshall syndrome do not require surgical treatment for midface hypoplasia. Functional interventions targeting the downstream consequences (airway, dentition, hearing) are far more commonly needed than skeletal surgery targeting the midface itself.

Orthodontic Interventions (During Growth)

  • Maxillary expansion: a palatal expander widens the maxillary arch to address crowding and crossbite. Most effective during childhood when the mid-palatal suture is still unfused.
  • Protraction facemask (reverse pull headgear): worn externally, this appliance applies forward traction to the maxilla through hooks on braces or palatal anchors. Effective only during active facial growth (generally before age 10–12). Can produce modest improvement in maxillary protrusion and ANB angle. Results are limited and partially relapse after growth ceases.
Timing Matters for Orthodontic Protraction
The window for protraction facemask therapy closes at or near the end of mid-palatal suture fusion, typically in the early teen years. Families should be aware that orthodontic intervention for maxillary underdevelopment is most impactful when started in middle childhood. A referral to a pediatric orthodontist or craniofacial orthodontist is appropriate at the time of Marshall syndrome diagnosis, even if treatment does not start immediately.

Surgical Options (Skeletally Mature, Severe Cases)

Surgical midface advancement is considered only when:

  • Midface hypoplasia causes significant functional impairment (severe airway obstruction, significant sleep apnea refractory to other treatment, severe malocclusion not amenable to orthodontic correction alone)
  • Skeletal growth is complete (typically late adolescence or adulthood)
  • Orthodontic preparation has been completed

Surgical approaches include:

  • Le Fort I osteotomy: moves the maxilla (tooth-bearing upper jaw) forward and/or downward at the level of the nasal floor. Addresses dental occlusion and modest midface projection.
  • Le Fort III osteotomy: moves the entire midface skeleton (including orbital rims and zygomas) forward as a single unit. Used for severe midface retrusion with orbital involvement. A major procedure with significant recovery.
  • Distraction osteogenesis: rather than moving bone acutely and fixing it, distraction slowly advances bone by placing a device that incrementally separates the osteotomy cut over weeks. Allows larger advancements with better stability. Can be performed during childhood in severe cases where waiting for skeletal maturity is not feasible due to functional severity.
Collagen Fragility and Surgical Planning
The COL11A1 mutation affects connective tissue throughout the body. Surgeons planning midface advancement in Marshall syndrome must be aware that wound healing, bone healing, and tissue quality may differ from the general population. Anesthesia planning for any craniofacial surgery requires detailed airway assessment and senior anesthesia involvement given the difficult airway anatomy. See Anesthesia Considerations.

Rhinoplasty in Adults

Some adults with Marshall syndrome consider rhinoplasty for the characteristic short, upturned nose. Important considerations include:

  • Collagen XI is present in nasal cartilage — the same cartilage being modified surgically. The structural properties of this cartilage may differ from typical patients, affecting how it heals, holds sutures, and responds to grafting.
  • Rhinoplasty in Marshall syndrome should be performed by a surgeon experienced with connective tissue disorders, with explicit discussion of the underlying collagen defect.
  • Results and stability may differ from the general rhinoplasty population.

Functional Interventions

These address the downstream consequences of midface hypoplasia and are more commonly indicated than skeletal surgery:

  • Adenoidectomy / adenotonsillectomy: reduces lymphoid tissue bulk contributing to nasopharyngeal obstruction. Often the first surgical intervention for sleep-disordered breathing.
  • Pressure equalization (PE) tubes: surgically placed through the tympanic membrane to bypass Eustachian tube dysfunction and drain middle ear fluid. See Hearing Management.
  • CPAP or BiPAP: positive airway pressure therapy for obstructive sleep apnea when adenotonsillectomy is insufficient. See Sleep-Disordered Breathing.
  • Nasal saline irrigation and topical therapy: for chronic sinusitis management.

Marshall vs. Stickler: Facial Features Compared

Midface hypoplasia is one of the features most useful for clinically distinguishing Marshall syndrome from Stickler syndrome, particularly Stickler Type 1. This comparison is relevant because both conditions are caused by mutations in genes encoding collagen fibrils (COL11A1, COL11A2, COL2A1), share overlapping features, and are sometimes misdiagnosed as each other.

When Diagnosis Is Uncertain
If a patient has COL11A1 pathogenic variant with prominent midface hypoplasia, thick calvaria, severe eye findings, and significant hearing loss, the clinical picture favors Marshall syndrome. If the same gene variant is found but the facial features are mild and the skull is normal, the diagnosis may be classified as Stickler Type 2. In some families, different members with the same variant are classified differently by different clinicians. See Marshall vs. Stickler Syndrome for a full comparison.

Coordinated Care for Midface Hypoplasia

Because midface hypoplasia has consequences across multiple systems, care is best coordinated through a craniofacial team or multidisciplinary clinic where available. Key specialists involved include:

  • Clinical geneticist: diagnosis, variant interpretation, coordination of surveillance
  • Craniofacial surgeon / plastic surgeon: evaluation for surgical candidacy, surgical planning if indicated
  • Pediatric orthodontist or craniofacial orthodontist: dental arch management, protraction therapy during growth
  • Otolaryngologist (ENT): airway, sinusitis, Eustachian tube dysfunction, PE tubes, adenotonsillectomy
  • Pediatric dentist: caries prevention, monitoring of dental eruption sequence
  • Audiologist: hearing assessment and monitoring
  • Speech-language pathologist: VPI and nasal resonance evaluation
  • Pulmonologist / sleep medicine: sleep-disordered breathing evaluation and management
  • Anesthesiologist: pre-operative airway assessment for any procedure requiring sedation or general anesthesia
