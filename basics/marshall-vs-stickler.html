Marshall vs. Stickler: Why the Distinction Matters

Evidence confidence guide
Established Published, peer-reviewed consensus Supported Published evidence in related conditions Hypothesis Reasonable extrapolation, not yet proven Speculative Theoretical, requires validation

Your geneticist may have told you Marshall syndrome and Stickler syndrome are “the same thing.” The peer-reviewed literature says otherwise — and the distinction matters for your child’s care.

This page exists because parents have been told this. Repeatedly. By well-meaning specialists who are relying on a single 1999 paper. The full body of evidence tells a different story, and understanding that story gives you the tools to advocate for the monitoring and precautions your child actually needs.

The Debate: Same Gene, Different Disease Established

Both Marshall syndrome and Stickler syndrome type II are caused by mutations in COL11A1, the gene encoding the α1 chain of type XI collagen, located on chromosome 1p21. Because they share the same gene, some researchers have proposed they are simply two names for the same condition.

The key paper behind the “lumping” argument is Annunen et al. (1999), which proposed that Marshall and Stickler type II are allelic variants of the same disorder. This paper has been widely cited and is the source most geneticists rely on when they tell families there is no meaningful difference.

But the evidence for keeping them separate is substantial:

  • Aymé & Preus (1984) performed rigorous cluster analysis on 53 clinical signs across 18 patients and found two statistically distinct phenotypic groups. Their conclusion: “There is no objective reason to consider that these two syndromes are not separate dominant disorders.”
  • Khalifa et al. (2014) provided “further evidence of a distinct phenotypic entity,” documenting features in Marshall patients that are absent or rare in Stickler type II.
  • A 2021 Clinical Dysmorphology review acknowledged that the distinction is real and clinically important.
  • OMIM maintains Marshall syndrome as a separate entry (#154780), distinct from Stickler syndrome type II (#604841).
  • Orphanet lists Marshall syndrome separately (ORPHA:560).

The world’s two largest rare disease databases both maintain the distinction. That is not an accident.

The Molecular Difference: Why Mutation Type Matters Established

This is the core scientific argument. Marshall and Stickler type II share the same gene, but the type of mutation is different, and that difference produces fundamentally different effects on the protein.

GeneCOL11A1COL11A1
Mutation typeSplice site mutations in 54-bp exons → exon skippingMissense and other variants

MechanismDominant-negative (mutant protein poisons normal collagen)Variable (can be milder)

Here is the key insight that makes this distinction matter at the molecular level:

Null alleles (haploinsufficiency — losing one copy of the gene entirely) can leave carriers clinically unaffected. When the body simply makes less collagen from one gene copy, the remaining copy can sometimes compensate. But Marshall’s splice site mutations do something worse: they produce a structurally abnormal protein that still gets incorporated into the collagen triple helix. Once there, the shortened chain actively sabotages the structure it joins.

Making bad collagen is worse than making less collagen. This is the dominant-negative mechanism, and it is why Marshall syndrome tends to sit at the more severe end of the COL11A1 spectrum.

What’s Different in Marshall: The Features That “Weigh Heavier”

Several clinical features are either more severe in Marshall or entirely absent from Stickler syndrome. These are the features that get missed when the diagnosis is collapsed:

What Gets Missed If Doctors “Treat It Like Stickler”

This is why the label matters. A diagnosis is not just a name — it determines which screening protocols are followed, which specialists are consulted, and which risks are communicated to schools, coaches, and emergency rooms.
  • Thermoregulation: Ectodermal features are absent from Stickler. A child with reduced sweating needs heat precautions communicated to schools, coaches, and the ER. A “Stickler” diagnosis would never prompt this screening or these precautions.
  • Airway severity: If an anesthesiologist prepares for “Stickler,” they may underestimate the airway difficulty caused by Marshall’s more severe midface hypoplasia. This is a safety issue in any procedure requiring intubation.
  • Hearing trajectory: Marshall hearing loss can be more severe and progressive than typical Stickler type II → needs more frequent monitoring (every 6 months in childhood rather than annually).
  • Skull findings: Calvarial thickening and intracranial calcifications need baseline imaging. These are not part of standard Stickler protocols because they do not occur in Stickler.
  • Skeletal severity: The dominant-negative mechanism means more severe collagen disruption → potentially earlier and more aggressive joint and spine degeneration than would be expected under a Stickler diagnosis.

The Six Types of Stickler Syndrome

To understand where Marshall fits in the broader picture, it helps to see that even within Stickler syndrome, there are six recognized types with different genes, inheritance patterns, and clinical risks:

Even within Stickler, these six types have meaningfully different risks. A child with type I needs aggressive retinal surveillance. A child with type III does not need an ophthalmologist for vitreous monitoring at all. Lumping them together already loses critical nuance. Adding Marshall — with its ectodermal features, calvarial thickening, and intracranial calcifications — into that same bucket compounds the problem.

Additional Monitoring Marshall Patients Need

Beyond standard Stickler protocols. If your child has been diagnosed with Marshall syndrome (or has a COL11A1 splice site mutation in a 54-bp exon), these are the additional evaluations to discuss with your medical team:
  1. Thermoregulation assessment — sweat testing or clinical counseling about heat intolerance. Communicate findings to schools and activity supervisors.
  2. Skull imaging — baseline assessment of calvarial thickening and intracranial calcifications (falx, tentorial, meningeal).
  3. Audiological monitoring every 6 months in childhood — Marshall hearing loss can be more progressive than Stickler and may reach severe levels.
  4. Facial/airway documentation — explicit difficult airway flag in medical records for any procedure requiring sedation or intubation.
  5. Growth monitoring — with potential endocrine evaluation if growth velocity falls off expected curves.
  6. Dental and ectodermal evaluation — assessment of dental development, skin, hair, and nail features.
  7. Baseline skeletal survey — evaluation for platyspondyly, epiphyseal irregularities, and spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia.

The Advocacy Argument

Ready to use. The following paragraph summarizes the case for maintaining the Marshall diagnosis. You can bring it to appointments, include it in letters to specialists, or use it when communicating with your child’s care team.
“Marshall syndrome and Stickler type II share the same gene (COL11A1), but Marshall is caused by a specific class of mutation — splice site mutations in 54-bp exons — that produces a more damaging protein through dominant-negative effects. This results in features absent from Stickler, including calvarial thickening, intracranial calcifications, ectodermal dysplasia with reduced sweating, and more severe midface hypoplasia. OMIM, Orphanet, and the peer-reviewed literature maintain them as distinct entities. Collapsing the diagnosis into ‘just Stickler’ means missing ectodermal screening, underestimating airway risk, and under-monitoring skeletal and neurological features specific to Marshall syndrome.”
