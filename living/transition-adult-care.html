Transition to Adult Care — The 10-Year Runway

Why Transition Planning Starts Now

The data is alarming. A 2023 ERN ReCONNET survey across 20 countries found that 50% of centers lack formal transition guidelines for patients with rare connective tissue disorders. Twenty-nine percent of centers transfer patients based on age alone, with no readiness assessment whatsoever. Only 13% use validated readiness questionnaires to determine whether a young person is actually prepared for adult care.

For a child with Marshall syndrome, the transition challenge is compounded by a fundamental problem: adult providers are often completely unfamiliar with the condition. Pediatric geneticists and multidisciplinary clinics develop deep expertise over years of managing your child. When that child turns 18, they may walk into an adult rheumatologist’s or orthopedist’s office where the provider has never seen another Marshall syndrome patient in their career.

The runway is 10 years. Start at age 9–10. Complete by age 18–20. This is not something you figure out senior year of high school — it is a deliberate, phased process that builds skills, identifies providers, and creates documentation over a full decade.

The Marshall Transition Challenge

Unlike a child with a single-organ condition, a child with Marshall syndrome has 6 or more specialist relationships that ALL need to transition simultaneously:

  • Ophthalmology — lifelong retinal detachment surveillance, myopia management, cataract and glaucoma monitoring
  • Audiology — progressive sensorineural hearing loss, hearing aid management, cochlear implant candidacy
  • Orthopedics / Spine — early-onset osteoarthritis, spinal stenosis, scoliosis follow-up, possible hardware revision
  • Genetics — the care coordinator, the provider who understands how all systems connect
  • Pain Management — chronic multimodal pain requiring ongoing multidisciplinary strategy
  • Dental / Craniofacial — midface hypoplasia, Pierre Robin sequence follow-up, TMJ management

Additional specialists may include: Cardiology (mitral valve prolapse screening), ENT (airway and sleep apnea), Neurology (cervical myelopathy, autonomic dysfunction), and Rheumatology (inflammatory vs. degenerative joint disease distinction).

The hardest transition of all is genetics. Adult geneticists who actively manage connective tissue disorders are rare. Most genetics clinics are pediatric-focused. University-based connective tissue disorder programs (often branded as “Marfan” or “hereditary connective tissue disorder” clinics) are the best starting point, even though Marshall syndrome is not Marfan.

Pediatric multidisciplinary clinics — like the Stickler Clinic at Massachusetts General Hospital — often do not have adult equivalents. The coordinated, one-stop model that served your child for years may simply not exist in the adult world. This means the family must actively build a new coordination network, which takes time and cannot be done in a crisis.

The Medical Summary Document

Create ONE comprehensive document that travels with the young adult. This is the single most important transition tool. Every new provider receives a copy at the first visit. It prevents retelling the entire medical history from scratch and ensures nothing critical is omitted.

The medical summary should contain:

  • Diagnosis: Marshall syndrome, COL11A1 mutation — include the specific variant (e.g., “splice site variant in exon 50 of COL11A1”)
  • Complete medication list with doses, frequencies, and purposes for each
  • Surgical history: All procedures with dates, surgeons, institutions, and key details
  • All specialist contacts: Names, phone numbers, institutions, and next appointment dates
  • Emergency protocols: Retinal detachment (sudden flashes/floaters = emergency), anesthetic resistance considerations, cervical spine precautions for intubation and procedures
  • Current accommodation needs: What was formerly covered under the 504 plan — these translate to ADA workplace accommodations
  • Genetic test results: Full report from clinical genetic testing, including variant classification

Update this document annually or after any significant medical change. Provide copies to each new adult provider. Keep digital and printed versions. Store a copy on the young adult’s phone for emergency access.

Self-Advocacy Skills to Build

These are the specific skills and phrases that should become second nature before transfer:

  • The elevator explanation: “I have Marshall syndrome, a genetic collagen disorder that affects my eyes, ears, joints, and spine.” Practice this until it flows naturally.
  • Knowing when to seek emergency care: Vision changes (flashes, floaters, curtain across vision = possible retinal detachment), new neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, electric shocks down the spine = possible cervical cord compression).
  • Requesting accommodations in college or the workplace — ADA protections replace the 504 plan at age 18. The young adult must learn to self-identify and request, because schools and employers will not proactively offer.
  • Managing medication refills and pharmacy interactions: Calling in refills, understanding prior authorizations, handling insurance denials for medications.
  • Navigating insurance: Understanding copays, deductibles, out-of-pocket maximums, prior authorizations, and the appeals process when claims are denied.
  • The universal opener for every new medical encounter: “I need you to know that I have a collagen disorder.” This single sentence primes every provider — surgeon, ER physician, dentist, anesthesiologist — to think about connective tissue implications before they proceed.

Insurance Transition

  • Parent’s insurance covers until age 26 (ACA mandate). This applies regardless of student status, marital status, or whether the young adult lives at home. Use this buffer period wisely.
  • Plan ahead for age 26: Employer-sponsored insurance, ACA marketplace plans, or Medicaid (if income-eligible). Do not wait until the last month — start researching options at age 24–25.
  • Pre-existing condition protections under the ACA are CRITICAL for rare disease patients. Under current law, insurers cannot deny coverage or charge higher premiums based on Marshall syndrome or any other pre-existing condition. Understand this protection and monitor any legislative changes that could affect it.
  • See the Insurance Navigation page for detailed coverage strategies, prior authorization tips, and appeals processes.

TRAQ Score — Measuring Readiness

The Transition Readiness Assessment Questionnaire (TRAQ) is a validated tool specifically designed to measure whether a young person is ready for the transfer to adult care.

  • Score interpretation: A TRAQ score >4.0 predicts successful transfer readiness. Scores below this threshold identify specific areas that need more development before transfer.
  • What it measures: Managing medications independently, keeping appointments without parental reminders, tracking symptoms, knowing emergency plans, understanding insurance basics, and communicating with providers.
  • When to start: Administer annually starting at age 14–15. This creates a longitudinal record showing progress and identifying persistent gaps that need targeted intervention.
  • Cost and access: The TRAQ is free to use. Ask your pediatric team to incorporate it into annual well visits. If they are unfamiliar with it, the tool is available through the Got Transition initiative (gottransition.org).

Evidence-Based Transition Frameworks

Got Transition Six Core Elements 3.0

The Got Transition initiative (gottransition.org) has published an evidence-based framework — the Six Core Elements of Health Care Transition 3.0 — that provides the most widely validated structure for moving young people from pediatric to adult care. While designed for all youth with chronic conditions, this framework is especially relevant for rare connective tissue disorders where provider familiarity cannot be assumed.

  1. Transition Policy (age 12–14): The pediatric practice establishes a formal, written transition policy and shares it with the patient and family. This policy states when and how the transition process will begin, what the practice’s role will be, and what is expected of the family. Having this in writing at age 12–14 eliminates the surprise of a sudden discharge to adult care.
  2. Tracking and Monitoring (ongoing): A transition registry or tracking system identifies all patients in the transition pipeline, their current stage, and any overdue milestones. This prevents patients from falling through the cracks — particularly important for rare disease patients who may be one of very few in a practice.
  3. Transition Readiness Assessment (annually from age 14): Validated tools such as the TRAQ (described above) are administered annually to measure the young person’s self-management skills, health literacy, and preparedness for independent care. Scores identify specific skill gaps that can be targeted before transfer.
  4. Transition Planning with Portable Medical Summary (by age 16): A comprehensive, portable medical summary is created and updated regularly. This document includes the diagnosis, genetic variant, complete surgical history, current medications, all specialist contacts, emergency protocols, and accommodation needs. By age 16, the young person should have a copy on their phone and in their records.
  5. Transfer of Care (warm handoff): The actual transfer is not a discharge — it is a warm handoff. The pediatric provider introduces the patient to the adult provider (ideally through a joint appointment or a direct provider-to-provider phone call), transfers all records, and confirms that the first adult appointment has been scheduled and attended.
  6. Transfer Completion Confirmation: After transfer, the pediatric practice follows up to confirm that the patient has successfully established care with the adult provider. This closes the loop and catches any patients who were transferred on paper but never actually connected with adult care.

ERN ReCONNET 2025 Findings

The European Reference Network for Rare Connective Tissue and Musculoskeletal Diseases (ERN ReCONNET) published 2025 survey data that reveals significant gaps in transition care across rare disease centers:

  • Only 40% of centers have a formal transition policy — meaning the majority of young people with rare connective tissue disorders are transferred without a structured process.
  • Only 13% use validated readiness tools (such as the TRAQ) to assess whether the patient is actually prepared for independent adult care.
  • 29% of centers transition based solely on age — typically the 18th birthday — with no assessment of readiness, no graduated handoff, and no confirmation that adult care has been established.

These numbers underscore why families must drive the transition process themselves rather than relying on the medical system to initiate it.

Marshall-Specific Transition Challenges

Beyond the general challenges of rare disease transition, Marshall syndrome presents specific specialist-finding difficulties:

  • Adult vitreoretinal specialist: The 65% lifetime retinal detachment risk requires lifelong surveillance by a vitreoretinal surgeon — not a general ophthalmologist. Pediatric vitreoretinal specialists who are familiar with the Stickler/Marshall spectrum may not have adult-practice counterparts. Identifying a vitreoretinal surgeon who understands the specific retinal phenotype (membranous or beaded vitreous, giant retinal tears) is critical and may require a university-based center.
  • Audiologist experienced in syndromic hearing loss: Marshall syndrome hearing loss is progressive and mixed (sensorineural with possible conductive component). An audiologist experienced in syndromic hearing loss — as opposed to age-related or noise-induced loss — understands the monitoring cadence, the progression patterns, and the threshold for cochlear implant evaluation.
  • Rheumatologist who understands hypermobility and early-onset osteoarthritis: Many adult rheumatologists are oriented toward inflammatory autoimmune conditions (RA, lupus). The degenerative joint disease, hypermobility-related pain, and biomechanical challenges of Marshall syndrome require a rheumatologist familiar with heritable connective tissue disorders — often found in specialized connective tissue disorder or Marfan/EDS clinics.

2025 AAP Coding & Payment Resource

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) published a 2025 Coding & Payment Resource specifically supporting transition-related services. This means that transition planning visits, readiness assessments, and care coordination are billable using established CPT codes. Families can reference this resource if their pediatric practice cites lack of reimbursement as a barrier to formal transition planning. Transition is a covered medical service, not an unfunded add-on.

Driving Assessment

Driving is a milestone that requires proactive assessment in Marshall syndrome, where vision, hearing, and cervical spine involvement all intersect with the skills needed to drive safely.

  • OT pre-driving evaluation at age 15–16: A certified driver rehabilitation specialist (CDRS) or occupational therapist trained in driving assessment should evaluate visual acuity, visual fields, contrast sensitivity, reaction time, cervical range of motion, and cognitive processing speed. This assessment should happen before learner’s permit application — not after a problem surfaces.
  • Visual considerations: High myopia, prior retinal detachment repair, and reduced visual fields all affect driving safety. A driving-specific ophthalmologic evaluation can determine whether corrective lenses meet state vision requirements and whether restrictions (daytime only, no highway driving) are needed.
  • Hearing considerations: While hearing loss alone does not disqualify someone from driving (deaf individuals drive safely), significant bilateral hearing loss requires compensatory strategies — wider mirror use, reduced in-car noise, visual alertness for emergency vehicles. The OT assessment addresses these adaptations.
  • Cervical spine considerations: Limited cervical rotation from cervical kyphosis, fusions, or degenerative changes affects the ability to check blind spots. Adaptive equipment such as wide-angle mirrors, panoramic rearview mirrors, and blind-spot detection systems can compensate. Some teens may benefit from vehicles with larger windows and better sightlines.
  • Adaptive equipment options: Hand controls, spinner knobs, seat cushions for short stature, pedal extensions, and assistive mirrors are all available and can be prescribed by a CDRS based on the individual’s specific needs.
Practical recommendation: Request a referral to a certified driver rehabilitation specialist through your occupational therapist or rehabilitation medicine physician at age 15. The evaluation produces a written report that can be shared with the driving instructor, and it identifies any adaptive equipment needed before behind-the-wheel training begins.

Telehealth and Access to Rare Disease Specialists

One of the greatest barriers in Marshall syndrome care — both during pediatric years and after transition — is geographic distance from specialists who understand the condition. Telehealth has fundamentally changed this equation.

  • Connecting to rare disease specialists regardless of location: Telehealth enables ongoing relationships with experts at major academic centers (geneticists, connective tissue disorder specialists, vitreoretinal surgeons for consultation) even when the patient lives hundreds of miles away. A young adult in a rural area does not need to fly to Boston or Baltimore for every follow-up.
  • Virtual multidisciplinary conferences: Some academic centers now offer virtual multidisciplinary team (MDT) meetings where the patient’s local providers and the rare disease specialists join a single video conference to review the case together. This replicates the coordinated care model of a pediatric multidisciplinary clinic in the adult world, even without a physical equivalent.
  • Hybrid model: The most effective approach combines periodic in-person visits (annually or biannually) to the academic center with telehealth follow-ups between visits. The in-person visit handles examinations, imaging, and procedures that require physical presence. Telehealth visits handle medication adjustments, symptom review, care coordination, and specialist-to-specialist communication.
  • State licensure: Telehealth regulations vary by state. The Interstate Medical Licensure Compact and COVID-era waivers have expanded access, but verify that the specialist is licensed to practice in your state. Many academic centers have dedicated telehealth coordinators who handle this.

Remote Patient Monitoring (RPM) for Marshall Syndrome

Remote patient monitoring extends telehealth beyond video visits by providing continuous objective data between appointments. For a multi-system condition like Marshall syndrome, RPM can track several domains simultaneously:

Monitoring DomainRPM TechnologyMarshall Syndrome Application
POTS / Autonomic Wearable heart rate monitors, continuous HR variability tracking, blood pressure cuffs with data upload Identifies orthostatic intolerance patterns, documents tachycardia episodes, tracks response to volume loading and medication adjustments between cardiology visits

SCRiPT: School-Based Telehealth

The SCRiPT (School-based Care, Research, and Practice in Telehealth) network demonstrates a model where telehealth specialists connect directly to school settings, providing subspecialty consultations without requiring the child to leave school. For Marshall syndrome patients who already miss significant school time for appointments, school-based telehealth can preserve educational continuity while maintaining specialist access.

Virtual Genetic Counseling

Genetic counseling — essential for family planning discussions, cascade testing of relatives, and variant interpretation updates — is highly suited to telehealth delivery. Several academic genetics programs now offer virtual genetic counseling appointments that are equally effective as in-person sessions for counseling, education, and psychosocial support. This is particularly valuable for extended family members who may live in different states and need counseling about their risk of carrying the COL11A1 variant.

Rural and underserved access: The 2025 federal Rural Health Transformation Initiative ($10 billion allocation) is expanding broadband infrastructure, telehealth reimbursement, and specialty access in rural areas. Families in underserved regions should check whether their state has received funding that improves telehealth coverage for rare disease subspecialty care. State telehealth parity laws — requiring insurers to reimburse telehealth at the same rate as in-person visits — now exist in most states and are critical for making RPM and virtual specialist visits financially sustainable.

Digital Self-Management Tools

Building self-management skills during the transition years is easier when supported by evidence-based digital tools. Several apps are specifically designed for adolescents and young adults managing chronic conditions:

  • WebMAP Mobile: A free cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) app developed at Seattle Children’s Hospital for ages 10–18 with chronic pain. It teaches pain coping strategies, relaxation techniques, activity pacing, and sleep hygiene through interactive modules. Particularly relevant for Marshall syndrome adolescents managing chronic musculoskeletal pain during the transition period.
  • iCanCope apps: A suite of digital tools for pain tracking and self-management in youth with chronic pain. Features include daily pain diaries, goal setting, coping skill modules, and trend visualization that help adolescents recognize patterns in their symptoms and communicate more effectively with providers.
  • Symptom tracking and provider communication: Encouraging the use of digital symptom trackers during the Growing Independence phase (age 12–15) builds the habit of systematic self-monitoring. By the time the young adult is managing their own care, they have years of data and the skills to maintain it.
Integration with transition phases: Digital tools map directly to the phased approach above. In the Foundation phase (9–12), parents set up tracking apps and review them together. In Growing Independence (12–15), the teen takes ownership of logging. By Pre-Transfer (15–17), the teen is using these tools independently and sharing reports with providers. This builds the self-management infrastructure that adult care will rely on.

Got Transition 3.0: Operationalizing the Six Core Elements

The Got Transition 3.0 update (gottransition.org) refines the Six Core Elements framework described above with specific operational guidance and implementation tools. For Marshall syndrome families, the 3.0 update adds particular value in three areas:

Transition Readiness Assessment from Age 14

Got Transition 3.0 formally recommends beginning validated readiness assessment at age 14, not 16 or 17 as many practices default to. For Marshall syndrome, starting at 14 is especially important because:

  • Multi-specialty complexity requires more lead time. A child with a single-organ condition may need to transition one or two specialist relationships. A child with Marshall syndrome has 6–10+ specialist relationships that must each be identified, matched to an adult provider, and handed off. This cannot be accomplished in a single year.
  • Readiness gaps compound with complexity. The TRAQ administered at age 14 may reveal that the adolescent can manage medications but cannot explain their diagnosis to a new provider, or can describe symptoms but has never called to schedule an appointment. Each gap takes time to close through practice, and more gaps mean a longer runway is needed.
  • Annual reassessment creates a trend line. TRAQ scores at 14, 15, 16, and 17 show whether the adolescent is progressing, plateauing, or regressing in readiness. A single score at 17 gives no trajectory information and no time to intervene if readiness is low.

Multi-Specialty Transition Coordination

Got Transition 3.0 specifically addresses the challenge of multi-specialty transition — transferring care across multiple specialties simultaneously. The framework recommends:

  • Designating a transition coordinator: One provider (usually the geneticist, primary care physician, or a nurse coordinator) takes responsibility for overseeing the transition across all specialties. Without a coordinator, each specialist assumes someone else is managing the transition, and no one actually does.
  • Staggered transfer: Rather than transferring all specialist relationships at age 18, Got Transition 3.0 recommends staggering transfers across the 16–20 age range. The first transfer (typically the most stable specialty relationship) builds the adolescent’s confidence for subsequent transfers. For Marshall syndrome, ophthalmology or audiology — where the relationship is monitoring-focused and relatively routine — may be appropriate to transfer first, while genetics and orthopedics — where decisions are more complex — transfer later.
  • Warm handoffs between pediatric and adult providers: For each specialty, the pediatric provider contacts the receiving adult provider directly (phone call or joint visit), transfers records, and confirms the first appointment. A written referral alone is insufficient — the warm handoff ensures the adult provider understands the condition’s complexity before the first visit.

Self-Advocacy Training: A Structured Curriculum

Got Transition 3.0 provides structured self-advocacy training materials that can be integrated into clinical visits. For Marshall syndrome, self-advocacy is especially critical because every new adult provider will be unfamiliar with the condition. The adolescent must become the expert educator in every clinical encounter.

  • Age 14–15: Can state diagnosis, list medications, describe main symptoms in their own words
  • Age 15–16: Can explain the condition to a new provider in 2 minutes, including the top 3 emergency concerns (difficult airway, cervical precautions, retinal detachment risk)
  • Age 16–17: Can schedule own appointments, call pharmacy for refills, request referrals, and communicate with insurance
  • Age 17–18: Can navigate a new-provider encounter independently: bring medical summary, present diagnosis, request specific monitoring, and advocate for necessary accommodations

Insurance Planning: The Critical Window

Got Transition 3.0 emphasizes insurance planning as a core transition element, not an afterthought. For rare disease patients, insurance continuity is existential — a gap in coverage can mean months without specialist access, interrupted hearing aid coverage, or missed retinal surveillance.

  • Begin insurance literacy at age 16: The adolescent should understand copays, deductibles, out-of-pocket maximums, prior authorizations, formularies, and the appeals process. Role-play insurance phone calls during clinic visits.
  • Research post-26 options by age 24: Employer-sponsored plans, ACA marketplace plans, and Medicaid (if income-eligible) all have enrollment windows and documentation requirements. The family and young adult should research options 2 years before the coverage deadline.
  • Document medical necessity for all devices and therapies. Create a comprehensive medical necessity file that can be transferred to any new insurer: hearing aids, orthotics, ophthalmologic surveillance, PT, and specialist visits. Prior authorization denials are common when the receiving insurer has no historical claims data for a rare condition.
Got Transition 3.0 toolkit. The complete Got Transition 3.0 implementation toolkit — including readiness assessment forms, portable medical summary templates, self-advocacy training modules, and transfer checklists — is available free at gottransition.org. Families can download the tools and bring them to the pediatric team to initiate the transition planning process. If the pediatric practice does not have a formal transition protocol, these materials provide a ready-made framework.

The Five Barriers to Successful Transition

Research Context (R427): A 2025 multi-center analysis identified five specific, measurable barriers that predict transition failure in young people with chronic multi-system conditions. For Marshall syndrome — where the transition involves 6–10+ specialist relationships simultaneously — each of these barriers is amplified. Recognizing them early allows families to intervene before the transition collapses.
BarrierHow It Manifests in Marshall SyndromeCountermeasure
1. Provider Knowledge Gap Adult providers are unfamiliar with Marshall syndrome, COL11A1, and the multi-system implications. The receiving provider may not understand why a “joint problem” patient needs retinal surveillance and hearing monitoring. This is the most fundamental barrier. The portable medical summary (described above) must include a one-page disease primer written for an adult provider unfamiliar with the condition. Include the key phrase: “Marshall syndrome is a systemic collagen XI disorder. Every organ containing collagen is potentially affected.” The young adult must be prepared to educate every new provider at every first visit.
2. Insurance Disruption Coverage gaps during the transition period (aging out of Medicaid, switching from parent’s plan) can interrupt access to hearing aids, specialist visits, retinal surveillance, and medications. A single missed renewal can create months of disruption. Begin insurance transition planning at age 16 (see Insurance Transition above). Document medical necessity for every device and therapy. Keep a “medical necessity file” that can be transferred to any new insurer with complete prior authorization histories.
3. Self-Advocacy Deficit The young adult cannot effectively communicate their needs to new providers, request accommodations, or navigate the adult healthcare system independently. This barrier is especially damaging in rare disease where the patient must be the expert in the room. Structured self-advocacy training starting at age 14 (Got Transition 3.0 framework). Practice the “elevator explanation,” role-play new-provider encounters, and build independence through the phased approach described above. The TRAQ score tracks readiness objectively.
4. Care Coordination Collapse The pediatric multidisciplinary clinic model (one-stop coordination) usually has no adult equivalent. Without a designated coordinator, each adult specialist operates in isolation, unaware of what others are doing. Medication interactions, conflicting advice, and duplicated testing result. Designate a transition coordinator (usually the geneticist or PCP) who maintains a master list of all providers and facilitates communication between them. Consider a care coordination app or shared medical record system. The young adult may need to serve as their own coordinator — a skill that must be explicitly taught.
5. Psychosocial Isolation Loss of the familiar pediatric team, peer support networks, and family-centered care model creates a sense of abandonment. Young adults with rare disease often feel uniquely alone in the adult system, where providers are less likely to provide the emotional scaffolding that pediatric teams offer. Connect with rare disease peer networks (Stickler Involved People, EDS Society, NORD Young Adult programs) before the transition. Establish adult mental health support proactively. The Psychological Support page provides resources.
Barrier assessment tool: At each annual transition readiness check (starting age 14), formally assess all five barriers. Rate each as green (addressed), yellow (in progress), or red (not yet addressed). The goal is to have all five barriers at green before the transfer date. If any barrier remains red at age 17, the transfer timeline should be extended rather than proceeding with known gaps.
