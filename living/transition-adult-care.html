Transition to Adult Care: A Step-by-Step Guide

Every child with Marshall syndrome will eventually become an adult with Marshall syndrome. A planned transition — starting at age 12–14 — prevents the dangerous gap that occurs when pediatric specialists hand off to adult providers who may never have seen this condition. Without a deliberate plan, young adults with rare connective tissue disorders frequently "fall through the cracks," losing coordinated care at exactly the age when new challenges emerge.

Why Transition Matters

Pediatric specialists in genetics, orthopedics, and ophthalmology often develop deep familiarity with Marshall syndrome over years of treating a child. They understand the COL11A1 mutation's downstream effects, anticipate complications, and coordinate across disciplines. When that child turns 18 and ages out of pediatric care, they enter an adult medical system where their new providers may have never seen another Marshall syndrome patient in their entire career.

This is not a hypothetical problem. Research on healthcare transitions in rare diseases consistently identifies several failure points:

  • Loss of coordinated surveillance: Pediatric teams typically manage the whole picture — eyes, ears, spine, joints, pain. In adult care, each specialist operates independently unless someone actively coordinates.
  • Knowledge gaps: Adult rheumatologists, orthopedic surgeons, and ophthalmologists may not recognize that early-onset osteoarthritis, retinal detachment risk, and sensorineural hearing loss are all manifestations of the same underlying collagen disorder.
  • Abrupt vs. planned transfer: Studies comparing abrupt transfer (turning 18, being told "find an adult doctor") versus planned transition (gradual, multi-year process) show significantly worse outcomes with abrupt transfer, including higher emergency department use, more hospitalizations, and longer gaps without specialist care.
  • The rare disease compounding factor: For common conditions like diabetes or asthma, adult providers have extensive experience. For Marshall syndrome, the adult provider may need education from the patient about their own condition — a dynamic that requires preparation.

The Got Transition initiative, supported by the Maternal and Child Health Bureau, outlines Six Core Elements of healthcare transition: transition policy, transition tracking and monitoring, transition readiness, transition planning, transfer of care, and transfer completion. These elements apply to all chronic conditions but are especially critical for rare genetic disorders where institutional knowledge is concentrated in a small number of pediatric centers.

Timeline: Starting at Age 12–14

Transition is a process that spans years, not a single event. The Got Transition framework recommends beginning readiness assessments at age 14–16, but for complex rare diseases like Marshall syndrome, starting conversations at age 12 is appropriate. The goal is to build skills incrementally so that by age 18, the young adult is prepared — not blindsided.

Ages 12–14: Foundation Building

  • Learn the name of the diagnosis: "I have Marshall syndrome, caused by a mutation in the COL11A1 gene"
  • Know the names of all current medications and why each is taken
  • Understand the basic systems affected: eyes, ears, joints, spine
  • Begin attending portions of medical appointments without a parent present (even 5 minutes)
  • Start a health journal or use a tracking app to log symptoms
  • Learn the concept of self-advocacy: "It's okay to ask your doctor questions"

Ages 14–16: Skill Development

  • Attend the first part of appointments alone, with the parent joining for the summary
  • Practice describing symptoms accurately: location, severity, duration, triggers, what helps
  • Know emergency protocols: when to go to the ER, what to tell emergency physicians about Marshall syndrome
  • Understand insurance basics: what insurance covers the family, what a copay is, how referrals work
  • Complete a formal transition readiness assessment (available from Got Transition) with your pediatric team
  • Begin learning about the condition in more depth — reading about COL11A1, understanding autosomal dominant inheritance
  • If applicable, discuss the 504 plan or IEP process for college transition

Ages 16–18: Active Transition Planning

  • Identify adult providers for each specialty (see section below)
  • Request complete medical records from all pediatric providers
  • Create the medical summary document (see section below)
  • Discuss insurance transition: will the young adult stay on a parent's plan, enroll through school, or need marketplace coverage?
  • Schedule "meet and greet" visits with prospective adult providers before the formal transfer
  • Pediatric specialists write detailed transfer letters to receiving adult providers
  • Discuss advance directives and healthcare proxy (age 18 means parents no longer have automatic medical decision-making authority)

Ages 18+: Transfer and Establishment

  • Complete formal transfer to adult providers
  • Establish care with each adult specialist — ideally with an overlap period where the pediatric team remains available for questions
  • Self-manage all appointments, medication refills, and insurance paperwork
  • Maintain and update the medical summary document at least annually
  • Build a new coordinated care team, identifying one provider (often the geneticist or primary care physician) as the "quarterback"
Practical tip: Many pediatric hospitals now have dedicated transition coordinators or social workers who specialize in helping patients with complex conditions navigate the transfer to adult care. Ask your pediatric team if this resource exists at your center — it can make the difference between a smooth handoff and a chaotic one.

Which Adult Specialists to Establish

Marshall syndrome requires lifelong multi-specialty care. Each pediatric specialist has an adult equivalent, but finding the right adult provider — one who understands genetic connective tissue disorders — is the critical challenge. For a detailed overview of each specialty and what they manage, see the Building Your Medical Team page.

Pediatric ProviderAdult EquivalentKey Considerations
Pediatric geneticist Adult genetics clinic or connective tissue disorder program Many genetics clinics focus on pediatrics; adult genetics is less common. University-based programs are more likely to have adult connective tissue expertise. This provider often becomes the care coordinator.
Pediatric orthopedic spine surgeon Adult spine specialist Not all adult spine surgeons manage genetic skeletal conditions. Look for those experienced with connective tissue disorders, early-onset arthritis, or adult scoliosis. If spinal instrumentation was placed in childhood, the adult surgeon must understand the hardware.
Pediatric rheumatologist Adult rheumatologist with osteoarthritis and connective tissue experience The shift from inflammatory joint concerns in childhood to degenerative joint disease in young adulthood requires a different management approach. Look for rheumatologists who manage early-onset OA and understand genetic contributors.
Pediatric ophthalmologist Vitreoretinal specialist Many pediatric Marshall patients already see an adult retina surgeon for retinal detachment surveillance. This transition is often the smoothest. If not already established, a vitreoretinal specialist is essential for lifelong retinal monitoring. See Eye Management.
Pediatric audiologist Audiologist (often the same provider) Audiology often does not change at transition — many audiologists see patients across the lifespan. The key is ensuring continued annual monitoring, as sensorineural hearing loss may progress. See Hearing Management.
Pediatric ENT Adult otolaryngologist Relevant if there are ongoing craniofacial or airway concerns. The midface hypoplasia seen in Marshall syndrome can affect adult ENT considerations including sleep apnea evaluation.
Pediatric pain specialist Adult pain medicine Chronic pain management shifts significantly in adulthood. Adult pain medicine includes more options but also more complexity around opioid management, interventional procedures, and interdisciplinary pain programs. See Pain Neuroscience.
Pediatric psychologist Adult psychologist or therapist experienced with chronic illness The psychological landscape shifts at transition (see Mental Health section below). Finding a therapist experienced with chronic illness in young adults is important. See Psychological Support.
The challenge of finding the right provider: For common conditions, any adult specialist will do. For Marshall syndrome, you may need to search regionally. Connective tissue disorder clinics at academic medical centers (often branded as "Marfan" or "hereditary connective tissue disorder" programs) are the best starting point, even though Marshall syndrome is not Marfan. These clinics understand the multi-system nature of collagen disorders and can coordinate referrals to appropriate sub-specialists.

The Medical Summary Document

A concise, portable medical summary is the single most important tool for transition. Every new provider should receive this document at the first visit. It prevents the need to retell the entire medical history from scratch and ensures nothing critical is omitted.

What to Include

  • Diagnosis: Marshall syndrome (also known as Marshall-Stickler syndrome, Stickler syndrome type II). Gene: COL11A1. Inheritance: autosomal dominant. Include the specific mutation if known (e.g., "splice site variant in exon 50 of COL11A1").
  • Affected systems: List which systems are affected in this specific patient — not every Marshall patient has every manifestation.
  • Surgical history: All surgeries with dates, surgeon names, and key details (e.g., "T4-L2 posterior spinal fusion with instrumentation, Dr. Smith, Children's Hospital, 2020").
  • Current medications: Name, dose, frequency, prescribing provider, and reason for each medication.
  • Active issues: Current problems being managed (e.g., "bilateral sensorineural hearing loss, aided with hearing aids; chronic bilateral knee pain managed with PT and NSAIDs; myopia with lattice degeneration, monitored every 6 months").
  • Surveillance schedule: What needs monitoring and how often (e.g., "dilated retinal exam every 6 months, audiogram annually, spine imaging as needed").
  • Specialist list: Names, institutions, phone numbers, and next appointment dates for all current providers.
  • Emergency protocols: What to do if — sudden vision changes (retinal detachment: emergency), new neurological symptoms (spinal stenosis), acute joint locking, severe pain crisis.
  • Allergies and sensitivities: Drug allergies, latex sensitivity if applicable, anesthesia history and any complications.
  • Anesthesia considerations: History of difficult intubation (midface hypoplasia can affect airway management), cervical spine precautions, any prior anesthesia complications.
  • Family history: Which family members are affected, any relevant family surgical or medical history.
Keep it to 1–2 pages. The summary should be scannable in under 5 minutes. Use bullet points, not paragraphs. Update it at least annually or after any significant change. Keep digital and printed copies. Store a copy in your phone for emergency situations. Some families create a laminated wallet card with the most critical information (diagnosis, emergency protocols, medication allergies).

Insurance Transition

In the United States, insurance transition is one of the most stressful aspects of moving to adult care. Understanding the timeline and options prevents gaps in coverage that could be devastating for someone requiring ongoing specialist care.

Key Milestones and Options

  • Under age 26: The Affordable Care Act (ACA) allows young adults to remain on a parent's health insurance until age 26, regardless of student status, marital status, or whether they live at home. This provides a buffer period for transition.
  • College students: Many universities offer student health insurance plans. Compare these carefully against remaining on a parent's plan — student plans may have narrower networks that exclude the specialists you need.
  • Employer-sponsored insurance: When starting employment, evaluate the plan's specialist network carefully. Does it include providers experienced with connective tissue disorders? What are the out-of-network benefits?
  • Marketplace (ACA exchange) plans: Available during open enrollment or with a qualifying life event (e.g., aging off a parent's plan). Plans cannot deny coverage or charge more for pre-existing conditions.
  • Medicaid: Available based on income in all states, with expanded eligibility in most states. Some adults with significant disabilities may qualify for Medicaid regardless of income through disability-based pathways. Medicaid often covers a broader range of services than private insurance, including some durable medical equipment and home modifications.
  • Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) / Supplemental Security Income (SSI): For individuals whose Marshall syndrome significantly limits their ability to work, SSI or SSDI may provide both income and automatic Medicaid or Medicare eligibility. This is a complex application process — consider consulting a disability attorney.

Prior Authorization Continuity

When switching insurance plans, existing prior authorizations for ongoing treatments (medications, hearing aids, physical therapy, imaging) do not automatically transfer. Plan ahead:

  • Document all current prior authorizations and their expiration dates
  • Ask new providers to submit prior authorization requests before the old plan ends
  • Keep copies of all approval letters from the old plan — they serve as evidence of medical necessity
  • For expensive items like hearing aids, check if the new plan covers replacements on a different schedule

Self-Advocacy Skills

Perhaps the most important transition skill is learning to advocate for yourself with providers who have no prior knowledge of your condition. This is a skill that can be practiced and improved over time.

How to Explain Marshall Syndrome to a New Doctor

Prepare a brief, clear explanation that you can deliver in under 60 seconds:

"I have Marshall syndrome, a genetic connective tissue disorder caused by a mutation in the COL11A1 gene. It affects my collagen throughout my body, so I have multiple systems involved: my eyes are at lifelong risk for retinal detachment, I have sensorineural hearing loss, and I develop early-onset osteoarthritis in my joints and spine. I've been managed by a multi-specialty team since childhood. I have a medical summary that covers my full history and current management plan."

Then hand them the medical summary document. This approach is clear, efficient, and positions you as an informed patient rather than asking the doctor to look up a condition they may never have encountered.

Building Effective Self-Advocacy

  • Bring evidence: When seeing a new specialist, bring 1–2 key research articles about Marshall syndrome relevant to their specialty (e.g., for a rheumatologist, bring a paper on COL11A1 and early OA). The Natural History page is a good starting point for understanding what to share.
  • Know when to push for referrals: If a primary care provider dismisses a symptom that you know is consistent with your condition, calmly explain the connection and request a referral. "I understand this might seem unrelated, but in Marshall syndrome, [symptom] can be a sign of [complication]. I'd like a referral to [specialist] to rule that out."
  • Understand your own red flags: Know which symptoms require urgent attention (sudden flashes/floaters = possible retinal detachment, new leg weakness/numbness = possible spinal stenosis, sudden severe headache with neck stiffness = requires immediate evaluation).
  • It's okay to say "I disagree": If a provider recommends something that contradicts your established management plan, you have the right to seek a second opinion. Being polite and firm is not being difficult.
  • Request written records: After every visit, request the visit summary or notes. Review them for accuracy. Errors in medical records can propagate and cause problems later.

Adult Manifestations That Differ from Childhood

Marshall syndrome does not stay the same across the lifespan. The dominant clinical challenges shift significantly from childhood to adulthood. Understanding these changes helps with anticipatory planning. For the full progression timeline, see Natural History of Marshall Syndrome.

SystemChildhood PredominanceAdult Shift
Joints Hypermobility, growing pains, occasional joint effusions Early-onset osteoarthritis becomes the dominant musculoskeletal issue, often beginning in the teens or twenties. Joint replacements may be needed decades earlier than typical. Weight-bearing joints (knees, hips) and the spine are most affected.
Eyes High myopia, vitreous abnormalities, early retinal tears Retinal detachment risk remains lifelong. Cataracts may develop earlier than typical. Glaucoma surveillance becomes important. Prior retinal surgeries require ongoing monitoring.
Hearing Sensorineural hearing loss (often stable after childhood) Hearing loss may slowly progress in some individuals. Age-related hearing decline compounds the existing deficit. Hearing aid technology needs may change. Cochlear implant candidacy should be reassessed if hearing deteriorates significantly.
Spine Scoliosis, kyphosis, vertebral changes Spinal stenosis can emerge as a significant issue, particularly in the cervical spine. Degenerative disc disease occurs earlier. Prior spinal fusion hardware may require revision. New neurological symptoms warrant urgent evaluation.
Pain Episodic, activity-related Chronic pain management becomes central. Pain shifts from acute/episodic to chronic/persistent. Multimodal approaches (PT, medication, interventional procedures, psychology) become essential. See Pain Neuroscience.
Fatigue Less prominent, activity-limited Fatigue and deconditioning become significant. The combination of chronic pain, impaired sleep (hearing loss, pain), reduced activity tolerance, and the psychological burden of chronic illness creates a fatigue pattern that requires proactive management.
Mental Health Adjustment challenges, school-related stress, self-image Identity, independence, relationships, career, family planning — all intersect with chronic illness in new ways. Anxiety about disease progression is common. Depression risk is elevated. See Psychological Support.

Pregnancy and Marshall Syndrome

For women (and those assigned female at birth) with Marshall syndrome, pregnancy involves specific considerations that require advance planning and a high-risk obstetric team. This is not a reason to avoid pregnancy — it is a reason to plan it carefully.

Genetic Considerations

  • Autosomal dominant inheritance: Each pregnancy carries a 50% chance of transmitting the COL11A1 mutation to the child.
  • Preimplantation genetic testing (PGT): Available through IVF for couples who wish to select unaffected embryos. Requires knowing the specific family mutation.
  • Prenatal testing: Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) at 10–12 weeks or amniocentesis at 15–18 weeks can determine if the fetus carries the mutation. These are personal decisions with no universally "correct" answer.
  • Genetic counseling before conception is strongly recommended. See the genetics resources on the Building Your Medical Team page.

Obstetric Management

  • High-risk OB / Maternal-Fetal Medicine (MFM): Essential. Marshall syndrome is a connective tissue disorder; pregnancy in connective tissue disorders warrants specialist oversight.
  • Cervical competence: Collagen disorders can affect cervical integrity. Cervical length monitoring via ultrasound may be indicated, similar to monitoring protocols used in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome pregnancies.
  • Joint instability: Pregnancy hormones (relaxin) increase joint laxity. In someone with an already-compromised collagen matrix, this can significantly worsen joint pain and instability. Physical therapy support throughout pregnancy is important.

Anesthesia and Delivery Considerations

  • Epidural placement with scoliosis or spinal instrumentation: If the patient has scoliosis or a prior spinal fusion, epidural catheter placement can be technically challenging. An anesthesia consultation before labor (ideally in the second trimester) allows the anesthesiologist to review imaging and plan the approach. Alternative pain management options should be discussed in advance.
  • Retinal detachment and delivery: Historically, there was concern that Valsalva maneuvers during pushing could trigger retinal detachment. Current evidence, including studies in Stickler syndrome specifically, suggests that history of retinal pathology alone is not a contraindication to vaginal delivery. A 2020 case report of pregnancy management in Stickler syndrome documented successful vaginal delivery with multidisciplinary planning. However, individual risk assessment by the vitreoretinal specialist is important — if there is active, untreated retinal pathology, delivery planning may differ.
  • Cesarean section considerations: C-section may be recommended for obstetric indications (not solely because of Marshall syndrome). If a C-section is planned, anesthesia considerations are similar to those for epidural placement. Wound healing should be monitored given the collagen disorder.
  • Airway management: Midface hypoplasia and potential cervical spine involvement in Marshall syndrome should be communicated to the anesthesia team. Intubation may require specialized techniques.
Multidisciplinary planning is key: The ideal approach involves a planning meeting (or at minimum, documented communication) between the MFM specialist, vitreoretinal specialist, anesthesiologist, and the patient — well before the due date. This team should agree on a delivery plan that accounts for all Marshall-specific risks.

Employment and Disability

Most adults with Marshall syndrome are capable of full employment, but may need accommodations to work sustainably. Understanding your rights and options allows you to make informed decisions about career planning.

ADA Workplace Accommodations (United States)

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) protects employees with conditions that substantially limit major life activities. Chronic pain, impaired vision, hearing loss, and limited mobility can all qualify. Common accommodations include:

  • Ergonomic workstation: Adjustable desk, ergonomic chair, monitor risers, specialized keyboard/mouse. Essential for managing joint pain and spinal issues.
  • Flexible scheduling: Modified start times to accommodate morning stiffness. Breaks for stretching or position changes. Ability to work from home on high-pain days.
  • Hearing accommodations: Captioned phone systems, real-time captioning for meetings, preferential seating in meetings, written follow-ups for verbal instructions.
  • Vision accommodations: Screen magnification software, large monitors, adjustable lighting, reduced screen glare.
  • Medical leave: FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act) provides up to 12 weeks of protected unpaid leave per year for medical reasons, including specialist appointments and flare-ups.
  • Remote work: For many roles, remote work eliminates commuting stress and allows better management of pain, fatigue, and medical appointment scheduling.

Disclosing a Medical Condition

You are not required to disclose your diagnosis to an employer. You do need to disclose that you have a condition requiring accommodations in order to receive them. Key principles:

  • Disclosure goes to HR or a designated ADA coordinator, not to coworkers
  • You can describe functional limitations without naming the condition: "I have a genetic condition that causes chronic joint pain and requires periodic specialist visits"
  • Accommodation information is confidential and cannot be placed in your personnel file
  • An employer may request medical documentation to verify the need for accommodations

When to Consider Disability Benefits

Some individuals with severe Marshall syndrome manifestations — significant visual impairment, severe joint disease limiting mobility, chronic debilitating pain — may qualify for Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) or Supplemental Security Income (SSI). Consider consulting a disability attorney if:

  • You are unable to maintain consistent full-time employment due to your condition
  • You require frequent medical appointments that exceed what FMLA can cover
  • Your symptoms are progressive and worsening despite treatment
  • You have been unable to find accommodations that make work sustainable

Mental Health in Transition

The transition to adult care is not just a medical logistics problem. It is a fundamental identity shift — from being a child whose parents manage a medical condition to being an adult who owns and manages their own health in the context of building a life.

Common Psychological Challenges at Transition

  • Identity renegotiation: Moving from "sick child" to "adult managing a condition" requires redefining how the condition fits into one's identity. Some young adults reject the illness, skip appointments, and stop medications. Others become consumed by it. The healthy middle ground takes time to find.
  • Grief for abilities: As adolescents see peers participating in activities they cannot (contact sports, certain careers, physical hobbies), grief is normal and should be acknowledged, not dismissed. This grief may resurface as osteoarthritis limits previously-possible activities in young adulthood.
  • Independence anxiety: Managing a complex condition independently for the first time is frightening. The safety net of the pediatric team and parental management is gone. Mistakes (missed appointments, lapsed medications, insurance gaps) have real consequences.
  • Relationship impacts: Explaining a rare genetic condition to romantic partners, navigating the 50% transmission risk in family planning conversations, and managing a partner's worry or discomfort with the condition are all new challenges.
  • Career uncertainty: Questions about physical sustainability of career choices, disclosure decisions, and the possibility of progressive disability create anxiety that can be paralyzing if not addressed.
  • Isolation: "No one understands what I'm dealing with" is a common feeling. Unlike more common conditions (diabetes, asthma), there are few peers with the same diagnosis. Rare disease support communities can help bridge this gap.

Resources and Strategies

  • Therapy: A psychologist or therapist experienced with chronic illness in young adults is invaluable. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) and acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT) both have evidence bases for chronic illness adjustment.
  • Peer support: Online communities for Stickler/Marshall syndrome, broader rare disease communities (NORD, Global Genes), and young adult chronic illness groups provide connection with others who understand.
  • Mentorship: If possible, connecting with an older adult who has Marshall syndrome can provide a roadmap and reassurance that a full life is achievable. Ask your geneticist if they can facilitate introductions (with appropriate privacy protections).
  • Transition support groups: Some pediatric hospitals offer group programs for young adults transitioning out of pediatric care. These address both the practical and emotional aspects of transition.

For more on psychological considerations, see the Psychological Support page.

Questions for the Transition

These questions are designed for different stages and audiences during the transition process:

Questions to Ask Your Pediatric Team (Ages 14–16)

  • "Do you have recommended adult providers who have experience with connective tissue disorders or genetic conditions? Can you provide a warm referral?"
  • "Can we create a formal medical summary document together? What would you want a receiving adult provider to know?"
  • "What surveillance schedule should continue into adulthood, and are there any new things that should be monitored as I get older?"

Questions to Ask Prospective Adult Providers

  • "Have you cared for patients with Stickler syndrome, Marshall syndrome, or other hereditary connective tissue disorders? If not, are you willing to learn and collaborate with my genetics team?"
  • "How do you approach early-onset osteoarthritis in young adults — specifically the balance between activity, pain management, and joint preservation?"
  • "What is your process for coordinating with my other specialists? Is there a care coordination mechanism in your practice?"

Questions to Ask Yourself

  • "Can I name my diagnosis, my gene mutation, my current medications, and my emergency protocols without looking them up?"
  • "Am I comfortable attending a medical appointment alone and communicating effectively with a provider?"
  • "Do I understand my insurance coverage, how to request referrals, and how to handle prior authorizations?"
