Strabismus (Eye Misalignment) in Marshall Syndrome

Evidence confidence guide

Clinical guidance synthesis. Recommendations on this page are based on published clinical literature and specialist consensus for connective tissue disorders. Marshall-specific evidence is limited due to the rarity of the condition. Always work with your child's medical team.
Strabismus in children with Marshall syndrome requires early detection and specialized management. Untreated eye misalignment during the critical period of visual development (birth to approximately age 7–8) can lead to permanent loss of binocular vision and amblyopia that cannot be fully corrected later. If you notice your child’s eyes are not aligned, seek evaluation by a pediatric ophthalmologist — not just a general eye doctor.

What Is Strabismus? Established

Strabismus is a condition in which the two eyes do not point in the same direction at the same time. One eye may look straight ahead while the other turns inward, outward, upward, or downward. The brain receives two different images and, in children, typically learns to suppress (ignore) the image from the misaligned eye to avoid double vision. This suppression, if it persists, leads to amblyopia — permanent visual impairment in the suppressed eye.

Types of Strabismus

TypeDirectionDescription
Esotropia Inward turning The most common form in young children. One eye turns toward the nose. Accommodative esotropia (triggered by focusing effort) is particularly relevant in highly myopic children, where the eyes must work harder to focus.
Exotropia Outward turning One eye drifts outward, away from the nose. Often intermittent initially — appearing when the child is tired, daydreaming, or looking at distant objects — and may become constant over time.
Hypertropia Vertical (upward) One eye sits higher than the other. Less common but can occur with oblique muscle dysfunction. May cause compensatory head tilting.
Hypotropia Vertical (downward) One eye sits lower than the other. Essentially the same as hypertropia described from the perspective of the lower eye.

Constant vs. Intermittent Strabismus

Constant strabismus means the misalignment is present at all times. Intermittent strabismus appears only under certain conditions — fatigue, illness, bright light, or when focusing at particular distances. Intermittent strabismus can be more difficult for parents to detect but still requires monitoring, as it frequently progresses to a constant deviation.

Strabismus vs. Amblyopia

These terms are often confused, but they describe different problems:

  • Strabismus is a structural/muscular problem — the eyes are physically misaligned.
  • Amblyopia (commonly called “lazy eye”) is a neurological problem — the brain has learned to ignore one eye’s input, resulting in reduced vision in that eye even with correct glasses.
  • Strabismus is one of the most common causes of amblyopia, but amblyopia can also result from a large difference in refractive error between the two eyes (anisometropia) or from visual deprivation (cataracts, ptosis).
  • A child can have strabismus without amblyopia (if the deviation is alternating), and amblyopia without strabismus (if caused by refractive error difference).
Why early detection matters: The visual cortex develops rapidly in the first 6–8 years of life. During this “critical period,” the brain is plastic enough to develop normal binocular vision if the misalignment is corrected. After this window closes, achieving full stereopsis (3D depth perception) becomes progressively more difficult or impossible. Early intervention is not just preferred — it is time-sensitive.

COL11A1 and Eye Muscle/Orbital Mechanics Hypothesis

Understanding why strabismus is more common in Marshall syndrome requires looking at several interconnected factors rooted in the COL11A1 mutation.

Extraocular Muscle Insertions

The six extraocular muscles that control each eye’s position attach to the sclera (the white outer coat of the eye) via tendons composed of collagen. Type XI collagen contributes to the structural integrity of these tendons and the sclera itself. When type XI collagen is defective:

  • Tendon compliance may be altered — the muscle-to-sclera connection may be more elastic or mechanically different than in unaffected individuals
  • Scleral insertion sites may be structurally weaker — this has direct implications for surgical correction (discussed below)
  • The force transmission from muscle contraction to eye rotation may be less efficient

Scleral Properties

Type XI collagen is a quantitatively minor but structurally critical component of the sclera. It regulates the diameter of the larger type II collagen fibrils. In COL11A1 mutations, the sclera is often thinner and has altered biomechanical properties — it stretches more readily. This affects how the extraocular muscles interact with the globe and is a significant surgical consideration.

High Myopia and Globe Shape

The high myopia characteristic of Marshall syndrome (often > −6 diopters) results from an elongated globe (axial myopia). This elongation changes the geometry of the extraocular muscles relative to the eye:

  • Muscle paths shift — in a longer eye, the effective lever arms and pulling angles of the muscles change
  • Heavy eye syndrome — in extreme axial myopia, the globe can herniate between the superior rectus and lateral rectus muscles, causing progressive esotropia with limited abduction. This is more commonly seen in adults with very high myopia.
  • Accommodative demands change — the relationship between accommodation (focusing) and convergence (turning the eyes inward) is altered by the refractive error

Connective Tissue Laxity in the Orbit

The orbital connective tissue — including Tenon’s capsule, intermuscular septa, and the pulley system that guides extraocular muscle paths — all contain collagen. Laxity in these structures can affect the mechanical stability and directional accuracy of eye movements, contributing to misalignment.

Prevalence in Marshall and Stickler Syndrome

Strabismus is significantly more common in individuals with Marshall and Stickler syndrome than in the general population:

  • General population prevalence: approximately 2–4% of children
  • Marshall/Stickler population: reported rates range from 15–30% depending on the cohort and mutation subtype, representing a 5–10 fold increase over baseline
  • Exotropia may be more common than esotropia in this population, which is unusual compared to the general pediatric population where esotropia predominates

Several factors contribute to this elevated prevalence:

  • High myopia alters the accommodative-convergence relationship, predisposing to both esotropia and exotropia depending on the specific refractive and biometric characteristics
  • Anisometropia (different refractive error between the two eyes) can develop if myopia progresses asymmetrically, driving suppression and strabismus
  • Structural vitreoretinal abnormalities may affect visual input quality, particularly if amblyopia develops from uncorrected high myopia
  • Progressive myopia, when present, can worsen an existing deviation or trigger a new one
Key point: Strabismus often presents alongside high myopia in Marshall syndrome and may worsen as myopia progresses. Any change in refractive error should prompt re-evaluation of eye alignment. Conversely, new-onset strabismus in a known Marshall syndrome patient should prompt assessment for changes in refractive error or retinal status.

Impact on the Child

Strabismus affects far more than cosmetic appearance. Its impacts span visual development, daily functioning, and psychosocial well-being:

Visual Consequences

  • Loss of binocular vision and stereopsis — depth perception requires the brain to fuse images from both eyes. Misaligned eyes send conflicting images, preventing the development or maintenance of stereopsis. This affects tasks like catching a ball, pouring liquids, navigating stairs, and eventually driving.
  • Amblyopia development — the brain suppresses the misaligned eye’s image, and without treatment, that eye’s visual acuity deteriorates permanently. In a child who already has high myopia and retinal risk from Marshall syndrome, losing functional vision in one eye compounds the overall visual vulnerability.
  • Reduced visual field — although each eye has a full visual field, loss of binocular function reduces the effective overlap and depth information in the central visual field.

Functional Difficulties

  • Reading difficulties — eye tracking, convergence, and sustained alignment are essential for reading. Children with strabismus may skip lines, lose their place, or fatigue quickly during reading tasks.
  • Headaches and eye strain — particularly with intermittent strabismus, where the child is constantly working to maintain alignment. The muscular effort to control the deviation causes fatigue-type headaches, especially by the end of the school day.
  • Compensatory head tilting or turning — children may adopt an abnormal head position to find a gaze angle where the eyes are better aligned. This can cause secondary neck and shoulder discomfort over time. Parents may notice the child consistently tilting their head to one side in photographs.

Psychosocial Impact

  • Social and cosmetic concerns — visible eye misalignment can be a source of teasing or social difficulty for school-age children. Studies consistently show that strabismus negatively affects perceived appearance and social interactions.
  • Self-consciousness — older children and adolescents may avoid eye contact or develop anxiety about their appearance.
  • Combined burden — for a child already managing multiple Marshall syndrome features (hearing aids, thick glasses, joint issues), strabismus adds another visible difference that can affect self-image.
For parents: If your child tilts their head consistently, squints or closes one eye in bright light, complains of headaches after school, or seems to have difficulty with depth-related tasks (catching, pouring, stairs), bring these observations to your ophthalmologist. These may be signs of strabismus even when the misalignment is not obvious to casual observation.

Diagnosis and Monitoring

Strabismus diagnosis relies on a combination of clinical tests performed by an ophthalmologist or orthoptist (a specialist in eye alignment and binocular vision).

Key Diagnostic Tests

TestWhat It MeasuresHow It Works
Cover test (unilateral) Manifest (constant) strabismus One eye is covered while the examiner watches the uncovered eye for a refixation movement. If the uncovered eye moves to pick up fixation, strabismus is present.
Alternate cover test Total deviation (manifest + latent) The cover is alternated between eyes, preventing binocular fusion. Any refixation movement indicates a deviation. This test reveals the full magnitude of the misalignment, including any latent component.
Prism cover test Magnitude of deviation in prism diopters Prisms of increasing strength are placed before the eye during the alternate cover test until the refixation movement is neutralized. This quantifies the deviation precisely.
Hirschberg test (corneal light reflex) Approximate alignment A penlight is held before the eyes, and the position of the light reflection on each cornea is compared. Useful for young children or infants who cannot cooperate with cover testing.
Sensory testing (Worth 4-dot, Titmus stereo) Binocular function and stereopsis Assesses whether the brain is fusing images from both eyes and the quality of depth perception.

When to Refer

  • Pediatric ophthalmologist is the preferred first referral for any child with suspected strabismus, as they are specifically trained in childhood eye alignment disorders, amblyopia management, and surgical correction in children.
  • A general ophthalmologist may identify the strabismus but should refer to a pediatric ophthalmologist or strabismus specialist for management, particularly in the context of a connective tissue disorder.
  • In Marshall syndrome, the strabismus specialist should coordinate closely with the vitreoretinal specialist managing the retinal and vitreous aspects of care.

Monitoring Frequency

  • Newly diagnosed strabismus: every 2–4 months initially to assess response to treatment (glasses, patching)
  • Stable, treated strabismus: every 4–6 months during the amblyopia treatment years (typically until age 8–10)
  • Post-surgical: frequently in the first weeks (1 day, 1 week, 1 month, 3 months), then every 6 months for the first 2 years to monitor for recurrence
  • Ongoing Marshall syndrome eye exams: alignment should be assessed at every routine eye exam, even if strabismus was previously treated successfully — recurrence rates are higher in connective tissue disorders

Treatment Options Established

Treatment depends on the type and magnitude of the strabismus, the child’s age, the presence of amblyopia, and the underlying cause. In Marshall syndrome, the relationship between high myopia and the deviation is particularly important.

TreatmentHow It WorksMarshall/Stickler Considerations
Glasses/prism correction Correcting the refractive error (especially hyperopia in accommodative esotropia, or high myopia) reduces the accommodative effort and can realign the eyes. Prisms in the lenses can optically compensate for small deviations. Essential first step in Marshall syndrome. Full correction of high myopia may partially or fully resolve accommodative components of the strabismus. Impact-rated polycarbonate lenses are mandatory for eye protection. Prisms can provide temporary alignment while other treatments are considered.
Patching (occlusion therapy) The stronger eye is covered for prescribed hours per day, forcing the brain to use the weaker (amblyopic) eye, strengthening neural connections. Compliance can be challenging, especially with thick myopia lenses. Ensure the patching schedule does not interfere with the child’s ability to function safely — depth perception is already compromised. Use under ophthalmologist guidance only.
Atropine penalization Atropine drops blur the vision in the stronger eye, achieving a similar effect to patching without the adhesive patch. Particularly useful for mild to moderate amblyopia. May be better tolerated than patching for some children. Note that atropine dilates the pupil — photosensitivity may be an issue. Discuss with your ophthalmologist whether this interacts with any concurrent eye treatments.
Vision therapy/orthoptics Structured exercises to improve binocular control, fusional vergence ranges, and eye coordination. Most effective for convergence insufficiency and intermittent exotropia. Can be a useful adjunct, particularly for intermittent deviations. Not a substitute for glasses or surgery when those are needed. Requires a motivated, cooperative child and consistent practice.
Surgical correction (recession/resection) Recession weakens a muscle by moving its insertion point further back on the sclera. Resection strengthens a muscle by removing a section and reattaching the shortened muscle closer to the front. Often combined (e.g., recess the medial rectus and resect the lateral rectus for esotropia). Significant COL11A1 considerations — see detailed section below. Scleral thinning affects suture placement and holding. Higher recurrence rates. Coordination with vitreoretinal specialist is essential.
Botulinum toxin (Botox) injection Injected into a specific extraocular muscle to temporarily weaken it, allowing the opposing muscle to pull the eye into better alignment. Effect lasts 3–6 months. Can be useful as a diagnostic tool (to predict surgical outcome), as a temporizing measure, or for small residual deviations after surgery. Avoids scleral suturing. May need repeated injections. Risk of ptosis (drooping eyelid) as a temporary side effect.
Practical sequence: In most cases, treatment follows a stepwise approach: (1) correct the refractive error fully with glasses, (2) treat any amblyopia with patching or atropine, (3) reassess the residual deviation, (4) consider surgery or Botox for any remaining misalignment. Do not skip straight to surgery without first optimizing glasses and treating amblyopia — the surgical target depends on the deviation measured with full optical correction in place.

Surgical Considerations with COL11A1 Supported

Strabismus surgery in patients with COL11A1 mutations carries specific risks that differ from the general population. The surgeon must be informed of the Marshall syndrome diagnosis and ideally have experience with connective tissue disorder patients. These considerations directly affect the surgical plan, technique, and expected outcomes.

Scleral Thinness: The Critical Factor

This is the most important surgical consideration. In Marshall syndrome, the sclera is thinner than normal due to defective type XI collagen. This has several direct consequences:

  • Suture placement risk — standard strabismus surgery involves placing sutures through the sclera to reattach the repositioned muscle. In thin sclera, sutures can pull through or cheese-wire, failing to hold the muscle in its new position. This is the mechanism behind many surgical failures in this population.
  • Scleral perforation risk — the needle passes through the partial thickness of the sclera during suture placement. With thin sclera, there is a higher risk of inadvertent full-thickness perforation, which could damage the retina or choroid underneath.
  • Modified surgical technique required — surgeons may use:
    • Spatulated (flat) needles rather than cutting needles to reduce perforation risk
    • Broader, shallower suture bites to distribute force over a larger area
    • Non-absorbable sutures for more secure long-term holding
    • Scleral reinforcement patches (donor sclera or synthetic) at the suture site in severe cases
    • Hang-back technique (the muscle is suspended from sutures rather than sutured directly to the sclera at the new insertion point)

Higher Recurrence Rates

Strabismus surgery in connective tissue disorders has higher rates of under-correction, drift, and recurrence compared to the general population. Contributing factors include:

  • Sutures loosening or pulling through over time due to scleral weakness
  • Progressive muscle stretching due to connective tissue laxity
  • Changes in globe size if myopia is still progressing, altering the muscle mechanics
  • Altered wound healing in collagen-deficient tissue

Families should be counseled that more than one surgery may be needed to achieve and maintain alignment. This is not a failure of the surgeon — it reflects the underlying connective tissue biology.

Anesthesia Concerns

Strabismus surgery is performed under general anesthesia in children. For patients with Marshall syndrome, there are specific anesthesia considerations related to the connective tissue disorder, including potential airway management issues (midface hypoplasia, micrognathia) and joint hypermobility affecting positioning.

Before any surgery requiring anesthesia, review the anesthesia-specific considerations for Marshall syndrome. Your anesthesiologist must be informed of the diagnosis. See the Anesthesia Considerations page for detailed guidance on airway management, intubation, and positioning in COL11A1-related connective tissue disorders.

Coordination with Retinal Specialist

If the child has concurrent retinal issues (or is at high risk, as all Marshall syndrome patients are), strabismus surgery planning should involve the vitreoretinal specialist:

  • Pre-operative retinal assessment — a dilated retinal exam should be performed before strabismus surgery to document baseline retinal status and rule out any active retinal pathology that would affect surgical planning
  • Surgical timing — if prophylactic retinal treatment (cryotherapy or laser) is also being planned, discuss whether it can or should be performed at the same session to reduce the number of general anesthetics
  • Scleral buckle considerations — if the child has had or may need a scleral buckle for retinal detachment, this directly affects the anatomy of the extraocular muscles and must be factored into the strabismus surgical plan
  • Post-operative monitoring — after strabismus surgery, continue regular retinal surveillance as manipulation of the extraocular muscles and sclera could theoretically affect vitreoretinal dynamics

Relationship to Other Eye Issues

Strabismus does not exist in isolation in Marshall syndrome. It interacts with and is influenced by other ocular features of the condition:

Connection to High Myopia

High myopia is the most common ocular finding in Marshall syndrome (approximately 80% prevalence) and directly contributes to strabismus risk through altered muscle mechanics and accommodative-convergence relationships. Management of myopia and strabismus must be coordinated — changes in one affect the other. See Eye Management: Vitreous, Retina, Myopia & Cataracts for comprehensive myopia guidance.

Strabismus Surgery and Retinal Detachment Risk

There is a theoretical concern about manipulating the sclera and extraocular muscles during strabismus surgery in eyes already at high risk for retinal detachment. While strabismus surgery is not a major independent risk factor for retinal detachment, the scleral manipulation, changes in globe pressure during surgery, and post-operative inflammation warrant heightened vigilance:

  • Post-operative retinal exam should be performed within the first few weeks after surgery
  • Patients and families should be educated about retinal detachment warning signs (sudden floaters, flashes, curtain/shadow over vision) and instructed to seek emergency care immediately if these occur in the post-operative period
  • If both strabismus surgery and a vitreoretinal procedure are needed, careful sequencing and coordination between the two specialists is essential

Cataract Surgery Timing

Some Marshall syndrome patients develop cataracts (reported in approximately 59% with COL11A1 mutations) that may eventually require surgical removal. If both strabismus surgery and cataract surgery are needed:

  • Address strabismus first if the cataract is not visually significant — accurate strabismus measurements require a clear visual axis, and cataract surgery changes the refractive status, which could alter the strabismus
  • Address cataract first if it is causing amblyopia or significantly reducing vision — the strabismus pattern may change after the refractive status is altered by cataract removal and intraocular lens implantation
  • Combined procedures are sometimes possible but add complexity and should be performed only by surgeons experienced with both procedures in the context of connective tissue disorders
Building your team: Ideally, a child with Marshall syndrome and strabismus should have a care team that includes a pediatric ophthalmologist/strabismus specialist, a vitreoretinal specialist, and an optometrist for refractive management. These providers should communicate directly with each other, particularly before any surgical intervention. See Building Your Specialist Team for guidance on coordinating multi-specialty care.

Questions for Your Eye Care Team

Bring these questions to your appointments. Not all providers will be familiar with the specifics of COL11A1-related strabismus:

  1. “Is the strabismus related to the high myopia, and would full correction of the refractive error reduce the deviation?” — Understanding the relationship between the myopia and the misalignment determines whether glasses alone may help.
  2. “Is there amblyopia in either eye, and what is the treatment plan?” — Amblyopia treatment is time-sensitive and should begin as early as possible during the critical period.
  3. “If surgery is recommended, what experience do you have operating on patients with thin sclera or connective tissue disorders?” — This is not a question to be shy about. The surgical technique must be modified for COL11A1 patients.
  4. “How will you coordinate with the vitreoretinal specialist before and after strabismus surgery?” — Ensure the retinal status is assessed pre-operatively and monitored post-operatively.
  5. “What are the expected recurrence rates, and what is the plan if the alignment drifts after surgery?” — Set realistic expectations. A plan for re-intervention should be discussed upfront.
  6. “Are there any anesthesia precautions specific to Marshall syndrome that need to be communicated to the anesthesiology team?” — Ensure the anesthesia considerations are reviewed before any procedure.
