School 504 Plan Template

A 504 Plan is a legally binding document under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (1973) that ensures your child receives accommodations to access education on equal footing with peers. Marshall syndrome affects multiple systems — spine, vision, hearing, joints, autonomic function — so accommodations must address the full spectrum, not just pain.

504 Plan vs. IEP — Know the Difference (Updated R523)
  • 504 Plan (Section 504, Rehabilitation Act): Provides accommodations — changes to how the child accesses the standard curriculum. No specialized instruction. Covers any disability that substantially limits a major life activity. This is what most children with Marshall syndrome need initially.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program, IDEA): Provides specialized instruction — modified curriculum, pull-out services, measurable goals. Requires the child to qualify under one of 13 disability categories. Consider if Marshall syndrome causes significant academic impact beyond what accommodations alone can address (e.g., prolonged absences, cognitive effects of chronic pain).
  • Key legal point: A 504 Plan is enforceable under federal law. The school must provide the accommodations. If the school refuses, file a complaint with the U.S. Department of Education’s Office for Civil Rights (OCR).
Research Update (R523): IEP vs. 504 Decision Guide for Marshall Syndrome

An IEP provides more comprehensive services than a 504 Plan but requires qualifying under IDEA disability categories. For Marshall syndrome, the decision should be guided by functional impact, not diagnosis alone:

  • Start with 504 when: the child is keeping up academically with accommodations (seating, FM system, extended time, modified PE), hearing loss is stable and well-amplified, and vision is correctable. Most newly diagnosed children with Marshall syndrome fall into this category
  • Shift to IEP when: progressive hearing loss requires specialized speech-language services, persistent absences exceed 15% of school days, chronic pain or fatigue significantly impairs academic performance despite accommodations, or the child needs a modified curriculum (not just modified access to the standard curriculum)
  • Progressive hearing loss is the most common trigger: A child who initially manages with a 504 Plan and FM system may require an IEP when hearing loss progresses to the point where speech-language pathology services, specialized instruction in auditory skills, or a teacher of the deaf becomes necessary. Under IDEA, hearing impairment is a qualifying disability category. Do not wait until the child is failing academically — request IEP evaluation as soon as hearing loss begins to affect classroom participation
  • Dual coverage is possible: A child can have both a 504 Plan (for accommodations across all settings) and an IEP (for specialized services like speech therapy). The IEP takes precedence where they overlap, but the 504 Plan can cover areas the IEP does not address
IHP vs 504 Plan — You Need Both

A critical distinction most families miss: the 504 Plan and the IHP (Individualized Healthcare Plan) serve different purposes, and your child likely needs both.

  • IHP (Individualized Healthcare Plan) — a medical document managed by the school nurse. It covers emergency protocols, medication administration, and clinical procedures. For Marshall syndrome, the IHP should include: retinal emergency recognition (new floaters, flashes, curtain effect = call 911), hearing aid troubleshooting, pain management protocols, and joint subluxation response.
  • 504 Plan — a legal document (Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act) managed by the school team (teachers, counselor, administrator). It covers educational accommodations. For Marshall syndrome, key 504 accommodations include: preferential seating (hearing), large print or digital materials (vision), modified PE (joints/spine), extended time (fatigue), elevator access, and rest breaks during pain flares.

The IHP tells the nurse what to do in a medical situation. The 504 Plan tells teachers how to teach your child effectively. One without the other leaves gaps.

IDEA/IEP may also apply: If hearing loss or vision impairment affects educational performance to the degree that specialized instruction is needed (not just accommodations), your child may qualify for an IEP under IDEA categories for hearing impairment or visual impairment. This is separate from and can coexist with a 504 Plan.

2025 COPAA guidance: The Council of Parent Attorneys and Advocates has highlighted that students with rare diseases are systematically underserved by 504 Plans alone, because school teams lack familiarity with the condition and default to generic accommodations. Push for condition-specific language — “modified PE” is not enough; specify which activities are safe and which are prohibited.

Documentation That Carries Weight
Ask the medical team for a letter on medical letterhead that includes: (1) the diagnosis (Marshall syndrome / COL11A1 mutation), (2) the specific functional limitations (vision, hearing, spinal pain, joint hypermobility, autonomic dysfunction), and (3) an explicit statement that these limitations substantially limit major life activities including learning, seeing, hearing, walking, and concentrating. This letter is your legal foundation.

Classroom Accommodations

  • Sit-stand desk or standing desk option — the child should be able to alternate between sitting and standing throughout the day. Sustained sitting is as harmful as sustained standing; the ability to change position is the key accommodation.
  • Permission to change positions during class — standing, stretching, shifting position without needing to ask or draw attention. This should be normalized, not treated as a disruption.
  • 30-minute movement break schedule — brief (2–5 minute) movement breaks every 30 minutes during seated instruction. This can be as simple as standing, walking to the water fountain, or doing gentle stretches at the back of the room.
  • Ergonomic seating — supportive chair with lumbar support, or permission to use a seat cushion and/or footrest
  • Preferential seating — near the door (for movement breaks) and with clear sightlines to visual aids (for vision accommodations if needed)

Materials and Supplies

  • Duplicate textbooks — one set at school, one at home. This eliminates the need to carry heavy books back and forth. Backpack weight should not exceed 10% of body weight (or use a rolling bag).
  • Digital textbooks when available — eliminates weight entirely
  • Rolling backpack permitted even if not standard at the school
  • Laptop or tablet for notetaking — reduces the physical strain of sustained handwriting (if writing is painful due to hand joint involvement)
  • Weighted pencil grips and ring splints permitted (R515): Children with hypermobile finger joints often experience significant handwriting fatigue and pain. Ring splints on the writing hand, weighted pencil grips, and slant boards are evidence-based OT interventions that should be explicitly permitted in the 504 Plan. See Orthotics & Bracing for details
Research Update (R518): School Attendance & the Belonging Crisis

Research on chronic illness and school attendance reveals alarming statistics directly relevant to Marshall syndrome:

  • 25.9% of children with chronic pain have persistent absence (defined as missing ≥10% of school days). In Marshall syndrome, where pain, fatigue, medical appointments, and flares all contribute, absence rates may be even higher
  • School belonging predicts attendance more than pain does: Children who feel they “belong” at school attend more consistently than children with lower pain but weaker social connection. Accommodations that maintain social participation (virtual attendance during flares, peer buddy systems, inclusion in social activities even during modified PE) directly improve attendance
  • Partial-day attendance tracking: Request that the 504 Plan include partial-day attendance credit. A child who attends 4 hours on a difficult day should receive partial credit, not be marked absent. Many schools count any day shorter than a full day as an absence, which inflates absence records and can trigger truancy interventions. Specify in the 504 Plan that partial attendance counts toward the attendance requirement
  • Proactive absence planning: Include a provision for a set number of “medical flex days” per quarter where absence is pre-approved for pain flares, medical appointments, or recovery days without requiring same-day parent contact or a physician’s note for each occurrence. A standing medical letter documenting the chronic condition should suffice

Vision Accommodations

CRITICAL — Retinal Detachment Protocol
All staff who interact with the child must know: sudden onset of flashing lights, shower of floaters, or a “curtain” across the visual field = medical emergency. Call 911 immediately. Retinal detachment in Stickler/Marshall syndrome can occur spontaneously and is a surgical emergency — every hour of delay reduces the chance of preserving vision.
  • Preferential seating for visual access — front row or unobstructed sightline to board, teacher, and visual aids
  • Large-print materials or permission to use magnification devices if myopia is significant
  • Digital copies of all handouts — allows the child to zoom and adjust contrast on a tablet or laptop
  • Extended time for visually demanding tasks — reading-intensive assignments, map work, detailed diagrams
  • Reduced glare — non-reflective surfaces, adjustable lighting, permission to wear tinted lenses indoors if photosensitive
  • No activities with risk of eye trauma — dodgeball, racket sports, contact sports. Protective eyewear (polycarbonate lenses) should be worn during all physical activities
  • Annual ophthalmology clearance documented in the plan, with the school informed of any activity restrictions after each exam

Hearing Accommodations

Sensorineural hearing loss affects the majority of children with Marshall syndrome and can be progressive. Even mild loss significantly impacts classroom learning.

  • FM system or remote microphone (Roger system) — teacher wears a transmitter, child wears a receiver. This is the single most impactful hearing accommodation in a classroom. The school district must provide this at no cost to the family under IDEA and Section 504 when documented as necessary by the audiologist (R556). The system dramatically reduces listening fatigue — the invisible cognitive burden that depletes a hearing-impaired child’s energy and attention long before the school day ends
  • Preferential seating for auditory access — close to the teacher, away from HVAC units, hallway doors, and other noise sources
  • Closed captioning on all video content
  • Written instructions in addition to verbal directions — do not rely on the child hearing announcements
  • Note-taking support — a peer note-taker or teacher-provided notes, since the child cannot simultaneously lip-read/listen and write
  • Reduced background noise — carpet, acoustic panels, tennis balls on chair legs, or assignment to a quieter classroom
  • Annual audiogram with results shared with the 504 team to adjust accommodations as hearing changes

Physical Education

  • Modified PE, not exemption — the child should participate in PE with specific modifications, not be excused entirely. Exercise is therapeutic (see Exercise as Medicine).
  • Safe activities: Swimming, cycling/stationary bike, walking, modified yoga, Pilates-based exercises, lightweight resistance bands, balance exercises
  • Activities to avoid: Running (sustained or sprinting), contact sports, trampolining, gymnastics, heavy lifting, dodgeball or activities with risk of impact to head/face
  • PE should not be first period (discs are maximally pressurized in the morning)
  • The PE teacher should be provided with the specific safe/avoid activity lists
Research Update (R579): Adapted PE Is a Legal Right Under IDEA — Not Optional

Under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), physical education is the only curriculum area specifically named in federal law as a required component of special education services. Schools must provide adapted PE — they cannot simply exempt a child from PE and leave it at that.

  • Modify, do not exempt: A blanket PE exemption is not compliant with IDEA and denies the child the documented health benefits of exercise, which are particularly important in Marshall syndrome for joint stability, bone density, cardiovascular fitness, and pain management. The correct approach is an individualized adapted PE plan that specifies which activities are safe, which require modification, and which are prohibited
  • Lifetime fitness focus: Adapted PE for children with chronic conditions should emphasize lifetime fitness skills (swimming, cycling, yoga, walking programs, resistance band training) rather than competitive team sports. These are the activities the child will use for health maintenance throughout adulthood. An adapted PE teacher who focuses only on modified versions of team sports misses the opportunity to build lifelong exercise habits
  • PE teacher training is required: The school must ensure the PE teacher has access to the child’s activity restrictions and medical guidance. Provide a written one-page summary (from the Sports Clearance page) listing green/yellow/red activities. A brief meeting between the PE teacher, parent, and physical therapist at the start of each school year prevents misunderstandings
  • Grading accommodation: If the child cannot perform standard fitness testing (e.g., mile run, sit-ups), the 504 Plan should specify alternative assessment methods. Grading on effort and participation in adapted activities — not on performance metrics designed for typical children — ensures the child is not penalized for a medical condition
Ergonomic Classroom Setup Checklist
A properly configured classroom workspace reduces pain, prevents fatigue, and keeps the child engaged in learning. Use this checklist when setting up accommodations:

Seating:

  • Adjustable chair with lumbar support — the chair back should support the natural curve of the lower spine, not force the child into a flat-backed posture
  • Footrest to keep feet flat (if chair height means feet dangle) — unsupported feet increase lumbar disc pressure
  • Angled desktop surface (15–20 degrees) for reading and writing — reduces cervical flexion, which is critical for children with cervical disc involvement. A simple desktop easel or slant board achieves this.

Visual environment:

  • Anti-glare lighting — avoid fluorescent flicker and direct overhead glare, which exacerbate visual fatigue in children with myopia or vitreous abnormalities
  • Document camera (ELMO) in the classroom — projects worksheets onto a large screen so the child can read from a distance rather than hunching over a desk copy
  • Tablet or laptop with adjustable font size — digital materials allow the child to enlarge text as needed without special large-print versions

Auditory environment:

  • Soundfield FM system — the teacher wears a microphone that broadcasts through classroom speakers, benefiting the child with hearing loss and improving signal-to-noise ratio for the entire class
  • Carpet, acoustic ceiling tiles, and soft surfaces — hard surfaces reflect sound and increase background noise, making hearing aids and FM systems less effective
  • Seated position near the teacher — reduces the distance sound must travel and allows lip-reading to supplement hearing

Physical environment:

  • Standing desk option — alternating between sitting and standing is the single most effective posture intervention. A simple adjustable desk converter placed on a standard desk works well.
  • Nearby storage (cubby, shelf, or low locker) — eliminates the need to carry materials across the room. Heavy textbooks should live at the desk, not in a distant locker.
  • Padded seat cushion — memory foam or gel cushion reduces pressure on the coccyx and ischial tuberosities during sustained sitting, particularly important for children with joint hypermobility

Assistive technology:

  • Speech-to-text software (e.g., Dragon Naturally Speaking, Google Voice Typing) — reduces the physical burden of handwriting and typing for children with hand or cervical pain
  • Recorded lectures — audio recording of class instruction allows the child to review material they may have missed due to hearing difficulties, pain distraction, or fatigue
  • Voice-to-text for writing assignments — dictation eliminates the cervical flexion posture of writing while maintaining academic output expectations

Building Access

  • Elevator access if the school is multi-story
  • Extra time between classes to avoid rushing on stairs or through crowded hallways
  • Locker placement in a central location to minimize walking with heavy materials

Autonomic & Temperature Regulation

Many children with Marshall syndrome experience autonomic dysfunction (see Autonomic Dysfunction) that affects temperature regulation, blood pressure, and energy levels.

  • Permission to carry water bottle and salty snacks at all times — hydration and sodium are first-line treatments for POTS-like symptoms (lightheadedness, fatigue, brain fog)
  • Permission to leave class without asking if feeling lightheaded or faint — the child should go to the nurse or sit/lie down immediately
  • Temperature-controlled environment — avoid placing the child near radiators, sunny windows, or in poorly ventilated rooms. Heat can trigger autonomic flares, flushing, and fatigue
  • Modified recess in extreme temperatures — indoor alternative when temperatures exceed 85°F or fall below 20°F
  • Compression garments permitted if prescribed for POTS management

Health and Pain Management

  • Nurse access for pain management — permission to visit the school nurse for medication, ice/heat, or a quiet rest period during pain flares without requiring teacher approval each time. A standing nurse pass eliminates the barrier of asking
  • Permission to carry water bottle at all times (hydration supports both disc health and autonomic function)
  • Excused absences for medical appointments and pain flare days without academic penalty — this includes no grade reduction for missed in-class work during excused absences
  • Modified homework load during flare periods — extended deadlines, not reduced expectations. Pain flares are unpredictable; flexibility is key
  • Access to a quiet, dimly lit space during pain episodes — not the hallway, not the cafeteria. A dedicated rest area
  • Pain flare action plan on file with the nurse: what medications are authorized, what non-pharmacological interventions to try (ice, heat, position change, rest), when to call the parent, and when to call 911

Bathroom Access & Pelvic Floor Considerations

Hypermobility affects the pelvic floor. Joint hypermobility in connective tissue disorders like Marshall syndrome can affect pelvic floor muscle function, leading to bladder urgency, frequency, or difficulty with continence — particularly in adolescents. These issues are underrecognized in the school setting and can cause significant embarrassment and avoidance behavior if not accommodated.
  • Unrestricted bathroom access — the child should be able to use the restroom at any time without asking permission or using a limited pass system. Bladder urgency in hypermobility is not a behavioral issue; it is a physiological consequence of pelvic floor laxity. Document this accommodation explicitly in the 504 Plan.
  • No penalties for bathroom frequency — some schools track or limit bathroom visits. This must be waived. Punitive bathroom policies cause children to avoid drinking water (worsening autonomic dysfunction) or to hold urine (worsening pelvic floor dysfunction).
  • Access to a private or single-stall restroom when available — for children who experience urgency-related anxiety, the privacy reduces stress and the risk of accidents.
  • Discrete signaling system — a pre-arranged signal (placing a card on the desk, a specific hand sign) that allows the child to leave for the restroom without drawing class attention

Interoception & Pain Self-Reporting

Children with chronic pain often struggle with interoception — the ability to accurately perceive and communicate their internal body states. A child living with daily pain may not know how to distinguish “normal” pain from pain that signals a problem, or may lack the vocabulary to communicate what they are experiencing to adults. This creates a dangerous gap: the child suffers silently, or reports pain inconsistently, leading adults to doubt them.

Traffic Light Pain Self-Reporting System
Implement a simple, visual pain communication tool that the child can use throughout the school day without disrupting class:
  • Green: “I’m managing — I can stay in class and participate.” This is the child’s baseline. It does not mean pain-free; it means functional.
  • Yellow: “Pain is increasing — I need a position change, movement break, or check-in with the nurse soon.” This is the early intervention window. Acting at yellow prevents escalation to red.
  • Red: “I cannot focus or function — I need to go to the nurse now for medication, rest, or parent contact.” This is not negotiable and should not require teacher approval.

The child can communicate their level using a colored card on the desk corner, a wristband system, or a quiet word to the teacher. The key principle: the child is the expert on their own pain. Adults should respond to the reported level without questioning or minimizing. Document the traffic light system in the 504 Plan so all staff use consistent language and responses.

Social Inclusion & Peer Connection

504 Plans and IEPs typically focus on academic accommodations — but for a child with a chronic condition, social needs are equally important to document. Children with Marshall syndrome may miss social opportunities due to medical absences, PE restrictions, hearing difficulties in noisy environments, or visible differences (hearing aids, thick glasses, facial features). Social isolation is a predictable consequence that deserves proactive intervention, not just reactive support.

  • After-school social inclusion clubs: Advocate for or help create low-physical-demand clubs that provide peer connection without the barriers of sports or high-energy activities. Art clubs, music ensembles, coding/robotics clubs, board game groups, creative writing circles, and drama (non-physical roles) all offer social engagement in environments the child can fully participate in.
  • Lunch buddy or social facilitation: For younger children, a structured lunch buddy system ensures the child is not eating alone. For older students, a school counselor can facilitate small-group social skills sessions that are inclusive, not stigmatizing.
  • Disability awareness education: When the family and child are comfortable with it, a brief, age-appropriate presentation to the class about Marshall syndrome (or about differences in general) can reduce bullying, increase empathy, and give the child language to explain their needs to peers.
  • Bullying prevention (R569): Children with visible differences (hearing aids, thick glasses, midface features) are at significantly elevated risk for peer bullying. Evidence-based anti-bullying programs such as KiVa (University of Turku, Finland) reduce bullying by 30–50% when implemented school-wide. Request that your school adopt a validated anti-bullying program. Include anti-bullying monitoring in the 504 Plan with language such as: “School counselor will conduct monthly bullying check-ins with student. Staff will receive training on recognizing disability-related bullying.” Bullying is a measurable source of anxiety, school avoidance, and depression that directly undermines every other accommodation in the plan.
  • Document social needs in the 504 Plan: Include language such as: “Student will have access to at least one extracurricular social activity per semester that accommodates their physical limitations” and “School counselor will check in with student monthly regarding peer relationships and social inclusion.” Making social support part of the legal document ensures it is not treated as optional.
Social accommodations are academic accommodations. Research consistently shows that children who feel socially connected at school perform better academically, attend more consistently, and report less pain interference with learning. A child who dreads school because they feel isolated will not benefit fully from even the best ergonomic setup and testing accommodations. Address the whole child.

Handwriting Support & Keyboarding Transition

Handwriting is one of the most physically demanding fine motor tasks in a school day, requiring sustained grip, precise finger movements, and cervical flexion posture. For children with Marshall syndrome, hand joint hypermobility, finger hyperextension, and cervical pain combine to make handwriting painful, slow, and exhausting. Proactive intervention at school entry — not after problems develop — is essential.

OT Assessment at School Entry

Request an occupational therapy (OT) assessment of fine motor function and handwriting readiness at kindergarten entry (age 5–6). This assessment should evaluate:

  • Grip pattern and finger joint stability during pencil grasp
  • Hyperextension at PIP and DIP joints during writing pressure
  • Hand fatigue onset time (how many minutes of continuous writing before pain or performance decline)
  • Cervical posture during writing tasks (sustained flexion worsens disc and spinal pain)

Interventions by Grade Level

Grade LevelPrimary StrategyTools
Pre-K to 1st Adapted grip and joint protection during handwriting instruction
  • Ring splints (silver or 3D-printed) to prevent PIP/DIP hyperextension during writing — the single most impactful handwriting accommodation for hypermobile fingers
  • Triangular or cushioned pencil grips to reduce grip force
  • Weighted pencils to provide proprioceptive feedback and reduce excess grip pressure
  • Slant board (20–30 degree angle) to reduce cervical flexion and wrist extension during writing — critical for children with cervical spine involvement
2nd grade Begin keyboarding instruction
  • Formal touch-typing curriculum (many are available as free online programs)
  • Target: functional keyboarding by end of 2nd grade so that by 3rd grade, when writing demands increase substantially, the child has a viable alternative to handwriting
  • Ergonomic keyboard setup: split keyboard or low-force keyboard to reduce finger strain
3rd grade+ Keyboarding as primary output with voice-to-text support
  • Voice-to-text (dictation) for longer writing assignments — Google Docs voice typing, Apple Dictation, Microsoft Dictate, Dragon Naturally Speaking. This eliminates both the hand strain of writing and the cervical flexion posture of looking down at a keyboard
  • Laptop or tablet for all written work (include in 504 Plan)
  • Handwriting reserved only for brief tasks (signing name, filling in single-word answers)
Do not wait for handwriting to become a problem. Many schools default to addressing handwriting difficulties reactively — after the child falls behind in written output, develops hand pain, or starts avoiding writing tasks. For a child with Marshall syndrome, the joint hypermobility and connective tissue fragility are present from day one. The OT assessment at school entry, ring splints for writing, a slant board on the desk, and keyboarding instruction by 2nd grade should all be written into the initial 504 Plan, not added as amendments after problems emerge.

Testing Accommodations

  • Extended time (typically 1.5x) — chronic pain, vision limitations, and hearing processing all slow work speed without reflecting ability
  • Movement breaks during testing — the same 30-minute break schedule applies during exams. Build break time into the extended time calculation
  • Separate testing room if FM system use or movement breaks would disrupt others
  • Large-print test materials or permission to use magnification
  • Oral administration of test questions if vision or writing endurance is a barrier
  • Flexible test scheduling — avoid morning testing when spinal stiffness is worst; allow make-up tests without penalty after flare days

Field Trips & Extracurricular Activities

  • Medical kit travels with the child — medications, emergency contact card, and the Emergency Medical Card
  • Activity pre-screening — parents notified in advance of physical activities so they can identify risks (e.g., trampolines, contact sports, extreme temperatures)
  • Accessible transportation — padded seating on buses for long trips; frequent stops for movement breaks
  • Buddy system — a trained peer or adult who knows the emergency protocols accompanies the child
  • Full participation with modifications — the goal is inclusion, not exclusion. The child participates in adapted versions of activities, not sitting out

Staff Training Requirements

Every adult who supervises the child must know these three things:
  1. Retinal detachment emergency: Sudden flashing lights, shower of floaters, or curtain across vision = call 911 immediately
  2. No high-impact activities: No trampolines, contact sports, heavy lifting, gymnastics, or activities with head/eye trauma risk
  3. Pain is real and invisible: This child has a structural collagen disorder. Pain is not behavioral, not attention-seeking, and not “just growing pains”
  • Beginning-of-year briefing for all teachers, PE staff, lunch monitors, bus drivers, and substitute teachers
  • Written summary card (laminated, one page) posted in the nurse’s office and provided to each teacher — diagnosis, key accommodations, emergency protocols
  • Substitute teacher protocol — the 504 Plan summary should be in the substitute folder for every class
School Nurse Emergency Training

A 2025 NASN (National Association of School Nurses) survey found that 67% of school nurses feel unprepared to manage students with rare genetic conditions. Marshall syndrome will almost certainly be new to your school nurse. Proactive training is essential.

Prepare a one-page laminated emergency card for the nurse’s office covering:

  • Retinal detachment signs: new floaters, flashing lights, curtain or shadow across vision = call 911 immediately. Do not wait to contact parents first.
  • Cervical instability: absolutely no contact sports, no heading in soccer, no trampolines, no gymnastics. If the child reports sudden neck pain with tingling in arms or legs, immobilize and call 911.
  • Hearing aid management: basic troubleshooting (battery replacement, feedback elimination, moisture issues), when to contact the audiologist, and FM system setup for each classroom.
  • Joint protection during PE: specific safe and prohibited activities list, subluxation/dislocation first aid, when to apply ice vs. heat.
  • Pain flare protocol: authorized medications with dosages and timing, non-pharmacological options (ice, heat, position change, quiet rest area), escalation criteria for calling parents, and criteria for calling 911.

Annual training session with the child’s specialist team is strongly recommended — even a 30-minute video call between the school nurse and the geneticist or orthopedist can transform the quality of school-based care. Templates for school emergency cards are available from the Society for Inherited Metabolic Disorders (SIP) and the National Organization for Rare Disorders (NORD).

School Nurse Training: Building Condition-Specific Competency (R415)

The 67% of school nurses who feel unprepared for rare conditions reflects a systemic gap, not a personal failing. Families can bridge this gap using a structured approach that empowers the school nurse rather than overwhelming them:

  • Individualized Healthcare Plan (IHP): Work with the school nurse to create a formal IHP — a medical management document separate from the 504 Plan. The IHP should contain condition-specific protocols for each of Marshall syndrome’s affected systems: retinal emergency response, hearing aid troubleshooting, joint subluxation first aid, pain flare escalation criteria, and cervical spine precautions. The IHP is the nurse’s clinical playbook; the 504 Plan is the teacher’s instructional playbook.
  • Emergency Action Plan (EAP): A one-page, color-coded emergency document posted in the nurse’s office with three tiers: (1) RED — call 911 immediately (retinal detachment signs, cervical spine injury, severe allergic reaction); (2) YELLOW — contact parents and consider medical evaluation (persistent pain escalation, hearing aid failure with no backup, joint subluxation); (3) GREEN — manage in the nurse’s office (routine pain flare, medication administration, position change break). Color-coding enables rapid triage by any staff member, including substitutes.
  • NASN resources: The National Association of School Nurses (NASN) provides condition-specific training modules through its online learning platform. While no Marshall syndrome-specific module exists, the NASN modules on rare genetic conditions, hearing loss management, and vision emergencies are directly applicable. Recommend that the school nurse complete these modules and document the training in the child’s school health file.
  • Specialist-to-nurse communication: Request a 30-minute video call between the school nurse and the child’s geneticist or primary specialist at the beginning of each school year. This single conversation — where the nurse can ask questions directly — provides more practical competency than any written document. Many specialists will accommodate this request at no additional cost when framed as a care coordination call.

Emergency Information

  • School nurse should have on file: diagnosis details, the Emergency Medical Card, and the retinal detachment emergency protocol
  • List of current medications, dosages, and when they can be administered
  • Contact information for all specialists: geneticist, ophthalmologist, audiologist, orthopedist, and primary care
  • Anesthetic alert: If the child requires emergency dental work or minor procedures at school events, inform providers that Marshall syndrome patients have up to 88% resistance to local anesthetics — see Medication Sensitivity
IHP vs. ECP vs. 504: Three Documents, Three Purposes (R547)

Families often confuse these three school documents. Each serves a distinct purpose and all three should be in place:

DocumentPurposeWho Uses ItWho Creates It
504 Plan Legally enforceable academic and physical accommodations Teachers, PE staff, administrators 504 team (parents + school)
Individualized Healthcare Plan (IHP) Condition-specific medical management protocols for the school setting School nurse School nurse in collaboration with the family and medical team
Emergency Care Plan (ECP) Step-by-step response for medical emergencies — retinal detachment, cervical injury, severe pain crisis, allergic reaction Any staff member present during an emergency School nurse with input from specialists

The IHP is the school nurse’s clinical playbook — it should include hearing aid troubleshooting, pain flare medication protocols, joint subluxation first aid, and activity restriction details. The ECP should be laminated, color-coded (red/yellow/green triage), and posted in the nurse’s office, the main office, and the child’s primary classroom. Staff training on the ECP should occur at the beginning of each school year and whenever substitute staff are assigned.

School Reintegration After Extended Absence

Children with Marshall syndrome frequently miss school due to pain flares, surgical recovery, and medical appointments. Research in pediatric chronic pain shows that children with complex conditions miss an average of 22% of school days — approximately one day per week. Extended or frequent absences create a reintegration challenge that, if not managed proactively, can lead to school refusal, social withdrawal, and academic decline.

IIPT Programs and School-Based Teachers

Intensive Interdisciplinary Pain Treatment (IIPT) programs (see Pain Management) increasingly integrate school teachers directly into the treatment team. These embedded educators ensure that academic progress continues during the treatment period and, critically, plan the reintegration before the child returns. Key features of this model:

  • Academic continuity during treatment: A teacher within the IIPT program maintains curriculum alignment with the child’s home school, so the child does not fall behind during the 3–4 week intensive program
  • Reintegration planning before discharge: The IIPT teacher communicates directly with the home school to establish a graduated return schedule, ensure accommodations are in place, and brief classroom teachers on the child’s needs
  • Functional school goals: IIPT programs set school attendance as a primary outcome measure — published data shows school attendance normalizes in approximately 70% of IIPT participants

The Graduated Return Schedule

Abrupt return to full school days after an extended absence frequently fails. A graduated schedule respects the child’s reconditioning needs and builds success incrementally:

WeekScheduleNotes
Week 12–3 hours per day, favorite subjects and social periods onlyPriority is re-establishing the habit of attending and reconnecting with peers. Academics are secondary.
Week 2Half days (4 hours), adding core academic subjectsMorning attendance is typical; afternoon fatigue is expected. Build in a rest break before the most demanding subject.
Week 3Extended days (5–6 hours) with one rest periodThe child should have access to a quiet rest area if needed. Monitor for pain escalation and adjust if needed.

Research Update (R493): School Nurse IHP — The 7-Tenet Framework for Complex Conditions

The Individualized Healthcare Plan (IHP) developed by the school nurse is the medical counterpart to the 504 Plan. For a child with Marshall syndrome, the IHP should be built around 7 core tenets identified in 2025 guidance for managing complex conditions in the school setting:

  1. Condition-specific emergency protocols: Written, step-by-step instructions for the three Marshall syndrome emergencies (retinal detachment: new floaters/flashes/curtain = call 911; cervical injury: do NOT move the child, stabilize neck; pain crisis: follow flare protocol, contact parent)
  2. Medication administration plan: Which medications can be administered at school, exact doses, timing, storage requirements, and who is authorized to administer. Include PRN (as-needed) medications with clear criteria for when to give them
  3. Daily management procedures: Hearing aid troubleshooting (battery changes, feedback management), pain assessment protocol, activity restriction reminders for PE and recess
  4. Communication tree: Who to call first, second, and third in each scenario. Include parent cell, pediatrician office, and specialist emergency lines
  5. Staff training requirements: Which school staff need to be trained on the IHP (not just the nurse — include classroom teachers, PE teacher, lunch aides, bus driver, substitute teacher protocol)
  6. Annual review with medical team: The IHP should be reviewed and updated at least annually with input from the child’s medical team, not just the school nurse working from outdated information
  7. Emergency Care Plan (ECP) quick reference: A single-page laminated summary that stays with the child at all times — in the classroom, at PE, on field trips, and on the bus. This is different from and simpler than the full IHP; it covers only “what to do right now” in an emergency

Practical tip: Create the ECP using the Emergency Medical Card template, customized with the child’s specific details, and provide laminated copies to every adult who supervises the child during the school day.

Teacher Education About Marshall Syndrome

Most teachers have never heard of Marshall syndrome. Unlike common conditions (asthma, diabetes, ADHD), Marshall syndrome has no standardized school education materials. This means the burden of educating the school falls entirely on the family. Prepare a brief, non-technical summary that covers: (1) what Marshall syndrome is in one sentence (“a genetic condition affecting collagen that causes pain, hearing loss, and vision problems”), (2) what the child can and cannot do, (3) the three emergencies every adult must know (retinal detachment, cervical injury, pain flare protocol), and (4) that the child’s pain is real, structural, and not behavioral.

Post-Surgical School Reintegration

Children with Marshall syndrome undergo an average of 3–7 surgeries during their school years (ophthalmologic, ENT, orthopedic, craniofacial). Each surgery creates an extended absence that requires a distinct reintegration approach — different from flare-related absences because post-surgical recovery follows a predictable trajectory with specific physical restrictions.

Surgery-Specific Reintegration Considerations
  • After eye surgery (retinal detachment repair, cataract removal): The child may return to school with positioning restrictions (no bending below the waist, no prone position), activity restrictions (no PE, no recess involving running or jumping for 4–6 weeks), and temporary vision changes (blurred vision, light sensitivity, depth perception changes if one eye is patched). 504 Plan accommodations should temporarily expand to include: seated-only activities, exemption from all physical activities, a designated aide to help navigate the school building safely, enlarged print materials, and modified screen time if eye strain is a concern. Communicate specific restrictions in writing to every adult who supervises the child.
  • After ear surgery (tympanostomy tubes, tympanoplasty, cochlear implant): Hearing may change post-operatively — sometimes temporarily worse before improving. Packing or dressings may affect hearing aid use. Update FM system settings with the audiologist before the child returns. Request a brief audiogram at 2 weeks post-surgery to calibrate accommodations. Water restrictions (no swimming, need to protect ears from water) may conflict with aquatic therapy schedules.
  • After spinal or orthopedic surgery: Lifting restrictions, brace wear, and modified seating are common for 6–12 weeks. The child may need a wheelchair or walker temporarily. Plan for elevator access, a rolling backpack, and physical assistance carrying trays in the cafeteria. PT recommendations for graduated return to activity should be shared with the PE teacher in writing.
  • After craniofacial surgery (palate repair, mandibular distraction, Le Fort osteotomy): Facial swelling, dietary restrictions (soft foods only, possibly liquid diet), and speech changes (temporary or permanent) will be visible to peers. Proactive peer education — a brief, age-appropriate explanation of what happened and why the child looks different temporarily — reduces bullying and staring. Consider having the school counselor facilitate this conversation with the child’s permission.
Pre-Surgical School Planning Checklist
Before any planned surgery, schedule a meeting with the 504 team to create a temporary modification plan that activates on the child’s first day back. This prevents the common problem of the child returning to school before accommodations are updated, resulting in a painful and discouraging first day. The plan should include: (1) expected absence duration, (2) homebound tutoring request if absence exceeds 10 days, (3) surgery-specific physical restrictions with end dates, (4) temporary additional accommodations beyond the standing 504 Plan, (5) the graduated return schedule, and (6) a designated staff point of contact for the parent during reintegration.

Online and Hybrid Learning Options During Flares

For periods when in-person attendance is not possible, ensure the 504 Plan explicitly includes provisions for alternative learning:

  • Synchronous virtual attendance: Live video connection to the classroom so the child can participate in real time. Most schools have infrastructure for this from the COVID era — the 504 Plan should specify that this is available on flare days without requiring advance notice.
  • Asynchronous options: Recorded lectures, digital assignments, and online platforms (Google Classroom, Canvas) that allow the child to complete work at their own pace during recovery.
  • Homebound instruction: If absences exceed 10 consecutive days, most school districts are required to provide homebound tutoring services. This must be requested formally and documented in the 504 Plan or IEP.
  • Dual enrollment: Some families find that dual enrollment in a virtual school program alongside the brick-and-mortar school provides a safety net — the child attends in person when able and switches to the virtual program during extended flares.

Early Intervention: Birth to Age 3 (IDEA Part C) (R512)

For children diagnosed with Marshall syndrome at birth or in infancy — often identified through Pierre Robin sequence, congenital cataracts, or family history — the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) Part C provides early intervention services from birth through age 3. This is a separate program from the 504 Plan and IEP systems that apply to school-age children, and it is critically underutilized in rare disease populations.

What is IDEA Part C?

IDEA Part C is a federal grant program that funds early intervention services for infants and toddlers (birth to age 3) with developmental delays or established conditions that have a high probability of resulting in developmental delay. Marshall syndrome qualifies under the “established condition” category in most states, meaning the child is eligible based on the diagnosis alone — without needing to demonstrate a measurable delay.

The IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan)

The early intervention equivalent of an IEP is the IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan). Unlike school-based plans, the IFSP is family-centered, not child-centered — it addresses the needs of the entire family in supporting the child’s development.

ServiceWhy It Matters for Marshall Syndrome
Speech-language therapy Hearing loss, cleft palate, VPI, and feeding difficulties all affect speech and language development. Early speech therapy during the critical language acquisition window (birth–3 years) produces significantly better outcomes than delayed intervention
Physical therapy Hypotonia, joint hypermobility, and delayed motor milestones benefit from early motor interventions. PT focused on strengthening, balance, and safe movement patterns establishes protective habits before the child is old enough to develop compensatory patterns
Occupational therapy Fine motor skill development, feeding therapy (particularly for Pierre Robin-related feeding difficulties), and sensory integration. OT can also address early self-care skills adapted for the child’s physical capabilities
Vision services For children with significant myopia or cataracts, a teacher of the visually impaired (TVI) can work with the family on visual stimulation, environmental modifications, and pre-literacy skills adapted for low vision
Audiology and hearing services Early hearing aid fitting and auditory-verbal therapy during the critical period for auditory brain development. Children fitted with hearing aids before 6 months of age have significantly better language outcomes than those fitted later
Family training and counseling Parent education on the condition, connection to family support networks, guidance on navigating the medical system, and psychological support for the adjustment period after diagnosis
How to access IDEA Part C services:
  • Referral: Any parent, physician, or caregiver can refer a child for evaluation. Contact your state’s early intervention program (search “[state name] early intervention program” or call the CDC’s “Learn the Signs. Act Early” helpline). Hospital NICU social workers often initiate referrals for infants diagnosed in the neonatal period
  • Evaluation is free: The initial evaluation to determine eligibility is provided at no cost to the family, regardless of insurance status
  • Services are free or low-cost: States may not charge for some services; others use a sliding fee scale. Most services are covered by Medicaid and private insurance. Families should never be denied services due to inability to pay
  • Services are delivered in “natural environments”: Typically the child’s home or childcare setting, not a clinic. This makes early intervention highly accessible and family-friendly
  • Transition at age 3: At age 3, the child transitions from Part C to Part B services (the school system). The IFSP team is required to develop a transition plan at least 90 days before the child’s third birthday, including referral for 504 Plan or IEP evaluation. Do not let this transition create a gap in services — request the transition planning meeting proactively

How to Request a 504 Plan

  1. Submit a written request to the school principal or 504 coordinator. Email creates a paper trail. State: “I am requesting an evaluation for a Section 504 Plan for [child’s name] due to Marshall syndrome (a genetic collagen disorder) that substantially limits major life activities.”
  2. Provide medical documentation — the letter from your medical team described above
  3. Attend the 504 meeting with your accommodation list prepared (this page). You know your child’s needs better than anyone in the room
  4. Review and sign the plan — ensure every accommodation is written specifically, not vaguely. “Movement breaks every 30 minutes” is enforceable; “movement breaks as needed” is not
  5. Request annual review — accommodations should be updated yearly as the child grows and needs change. Hearing loss may progress. Vision may change. Pain patterns shift
  6. Know your rights: If the school denies the request, you can appeal to the district 504 coordinator, file an OCR complaint, or consult a special education advocate. Rare disease does not mean ineligible — the law covers any disability that substantially limits a major life activity
