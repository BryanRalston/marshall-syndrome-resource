Easy Bruising: Understanding the Cause & Protecting Your Family

This Page Addresses a Sensitive Topic
Children with Marshall syndrome bruise easily due to their underlying collagen disorder. This bruising can be mistaken for signs of child abuse — a situation that is deeply distressing for families. This page exists to help families understand why the bruising occurs, how it differs from inflicted injury, and what proactive steps can prevent misunderstandings. Healthcare providers who suspect abuse are acting on appropriate professional obligation. The goal is not to undermine that vigilance but to ensure that children with known connective tissue disorders are not subjected to unnecessary investigations, and that families are prepared.

Easy bruising is one of the most visible and socially complicated features of Marshall syndrome. While the medical explanation is straightforward — defective type XI collagen weakens blood vessel walls and the surrounding connective tissue — the practical consequences extend far beyond the skin. A child who comes to school with unexplained bruises in various stages of healing will, appropriately, attract concern. Families need both the medical understanding and the practical tools to navigate this reality.

Why Easy Bruising Occurs

The COL11A1 gene encodes the alpha-1 chain of type XI collagen, a fibril-forming collagen whose primary known role is in cartilage. However, collagen biology is not compartmentalized so neatly. Type XI collagen participates in organizing and regulating the diameter of collagen fibrils in multiple tissue types, and its absence or dysfunction has effects beyond joints and eyes.

Vascular Collagen and Blood Vessel Integrity

Blood vessel walls — particularly the smaller vessels (arterioles, venules, and capillaries) — depend on a collagen-rich extracellular matrix for structural integrity. While type I and type III collagen are the dominant structural proteins in vessel walls, the collagen fibril network functions as an interconnected system. Type XI collagen helps regulate fibril diameter and spacing; when it is abnormal, the overall fibrillar architecture is disrupted. This means:

  • Reduced vessel wall tensile strength: Vessels rupture more easily under mechanical stress that would not damage normal vasculature
  • Impaired perivascular support: The connective tissue surrounding small vessels (the adventitia and perivascular matrix) provides external support. When this tissue is lax or structurally weak, vessels are more vulnerable to shearing forces
  • Capillary fragility: The smallest vessels have the least structural support and are most dependent on the surrounding connective tissue matrix. Even minor bumps or pressure can cause capillary rupture

Dermal Collagen Thinning

The dermis (the structural layer of skin) is predominantly type I and type III collagen, but the organizational network that determines fibril size, packing, and cross-linking involves multiple collagen types. In connective tissue disorders affecting fibrillar collagen, the dermis is thinner and less resistant to deformation. This means:

  • Less tissue between the skin surface and underlying blood vessels
  • Less cushioning to absorb impact forces before they reach the vasculature
  • Bruises may appear more vivid and spread more widely because there is less tissue to contain the extravasated blood

Overlap with Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome

The bruising phenotype in Marshall syndrome shares significant overlap with that seen in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS), particularly the classical and hypermobile types. This is not coincidental — both are disorders of fibrillar collagen that affect the structural integrity of skin and blood vessels. In EDS, easy bruising is a recognized major diagnostic criterion. In Stickler and Marshall syndromes, bruising has been reported but receives less clinical attention because the focus tends to be on eyes, hearing, and joints. Families should be aware that the bruising is a real, expected feature of the collagen disorder — not a coincidence, and not something to dismiss or minimize.

It is not “just clumsy.” Children with Marshall syndrome may also bruise more because joint hypermobility makes them less coordinated and more prone to falls and bumps. But the bruising itself is disproportionate to the trauma — a minor bump that would leave no mark on a typical child produces a large, dramatic bruise. This disproportionality is the hallmark of a connective tissue contribution, not simply increased injury frequency.

The Child Abuse Concern

This Section Exists to Protect Children and Families
Mandatory reporting of suspected child abuse is a legal obligation for healthcare providers, teachers, school nurses, and many other professionals. This obligation exists to protect children, and it saves lives. Nothing on this page should be interpreted as discouraging appropriate reporting. The purpose is to help families with a confirmed genetic diagnosis prepare for and navigate situations where their child’s medical condition may be misinterpreted — and to ensure that professionals evaluating bruising have the information they need to make accurate assessments.

The reality that families with connective tissue disorders face is this: a child who frequently presents with multiple bruises in various stages of healing, often with no clear history of significant trauma, matches one of the classic patterns that triggers concern for non-accidental injury (NAI). This concern is appropriate and well-intentioned. Child abuse is tragically common, and bruising is its most visible sign. Professionals who raise concerns are doing their job.

However, for the family of a child with Marshall syndrome, an abuse investigation is devastating. It can involve:

  • Interviews of the child by social workers, sometimes at school without parental notification
  • Home visits and investigations by child protective services
  • Separation of the child from the family during investigation
  • Skeletal surveys and additional medical examinations
  • Emotional trauma to the child and family, even when the investigation concludes with no findings
  • Damage to the family’s relationship with the school or healthcare provider who made the report

The medical literature documents cases of families with Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, osteogenesis imperfecta, and other connective tissue disorders being investigated for child abuse before the underlying diagnosis was recognized. In some cases, children were temporarily removed from their homes. For families who already have a confirmed COL11A1 diagnosis, these situations are preventable with proactive documentation.

If you are currently facing an investigation: Stay calm. Cooperate fully. Provide your child’s genetic test results and medical records documenting the Marshall syndrome diagnosis. Request that the evaluating physician consult with a geneticist familiar with connective tissue disorders. Contact your child’s geneticist and ask them to provide a letter or speak directly with the investigating team. You have done nothing wrong, and the system is working as designed — it simply needs the correct medical context.

Distinguishing Connective Tissue Bruising from Inflicted Bruising

While no single feature definitively distinguishes connective tissue bruising from abuse, the overall pattern is different. Understanding these differences helps families explain their child’s bruising and helps evaluating professionals make accurate assessments.

Feature Connective Tissue Disorder Bruising Inflicted (Abuse) Bruising
Location Predominantly over bony prominences — shins, knees, forearms, forehead, elbows. These are areas that experience normal childhood impact during play and daily activities. Protected or unusual areas — torso, abdomen, back, ears, neck, buttocks, upper arms, inner thighs. These areas are rarely injured in normal play.
Pattern Random distribution; no recognizable shape; multiple bruises in various stages of healing (different colors) reflecting ongoing minor trauma over time. May show recognizable patterns — hand shape, finger marks, linear marks from a belt or cord, loop marks, bite marks. May cluster in specific regions.
History Bruising is disproportionate to the reported trauma. Parent says “she bumped the table” and the resulting bruise is large and dramatic. History is consistent and proportional to mechanism, just exaggerated in severity. History is inconsistent, changes between retellings, or is incompatible with the injury. The explanation does not match the injury type, location, or the child’s developmental ability.
Associated findings Joint hypermobility, skin hyperextensibility or fragility, family history of the same features, confirmed genetic diagnosis, normal development and affect. May be accompanied by other injuries: fractures (especially in non-ambulatory children), burns, oral injuries, retinal hemorrhages, subdural hematomas.
Healing May heal more slowly than expected due to the underlying collagen disorder. Bruises may persist longer and spread more widely. Some individuals notice bruises that appear without any recalled trauma at all. Healing follows a normal timeline unless there is re-injury to the same area.
Child’s behavior Child is typically comfortable discussing bruises, may point them out casually, and is developmentally and emotionally appropriate. Child may be withdrawn, fearful, have difficulty explaining injuries, or show signs of emotional distress around caregivers.
No table replaces clinical judgment. The features above are generalizations. A child with a connective tissue disorder can also be a victim of abuse — one does not exclude the other. These distinguishing features are meant to help evaluating professionals consider the full clinical picture, not to dismiss bruising concerns automatically. The key question is whether the pattern and distribution of bruising are consistent with the known connective tissue diagnosis.

What Families Should Do: Proactive Documentation

The single most effective way to prevent misunderstandings is to document your child’s diagnosis and expected bruising tendency before a concern arises. Reactive explanations — provided only after a report has been made — are inherently less credible than proactive documentation that was already on file.

1. Obtain a Geneticist Letter

Ask your child’s geneticist to write a letter on institutional letterhead that includes:

  • The child’s confirmed diagnosis of Marshall syndrome with the specific COL11A1 mutation identified
  • A brief explanation that Marshall syndrome is a type XI collagen disorder affecting connective tissues including skin and blood vessels
  • An explicit statement that easy and disproportionate bruising is an expected feature of this collagen disorder
  • A note that the bruising pattern — multiple bruises in various stages of healing, primarily over bony prominences, often disproportionate to minor trauma — is consistent with the underlying diagnosis
  • The geneticist’s contact information for follow-up questions

2. Distribute Copies Strategically

  • School file: Provide a copy to the school nurse, principal, and your child’s teacher at the start of each school year. Request that it be placed in the student’s health record.
  • Pediatrician’s chart: Ensure the letter is in the medical record, prominently noted
  • Daycare or childcare providers: Give a copy to anyone who regularly cares for your child
  • After-school programs and sports coaches: Particularly relevant for PE teachers and coaches who may see bruises during physical activity
  • Home copy: Keep the original letter and copies at home where you can produce them quickly if needed

3. Create an Ongoing Bruise Log

When your child gets a bruise from a known minor incident, photograph it with a timestamp:

  • Date, time, and brief description of the incident (e.g., “bumped coffee table while walking past”)
  • Photo showing the bruise size relative to the incident severity
  • Follow-up photos showing how the bruise progresses and resolves
  • This log demonstrates the pattern over time: minor trauma, disproportionate bruising, predictable locations
The phone timestamp is your friend. Modern smartphone cameras embed date and time metadata in every photo. Simply photographing bruises as they appear from known incidents creates a time-stamped record that demonstrates the pattern. You do not need to photograph every bruise — a representative set over several months is sufficient to establish the pattern.

4. Inform Caregivers Proactively

  • At the start of each school year, have a brief conversation with the school nurse and teacher: “Our child has a genetic connective tissue disorder that causes easy bruising. Here is the documentation from our geneticist. You may see bruises that look concerning — they are from normal childhood activity.”
  • Do not wait until a concern is raised. Proactive disclosure reframes the conversation entirely.
  • If your child participates in sports, inform the coach and provide documentation

5. Consider a Medical ID

A medical identification bracelet or necklace that mentions “connective tissue disorder” serves multiple purposes:

  • Alerts emergency medical personnel to potential cervical spine and anesthesia considerations
  • Provides immediate context for bruising in emergency department settings
  • Available from organizations like MedicAlert, with a file number that links to complete medical information

Medical Workup for Bruising

Even with a confirmed COL11A1 diagnosis, it is important to rule out concurrent bleeding disorders. A connective tissue disorder does not make a child immune to also having a coagulation abnormality — and some bleeding disorders are common enough that co-occurrence is plausible.

Recommended Baseline Studies

TestWhat It EvaluatesWhy It Matters
CBC with differential Platelet count, white blood cell count, hemoglobin Rules out thrombocytopenia (low platelets) as a contributing cause of bruising. Also screens for leukemia, which can present with bruising and petechiae.
PT (prothrombin time) Extrinsic coagulation pathway Detects deficiencies in factors VII, X, V, II, and fibrinogen. Prolonged PT with normal PTT may indicate factor VII deficiency.
PTT (partial thromboplastin time) Intrinsic coagulation pathway Detects deficiencies in factors VIII, IX, XI, XII and von Willebrand factor. Prolonged PTT is the most common abnormality in von Willebrand disease.
INR Standardized PT ratio Provides a standardized measure of coagulation function across different laboratories
Von Willebrand panel VWF antigen, VWF activity (ristocetin cofactor), factor VIII level Von Willebrand disease is the most common inherited bleeding disorder (affects ~1% of the population). It causes easy bruising, prolonged bleeding from cuts, heavy menstrual periods, and mucosal bleeding — symptoms that can overlap with or be masked by the connective tissue bruising.
Platelet function assay (PFA-100) Platelet adhesion and aggregation Screens for platelet function defects that would not be detected by platelet count alone. Normal platelet count with abnormal function can cause bruising.
Timing matters for von Willebrand testing. Von Willebrand factor levels fluctuate and can be falsely elevated by stress, exercise, and inflammation. Blood should be drawn in a calm, rested state. In children, this can be challenging — discuss timing with your hematologist. Additionally, VWF levels are lower in individuals with blood type O, which can affect interpretation.

If baseline studies are normal, the bruising can be attributed to the connective tissue disorder with reasonable confidence. If any abnormality is found, referral to a pediatric hematologist is warranted for further evaluation and management. Having a connective tissue disorder and a bleeding disorder simultaneously would produce more severe bruising than either condition alone.

When Bruising Needs Medical Attention

Most bruising in Marshall syndrome, while dramatic in appearance, is medically benign. However, certain bruising patterns or associated symptoms warrant prompt medical evaluation:

  • Large hematomas: A hematoma is a collection of blood that forms a firm, raised swelling rather than a flat bruise. Large hematomas can compress underlying structures, cause significant pain, and occasionally require drainage. If a hematoma is growing, tense, or extremely painful, seek medical evaluation.
  • Bruising after dental procedures: Excessive bleeding or bruising in the mouth after dental work (mucosal bleeding) may indicate a concurrent bleeding disorder. Mucosal bleeding is more characteristic of platelet or von Willebrand disorders than of connective tissue bruising alone. Report this to both your dentist and hematologist.
  • Bruising with petechiae: Petechiae are pinpoint red or purple dots (1–2mm) that do not blanch when pressed. Unlike bruising, which results from vessel rupture under mechanical stress, petechiae typically indicate a platelet problem (low count or poor function) or vasculitis. If your child develops petechiae, this is a different mechanism from connective tissue bruising and needs urgent evaluation.
  • Bruising that does not resolve: Normal bruises progress through a color sequence (red/purple → blue/black → green → yellow/brown) over approximately 2–3 weeks. A bruise that remains unchanged for more than 3–4 weeks, or that appears to worsen rather than improve, should be evaluated.
  • Bruising with swelling in a joint: A hemarthrosis (bleeding into a joint) causes joint swelling, warmth, pain, and reduced range of motion. While this is more commonly associated with hemophilia, it can occur in connective tissue disorders. A swollen, painful joint after minor trauma needs evaluation to distinguish hemarthrosis from joint sprain or dislocation (both of which are also more common in hypermobile joints).
  • Bruising around the eyes without head trauma: Bilateral periorbital bruising (raccoon eyes) without a history of facial injury can be a sign of a basilar skull fracture or, rarely, neuroblastoma. This always warrants urgent evaluation.
Trust your instincts. If the bruising pattern changes — suddenly more frequent, more severe, in new locations, or accompanied by new symptoms — do not assume it is “just the collagen disorder.” New-onset changes deserve fresh evaluation. The connective tissue disorder is a known baseline; deviations from that baseline need attention.

Practical Management

While the underlying collagen defect cannot be corrected, several practical measures can reduce bruising frequency and severity:

Protective Clothing and Equipment

  • Shin guards: Lightweight soccer-style shin guards worn under long pants during active play can significantly reduce lower leg bruising — the most common site for childhood bruises
  • Padded clothing: Long sleeves and pants provide a basic layer of protection during play. Some families find that lightly padded compression garments reduce bruising on the arms and legs
  • Knee and elbow pads: For younger children or during activities with a higher fall risk (cycling, skating, playground equipment)
  • Helmet use: Standard recommendation for cycling and skating, but additionally important because facial and forehead bruising is particularly alarming to observers

Compression Garments

Light compression garments (similar to athletic compression sleeves) provide gentle external support to blood vessels and may reduce bruising by:

  • Supporting the perivascular connective tissue against mechanical stress
  • Reducing the spread of extravasated blood after a vessel rupture
  • Providing a small amount of padding and friction reduction

The evidence for compression garments specifically reducing bruising in connective tissue disorders is limited, but the mechanism is plausible and the intervention is low-risk. Some families report noticeable improvement, particularly on the forearms and lower legs.

Nutritional Support

  • Vitamin C: Vitamin C is essential for collagen synthesis and cross-linking. While supplementation will not correct a genetic collagen defect, ensuring adequate vitamin C intake supports whatever normal collagen production is occurring. Severe vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) causes bruising and capillary fragility through impaired collagen synthesis. The recommended daily intake for children is 15–45mg depending on age; some clinicians recommend modest supplementation (100–250mg daily) for children with connective tissue disorders, though evidence for benefit is limited.
  • Vitamin K: Ensure adequate dietary intake (green leafy vegetables). Vitamin K is essential for coagulation factor production. Deficiency causes bruising and bleeding through a different mechanism than the collagen disorder, but ensuring adequacy rules out one more contributing factor.
  • Iron: Children who bruise frequently and have heavy bruising may develop mild iron deficiency from chronic blood loss into tissues. If your child seems fatigued or pale, checking a ferritin level is reasonable.

Topical Treatments

  • Arnica: Arnica (Arnica montana) is a traditional remedy for bruising, available as creams and gels. The scientific evidence for its effectiveness is mixed — some small studies show modest acceleration of bruise resolution, while others show no significant benefit. It is generally safe for topical use (do not apply to broken skin) and many families find it helpful, even if the evidence is not definitive.
  • Cold application: Applying ice or a cold pack immediately after a bump (within the first 10–15 minutes) constricts blood vessels and can reduce the extent of bruising. Wrap the cold pack in cloth to protect the skin.
  • Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs when possible: Ibuprofen and aspirin inhibit platelet function and can worsen bruising. When pain relief is needed, acetaminophen (paracetamol) does not affect platelets. If NSAIDs are medically necessary (e.g., for joint pain management), discuss the bruising trade-off with the prescribing physician.

Activity Modification

The goal is not to restrict activity — physical activity is important for joint stability, cardiovascular health, and emotional well-being. The goal is to make activity safer:

  • Favor activities with lower collision risk: swimming, cycling (with helmet and pads), hiking, yoga
  • If the child participates in contact sports, ensure appropriate protective equipment and inform coaches about the bruising tendency
  • Environmental modifications: pad sharp furniture corners in the home, ensure good lighting to reduce bumping into objects
  • Let the child participate fully in age-appropriate activities — overprotection has its own costs in social development and self-esteem
Talk to your child about their bruises. As children grow older, they become aware that they bruise more than their peers. Give them age-appropriate language to explain it: “I have a condition that makes my skin bruise easily — it looks worse than it is.” Helping them own the explanation reduces anxiety and prevents them from feeling like something is wrong with them. For older children and teens, understanding the genetics (“the collagen in my blood vessels is a bit different”) can be empowering.
