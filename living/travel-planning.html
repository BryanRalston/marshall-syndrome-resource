Travel & Vacation Planning

Travel is entirely possible with Marshall syndrome, but it rewards planning. The same features that require daily management at home — fragile vitreous humor, sensorineural hearing loss, joint hypermobility, spinal and cervical vulnerability, and cardiac considerations — do not disappear when you leave home. They interact with new environments in predictable ways: cabin pressure changes, altitude, unfamiliar physical demands, disrupted medication schedules, and distance from established specialists. This page addresses each of those intersections so that families can travel confidently, with contingencies in place rather than discovered in the moment.

Planning is not restriction. The goal of this page is not to build a list of things people with Marshall syndrome cannot do. It is to give families the information needed to make informed decisions, adapt itineraries intelligently, and handle problems quickly when they arise.

Flying with Retinal Detachment Risk

The vitreous humor of the eye is structurally abnormal in Marshall syndrome due to the COL11A1 mutation. The vitreous is more liquid, less gel-like, and more prone to tractional forces on the retina. This makes retinal detachment a lifetime risk, not just a pediatric one. Commercial air travel adds variables that interact directly with this risk.

Cabin Pressure Effects on the Vitreous

Commercial aircraft cabins are pressurized to an equivalent altitude of approximately 6,000–8,000 feet (1,800–2,400 m) above sea level, even when the plane is cruising at 35,000 feet. This is lower than ambient pressure at sea level. The pressure change during ascent and descent is gradual and modest compared to the effects of altitude itself, but there are two relevant considerations:

  • Intraocular pressure (IOP): Some studies have found transient IOP fluctuations during flight, though the clinical significance in otherwise healthy eyes is modest. In eyes with pre-existing vitreoretinal pathology, any IOP fluctuation that increases traction on a vulnerable area of retina deserves consideration.
  • Gas expansion: Any gas bubble inside the eye — whether from intravitreal injection or retinal repair surgery — will expand as ambient pressure decreases during ascent. This is not a minor effect; gas can expand significantly and cause acute, vision-threatening pressure spikes.
Flying with an intraocular gas bubble is a medical contraindication. After retinal detachment repair using pneumatic retinopexy, vitrectomy with gas tamponade (SF6, C3F8, or similar), or any procedure that places gas inside the eye, flying is absolutely contraindicated until the bubble has completely resorbed. Your retinal surgeon will specify when flying is safe again — this can range from two weeks to three months depending on the gas type and bubble volume. Do not fly until cleared explicitly by the surgeon. This applies to all aircraft including small private planes, which may fly at altitudes with even less pressurization.

Timing After Retinal Procedures

The contraindication period for flying after gas bubble procedures depends on the specific gas used:

  • Air (100%): Resorbs in approximately 5–7 days. Flying may be possible after confirmed resorption by ophthalmoscopy.
  • SF6 (sulfur hexafluoride): Commonly used; a 20% fill resorbs in approximately 10–14 days, but larger fills take longer. Surgeon confirmation is required.
  • C3F8 (perfluoropropane): Long-acting gas; a 12–14% fill can last 6–8 weeks. A full fill may last 2–3 months. This gas type has the longest no-fly restriction.
  • Silicone oil: Unlike gas, silicone oil does not expand with pressure changes. There is no specific no-fly restriction for silicone oil tamponade, though travel plans should still be reviewed with the retinal surgeon.
Carry an ophthalmic alert card. If a gas bubble is present, carry a card in your wallet that reads "Do not administer nitrous oxide — intraocular gas bubble present." Nitrous oxide diffuses rapidly into gas-filled cavities and can cause catastrophic IOP elevation during anesthesia. Any anesthesiologist you encounter should see this card before any procedure.

Routine Flying Without Active Eye Problems

For individuals with Marshall syndrome who have no recent retinal procedure and no known active vitreoretinal pathology, commercial air travel is generally safe. Sensible precautions include:

  • Know the symptoms of retinal detachment (new floaters, flashes of light, a shadow or curtain across vision) and have a plan for seeking emergency ophthalmology wherever you are traveling.
  • Avoid prolonged head-down positions during long flights. Vitreous movement in an already abnormal vitreous cavity is more tractional in head-down posture.
  • Stay well hydrated; dehydration affects vitreous gel stability.
  • Consider an ophthalmology check-up before travel if it has been more than six months since the last dilated fundus exam.

Altitude Considerations

Mountain destinations — ski resorts, hiking trips, high-altitude national parks — present a distinct set of considerations from flying. Unlike the pressurized cabin of an aircraft, high-altitude environments are genuinely at reduced barometric pressure, and the effects are sustained rather than transient.

Intraocular Pressure at Altitude

IOP is maintained by the balance of aqueous humor production and outflow. At altitude, barometric pressure decreases, and the relative pressure differential across ocular tissues changes. Research in glaucoma populations has documented IOP increases at altitude, particularly above 10,000 feet (3,000 m). For individuals with Marshall syndrome:

  • Eyes that have undergone prior surgical procedures (trabeculectomy, tube shunts, lens implants, vitrectomy) may respond differently to IOP fluctuations at altitude than eyes with unaltered anatomy.
  • The risk is not zero, but it is generally manageable with monitoring. If traveling to elevations above 8,000 feet, carry glaucoma drops if prescribed and have a plan for obtaining emergency ophthalmology in the destination region.
  • Symptoms of acute IOP elevation include eye pain, headache localized to the eye or brow, blurred vision, and halos around lights. These warrant same-day evaluation.

Ski Resorts: Retinal and Spinal Considerations

Skiing and snowboarding carry specific risks that intersect directly with Marshall syndrome features:

  • Retinal risk from falls: High-speed falls involve sudden deceleration forces. In an eye with vitreoretinal traction, a fall impact can precipitate retinal tear or detachment. This does not mean skiing is prohibited, but protective goggles, conservative speed choices, and lesson-based skill progression are important risk reducers.
  • Cervical spine risk: Marshall syndrome is associated with atlantoaxial instability and cervical vertebral anomalies in some individuals. A high-speed fall or collision that causes hyperflexion or hyperextension of the cervical spine can be dangerous. Before skiing, confirm that cervical spine status has been assessed — ideally with flexion-extension cervical radiographs within the past two years. If cervical instability is present, contact sports and skiing at high speed are contraindicated.
  • Joint hypermobility: Ski binding release settings should be calibrated conservatively for individuals with hypermobile joints. The standard release threshold assumes normal ligamentous stability; a lower setting reduces the risk of ligamentous injury when the binding releases.

Spinal Pressure at Altitude

Individuals who have had spinal surgery, atlantoaxial arthrodesis, or who have known cervical or lumbar anomalies should be aware that altitude-related dehydration and physical exertion can worsen spinal symptoms. Adequate hydration, rest breaks, and avoiding activities that involve sudden axial loading (jumping, mogul skiing) are reasonable precautions.

Altitude acclimatization. At elevations above 8,000 feet, plan a one-day acclimatization period before engaging in strenuous physical activity. Acute mountain sickness (headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness) can mimic neurological symptoms, complicating the assessment of headaches or vision changes that in another context would warrant urgent evaluation. Acclimatization makes it easier to recognize genuine symptoms.

Packing a Medical Travel Kit

The single most important travel preparation for anyone with Marshall syndrome is assembling a medical travel kit before every trip. This kit should be carried in a carry-on bag, not checked luggage, and should be reviewed and updated before each travel episode.

Medications

  • All current medications in original labeled pharmacy bottles (important for customs and TSA verification)
  • At least a two-week surplus beyond the travel duration — delays happen
  • Ophthalmic drops: glaucoma medications, lubricating drops (dry eyes are common in Marshall syndrome), anti-inflammatory drops if prescribed
  • Pain management medications as appropriate for joint and spinal symptoms
  • If traveling internationally, research the legal status of any controlled substances in the destination country; some common medications are restricted abroad

Emergency Information Card

Prepare a laminated or printed card (wallet-sized) that includes:

  • Diagnosis: Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutation)
  • Key medical history: retinal detachment history, prior eye surgeries, hearing device use, cervical spine status, cardiac findings, prior anesthesia complications
  • Current medications with doses
  • Known anesthesia considerations (malignant hyperthermia risk if applicable, airway management considerations due to midface anatomy)
  • Primary care physician and specialist contact information
  • Emergency contact
Anesthesia alert for Marshall syndrome. The midface anatomy in Marshall syndrome (midface hypoplasia, potential submucous cleft) can complicate airway management during anesthesia. Document any prior difficult intubation experiences. Carry a card noting "Marshall syndrome — possible difficult airway, midface hypoplasia, please review with anesthesiologist prior to any procedure."

Specialist Contact Information

  • Retinal specialist: direct office number, after-hours emergency line
  • Audiologist and otolaryngologist: for hearing device troubleshooting or acute ear issues
  • Genetics team or primary care physician who coordinates overall care
  • The name, dose, and prescribing physician for every current medication (enables a local physician to verify and refill if needed)

Hearing Aid Batteries and Charging Equipment

  • Pack at minimum twice the estimated battery consumption for the trip duration. Batteries drain faster in cold weather and during heavy device use in noisy environments.
  • For rechargeable devices: carry the charger and a backup charging cable. Research the power outlet standards at the destination and bring appropriate adapters.
  • For individuals with cochlear implants: carry the processor charging equipment, backup processor cable if applicable, and the device card (a wallet card identifying the implant, for airport security).
  • Store batteries at room temperature. Extreme heat (car dashboards, beach bags in direct sun) degrades battery life significantly.
  • Pack hearing aids in a hard case, not a soft pouch, during transport.

Spare Glasses

  • Anyone who requires optical correction — which includes nearly everyone with Marshall syndrome who has had lens surgery — should carry a spare pair of glasses or contact lenses on every trip.
  • The spare prescription should be reasonably current. A two-year-old prescription is better than nothing but may not fully correct vision after recent surgical changes.
  • Carry the optical prescription in writing in case glasses are lost or broken and need to be replaced at the destination.

Airport Security with Medical Devices

Medical devices used by individuals with Marshall syndrome interact with airport security systems in specific ways. Being prepared prevents delays and reduces stress.

Hearing Aids

  • Standard behind-the-ear and in-the-canal hearing aids do not need to be removed for standard metal detector screening. They contain metal components but are generally well below the threshold that triggers an alarm.
  • Millimeter wave AIT (Advanced Imaging Technology) scanners — the booths where you raise your arms — are safe for hearing aids and do not require removal.
  • If asked to pass through or be scanned, tell the agent you wear hearing aids. Some individuals prefer to opt out of advanced imaging if they have concerns; you have the right to request a pat-down instead.
  • In high-noise airport security environments, hearing aid users may have difficulty understanding agent instructions. Inform the agent at the outset that you wear hearing aids and may need instructions repeated or given face-to-face.

Cochlear Implants

  • Cochlear implant processors (the external components) should generally be handled like hearing aids through security.
  • The internal implant is a surgically implanted metal device. Carry the implant identification card (issued at implantation) at all times. The card documents the implant make and model, which is relevant for MRI restrictions and security screening.
  • Metal detectors will alarm in response to the internal implant. Inform security staff that you have a surgically implanted cochlear device; they will conduct alternative screening (handheld wand or pat-down).
  • Current cochlear implants from major manufacturers are generally safe in MRI environments at 1.5T (check specific device), but you must inform any radiology team before any MRI scan.

CPAP and BiPAP Devices

Obstructive sleep apnea occurs at elevated rates in individuals with Marshall syndrome due to midface hypoplasia and adenotonsillar hypertrophy. CPAP or BiPAP users should be aware of the following:

  • CPAP machines are exempt from the TSA carry-on size restriction and do not count toward your personal item or carry-on bag allowance (TSA policy; confirm with your specific airline).
  • CPAP machines go through the X-ray scanner but do not need to be set up or run through security. You may be asked to open the bag for inspection.
  • Distilled water for the humidifier reservoir is not always available at destinations. Consider traveling without distilled water and purchasing at the destination, or using the CPAP without humidification for short trips.
  • For international travel: CPAP machines are generally dual-voltage (100–240V), but verify for your specific device. The inlet port may require an outlet adapter.

Medications Through Security

  • Liquid medications in quantities greater than 3.4 oz (100 ml) are permitted through TSA security when medically necessary. Declare them separately at the checkpoint.
  • Eye drops (including glaucoma medications) are subject to the same rules as other liquids. A letter from the prescribing physician documenting medical necessity is helpful but not always required.
  • Syringes and needles are permitted when accompanied by labeled medication.
TSA Notification Card. The TSA offers a free voluntary notification card that discloses medical conditions or devices to security officers discreetly. The card does not guarantee expedited screening but allows communication without announcing your condition aloud in a busy checkpoint. Download and print from the TSA website, or write your own equivalent.

Managing Medications Across Time Zones

Time zone changes disrupt medication schedules in ways that matter more for some drugs than others. Most medications taken once or twice daily have enough pharmacokinetic flexibility that a one-to-two-hour schedule shift on travel days is inconsequential. A few categories require more thought.

Glaucoma Drops

Glaucoma medications (prostaglandin analogs, beta-blockers, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors) are typically dosed on a fixed schedule tied to IOP rhythms and the body clock. When crossing more than two time zones:

  • Contact the prescribing ophthalmologist before travel to ask for explicit guidance on transitioning the schedule at the destination.
  • A general principle: shift the dosing time gradually by 1–2 hours per day toward the destination time zone, starting two days before departure if possible.
  • Never simply skip a dose to "resynchronize." Missed glaucoma doses can cause IOP spikes.

Melatonin and Timed Supplements

Melatonin is used both for sleep regulation and sometimes as part of antioxidant protocols in connective tissue conditions. Its timing is circadian-dependent — it is only effective taken at the right phase of the body clock:

  • For eastward travel (advancing the clock): take a low dose (0.5–1 mg) at the target bedtime in the new time zone, starting on arrival day.
  • For westward travel (delaying the clock): take melatonin at a later hour aligned with the new time zone's bedtime, starting after arrival.
  • High doses (5–10 mg) are not more effective for jet lag than low doses and produce a longer duration of sedative effect that can work against acclimatization.

General Principles for All Scheduled Medications

  • Set phone alarms using the local time at the destination (not home time) from the first morning of arrival. Relying on a body clock that is still synchronized to the departure city leads to missed or doubled doses.
  • For medications taken with food, time zone adjustment may naturally sync with meal schedules if you adapt to local mealtimes promptly on arrival.
  • For any medication with a narrow therapeutic window, consult the prescribing physician before significant time zone changes (>4 hours).
Carry-on rule for medications. Never check medications in luggage. Checked bags are delayed, lost, or stolen far more often than carry-on items. All medications go in the carry-on bag, every time, without exception.

Finding Specialists at Travel Destinations

Marshall syndrome is rare, and most ophthalmologists, audiologists, and otolaryngologists at travel destinations will have never managed it. This does not mean they cannot help in an emergency — it means the family needs to arrive prepared to be the expert on their own condition.

Preparing Before You Leave

  • Identify the nearest academic medical center or children's hospital at the destination. In an eye emergency, a retinal specialist is needed — not a general ophthalmologist, and certainly not urgent care. Academic centers are most likely to have retinal surgery capability on short notice.
  • Ask your home retinal specialist to write a brief summary letter covering: diagnosis, surgical history, current eye status, and guidance for a covering physician. This letter is invaluable at a destination emergency room.
  • Research the distance from your accommodation to the nearest hospital with ophthalmology services. Know the emergency phone number for the country you are visiting.

Carrying Medical Records

A portable medical record packet for travel should include:

  • Most recent dilated fundus exam report (retinal status, cup-to-disc ratio, any vitreoretinal findings)
  • Most recent audiogram
  • Operative reports from any eye surgeries, including lens implant specifications (IOL type and power)
  • Cochlear implant card (if applicable)
  • Most recent cardiology evaluation if any cardiac abnormalities are documented
  • Most recent cervical spine imaging report if cervical instability is a concern
  • Immunization records (relevant for international travel)
Digital backups. Store copies of all medical records in a cloud-accessible folder (password protected) that can be accessed from any device. Email PDF copies to yourself so they are searchable from your inbox even without internet access to a cloud service.

When You Need Care at the Destination

  • In an eye emergency, lead with the retinal detachment risk. Say clearly: "This patient has Marshall syndrome with known vitreoretinal pathology and elevated lifetime risk of retinal detachment. Please contact retinal surgery immediately."
  • For hearing device failure: contact the device manufacturer's international support line. Most major cochlear implant and hearing aid manufacturers have 24/7 support and can direct you to authorized service centers near any major city.
  • For joint or spinal emergencies: seek orthopedics or emergency medicine. Carry the cervical spine imaging report if cervical instability is documented — this affects how the spine is positioned during any procedure.

Beach and Pool Safety

Water environments are among the most common family vacation settings, and they present specific management challenges for hearing aids, optical correction, and retinal safety.

Hearing Aids at the Beach and Pool

  • Most standard hearing aids are water-resistant (IP54 or IP68 rating) but not waterproof. Swimming, snorkeling, and submersion will damage them. Remove hearing aids before entering the water.
  • Store hearing aids in a hard case away from direct sunlight and sand. Sand and heat are damaging to the device and battery contacts.
  • After any exposure to salt spray, wipe devices with a dry cloth and allow to air dry before returning to the case. Salt corrosion is a significant cause of hearing aid failure.
  • For extended beach or pool trips, consider a drying kit (electronic desiccant dryer) to remove moisture at the end of each day.
  • Cochlear implant processors are not waterproof in their standard configuration. Waterproof covers or aqua-cases are available from most manufacturers for water activities. Confirm the IP rating of the specific processor before submersion.

Myopia and Water Safety

The high myopia in Marshall syndrome means that without correction, functional vision at the pool or beach may be significantly impaired:

  • Individuals who have had cataract/lens surgery and are corrected with implanted lenses retain functional vision in water without glasses.
  • Those wearing glasses or soft contact lenses lose useful vision when removing correction for water activities. Establish clear protocols: a designated safe area, a buddy system, and clearly communicated limits on how far from the group a child should go without correction.
  • Prescription swim goggles are available in most common myopic ranges and are a practical solution for open-water swimming and beach activities.
  • Do not wear soft contact lenses while swimming. Water-borne microorganisms (particularly Acanthamoeba) can cause devastating corneal infections under contact lenses exposed to pool, lake, or ocean water.
Diving and retinal risk. Scuba diving involves significant pressure changes (increased pressure at depth, then decompression on ascent) that are relevant to retinal and vitreoretinal health. These pressure changes are distinct from flying and more intense. Individuals with Marshall syndrome, particularly those with prior retinal pathology, should consult their retinal specialist before any scuba diving. Snorkeling at the surface does not involve the same pressure dynamics and is generally safe.

International Travel

Medical Translation Cards

In countries where your native language is not spoken, communication of medical needs can fail at exactly the moment it matters most. Prepare translation cards before travel:

  • A card in the local language that explains the diagnosis, key risks (retinal detachment, airway management, hearing loss), and urgent action needed
  • Include the genetic terminology: "Marshall syndrome / COL11A1 mutation" — this is often recognizable to physicians even without translation, as it is a standardized genetic diagnosis
  • Professional translation services (not machine translation alone) should be used for medical cards. Errors in medical translation have clinical consequences.
  • Many rare disease organizations offer translation resources; the Marshall syndrome or COL11A1 patient community may have prepared materials.

Travel Insurance for Pre-Existing Conditions

Standard travel insurance policies typically exclude pre-existing conditions unless specifically covered by a rider or a specialized policy:

  • Disclose Marshall syndrome fully when purchasing travel insurance. Failure to disclose a pre-existing condition is grounds for claim denial.
  • Look specifically for policies that cover emergency medical evacuation. If a retinal detachment requires surgery at the destination and adequate facilities are not available locally, medical evacuation to a facility with retinal surgery capability can cost tens of thousands of dollars without coverage.
  • Confirm that the policy covers emergency ophthalmology, audiology device replacement, and any other category relevant to the specific individual's medical profile.
  • Annual multi-trip policies with pre-existing condition coverage exist and are more cost-effective for families who travel multiple times per year.
Medications abroad. Prescription medication names, formulations, and availability vary by country. Glaucoma drops available by one name in the United States may be sold under a different trade name in Europe or Asia. Keep the generic (INN) name of each medication documented so that a local pharmacist can identify an equivalent product. Controlled substances (including some pain medications) may be illegal to import in certain quantities or may be unavailable; plan accordingly.

Road Trips

Car Seat Positioning for Spinal and Joint Issues

Long car rides involve prolonged static posture, which is poorly tolerated by individuals with joint hypermobility, spinal anomalies, or cervical instability:

  • Seat position: Avoid reclining the car seat to a very low angle, which increases cervical flexion. A more upright position with the head supported by the headrest in the neutral position is preferable.
  • Lumbar support: A portable lumbar roll or travel cushion placed in the small of the back reduces the sustained lumbar flexion that contributes to back pain on long drives.
  • Headrest adjustment: The headrest should be positioned so that the center of the head is supported, not just the neck. This reduces whiplash risk if there is a rear impact and reduces neck fatigue during long drives.
  • Children in car seats: Standard car seat guidelines apply. For children with known cervical instability, discuss positioning with the orthopedic or neurosurgical team. Some children with atlantoaxial instability may benefit from a cervical collar during car travel following any significant crash, even if apparently uninjured.

Frequent Stops

  • Plan rest stops every 60–90 minutes. This is more frequent than the standard recommendation for typical travelers, but it reflects the reality of joint stiffness and spinal fatigue in connective tissue conditions.
  • Rest stops should involve genuine movement — walking around the vehicle, gentle stretching, not just a bathroom stop and return to the seat.
  • For individuals with hearing aids or cochlear implants, rest stops in quieter environments allow a break from the cognitive load of listening in the variable noise environment of a moving vehicle. This matters more than it may seem; listening fatigue is real and cumulative.
  • Hydration during road trips is often inadequate. Carry water accessible from the seat (not only in the trunk) and actively encourage drinking at each stop.
Cervical instability and emergency response. If a car accident occurs and a person with documented cervical instability complains of neck pain, tingling in the arms, or weakness, emergency responders should be informed of the cervical instability diagnosis before spinal precautions are managed. Standard cervical collar sizes may not fit individuals with the midface and neck proportions seen in Marshall syndrome. Request pediatric or small sizing as needed.

Amusement Parks and Attractions

Theme parks are a common vacation destination for families, and most children with Marshall syndrome can enjoy them with thoughtful ride selection. The relevant concerns are retinal safety, cervical stability, and cardiac status.

Ride Restrictions: Retinal Concerns

  • High-force rides (roller coasters with sharp drops, high-speed turns, or strong G-forces) generate forces that can stress vitreoretinal attachments. For individuals with a history of retinal detachment, prior retinal tears treated with laser, or known vitreoretinal pathology, these rides should be avoided.
  • Water rides with sudden drops or high-impact landings carry similar considerations. The impact at the end of a drop generates an abrupt deceleration force.
  • For individuals who have had retinal surgery within the past 12 months, consult the retinal surgeon before any amusement park visit. The healing vitreoretinal interface is more vulnerable than a stable, fully healed eye.

Ride Restrictions: Cervical and Spinal Concerns

  • Rides that produce strong neck snap movements (head-banging, rapid directional changes) are contraindicated in the presence of confirmed cervical instability. This includes many classic roller coaster experiences.
  • If cervical spine status has never been formally evaluated with flexion-extension radiographs, and the individual is about to participate in high-force rides, this evaluation should be obtained before the trip.
  • Carousel rides, gentle swing rides, flume boats, and dark rides generally do not produce cervical stress and are appropriate for most individuals.

Cardiac Considerations

  • Individuals with known mitral valve prolapse or other cardiac findings should be aware that rides producing extreme startle responses, sustained high G-forces, or significant physiological stress may not be appropriate. Discuss with the cardiologist before a park visit if there is any uncertainty.
  • High-altitude rides (tall drop towers, high-elevation gondolas at theme parks) do not typically involve medically significant altitude changes but can induce anxiety-driven physiological responses that may be worth discussing with cardiologists in individuals with documented arrhythmias.

Practical Tips for Park Visits

  • Hearing aids at noisy theme parks: the loud, echoing environments of most parks are challenging for hearing aid users. Use directional microphone settings if available. Brief the child on a lost-and-found meeting point in case of separation, using a visual cue (a distinctive landmark) rather than relying solely on calling out names in a crowd.
  • Sun protection: outdoor parks in summer involve prolonged sun exposure. UV damage to already vulnerable eyes (anterior segment complications, elevated IOP risk) makes sunglasses with UV protection important, not optional. Wide-brim hats provide additional periorbital protection.
  • Hydration and fatigue: children with Marshall syndrome often fatigue more quickly than peers due to the cumulative physical and cognitive demands of managing multiple sensory challenges simultaneously. Build rest periods into the park day deliberately; do not wait until a child is overtired to seek a break.

Camp and Overnight Programs

Overnight camps, school outdoor programs, and similar activities involve handing care responsibilities to adults who may have no familiarity with Marshall syndrome. Preparation is the difference between a successful experience and a medical incident that could have been prevented.

What to Tell Counselors and Program Staff

Prepare a one-page medical summary specifically designed for the camp or program context. This is different from the full medical record packet — it should be readable by a non-medical adult and focused on actionable guidance rather than clinical background. Include:

  • Diagnosis in plain language: "Marshall syndrome is a genetic condition affecting the eyes, ears, and joints. It requires specific precautions."
  • Eye precautions: "Do not allow participation in activities with a significant risk of blow to the head or eye (contact sports, dodgeball with hard balls, high-speed activities). If [child] reports sudden new floaters, flashes of light, or a shadow in their vision, this is a medical emergency. Take to the nearest emergency room immediately and call us."
  • Hearing: "Wears hearing aids / cochlear implant processor. Remove before swimming. Please repeat instructions face-to-face in noisy environments. If the device stops working, contact us — we will advise on troubleshooting."
  • Spine and activity restrictions: If cervical instability is documented, list specific restricted activities clearly. Do not rely on the counselor to extrapolate from a diagnosis.
  • Medications: List all medications, schedule, and administration instructions. Note any medications that require refrigeration.
  • Emergency contacts: Two contacts who can be reached at any time of day.

Preparing the Child

  • Age-appropriate self-advocacy is a goal, not just an expectation. A child who can explain their own hearing aid, describe what they see when they experience visual symptoms, and ask for what they need is better protected than one who relies entirely on adult mediation.
  • Practice the eye symptom script before camp: "If I ever see new floaters or flashes in my eye, or a shadow at the edge of my vision, I need to tell an adult immediately because it could mean something is wrong with my eye."
  • Ensure the child knows the name of the nurse or health staff person at the program, and that they have permission to go to that person at any time, including at night.
Overnight programs and retinal emergencies. A retinal detachment that begins overnight and is not recognized until the next morning may involve several more hours of detachment progression — and the macula may become involved. Instruct the child to wake a counselor if any visual symptoms occur at night. This should be communicated explicitly to the program director, not assumed.

Medications at Camp

  • Most overnight programs have a health center that stores and administers medications. Provide medications in original pharmacy bottles with clearly labeled instructions.
  • For ophthalmic drops that require refrigeration, confirm that the health center has appropriate refrigeration and is aware of the storage requirement.
  • For hearing aid batteries, provide a generous supply and instruct staff on the battery-change procedure for the specific device. A hearing aid with a dead battery is effectively no hearing aid at all.
Pre-camp medical consultation. Schedule a visit with the child's primary care physician approximately 4–6 weeks before an overnight program. Update immunizations, review activity restrictions, confirm that the medical summary is current, and ask the physician to sign the camp health form. Doing this early avoids last-minute scheduling pressure and allows time to address any concerns that arise from the review.
