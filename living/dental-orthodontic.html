Dental & Orthodontic Care: Teeth, Jaw & COL11A1

Coordination Is Everything
Dental and orthodontic management in Marshall syndrome requires coordination between the dentist, orthodontist, and craniofacial team. Micrognathia, cleft palate history, and defective collagen in the periodontal ligament all affect treatment decisions. Most dental professionals have never encountered Marshall syndrome — bringing the right information to appointments is as important as the treatment itself.

Children with Marshall syndrome face a convergence of dental challenges that no single specialist typically sees in combination: a small jaw from the Pierre Robin sequence, possible cleft palate repair affecting the upper arch, connective tissue abnormality in the periodontal ligament and TMJ, and potential for temporomandibular joint degeneration. Understanding how COL11A1 mutations affect every layer of dental anatomy — from dentin to the periodontal ligament to the TMJ disc — transforms dental care from reactive cavity-filling to proactive structural management.

COL11A1 and Dental Development

Type XI collagen, encoded by COL11A1, is a fibril-forming collagen found primarily in cartilage, but it also plays important roles in dental tissues. Its function is to regulate the diameter and spacing of type II collagen fibrils, acting as a structural organizer. In dental tissues, type XI collagen is present in:

  • Predentin and dentin: Type XI collagen is expressed during odontogenesis (tooth formation). It helps organize the collagen matrix that mineralizes into dentin. While type I collagen is the dominant structural protein in mature dentin, type XI collagen contributes to the organizational scaffold during development. Mutations may result in subtle dentin structural abnormalities, though clinically obvious dentin defects (like dentinogenesis imperfecta) have not been consistently reported in Stickler/Marshall syndrome.
  • Periodontal ligament (PDL): The PDL is a dense connective tissue that anchors each tooth to the alveolar bone. While type I collagen comprises roughly 70% of PDL collagen, type XI collagen is expressed at higher levels in PDL tissue than in dental pulp. COL11A1 mutations may alter the biomechanical properties of the PDL — the very tissue that transmits and absorbs orthodontic forces.
  • TMJ disc and condylar cartilage: The TMJ disc is fibrocartilage rich in collagen I, but the condylar cartilage contains type II collagen fibrils whose diameter and organization depend on type XI collagen. Mouse models with COL11A1 haploinsufficiency show accelerated condylar cartilage degeneration, directly relevant to the TMJ problems seen in Marshall syndrome.
  • Alveolar bone: Type XI collagen is present in the cartilage template during bone development. Altered collagen organization may contribute to the mild osteopenia sometimes observed in Stickler/Marshall syndrome, potentially affecting alveolar bone density.

What Does This Mean Clinically?

Published dental data specific to Marshall syndrome is sparse. Case reports in Stickler syndrome describe:

  • Normal enamel formation (enamel is not collagen-dependent — it is hydroxyapatite deposited by ameloblasts)
  • Possible subtle dentin abnormalities, though these are rarely clinically significant
  • Progressive periodontal changes that may be disproportionate to oral hygiene status
  • Root morphology that may show shorter or thinner roots on panoramic radiographs
  • TMJ disc and condylar changes visible on MRI, even in children
Baseline dental imaging: A panoramic radiograph (OPG) at age 6–7 establishes baseline root morphology, jaw growth, and tooth development. This becomes the reference for all future orthodontic planning and for monitoring root resorption during treatment.

Malocclusion and the Pierre Robin Connection

The majority of children with Marshall syndrome who had Pierre Robin sequence as neonates will develop some degree of malocclusion. The chain of causation is direct:

Studies of Pierre Robin sequence outcomes show that approximately 39% of conservatively managed patients ultimately require orthognathic surgery at skeletal maturity, with the vast majority needing mandibular advancement for Class II correction. Partial mandibular catch-up growth does occur — facial profile often normalizes by age 4–6 — but the degree of catch-up is variable and less predictable when the underlying cause is a collagen disorder rather than isolated Robin sequence.

The catch-up question: In isolated Pierre Robin sequence, many children experience significant mandibular catch-up growth. In Marshall syndrome, where COL11A1 affects the cartilage growth plate of the mandibular condyle itself, catch-up may be less complete. Serial cephalometric radiographs (lateral skull X-rays) at ages 6, 9, and 12 track the growth trajectory and inform whether surgical intervention will likely be needed.

Orthodontic Timing

Orthodontic treatment in Marshall syndrome is typically staged in coordination with the craniofacial team:

Phase I: Interceptive Treatment (Ages 7–10)

  • Maxillary expansion: Rapid palatal expander (RPE) to widen the narrow upper arch, especially after cleft repair. This is time-sensitive — the midpalatal suture becomes increasingly resistant to non-surgical expansion after about age 14.
  • Functional appliances: Twin Block, Herbst, or Bionator appliances to encourage mandibular forward growth. These work best during the pubertal growth spurt. In Marshall syndrome, the response may be less predictable due to abnormal condylar cartilage.
  • Space management: Maintaining space for unerupted permanent teeth, especially if the upper arch is crowded from cleft repair scarring.
  • Crossbite correction: Early correction prevents asymmetric mandibular growth.

Phase II: Comprehensive Treatment (Ages 11–14)

  • Fixed appliances (braces): Full alignment and occlusal correction once most permanent teeth have erupted.
  • Coordination with growth: Timing around the pubertal growth spurt is critical. For girls, this is typically 10–12; for boys, 12–14. Hand-wrist radiographs or cervical vertebral maturation staging can assess remaining growth.
  • Modified force levels: Lower forces may be appropriate given the COL11A1-related PDL abnormality (discussed below).
  • Alveolar bone grafting: If the child had alveolar cleft, bone grafting is typically performed before canine eruption (age 9–11) and must be coordinated with orthodontic timing.

Phase III: Post-Growth Surgical Correction (Age 16+)

  • Orthognathic surgery: If mandibular retrognathia persists after growth is complete, surgical mandibular advancement (bilateral sagittal split osteotomy) may be needed. This is combined with pre-surgical orthodontics to align the teeth within each arch.
  • Timing: Surgery is typically deferred until skeletal maturity (confirmed by serial cephalometrics showing no further growth).
Craniofacial team coordination: Orthodontic decisions should not be made in isolation. The orthodontist, oral surgeon, ENT, and speech pathologist all have input. Many children’s hospitals run combined craniofacial clinics where all specialists see the child on the same day — this is the gold standard for Marshall syndrome dental care.

Orthodontic Considerations with COL11A1

COL11A1 mutations create specific risks during orthodontic treatment that are not present in the general population. These are not theoretical — they follow directly from what type XI collagen does in the periodontal ligament:

Root Resorption Risk

Root resorption (shortening of tooth roots) is a known side effect of orthodontic treatment in the general population, affecting roughly 1–5% of patients to a clinically significant degree. In Marshall syndrome, the risk is likely elevated because:

  • The periodontal ligament contains abnormal type XI collagen, potentially altering how forces are distributed to the root surface
  • Orthodontic loading changes stress distribution in the PDL and induces collagen remodeling — in a PDL with already-abnormal collagen architecture, this remodeling may be more destructive
  • Research shows that after orthodontic force, immature type III collagen fibers increase significantly in the apical (root tip) region, with stress concentration in compression zones — this is precisely where resorption occurs
Monitoring protocol: Periapical radiographs of the upper incisors (most vulnerable teeth) should be taken at 6-month intervals during active orthodontic treatment. If root shortening exceeds 2mm, treatment should be paused and force levels reconsidered. Some orthodontists recommend a 3-month rest period before resuming with lighter forces.

Tooth Movement Rate

Tooth movement depends on the PDL’s ability to transmit mechanical stress to trigger bone remodeling. With abnormal collagen in the PDL:

  • Teeth may move faster than expected with standard forces — looser connective tissue offers less resistance
  • Movement may be less predictable — teeth may tip rather than translate (bodily movement) because the PDL does not distribute force evenly
  • There is limited published data on tooth movement rate in Stickler/Marshall syndrome specifically, but case reports describe using lighter forces with acceptable outcomes

Retention and Relapse

After braces are removed, teeth are held in position by retainers while the PDL remodels. In Marshall syndrome:

  • The PDL may remodel more slowly or incompletely, increasing relapse risk
  • Lax periodontal ligament fibers (from abnormal collagen) provide less natural retention
  • Permanent (bonded) retainers are strongly recommended — both upper and lower lingual retainers should be considered as long-term or lifelong retention
  • If removable retainers are used, extended full-time wear (12+ months rather than the typical 6) before transitioning to nighttime-only wear

TMJ and Orthodontics

The temporomandibular joint deserves special attention during orthodontic treatment in Marshall syndrome. See the full TMJ & Craniofacial Pain page for detailed management; this section covers the orthodontic intersection specifically.

  • TMJ assessment before orthodontics: Any child with Marshall syndrome should have TMJ evaluation (clinical exam, and MRI if symptomatic) before starting orthodontic treatment. Pre-existing disc displacement or condylar degeneration changes the treatment plan.
  • Orthodontics can help or harm: Correcting a Class II malocclusion reduces chronic TMJ strain from mandibular retrognathia. However, aggressive mandibular advancement (functional appliances or surgery) in a joint with already-degenerating condylar cartilage can accelerate damage.
  • Avoid excessive mandibular advancement: Mouse models show that COL11A1 haploinsufficiency causes accelerated condylar cartilage degeneration. Functional appliances that force the mandible forward place additional mechanical stress on condylar cartilage that may already be compromised.
  • Splint therapy first: If TMJ symptoms (pain, clicking, locking, limited opening) are present, stabilization splint therapy should be completed before initiating orthodontic treatment. Starting orthodontics on an unstable TMJ risks worsening symptoms.
  • Monitor during treatment: TMJ symptoms should be assessed at every orthodontic adjustment appointment. New clicking, pain on opening, or changes in maximum opening distance warrant pausing treatment and re-evaluating.
The paradox: Mandibular retrognathia from Pierre Robin sequence causes TMJ strain, and correcting it (moving the jaw forward) can relieve that strain. But the same correction places mechanical load on condylar cartilage that has abnormal type XI collagen. The solution is moderate correction with careful monitoring, not aggressive advancement to an “ideal” position.

Kinesiology Taping as a TMJ Support Adjunct

Kinesiology tape (elastic therapeutic tape applied to the skin) is emerging as a low-risk adjunct for TMJ symptom management in connective tissue disorders. While not a standalone treatment, it can complement splint therapy and physiotherapy:

  • Mechanism: Kinesiology tape applied over the masseter and temporalis muscles provides gentle proprioceptive input — it does not restrict movement like rigid athletic tape, but instead creates a sensory reminder that reduces unconscious clenching and encourages relaxed jaw positioning.
  • Application: A Y-strip applied from the mandibular angle upward along the masseter, with the tails fanning over the temporalis, provides support without restricting mouth opening. Decompression strips over the TMJ itself (applied with no stretch while the mouth is open) can reduce local pressure sensation.
  • Evidence: Small trials in TMJ dysfunction show reduced pain scores and improved maximum mouth opening when kinesiology taping is combined with conventional therapy. The effect appears primarily proprioceptive and neuromuscular rather than mechanical.
  • Practical advantages: Non-invasive, can be worn during sleep (unlike some splints that children resist), compatible with orthodontic treatment, and can be applied by the patient or parent after instruction from a physiotherapist.
  • Skin considerations: Some individuals with connective tissue disorders have more fragile or sensitive skin. Use hypoallergenic tape, test a small area first, and remove gently with oil-based adhesive remover rather than pulling. If skin irritation occurs, discontinue use.

Dental Anesthesia Considerations

Local anesthesia effectiveness is a legitimate concern in connective tissue disorders. While the published data comes primarily from Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS), the underlying mechanism is relevant to any collagen disorder including Marshall syndrome.

Local Anesthetic Resistance

  • In EDS populations, survey data shows nearly three times the rate of local anesthetic non-response compared to controls
  • Up to 88% of EDS patients report some degree of anesthetic resistance — complete, partial, or rapidly waning effect
  • Both EDS patients and controls achieved initial anesthesia 5 minutes after lidocaine infiltration, but the effect wore off at 60 minutes in EDS patients while persisting in controls
  • The proposed mechanism: abnormal connective tissue allows faster vascular uptake or diffusion of the anesthetic away from the target nerve, reducing duration and depth of numbness

Practical Implications for Marshall Syndrome

  • Alert the dentist before any procedure that connective tissue disorder patients may have reduced anesthetic effectiveness
  • Articaine 4% may provide marginally better results than lidocaine due to its ability to diffuse through bone, though the difference in EDS populations was modest
  • Nerve blocks over infiltration: Inferior alveolar nerve blocks deliver anesthetic directly to the nerve trunk and may be more effective than infiltration in tissues with abnormal collagen
  • Top-up doses: Be prepared for the need for additional anesthetic during longer procedures
  • Timing: Schedule procedures to begin promptly after anesthesia takes effect, rather than waiting

Sedation and General Anesthesia

For extensive dental work, sedation or general anesthesia may be more appropriate than repeated local anesthetic attempts. See the Anesthesia & Sedation page for full details on Marshall syndrome-specific anesthesia considerations, including:

  • Airway management challenges from micrognathia and limited mouth opening
  • Cervical spine precautions during intubation
  • Mitral valve prolapse considerations (if present)
Dental Sedation Options — Safety Hierarchy

When local anesthesia alone is insufficient or the child cannot cooperate for dental procedures, sedation options exist on a spectrum. In Marshall syndrome, always use the minimum sedation level needed to accomplish the procedure safely:

  • MINIMAL sedation (nitrous oxide / laughing gas): The safest option. The child remains awake and responsive. No fasting required. Good for routine procedures, mild anxiety, and supplementing local anesthesia. Recovery is immediate once the gas is discontinued.
  • MODERATE sedation (oral midazolam): The child is drowsy but responsive to verbal commands and light touch. Protective reflexes (coughing, swallowing) are maintained. Requires brief fasting (2 hours clear liquids, 6 hours solids). Useful for longer procedures or moderate anxiety.
  • DEEP sedation (IV propofol or ketamine): The child is not easily aroused and may not respond to commands. Requires an anesthesiologist or specifically trained sedation provider. Continuous monitoring of oxygen saturation, heart rate, blood pressure, and end-tidal CO2 is mandatory. Reserve for procedures requiring complete stillness or significant duration.
  • GENERAL anesthesia (intubation): Full unconsciousness with controlled ventilation. Reserve for extensive surgical procedures only. Requires FULL airway precautions specific to Marshall syndrome: anticipated difficult airway from micrognathia and midface hypoplasia, fiberoptic intubation backup available, cervical spine neutral positioning (no hyperextension).

At ALL sedation levels in Marshall syndrome:

  • Airway assessment before sedation (Mallampati score, mouth opening, neck extension range)
  • Neutral neck positioning — no hyperextension due to potential cervical spine instability
  • Eye protection — tape eyelids closed or use eye shields to prevent corneal exposure and inadvertent pressure on the globe
  • Fiberoptic intubation equipment immediately available as backup, even for moderate sedation

Always recommend the minimum sedation level needed for the planned procedure. Escalate only if the lower level proves insufficient.

Nitrous oxide (laughing gas): Nitrous oxide is generally safe in Marshall syndrome for dental anxiety and mild sedation. It does not interact with collagen disorders specifically. However, children with significant midface hypoplasia may not get a good seal with the nasal mask, reducing effectiveness. Discuss with the dentist beforehand.
Dental Anesthesia — Modified Protocol Needed
Local anesthetic may have shorter duration in connective tissue disorders due to abnormal collagen allowing faster diffusion and vascular uptake of the anesthetic away from the target nerve. A modified dental anesthesia protocol improves outcomes:
  • Articaine 4% preferred over lidocaine: Articaine has superior tissue penetration (it diffuses through bone more effectively than other local anesthetics) and achieves a higher success rate in the mandibular teeth, where standard infiltration with lidocaine frequently fails
  • Allow 8–10 minutes onset time: Do not begin the procedure at the standard 5-minute mark. In connective tissue disorders, the anesthetic may take longer to achieve adequate nerve blockade. Test with a sharp explorer before proceeding.
  • Supplement with buccal infiltration: Even when using an inferior alveolar nerve block, add a buccal infiltration at the treatment site. The dual approach provides redundancy in a population where single-technique anesthesia is less reliable.
  • Topical anesthetic gel before injection: Apply 20% benzocaine or similar topical gel to the injection site for 2 minutes before the needle. This reduces injection pain, decreases anxiety (which itself reduces anesthetic effectiveness), and provides a small supplemental anesthetic effect at the superficial tissues.
  • Nitrous oxide for anxious patients: Nitrous oxide provides anxiolysis and mild analgesia that complements local anesthesia. It is particularly valuable in children with Marshall syndrome who have developed dental anxiety from previous experiences of inadequate numbness.
  • Avoid general anesthesia for routine dental work: General anesthesia carries elevated risk in Marshall syndrome due to difficult airway management (micrognathia, midface hypoplasia), cervical spine instability requiring careful positioning during intubation, and the disproportionate risk-benefit ratio for routine procedures. Reserve general anesthesia for extensive surgical work.
  • Create a dental anesthesia protocol card: Document what works for each patient — which anesthetic agent, concentration, technique (block vs. infiltration), onset time, and duration of effect. This card travels with the patient to every dental appointment, eliminating trial-and-error at each visit and ensuring consistency across providers.

Preventive Dental Care

Children with Marshall syndrome are at elevated risk for dental caries (cavities) and periodontal disease through multiple overlapping mechanisms:

Mouth Breathing and Dry Mouth

Midface hypoplasia and nasal obstruction (from the flat nasal bridge and possible deviated septum) promote chronic mouth breathing. Research consistently shows that mouth-breathing children have significantly higher caries rates because:

  • Oral airflow evaporates saliva, reducing its protective buffering capacity
  • Saliva is the primary antibacterial and remineralizing agent for tooth enamel
  • Chronic dry mouth shifts oral flora toward cariogenic (cavity-causing) bacteria
  • The anterior teeth are particularly vulnerable — the labial (front) surfaces dry out first

Medication Effects

Several medications commonly used in Marshall syndrome management can cause or worsen dry mouth:

  • Antihistamines (for allergic rhinitis, which is common with midface hypoplasia)
  • Certain pain medications
  • Some antidepressants or anxiety medications
  • Decongestants used for nasal obstruction

Dental Surgery Considerations

Wisdom Teeth (Third Molars)

  • Early assessment: Panoramic radiograph at age 14–16 to evaluate wisdom tooth development and position. In Marshall syndrome with a small mandible and narrow arches, impaction is very common.
  • Proactive extraction: If impaction is predicted, earlier extraction (age 16–18) is often easier than waiting — roots are less developed and bone is less dense.
  • Healing considerations: COL11A1 mutations may affect soft tissue healing. While wound dehiscence has not been specifically reported in Stickler/Marshall dental surgery, it is prudent to allow extended healing time and use absorbable sutures.
  • Anesthesia planning: Wisdom tooth extraction under local anesthesia may require the considerations discussed above. For bilateral extraction or complex surgical extraction, sedation or general anesthesia may be preferable.

Dental Implants

  • Bone density: The mild osteopenia sometimes seen in Stickler/Marshall syndrome may affect osseointegration (the implant bonding to bone). DEXA scan results, if available, can inform implant planning.
  • Longer healing: Standard implant integration takes 3–6 months. With potentially reduced bone density, planning for the longer end of this range (or using bone-condensing techniques rather than drilling) may improve outcomes.
  • Bone grafting: If alveolar bone is insufficient (common after cleft repair or tooth loss), bone grafting before implant placement requires careful assessment. Collagen membranes used in guided bone regeneration may behave differently in a COL11A1-affected individual.
  • Age consideration: Implants should not be placed until skeletal growth is complete (typically age 18+ for the mandible, later for the maxilla in some individuals).
  • Soft tissue integration: Peri-implant soft tissue (the gingival seal around the implant abutment) depends on collagen fiber attachment to the implant surface. With COL11A1-related collagen deficiency, this biological seal may be weaker, increasing susceptibility to peri-implant mucositis and peri-implantitis. More frequent maintenance visits (every 3–4 months rather than standard 6-month intervals) are warranted to monitor soft tissue health around implants.
  • Delayed loading protocol: In the general population, immediate or early loading protocols (placing a functional crown within days to weeks of implant surgery) are increasingly common. In Marshall syndrome, a delayed loading protocol is advisable — allowing a longer osseointegration period (minimum 4–6 months in the mandible, 6–8 months in the maxilla) before placing the final restoration under functional load. The combination of potentially reduced bone density and altered collagen remodeling means rushing to load carries higher failure risk.
  • Midface hypoplasia and bone volume: Midface hypoplasia in Marshall syndrome affects not only facial profile but also the volume and architecture of the maxillary alveolar bone available for implant placement. CBCT (cone-beam computed tomography) imaging is essential for pre-implant planning to assess bone height, width, and proximity to the maxillary sinus. Sinus lift procedures may be required more frequently than in the general population due to reduced maxillary bone height.
  • Surface-modified implants: Implants with roughened or bioactive surfaces (sandblasted and acid-etched, or hydroxyapatite-coated) promote greater bone-to-implant contact and may partially compensate for the reduced bone density and altered collagen organization. Discuss surface selection with the implant surgeon — smooth-surface implants are generally not recommended when bone quality is a concern.
  • Nutritional optimization pre-implant: Ensuring adequate vitamin D (target 40–60 ng/mL), calcium, and vitamin K2 levels before implant placement supports both osseointegration and soft tissue healing. A 25-OH vitamin D level should be checked 2–3 months before planned implant surgery, with supplementation to reach target before the procedure.

Alveolar Cleft Bone Grafting

Children who had cleft palate with extension into the alveolar ridge may need bone grafting to close the bony defect:

  • Typically performed at age 9–11, before the permanent canine erupts into the cleft site
  • Iliac crest (hip) bone graft is the gold standard donor site
  • Coordination with the orthodontist is essential — expansion of the maxillary arch is usually done first to align the bone segments before grafting
  • Post-grafting orthodontics guides the canine tooth into the grafted bone

The Dental-Medical Communication Gap

Most general dentists and even many orthodontists will never have treated a patient with Marshall syndrome. This is not a criticism — it is a rare condition. But it means the family must be the bridge between the medical team and the dental team.

What to Tell Your Dentist

  • Marshall syndrome is a genetic connective tissue disorder caused by a mutation in COL11A1 (type XI collagen)
  • Type XI collagen is present in the periodontal ligament, TMJ disc, and dentin
  • Local anesthesia may be less effective or shorter-lasting — be prepared for additional doses
  • Root resorption risk may be elevated during orthodontic treatment
  • TMJ should be assessed before and during orthodontic treatment
  • Cervical spine instability may be present — avoid extreme neck extension during dental procedures (this affects chair positioning and intubation)
  • If mitral valve prolapse is present, discuss whether antibiotic prophylaxis is warranted (see below)

Creating a Dental Information Card

A simple one-page document for the dental chart should include:

  • Diagnosis: Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutation — type XI collagen disorder)
  • Key dental implications: PDL collagen abnormality, TMJ concerns, possible anesthetic resistance
  • Cervical spine status (stable/unstable, last imaging date)
  • Cardiac status (MVP present/absent, echocardiogram date)
  • Current medications
  • Craniofacial team contact information
  • Allergies and prior anesthetic responses

Endocarditis Prophylaxis: The MVP Question

Some children with Marshall syndrome have mitral valve prolapse (MVP). The current American Heart Association guidelines (2007, reaffirmed 2021) state that MVP alone does not require antibiotic prophylaxis before dental procedures. Prophylaxis is only recommended for:

  • Prosthetic heart valves or prosthetic repair material
  • Previous infective endocarditis
  • Certain congenital heart defects (unrepaired cyanotic defects, repaired defects with residual issues)
  • Cardiac transplant patients with valvulopathy

However, if there is MVP with significant regurgitation, some cardiologists still recommend prophylaxis on a case-by-case basis. The definitive answer comes from the child’s cardiologist, not the dentist. See the Cardiac Screening page for more on MVP monitoring.

Annual dental clearance: Even without an endocarditis prophylaxis requirement, maintaining excellent oral health reduces bacteremia risk from daily activities (toothbrushing, chewing) that is actually a greater cumulative source of bacteremia than dental procedures. Good oral hygiene is cardiac protection.

Questions for the Orthodontist or Dentist

  1. “Are you familiar with treating patients with connective tissue disorders, and are you willing to modify force levels and monitoring frequency for orthodontic treatment?” — This gauges whether the orthodontist will adapt their approach or apply a one-size-fits-all protocol.
  2. “How will you monitor for root resorption during treatment, and what is your threshold for pausing treatment?” — The answer should include periodic radiographs (every 6 months) and a specific threshold (typically 2mm of root shortening).
  3. “What is your plan for TMJ evaluation before and during orthodontic treatment?” — TMJ assessment should be part of the initial workup and ongoing monitoring, not an afterthought.
  4. “What retention strategy do you recommend after treatment, given that the periodontal ligament has a collagen abnormality?” — The answer should lean toward permanent bonded retainers rather than relying solely on removable retainers.
  5. “Will you coordinate with our craniofacial team, and are you willing to have the team’s oral surgeon review the treatment plan?” — Orthodontic treatment in Marshall syndrome should not happen in isolation from the broader craniofacial care team.
