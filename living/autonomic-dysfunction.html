Autonomic Dysfunction & Marshall Syndrome

IMPORTANT — Discuss with Your Medical Team
This page represents a novel cross-domain synthesis connecting COL11A1 mutations to autonomic dysfunction. Not all children with Marshall syndrome will develop dysautonomia, but the mechanistic case for screening is strong. The connections described here draw from established research in connective tissue disorders, spinal pathology, and autonomic medicine. Always discuss screening and treatment decisions with your child's medical team.

Why This Matters for Marshall Syndrome

No published studies have directly linked Marshall syndrome to dysautonomia (autonomic nervous system dysfunction). However, the mechanistic case is compelling through at least four independent pathways. The Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS) community has thoroughly established the connective-tissue-to-dysautonomia connection — research shows that up to 80% of patients with hypermobile EDS also have postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS). The same biological logic applies to COL11A1 mutations in Marshall syndrome.

The autonomic nervous system controls everything your body does automatically: heart rate, blood pressure, digestion, temperature regulation, sweating. When connective tissue is defective, the structures that protect and support autonomic nerves can be compromised. In Marshall syndrome, four distinct pathways create risk for this kind of disruption.

The Dysautonomia Trifecta: EDS + POTS + MCAS

In the connective tissue disorder community, three conditions cluster together so frequently that they are called the “dysautonomia trifecta.” The prevalence data is striking:

The Four Pathways: Connective Tissue to Autonomic Dysfunction

In hypermobile EDS, where this mechanism is well-studied, 80% of patients develop POTS. The degree of vascular laxity in Marshall syndrome is unknown but plausible given widespread collagen involvement. Four specific sub-mechanisms are involved:
  • Vascular compliance: Abnormal collagen XI increases venous compliance, so blood pools in the legs on standing, reducing venous return and triggering compensatory tachycardia.
  • Venous pooling: Lax connective tissue increases both arterial elasticity and venous distensibility, impairing the normal vasoconstrictive response to gravity.
  • Autonomic nerve support: Peripheral autonomic nerves run through connective tissue sheaths — abnormal collagen may physically alter nerve transmission along these pathways.
  • Baroreceptor dysfunction: Baroreceptors (blood pressure sensors) sit in the collagen-rich carotid sinus and aortic arch. Altered connective tissue compliance changes their sensitivity, potentially blunting the body’s ability to detect and correct blood pressure changes on standing.
Mast Cell Activation Speculative The EDS/POTS/MCAS (mast cell activation syndrome) triad is increasingly recognized in connective tissue disorders. Approximately 24% of EDS patients have confirmed MCAS. Defective collagen may mechanically irritate mast cells embedded in connective tissue, triggering inappropriate histamine release, vasodilation, and autonomic instability. Among POTS patients with EDS, 31% also have MCAS. Whether this extends to COL11A1 mutations is unknown but warrants awareness, as all three conditions share collagen-dependent vascular mechanisms.

Red Ear Syndrome & Autonomic Flushing

Some children with Marshall syndrome experience episodes of sudden redness and burning in one or both ears. This is called Red Ear Syndrome (RES), and it is not random — it follows specific autonomic nerve distribution patterns.

  • Red Ear Syndrome (RES): Episodic redness, warmth, and burning of one or both ears, lasting minutes to hours. Connected to cervical spine pathology through the cervical sympathetic chain, AND to TMJ dysfunction through the auriculotemporal nerve. See the dedicated TMJ & Craniofacial Pain page for the second pathway.
  • Cervical mechanism: In Marshall syndrome, cervical spine abnormalities (disc degeneration, vertebral instability) can irritate the superior cervical sympathetic ganglion, which sits at the C2–C3 level. This ganglion controls blood vessel tone in the face and ears.
  • TMJ-auriculotemporal mechanism (second pathway): TMJ disc displacement or inflammation can irritate the auriculotemporal nerve (a branch of the trigeminal nerve that innervates both the TMJ and the ear), producing vasodilation and redness in the ear. Triggers that involve jaw movement (chewing, yawning) point to this pathway. Both pathways may be active simultaneously.
  • Erythromelalgia-like symptoms: Red, hot extremities — arms, cheeks, fingers, toes — represent small fiber neuropathy or autonomic nerve irritation. These episodes may be misdiagnosed as “just flushing” or allergic reactions.
  • The pattern matters: These symptoms follow autonomic nerve distribution. Unilateral ear redness implicates the ipsilateral cervical sympathetic chain. Bilateral symptoms may indicate more central autonomic dysregulation.

Lhermitte’s Sign: “Neck Zaps” — A Red Flag

Lhermitte’s Sign Is a Red Flag
Lhermitte’s sign is an electric shock sensation that shoots down the spine or into the limbs when the neck is bent forward. It indicates irritation of the dorsal columns in the cervical spinal cord and requires prompt medical evaluation.
  • What it feels like: An electric shock, buzzing, or tingling that radiates down the back or into the arms and legs when flexing the neck. Children may describe it as “zapping” or “buzzing” or simply avoid bending their neck.
  • What it means: The dorsal columns of the cervical spinal cord are being mechanically irritated — something is pressing on or stretching the cord when the neck moves.
  • In Marshall syndrome, the cause is almost certainly MECHANICAL: Disc herniation, vertebral instability, or ossification compressing the spinal cord. This is different from the demyelinating causes (like multiple sclerosis) that neurologists typically associate with Lhermitte’s sign.
  • Published evidence: A case of cervical myelopathy with Brown-Séquard syndrome (half-cord compression) has been reported in Stickler syndrome (J Med Genet, 1992). Thoracic disc herniation causing paraplegia has also been documented in Stickler syndrome.
  • Genetic basis: COL11A1 polymorphism has been significantly associated with disc herniation susceptibility (Am J Hum Genet, 2007), providing a direct genetic link between the Marshall mutation and the mechanical cause of cord compression.

Symptoms to Watch For in Children

Autonomic dysfunction in children often looks different than in adults. Children may not be able to articulate what they feel — they say “I feel weird” or “my tummy hurts” instead of describing dizziness or nausea. Watch for behavioral patterns like refusing to stand in line, preferring to lie down, or avoiding activities that require prolonged standing.

Cardiovascular:

  • Dizziness or lightheadedness when standing up
  • Racing heart (palpitations), especially after standing
  • Wanting to sit or lie down frequently
  • Symptoms worse in the morning or after sleep
  • Symptoms worse in heat or after hot showers

Energy & Exercise:

  • Exercise intolerance that seems disproportionate to their pain level
  • Disproportionate fatigue — far more tired than expected for their activity level
  • Post-exertional crashes — feeling terrible hours after or the day after activity

Temperature Regulation:

  • Abnormal sweating (too much or too little, or asymmetric)
  • Cold hands and feet despite warm environment
  • Poor overall temperature regulation
  • Mottled or purple legs when standing (acrocyanosis — blood pooling)

Gastrointestinal:

  • Morning nausea (not related to food)
  • Unexplained abdominal pain (vagus nerve dysfunction)
  • Constipation, bloating, or early satiety (vagal tone problems)
  • Gastroparesis or delayed gastric emptying — common in POTS + collagen disorders
  • Chronic reflux that does not fully respond to standard treatment
The Gut–Autonomic Connection
The combination of POTS and collagen disorders frequently produces gastroparesis, reflux, and bloating. Vagal dysfunction impairs GI motility, which can amplify the gut–disc axis (gut dysbiosis worsening systemic inflammation and disc degeneration). Autonomic dysfunction may actually be an upstream driver of gut problems rather than a consequence — treating the autonomic component can improve GI symptoms that previously seemed unrelated.
2025 AGA Clinical Practice Update: The American Gastroenterological Association now recognizes the hEDS + POTS + MCAS + gut-brain disorder cluster as a clinical entity. In hEDS, >50% report chronic GI complaints: abdominal pain 69%, constipation 73%, diarrhea 47%, and postprandial fullness 34% (vs ~16–27% in controls). Marshall syndrome likely belongs in this spectrum. Four converging pathways drive gut dysfunction: (1) connective tissue laxity in the gut wall, (2) vagal autonomic dysfunction, (3) mast cell activation and histamine release, and (4) altered microbiome from impaired motility. Consider gastroenterology referral if GI symptoms are present — they are not “separate problems” but part of the collagen disorder.

Other:

  • Positional headaches (worse standing, better lying down)
  • Brain fog — difficulty concentrating, especially when upright
  • Sleep disturbances despite being exhausted
  • Anxiety that appears physiological rather than psychological (adrenaline surges from autonomic dysregulation can mimic or trigger anxiety)

The Evening Worsening Pattern

If your child’s neurological or autonomic symptoms consistently get worse by evening, this is not random — it is actually a diagnostic clue pointing to dynamic mechanical compression in the cervical spine. Here is why:

  • Gravity compresses the cervical spine all day. By evening, cumulative gravitational loading has maximally compressed discs and narrowed spinal canal space.
  • Stabilizing muscles fatigue. The muscles that actively stabilize the cervical spine tire over the course of the day, allowing more instability and more compression of neural structures.
  • Inflammatory mediators accumulate. Repeated mechanical irritation throughout the day triggers progressive inflammation around nerve structures.
  • In a child with COL11A1 mutations, this is amplified: Defective type XI collagen means ligaments provide less passive stability than normal. The muscles must compensate harder, they fatigue faster, and the evening decompensation is more pronounced.

If you observe this diurnal pattern, document it and bring it to your medical team. It strongly suggests dynamic mechanical compression rather than a systemic or inflammatory cause, and it guides imaging decisions (flexion-extension MRI rather than static imaging).

Testing: Start Simple

The Active Stand Test — Free, 15 Minutes, Any Office
This is the simplest first screen for orthostatic intolerance. Have your child lie flat for 5 minutes, then record heart rate and blood pressure. Have them stand still for 10 minutes, recording heart rate and blood pressure at 2, 5, and 10 minutes. In children, a heart rate increase of 40 bpm or more (or sustained rate above 130 bpm) meets criteria for POTS. You can even do this at home with an inexpensive pulse oximeter to decide whether formal testing is warranted.
  • Active Stand Test (first step): Free, takes 15 minutes, can be done in any doctor’s office. Lie flat 5 minutes, record heart rate and blood pressure. Stand 10 minutes, record at 2, 5, and 10 minutes. In children, a 40+ bpm increase meets POTS criteria.
  • 24-hour Holter monitor with heart rate variability (HRV) analysis: Low-burden test that provides extensive data on autonomic function throughout a normal day.
  • Tilt table test: Gold standard for POTS diagnosis if the Active Stand Test is suggestive. Performed in a hospital setting with continuous monitoring.
  • MRI of the cervical spine: URGENT if Lhermitte’s sign or any leg weakness is present. Do not delay.
  • Flexion-extension MRI: Critical for children with skeletal dysplasia. Standard (static) MRI shows the spine in one position. Flexion-extension MRI reveals dynamic instability — compression that only occurs in certain neck positions. This is essential in Marshall syndrome where ligamentous laxity may allow abnormal movement.

Formal Autonomic Function Testing: The Three-Domain Assessment

Research Update (R435): Formal autonomic function testing has matured into a standardized, three-domain assessment that provides a comprehensive map of autonomic nervous system function. For Marshall syndrome patients with suspected dysautonomia, this structured testing replaces the piecemeal approach of ordering individual tests and provides the diagnostic clarity needed to guide targeted treatment.

The three domains of standardized autonomic testing evaluate the cardiovascular, sudomotor (sweating), and adrenergic/cardiovagal branches of the autonomic nervous system separately, because damage can be asymmetric — a patient may have severe cardiovascular dysautonomia with preserved sudomotor function, or vice versa.

DomainTestWhat It MeasuresMarshall Syndrome Relevance
Cardiovascular Head-up tilt table test Heart rate and blood pressure response to passive upright positioning (70° tilt for 10–45 minutes). Diagnoses POTS (HR ≥40 bpm increase in children), orthostatic hypotension (≥20/10 mmHg drop), and vasovagal syncope. The single most important cardiovascular autonomic test in Marshall syndrome. Distinguishes between POTS subtypes (neuropathic, hyperadrenergic, hypovolemic) that require different treatment approaches. Performed at specialized autonomic centers with continuous beat-to-beat monitoring.
Sudomotor QSART (Quantitative Sudomotor Axon Reflex Test) Measures sweat output at four standardized body sites (forearm, proximal leg, distal leg, foot) in response to iontophoresis of acetylcholine. Assesses the integrity of postganglionic sympathetic sudomotor C-fibers. Directly evaluates the small fiber autonomic nerves that control sweating. Abnormal QSART in a length-dependent pattern (worse distally) confirms small fiber neuropathy affecting autonomic fibers — a finding that redirects treatment toward neuropathic pain agents and potentially IVIG if autoimmune SFN is suspected. See the SFN section below for treatment implications.
Cardiovagal Heart rate variability (HRV) during deep breathing and Valsalva maneuver Measures the parasympathetic (vagal) contribution to heart rate modulation. Normal deep-breathing HRV requires intact vagal tone. The Valsalva ratio (max HR during strain / min HR after release) assesses both sympathetic and parasympathetic limbs. Reduced HRV suggests vagal dysfunction, which connects directly to the GI symptoms (gastroparesis, reflux, bloating) common in connective tissue disorders. A low Valsalva ratio may also be relevant to delivery planning in pregnant Marshall syndrome patients (see Pregnancy Management), as impaired autonomic responses affect labor tolerance.

When to Pursue Formal Autonomic Testing

The active stand test (described above) is sufficient as an initial screen. Formal three-domain autonomic testing should be requested when:

  • The active stand test is positive (HR increase ≥40 bpm) and treatment is being considered — the tilt table test differentiates POTS subtypes that respond to different medications
  • Temperature dysregulation and sweating abnormalities are prominent symptoms — QSART quantifies sudomotor dysfunction and may reveal small fiber neuropathy
  • GI symptoms (gastroparesis, chronic nausea, early satiety) are present alongside orthostatic symptoms — cardiovagal testing evaluates vagal nerve function that controls GI motility
  • Symptoms are unexplained by structural findings and the clinical picture suggests autonomic involvement beyond simple orthostatic intolerance
  • Pre-surgical evaluation for major procedures — documented autonomic dysfunction informs anesthetic planning and perioperative monitoring

Where to Get Tested

Formal autonomic testing requires specialized equipment and interpretation expertise. Major autonomic centers include:

  • Mayo Clinic Autonomic Disorders Center (Rochester, MN) — the largest autonomic testing program in the world
  • Cleveland Clinic Autonomic Lab
  • Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) — pediatric-specific autonomic program
  • Vanderbilt Autonomic Dysfunction Center
  • NYU Langone Dysautonomia Center

Many autonomic centers accept referrals from out of state and can coordinate with local providers for follow-up care. Telehealth consultation for result interpretation is increasingly available.

Request the complete autonomic reflex screen (ARS). Rather than ordering individual tests piecemeal, request the composite autonomic scoring scale (CASS) or equivalent standardized battery that includes all three domains in a single visit. The composite score (0–10) provides a severity index that is useful for tracking treatment response over time and for documenting disability for insurance or school accommodation purposes.

Treatment Overlap: Helping Both Marshall and Dysautonomia

Many interventions help both the underlying Marshall syndrome and autonomic dysfunction simultaneously. This overlap means that screening for dysautonomia can directly improve your child’s treatment plan even if formal POTS criteria are not met.

  • Aquatic therapy: The single best exercise for both conditions. Hydrostatic pressure from water immersion prevents blood from pooling in the legs, supporting blood pressure and cardiac output while simultaneously providing joint-safe resistance exercise. Keep water temperature cool (82–86°F / 28–30°C) — warmer water can worsen autonomic symptoms.
  • Recumbent exercise: Recumbent bike, rowing machine, or swimming — any exercise that avoids prolonged upright posture. This reduces the gravitational stress on the autonomic system while still providing cardiovascular conditioning and the molecular benefits of exercise.
  • CHOP Modified Dallas Protocol: A graduated exercise program developed at Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia specifically for adolescents with POTS. Starts with recumbent exercise only, gradually introduces upright exercise over 3 months. In published studies, 53% of participants no longer met POTS criteria after completing the protocol.
    POTS Exercise Rehabilitation: The 5-Phase Protocol

    Structured exercise rehabilitation is the single most effective long-term treatment for POTS in connective tissue disorders. The protocol below synthesizes the CHOP Modified Dallas Protocol with adaptations for hypermobile patients. Each phase lasts a minimum of 2 weeks; advance only when the current phase is tolerated without symptom flares.

    • Phase 1 — Supine only: Recumbent bike, rowing machine, or swimming. 15–20 minutes, 3–4 days per week. Heart rate target: 50–60% of age-predicted maximum. No upright exercise. Monitor for post-exertional malaise (PEM) the following day; if PEM occurs, reduce duration by 5 minutes.
    • Phase 2 — Semi-recumbent: Add seated resistance exercises (seated leg press, seated row) and increase aerobic duration to 25–30 minutes. Continue 3–4 days per week. Heart rate target: 60–70% max. Begin isometric core exercises in supine position.
    • Phase 3 — Incline transition: Introduce incline treadmill walking (start at 2–3 degree incline, 10 minutes) while maintaining recumbent aerobic base. Begin light standing exercises with wall support. Total session: 30–35 minutes.
    • Phase 4 — Upright introduction: Upright walking (flat ground, 15 minutes), standing resistance exercises with support. Combine with continued recumbent aerobic work. Total session: 35–40 minutes, 4–5 days per week.
    • Phase 5 — Maintenance: Independent exercise combining upright and recumbent modalities. Target: 30–45 minutes, 5 days per week. Aquatic exercise (pool walking, swimming) can substitute for any session and provides excellent maintenance with built-in orthostatic support from hydrostatic pressure.

    Key adaptations for Marshall syndrome: (1) Avoid high-impact activities at all phases — substitute swimming or cycling for running; (2) monitor cervical symptoms during rowing; (3) keep pool temperature at 82–86°F for aquatic sessions; (4) hydrate with electrolytes 30 minutes before every session; (5) if a phase triggers increased joint pain or subluxations, extend that phase rather than advancing.

  • Hydration + salt loading: Increases total blood volume, reducing the tendency for blood to pool in the legs. Simple, essentially no risk. Target 2–3 liters of fluid daily with added electrolytes. Discuss specific sodium targets with your physician.
  • Compression garments: Abdominal binders or waist-high compression stockings reduce venous pooling if orthostatic symptoms are confirmed. Abdominal compression is often more effective and better tolerated than leg-only compression in children.
  • Modifications to physical therapy: If your child has confirmed or suspected orthostatic intolerance, break standing PT exercises into shorter intervals (2–3 minutes standing, then sit or recline). Monitor for dizziness during all upright exercises. Inform the physical therapist about the autonomic component.
POTS Management Protocol for Connective Tissue Disorders

Postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome (POTS) affects an estimated 25% or more of patients with hypermobility-spectrum connective tissue disorders. In Marshall syndrome, where COL11A1 mutations affect vascular connective tissue and autonomic nerve support structures, POTS should be actively screened for rather than discovered incidentally.

Non-Pharmacological First-Line (Start Here)

  • Fluid intake: 2–3 liters per day. Water alone is insufficient — add electrolytes (oral rehydration solutions, electrolyte tablets, or sports drinks diluted 50/50 with water).
  • Salt loading: 3–5 grams of sodium per day (above normal dietary intake). Salt tablets, broth, or electrolyte drinks. This expands blood volume and reduces venous pooling.
  • Compression garments: Waist-high compression stockings (20–30 mmHg) are significantly more effective than knee-high. Abdominal binders provide additional benefit. Compression must extend above the knee to prevent blood pooling in the large venous reservoirs of the thighs and abdomen.
  • Graduated exercise: Start with recumbent exercise only (recumbent bike, rowing, swimming). Gradually introduce semi-upright and then upright exercise over 3 months. The CHOP Modified Dallas Protocol provides a structured, evidence-based progression.
  • Elevate the head of the bed: Raise the head end of the bed 4–6 inches (use bed risers, not pillows). This promotes overnight fluid redistribution and reduces morning symptom severity.

Pharmacological Options (If Non-Pharmacological Measures Are Insufficient)

  • Fludrocortisone: Expands blood volume through sodium and water retention. Start low (0.05 mg/day), titrate slowly. Monitor potassium.
  • Midodrine: Alpha-1 agonist that constricts blood vessels. Give 30 minutes before rising. Do not give within 4 hours of bedtime.
  • Propranolol: Low-dose beta-blocker for heart rate control. Use LOW doses only — high doses worsen exercise intolerance.
  • Ivabradine: Selective sinus node inhibitor. Reduces heart rate without lowering blood pressure or impairing exercise capacity. Increasingly preferred in pediatric POTS.

Screening trigger: Perform an active stand test (see above) if your child has any of the following: red ears or facial flushing episodes, temperature sensitivity or poor thermoregulation, or exercise intolerance that seems disproportionate to their musculoskeletal limitations. These are autonomic red flags that warrant formal evaluation.

Pharmacological Treatment Tiers (When Lifestyle Isn’t Enough)

If hydration, salt loading, compression, and graduated exercise do not adequately control symptoms, pharmacological options are available. These should be managed by a physician experienced with pediatric dysautonomia.

Clinical Pearl
In collagen disorders with POTS, combination therapy at low doses often works better than monotherapy at high doses. A common effective combination: fludrocortisone (volume expansion) + midodrine (vasoconstriction) + ivabradine (heart rate control). Each targets a different mechanism of orthostatic intolerance.

The Mast Cell Connection

The triad of EDS + POTS + MCAS (mast cell activation syndrome) is increasingly recognized in the connective tissue disorder community. Whether this extends to Marshall syndrome and COL11A1 mutations is unproven, but parents should be aware of the possibility.

Symptoms suggesting MCAS:

  • Unexplained flushing (face, chest, ears)
  • Unexplained rashes or hives, especially triggered by pressure, heat, or stress
  • Exaggerated reactions to foods, temperature changes, or fragrances
  • Brain fog and fatigue that seem disproportionate
  • GI symptoms (nausea, cramping, diarrhea) that overlap with autonomic GI dysfunction

Screening tests for MCAS:

  • Serum tryptase (baseline and during a flare if possible)
  • 24-hour urine N-methylhistamine
  • Prostaglandin D2 levels

Treatment if confirmed:

  • H1 antihistamine (cetirizine or fexofenadine) + H2 antihistamine (famotidine) combination
  • Mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn sodium, ketotifen) if antihistamines alone are insufficient
  • Trigger avoidance (heat, stress, specific foods identified through elimination)

Temperature Dysregulation

Temperature dysregulation is a frequently underrecognized feature of autonomic dysfunction in hypermobile connective tissue disorders. Children with Marshall syndrome may experience a range of thermoregulatory symptoms that are often dismissed as behavioral or attributed to other causes.

Common Presentations

  • Heat intolerance: Excessive fatigue, flushing, nausea, or worsening of POTS symptoms in warm environments or after exertion. Heat causes peripheral vasodilation, which exacerbates venous pooling in individuals with lax vascular connective tissue
  • Cold intolerance: Disproportionate sensitivity to cold temperatures, with extremities that become painful, white, or blue. May reflect impaired autonomic vasoconstriction or small fiber neuropathy
  • Abnormal sweating: Hyperhidrosis (excessive sweating) in some body regions with anhidrosis (absent sweating) in others. Asymmetric sweating patterns suggest regional autonomic nerve dysfunction rather than a systemic cause
  • Paradoxical temperature sensations: Feeling cold when objectively warm, or experiencing burning sensations in cool environments. These indicate central or peripheral thermoregulatory processing errors
Connective Tissue & Thermoregulation: What the Research Shows

Emerging research on thermoregulation in connective tissue disorders reveals that the temperature dysregulation seen in Marshall syndrome is not merely a secondary consequence of autonomic dysfunction — it involves primary structural deficits in the thermoregulatory apparatus itself. Three mechanisms converge:

  • Collagen-dependent vascular reactivity: The walls of cutaneous arterioles and venules contain type XI collagen. In COL11A1 mutations, altered vascular wall compliance impairs the vasoconstrictive and vasodilatory responses that are the body’s primary mechanism for heat conservation and dissipation. This means the blood vessels responsible for thermoregulation are structurally compromised, not just poorly innervated.
  • Sweat gland connective tissue sheath: Eccrine sweat glands are surrounded by a connective tissue capsule. Defective collagen in this capsule may alter mechanical signaling to the glandular cells, contributing to the patchy anhidrosis (absent sweating) and regional hyperhidrosis (excessive sweating) patterns observed in hypermobile connective tissue disorders.
  • Skin thickness and thermal conductivity: The dermis is primarily collagen. In connective tissue disorders, altered dermal collagen architecture changes the skin’s thermal conductivity — affecting how efficiently heat is transferred from the body core to the skin surface for dissipation. This may explain why some children with Marshall syndrome feel “internally hot” despite cool skin, or vice versa.

Practical implication: Because thermoregulation deficits in Marshall syndrome involve structural connective tissue problems (not just autonomic nerve dysfunction), treatments that target only the autonomic nervous system (e.g., fludrocortisone, midodrine) may incompletely address temperature dysregulation. Environmental modifications — climate control, cooling vests, pre-cooling strategies — become even more important because they bypass the defective physiological thermoregulatory apparatus entirely.

Raynaud Phenomenon

Raynaud phenomenon — episodic vasospasm of the digital arteries causing color changes (white → blue → red) in the fingers or toes — deserves special attention in Marshall syndrome. Compromised collagen in blood vessel walls may predispose the vessels to exaggerated spasm in response to cold or stress. The classic triphasic color change is diagnostic, but many children present with an incomplete pattern (pallor and cyanosis without the red reperfusion phase).

Mechanisms in Marshall Syndrome

Four converging pathways contribute to temperature dysregulation in collagen XI disorders:

  • Autonomic dysfunction: The sympathetic nervous system controls blood vessel tone and sweating. Dysautonomia from any of the four pathways described above (spinal, deconditioning, connective tissue laxity, mast cell activation) directly impairs thermoregulatory reflexes
  • Small fiber neuropathy (SFN): Small unmyelinated C-fibers carry temperature and pain signals and control sweating and blood vessel tone. SFN is increasingly recognized in hypermobile connective tissue disorders and produces both abnormal temperature perception and impaired thermoregulatory effector responses
  • Craniocervical instability affecting thermoregulatory centers: The hypothalamus is the brain’s thermostat. In Marshall syndrome, cervical spine instability or craniocervical instability (CCI) can compromise brainstem and hypothalamic function through intermittent compression or altered cerebrospinal fluid dynamics, disrupting central thermoregulation
  • Mast cell activation: MCAS produces episodic vasodilation, flushing, and temperature dysregulation through inappropriate histamine and prostaglandin release. These episodes may be triggered by temperature changes themselves, creating a feedback loop

Management

  • Hydration: Adequate fluid and electrolyte intake supports blood volume, which improves both orthostatic tolerance and thermoregulatory blood flow redistribution
  • Compression garments: Waist-high compression stockings reduce peripheral blood pooling in heat, improving the body’s ability to redirect blood flow for thermoregulation
  • Layered clothing: Multiple light layers allow rapid adjustment to environmental temperature changes and the unpredictable temperature fluctuations these children experience
  • Heated gloves and socks for Raynaud: Battery-powered heated gloves and socks provide targeted warmth during cold exposure. Chemical hand warmers in pockets or mittens are a lower-cost alternative
  • Nifedipine for severe Raynaud: If Raynaud phenomenon causes digital ulceration, significant pain, or functional limitation that does not respond to conservative measures, nifedipine (a calcium channel blocker) is the first-line pharmacological treatment. This requires physician supervision and monitoring for hypotension, which can worsen POTS symptoms

SFN and Temperature Sensing Impairment

Small fiber neuropathy (SFN) — discussed in detail below — provides a direct neurological mechanism for the temperature dysregulation seen in Marshall syndrome. The small unmyelinated C-fibers that carry temperature sensation are the same fibers damaged in SFN. When these fibers are impaired:

  • Temperature detection thresholds rise: The child may not perceive gradual environmental temperature changes until they are extreme, missing the window for early compensatory responses (seeking shade, removing a layer, drinking water)
  • Thermoregulatory reflexes are delayed: The autonomic C-fibers that control sweating and skin blood vessel tone respond sluggishly or not at all, impairing the body’s automatic cooling and warming mechanisms
  • Paradoxical symptoms emerge: Damaged small fibers may fire spontaneously, producing burning pain in cool environments or cold sensations when warm — symptoms that seem “impossible” but have a clear neurological basis
Safety implication: Children with SFN-related temperature sensing impairment are at genuine risk for heat illness and cold injury because their bodies fail to detect and respond to temperature extremes normally. This is not a comfort preference — it is a neurological deficit that requires environmental protection.

Exercise Environment Control

Exercise is essential for Marshall syndrome management (see Exercise as Medicine), but thermoregulatory dysfunction requires careful environmental control during physical activity:

  • Temperature range: Exercise in environments between 65–75°F (18–24°C) whenever possible. Outdoor exercise above 80°F (27°C) or below 40°F (4°C) should be avoided or closely monitored
  • Pre-cooling strategies: For exercise in warmer environments, pre-cooling with cold towels on the neck, cold water intake 15–20 minutes before activity, or a cooling vest can extend the child’s exercise tolerance before thermoregulatory failure
  • Humidity awareness: High humidity impairs evaporative cooling (sweating), which is often already compromised by autonomic dysfunction. Indoor, climate-controlled exercise environments are preferable in humid conditions
  • Aquatic therapy temperature: Pool temperature of 82–86°F (28–30°C) is optimal — warm enough to prevent cold-induced vasoconstriction but cool enough to avoid heat-related autonomic decompensation. Therapeutic “warm pools” (90°F+) may trigger POTS symptoms and should be used cautiously
  • Hydration timing: Begin hydrating with electrolyte-containing fluids 30 minutes before exercise, not just during. Children with autonomic dysfunction start exercise at a hydration deficit more often than healthy peers
  • Recovery environment: After exercise, transition to a cool (not cold), climate-controlled space. Rapid environmental temperature changes post-exercise can trigger autonomic instability, vasovagal responses, or rebound flushing

School Accommodations for Temperature Dysregulation

  • Indoor recess during extreme weather: Both excessive heat and cold can trigger symptom flares. A 504 plan or IEP should specify indoor recess options during temperature extremes
  • Unrestricted water access: Hydration is a primary thermoregulatory tool. The child should have water access at all times, including during class, not only at designated breaks
  • Layered clothing permission: Allow the child to add or remove layers as needed, even if this deviates from dress code policies. Temperature perception in these children is genuinely dysregulated, not a preference
  • Proximity to climate-controlled spaces: Seating away from windows, radiators, or exterior walls helps minimize environmental temperature variability
  • Modified PE in temperature extremes: Outdoor PE should move indoors when temperatures exceed 85°F or fall below 40°F. The PE teacher should be briefed on the child’s thermoregulatory vulnerability

Small Fiber Neuropathy (SFN) in Connective Tissue Disorders

Small fiber neuropathy (SFN) is emerging as a major contributor to the pain, autonomic dysfunction, and sensory symptoms seen in hypermobile connective tissue disorders. Recent studies have revealed a striking prevalence: 61–100% of patients with hypermobile EDS (hEDS) tested positive for SFN on skin punch biopsy, and 90% of confirmed SFN patients demonstrated multi-domain autonomic failure on standardized autonomic testing. While SFN has not been studied specifically in Marshall syndrome, the shared connective tissue pathology and overlapping clinical features strongly suggest it is relevant.

What Is Small Fiber Neuropathy?

Small fiber neuropathy is damage to the small unmyelinated C-fibers and thinly myelinated A-delta fibers that innervate the skin, blood vessels, sweat glands, and visceral organs. These fibers carry:

  • Pain and temperature signals — their damage produces burning pain, allodynia (pain from light touch), and abnormal temperature perception
  • Autonomic commands — they control sweating, blood vessel tone, heart rate modulation, GI motility, and bladder function
  • Proprioceptive input — contributing to the impaired body awareness that compounds joint instability in hypermobile patients

Critically, small fiber neuropathy does not appear on standard nerve conduction studies (NCS) or EMG, which test only large fibers. This means SFN is routinely missed unless specifically tested for, and children’s symptoms are often attributed to “anxiety,” “growing pains,” or “hypermobility pain” without recognizing the neuropathic component.

Diagnostic Testing

TestWhat It MeasuresRole in Diagnosis
Skin punch biopsy Intraepidermal nerve fiber density (IENFD) at the distal leg (ankle) and proximal thigh Gold standard. A 3mm skin punch is taken under local anesthetic (minimally invasive). Reduced IENFD below age- and sex-adjusted norms confirms SFN. The procedure takes minutes and can be performed in clinic.
Quantitative sudomotor axon reflex test (QSART) Sweat gland innervation and autonomic C-fiber function Non-invasive test measuring sweat output in response to acetylcholine iontophoresis. Reduced sweating at distal sites indicates autonomic small fiber dysfunction. Available at specialized autonomic centers.
Corneal confocal microscopy Corneal nerve fiber density and morphology Non-invasive imaging of the corneal sub-basal nerve plexus. Corneal nerves are small fibers, and reduced density correlates with systemic SFN. Particularly relevant in Marshall syndrome where ophthalmology visits are already routine.
Quantitative sensory testing (QST) Thermal detection and pain thresholds Non-invasive but subjective. Abnormal warm/cold detection thresholds suggest small fiber dysfunction. Useful as a screening tool but not diagnostic alone.

Treatment of SFN

  • Neuropathic pain agents: When SFN is confirmed, the pain is neuropathic in mechanism and responds to neuropathic pain medications rather than standard analgesics. First-line options include gabapentin, pregabalin, and duloxetine. Low-dose amitriptyline may also be effective, particularly when sleep disruption is a component.
  • Topical treatments: Capsaicin 8% patches (applied by a clinician) can desensitize the affected small fibers. Lidocaine patches or cream provide localized relief for areas of severe allodynia.
  • IVIG for autoimmune SFN: In cases where SFN is suspected to have an autoimmune etiology (suggested by positive autoimmune markers, acute onset, or rapid progression), intravenous immunoglobulin (IVIG) has shown benefit in clinical series. IVIG is expensive and requires infusion visits, but for confirmed autoimmune SFN that does not respond to standard neuropathic pain agents, it can be transformative. Testing for associated antibodies (anti-FGFR3, anti-TS-HDS, trisulfated heparan disaccharide antibodies) can help identify autoimmune cases.
Why SFN Matters for Marshall Syndrome
Small fiber neuropathy may explain several “unexplained” features of Marshall syndrome that are otherwise attributed to separate, unconnected causes:
  • Burning pain that does not match the expected pattern of musculoskeletal or joint pain
  • Autonomic symptoms (POTS, temperature dysregulation, GI dysmotility, sweating abnormalities) without a clear structural cause
  • Allodynia (pain from clothing, light touch, bedsheets) that seems “psychological” but is actually neuropathic
  • Exercise intolerance beyond what deconditioning alone would explain
If your child has these features, ask about skin punch biopsy for SFN. The test is minimally invasive, definitive, and may redirect treatment from ineffective analgesics to appropriate neuropathic pain management.
