Eye Management: Vitreous, Retina, Myopia & Cataracts

The eyes are one of the most affected organ systems in Marshall syndrome. With a 65% lifetime risk of retinal detachment — higher than any other known predisposing condition — proactive monitoring and prophylactic treatment can be sight-saving. Every person with a confirmed COL11A1 mutation needs a vitreoretinal specialist, not just a general ophthalmologist.

Vitreous Degeneration: The Core Problem

The vitreous humor — the clear gel that fills the eye — is made primarily of water, hyaluronic acid, and collagen fibrils. Type XI collagen, produced by the COL11A1 gene, plays a critical structural role: it regulates the diameter and spacing of the larger type II collagen fibrils that form the vitreous scaffold. Think of type XI collagen as the rebar spacing in concrete — without it, the structural fibers form incorrectly.

In Marshall syndrome and Stickler syndrome type II (both caused by COL11A1 mutations), insufficient type XI collagen leads to:

  • Short collagen fibrils with variable, irregular diameter instead of the uniform thin fibrils seen in normal vitreous
  • Abnormal lamellar bundle organization — the gel structure is fundamentally disorganized
  • Premature vitreous liquefaction (syneresis) — the gel breaks down far earlier than it would in an unaffected person
  • Abnormal vitreoretinal adhesion — the vitreous attaches to the retina in abnormal, tenacious ways

The "Beaded" Vitreous Phenotype (Type 2)

COL11A1 mutations produce a distinctive vitreous appearance called the type 2 or "beaded" vitreous phenotype. On slit-lamp examination, the vitreous lamellae (sheets of vitreous structure) have an irregular, beaded appearance rather than the smooth membranes seen in COL2A1-related Stickler syndrome (type 1 or "membranous" vitreous).

Diagnostic significance: The vitreous phenotype is so consistent that it can help identify the genetic subtype. In a study of 31 affected members from 6 families with confirmed COL11A1 mutations, 100% exhibited the beaded type 2 vitreous phenotype. An experienced vitreoretinal specialist can often identify the vitreous type on slit-lamp exam before genetic testing results return.

Other vitreous findings that may be present:

  • Vitreous veils and membranes — sheet-like structures floating within the vitreous cavity
  • Vitreous condensation — areas of thickened, visible vitreous strands
  • Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) at an unusually young age — can occur in teens or young adults rather than the typical 60+ years

Retinal Detachment: The Greatest Threat

Retinal detachment is the most serious ocular complication in Marshall syndrome. The lifetime risk is approximately 65%, and detachments tend to occur between ages 10 and 30, though they can happen at any age, including childhood.

Why Is the Risk So High?

Multiple factors compound to create an extraordinarily high detachment risk:

Risk FactorWhat Happens
Abnormal vitreoretinal adhesionThe malformed vitreous adheres tenaciously to the peripheral retina. When the vitreous eventually separates (PVD), it pulls on the retina with abnormal force.
Giant retinal tears (GRT)Instead of small retinal holes, the tractional force can create massive tears spanning 90° or more of the retinal circumference. These are surgical emergencies.
Lattice degenerationRadial perivascular lattice degeneration — thin, weakened patches in the peripheral retina — is present in up to 100% of patients. These areas are predisposed to tears.
High myopiaElongated eyes stretch the retina thinner, making it more vulnerable to tearing.
Peripheral pigmentary changesCircumferential pigment deposition near the equator indicates areas of retinal degeneration.

Warning Signs — Know These by Heart

EMERGENCY: If any of these occur, seek immediate emergency eye care. Do not wait until morning. Do not wait for a scheduled appointment.
  • Sudden shower of new floaters — small specks, dots, cobwebs, or dark spots that move with your gaze, appearing suddenly and in large numbers
  • Flashes of light (photopsia) — lightning-streak flashes or flickering lights in the peripheral vision, especially noticeable in dark rooms
  • A shadow or curtain over part of your vision — this means the retina is already detaching and is a true emergency
  • Sudden decrease in vision — particularly if the central (macular) area is involved
  • A sensation of heaviness in the eye

What to Do if You Suspect Retinal Detachment

  1. Go to the emergency department immediately or call your vitreoretinal specialist’s emergency line. Time is critical — the longer the retina is detached, the greater the risk of permanent vision loss.
  2. Do not eat or drink (NPO) — surgery may be needed urgently, and an empty stomach is required for general anesthesia.
  3. Minimize eye movement — avoid looking around, reading, or using screens. Keep both eyes relatively still (moving one eye moves the other).
  4. Your specialist may advise specific head positioning depending on where the detachment is, to use gravity to keep the retina in place until surgery.
  5. Protect the eye — if there was any trauma, use a rigid eye shield (not a pressure patch). Do not rub or press on the eye.

Surgical Repair Options

ProcedureWhat It InvolvesNotes for Marshall/Stickler
Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV) The vitreous gel is removed and replaced with a gas bubble or silicone oil that presses the retina back into place. Laser or cryotherapy seals the tear. Often preferred in Stickler syndrome. The abnormal vitreous is the root cause, so removing it addresses the underlying problem.
Scleral buckle (SB) A silicone band is sutured around the outside of the eye, indenting the eye wall inward to relieve traction on the retina. Some studies show better visual acuity outcomes in eyes with optically empty vitreous. May be combined with PPV.
Combined PPV + scleral buckle Both procedures performed together. Often recommended for giant retinal tears and complex detachments, which are common in this population.
Pneumatic retinopexy A gas bubble is injected into the eye in an office setting, followed by laser or cryotherapy. Less commonly used in Stickler/Marshall due to the complexity of the detachments, but may be considered for simple, superior tears.

Prognosis After Repair

  • Anatomic success (retina reattached) is achievable in most cases, but may require multiple surgeries
  • Visual recovery depends heavily on whether the macula (central vision area) was detached and for how long
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR) — scar tissue forming on the retina — is a significant complication and the leading cause of surgical failure in this population
  • Re-detachment risk is high — recurrent RD has been reported in 46–63% of initial repairs in some Stickler cohorts
  • The fellow (other) eye is also at high risk — patients who have had a detachment in one eye have an 8.4-fold increased risk in the other eye compared to prophylactically treated eyes
Key takeaway: Prevention is far more effective than repair. Prophylactic treatment (see below) reduces the detachment rate from over 50% to under 10%. If your child or you have a confirmed COL11A1 mutation, discuss prophylaxis before a detachment occurs.

Prophylactic Treatment: The Cambridge Protocol

The most important advancement in Stickler/Marshall eye care is prophylactic retinal treatment — a preventive procedure that dramatically reduces the risk of retinal detachment before it happens. The evidence is strong enough that this should be discussed with every patient who has a confirmed diagnosis.

The Cambridge Prophylactic Cryotherapy Protocol

Developed by the Vitreoretinal Service at Addenbrooke’s Hospital (Cambridge, UK), this is the most extensively studied prophylactic protocol. Published in Ophthalmology (2014) with follow-up data in the New England Journal of Medicine (2022), it involves:

  1. 360-degree transconjunctival cryotherapy — a freezing probe is applied through the conjunctiva (the clear membrane covering the white of the eye) in a continuous ribbon around the entire circumference of the eye
  2. The treatment is applied at the junction between the post-oral retina and the pars plana (the area where retinal tears most commonly originate)
  3. This creates a strong adhesion (chorioretinal scar) that prevents the retina from lifting off, even if a tear develops
  4. The procedure is performed under general anesthesia

Published Outcomes

GroupRetinal Detachment RateRelative Risk
No prophylaxis (control)53.6%Reference
Bilateral prophylaxis8.3%7.4-fold reduction
Fellow eye after RD in first eye (no prophylaxis)High8.4-fold higher than treated eyes

The study included 778 patients with type 1 Stickler syndrome, making it one of the largest studies in any rare disease. The treatment effect is described as “large” and represents a meaningful reduction in the risk of blindness.

Alternative: 360-Degree Laser Retinopexy

Some centers use laser photocoagulation instead of cryotherapy to achieve the same goal. A study of 360-degree extended vitreous base laser prophylaxis showed:

  • 3% RD rate in laser-treated eyes vs. 73% in untreated eyes
  • Treated eyes had approximately 8 lines better visual acuity on average compared to untreated eyes

Both cryotherapy and laser appear effective. The choice between them may depend on the specialist’s experience and the patient’s age.

Timing and Practical Considerations

  • When: Ideally performed in childhood or early adolescence, before the age when retinal detachments become common (typically before age 10–15). Exact timing depends on the specialist’s judgment and the child’s vitreous status.
  • Anesthesia: General anesthesia is required for children. Some adults may tolerate the procedure under local anesthesia.
  • Recovery: Mild discomfort, redness, and swelling for several days. Anti-inflammatory eye drops are typically prescribed. Most patients return to normal activities within 1–2 weeks.
  • Risks: The procedure is considered safe with no significant long-term side effects reported in the Cambridge cohort. Temporary risks include mild inflammation and discomfort.
  • Bilateral treatment: Both eyes should be treated, as the risk is bilateral.
Important note on genetic subtype: The Cambridge protocol was developed and validated primarily in type 1 Stickler syndrome (COL2A1 mutations). While prophylactic treatment is also recommended for COL11A1-related disease (Marshall/Stickler type 2), the evidence base is smaller. Discuss this distinction with your vitreoretinal specialist — the principle of creating peripheral chorioretinal adhesion applies regardless of subtype.

High Myopia (Nearsightedness)

High myopia is present in approximately 80% of patients with COL11A1-related disease. It is typically detectable in the newborn period and results from megalophthalmos (abnormally large eyes) — the eye globe grows too long, causing axial myopia.

Characteristics of Myopia in Marshall/Stickler

FeatureDetails
TypePrimarily axial myopia (the eye is physically longer than normal), not refractive (the lens is not the main problem)
SeverityOften high (> −6 diopters), can be very high (> −10 diopters). Variable between individuals even within the same family.
ProgressionGenerally considered minimally progressive or nonprogressive — the high myopia is congenital/early-onset and largely stable, unlike typical childhood progressive myopia. Exception: may progress if childhood glaucoma is present.
Myopic maculopathyPresent in approximately 42–43% of patients. The odds of higher-category maculopathy increase by a factor of 2.15 per millimeter of axial elongation.

Correction Options and Safety Considerations

  • Spectacles: The safest option. Use impact-rated polycarbonate lenses (see Eye Protection below) — they correct vision and provide physical eye protection simultaneously.
  • Contact lenses: Generally safe for vision correction. However, patients must be vigilant about hygiene to avoid corneal infections, which could complicate an already complex eye situation. Scleral lenses may be an option for very high prescriptions and can provide better optical quality than spectacles.
  • Atropine for myopia control: Low-dose atropine (0.01–0.05%) has shown promise for slowing progressive myopia in the general population. However, since myopia in Marshall/Stickler is primarily structural (axial) and generally nonprogressive, the benefit may be limited. Discuss with your ophthalmologist — it is unlikely to cause harm but may not be as effective as in typical childhood myopia.
  • Orthokeratology (ortho-K): Overnight contact lens reshaping. Use with extreme caution in vitreous abnormality patients — the pressure changes and corneal reshaping have not been specifically studied in this population. Most specialists would advise against it.
  • Refractive surgery (LASIK/PRK): Generally not recommended in Stickler/Marshall patients. The underlying connective tissue disorder affects corneal biomechanics, and the procedure does not address the axial length or retinal risks. The risk-benefit calculation is unfavorable.

Cataracts

Cataracts are more common in COL11A1-related disease than in COL2A1-related Stickler syndrome, reported in approximately 59% of patients with COL11A1 mutations compared to 36% with COL2A1 mutations.

Characteristics

  • Classic pattern: Quadrantic lamellar (cortical) opacity — the cataract affects one quadrant or section of the lens cortex. This is a characteristic finding that can aid diagnosis.
  • May be congenital or juvenile: Unlike age-related cataracts that appear in the 60s–70s, these can be present at birth or develop in childhood/early adulthood.
  • May not involve the visual axis: Some cataracts are located peripherally and may not significantly affect vision initially, though they can progress.
  • Other types: Posterior subcapsular and nuclear cataracts can also occur, sometimes as a consequence of retinal surgery or chronic steroid use.

Surgery Considerations

Cataract surgery carries additional risk in Marshall/Stickler patients. The decision to proceed must carefully weigh the visual benefit against the increased risk of post-operative retinal detachment. This surgery should only be performed by a surgeon experienced with vitreoretinal pathology in connective tissue disorders.
  • Increased retinal detachment risk: Cataract surgery is a known risk factor for retinal detachment in the general population. In patients who already have a 65% baseline RD risk, this compounds the danger significantly.
  • Prophylactic retinal treatment first: Ideally, prophylactic cryotherapy or laser should be completed before cataract surgery to reduce the post-operative RD risk.
  • IOL (intraocular lens) selection: Standard posterior chamber IOL implantation is generally feasible. However, zonular weakness (the fibers that hold the lens in place) may be present due to the connective tissue disorder, potentially affecting lens stability. Long-term IOL dislocation has been reported after vitrectomy.
  • Combined procedures: Some surgeons may combine cataract surgery with vitrectomy in select cases to address both the cataract and the vitreous pathology simultaneously.
  • Close post-operative monitoring: More frequent follow-up than standard cataract surgery is essential, with vigilance for retinal detachment symptoms in the weeks and months following surgery.

Glaucoma

Glaucoma occurs in approximately 10% of patients with Stickler/Marshall syndrome. It results from anterior segment dysgenesis (developmental abnormality of the front part of the eye) and is typically open-angle glaucoma, though acute angle-closure has also been reported.

  • Can occur at any age, including childhood
  • If present in childhood, glaucoma can cause additional progressive myopia (on top of the congenital axial myopia)
  • Requires regular intraocular pressure (IOP) monitoring at every eye exam
  • Treatment follows standard glaucoma protocols (topical medications, laser, or surgery as needed)

Monitoring Schedule

Regular surveillance by a vitreoretinal specialist — not just a general ophthalmologist — is essential. The abnormal vitreous, peripheral retinal changes, and high RD risk require subspecialty expertise.

For children: Slit-lamp examination of infants and young children is technically difficult. Early ophthalmologic examination is critical, and follow-up is essential even when initial exams appear normal — findings develop and progress with age. A negative exam in infancy does not rule out future problems. Exam under anesthesia (EUA) may be needed for young children who cannot cooperate with a standard exam.

Practical Eye Protection

Because the retina is structurally vulnerable, physical eye protection is a lifelong consideration. Even minor trauma that would be inconsequential to a normal eye can precipitate a retinal detachment in someone with COL11A1 mutations.

Everyday Protective Eyewear

  • Polycarbonate lenses are essential — not just recommended. Polycarbonate is impact-rated and 10x more impact-resistant than standard plastic lenses. All prescription eyeglasses should use polycarbonate or Trivex lenses.
  • UV protection: UV-blocking coatings on all eyewear. UV exposure contributes to cataract progression and may affect the compromised vitreous. Sunglasses should block 99–100% of UVA and UVB.
  • Sports goggles: ASTM F803-rated sports goggles for any permitted athletic activity. Regular glasses are not sufficient protection for sports.

Activities: What to Avoid

Risk LevelActivitiesGuidance
Avoid completely Contact sports (boxing, martial arts, wrestling, rugby), bungee jumping, skydiving, roller coasters with sudden deceleration, heading the ball in soccer Risk of direct eye/head impact or extreme G-forces that can trigger retinal detachment
High caution Basketball, baseball, tennis, racquetball, squash, paintball, hockey If permitted by specialist, only with ASTM F803-rated eye protection. Discuss individually with vitreoretinal specialist.

For school-age children: Work with the school to ensure appropriate accommodations in physical education class. A 504 plan or IEP should include restrictions on contact sports and requirement for protective eyewear. See also Multi-Organ Protection for how eye protection fits into a broader safety strategy.

Questions for Your Vitreoretinal Specialist

Bring these questions to your appointment. Not all providers will be familiar with the specifics of COL11A1-related eye disease:

  1. “What is my/my child’s vitreous phenotype, and what does it tell us about the retinal detachment risk profile?” — Understanding the specific vitreous type (beaded vs. membranous) helps assess risk and confirms the genetic subtype.
  2. “Should we pursue prophylactic cryotherapy or laser retinopexy, and if so, when?” — This is the single most important conversation to have. If your specialist is not familiar with the Cambridge protocol, bring published references.
  3. “What specific peripheral retinal findings are present (lattice degeneration, retinal thinning, pigmentary changes), and how do they affect the risk assessment?” — Get a detailed map of what the retinal periphery looks like.
  4. “What is the plan if a retinal detachment occurs — who performs the surgery, and what approach would be used?” — Have an emergency plan in place before it is needed. Know who to call and where to go.
  5. “Are there signs of myopic maculopathy, and is OCT monitoring warranted?” — Myopic maculopathy can cause gradual central vision loss independently of retinal detachment.
  6. “If cataract surgery becomes necessary in the future, how would you approach it given the vitreous abnormality and retinal risk?” — Planning ahead ensures the right surgeon and technique when the time comes.
