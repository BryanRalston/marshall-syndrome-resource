Eye Management: Vitreous, Retina, Myopia & Cataracts

The eyes are one of the most affected organ systems in Marshall syndrome. With a 65% lifetime risk of retinal detachment — higher than any other known predisposing condition — proactive monitoring and prophylactic treatment can be sight-saving. Every person with a confirmed COL11A1 mutation needs a vitreoretinal specialist, not just a general ophthalmologist.

Vitreous Degeneration: The Core Problem

The vitreous humor — the clear gel that fills the eye — is made primarily of water, hyaluronic acid, and collagen fibrils. Type XI collagen, produced by the COL11A1 gene, plays a critical structural role: it regulates the diameter and spacing of the larger type II collagen fibrils that form the vitreous scaffold. Think of type XI collagen as the rebar spacing in concrete — without it, the structural fibers form incorrectly.

In Marshall syndrome and Stickler syndrome type II (both caused by COL11A1 mutations), insufficient type XI collagen leads to:

  • Short collagen fibrils with variable, irregular diameter instead of the uniform thin fibrils seen in normal vitreous
  • Abnormal lamellar bundle organization — the gel structure is fundamentally disorganized
  • Premature vitreous liquefaction (syneresis) — the gel breaks down far earlier than it would in an unaffected person
  • Abnormal vitreoretinal adhesion — the vitreous attaches to the retina in abnormal, tenacious ways

The "Beaded" Vitreous Phenotype (Type 2)

COL11A1 mutations produce a distinctive vitreous appearance called the type 2 or "beaded" vitreous phenotype. On slit-lamp examination, the vitreous lamellae (sheets of vitreous structure) have an irregular, beaded appearance rather than the smooth membranes seen in COL2A1-related Stickler syndrome (type 1 or "membranous" vitreous).

Diagnostic significance: The vitreous phenotype is so consistent that it can help identify the genetic subtype. In a study of 31 affected members from 6 families with confirmed COL11A1 mutations, 100% exhibited the beaded type 2 vitreous phenotype. An experienced vitreoretinal specialist can often identify the vitreous type on slit-lamp exam before genetic testing results return.
Vitreous Phenotype — Type 2 Beaded Anomaly in COL11A1

COL11A1 mutations produce the Type 2 (beaded) vitreous phenotype, which is structurally and clinically distinct from the Type 1 (membranous) vitreous caused by COL2A1 mutations in Stickler syndrome type 1.

Mechanism: Collagen XI normally regulates fibril diameter by acting as a molecular spacer on the surface of type II collagen fibrils. When COL11A1 is mutated, this diameter control is lost, causing irregular, thickened lamellae that resemble beads on a string rather than the smooth, sheet-like membranes seen in COL2A1-related disease. The beaded appearance results from abnormal fibril aggregation into clumped bundles with variable diameter.

Clinical significance:

  • The beaded phenotype is potentially diagnostic on slit-lamp examination — an experienced vitreoretinal specialist can often distinguish Type 2 from Type 1 vitreous before genetic testing confirms the mutation
  • Type 2 (beaded) vitreous is associated with different retinal detachment patterns than Type 1 (membranous) — the abnormal vitreoretinal adhesion patterns and traction forces differ based on the vitreous architecture
  • Prophylactic laser or cryotherapy may need adjustment based on the vitreous type, since the tractional forces and peripheral retinal pathology can differ between Type 1 and Type 2

Important exception: Some COL11A1 families have been reported with membranous rather than beaded vitreous, indicating that the phenotype alone cannot replace genetic testing. Vitreous classification is a valuable clinical clue but not a definitive diagnostic test.

Action: If your ophthalmologist is unfamiliar with the Type 2 vitreous classification, educate them about the distinction. Many general ophthalmologists are aware of the membranous vitreous in COL2A1 Stickler but may not know that COL11A1 produces a different, beaded pattern with its own clinical implications.

Other vitreous findings that may be present:

  • Vitreous veils and membranes — sheet-like structures floating within the vitreous cavity
  • Vitreous condensation — areas of thickened, visible vitreous strands
  • Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) at an unusually young age — can occur in teens or young adults rather than the typical 60+ years

Vitreous Humor Pathology: A Congenital Structural Defect

It is important to understand that the vitreous abnormality in Marshall syndrome is fundamentally different from age-related vitreous degeneration. In typical aging, the vitreous gel slowly liquefies over decades, with posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) usually occurring after age 60. In COL11A1 disorders, the vitreous is structurally abnormal from birth.

The Collagen V/XI Hybrid in Normal Vitreous

Normal vitreous humor is a highly organized gel composed of three key structural components:

  • Collagen type II — the primary structural fibril forming the vitreous scaffold
  • Collagen V/XI hybrid — a heterotypic collagen that acts as a molecular spacer on the surface of type II fibrils, regulating their diameter and spacing. This hybrid is a critical structural component of the vitreous cortex (the outer shell of the vitreous that interfaces with the retina).
  • Collagen type IX — a surface-associated collagen that cross-links fibrils and connects them to the hyaluronic acid matrix

These collagen fibrils are suspended in a hyaluronic acid gel that maintains the vitreous volume and transparency. The entire structure depends on precise collagen fibril architecture — and that architecture depends on functional collagen V/XI.

When COL11A1 is mutated, the collagen V/XI hybrid is defective from the moment the eye develops in utero. The consequences are present at birth:

  • Type 1 membranous vitreous (seen primarily in COL2A1 mutations): smooth, sheet-like membranes visible on slit-lamp examination
  • Type 2 beaded vitreous (characteristic of COL11A1 mutations including Marshall syndrome): irregular, clumped lamellae with a beaded appearance due to loss of fibril diameter control
  • Both patterns are congenital — they are not the result of degeneration over time but of abnormal development from the start
Why this distinction matters clinically: Because the vitreous is structurally abnormal from birth, it creates abnormal tractional forces on the retina throughout life. When posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) eventually occurs — often decades earlier than in unaffected individuals — the abnormally adherent, malformed vitreous exerts traction that can tear the retina. This is not the gentle, gradual separation seen in age-related PVD. It is a mechanical event driven by lifelong structural pathology pulling on a retina that was never meant to withstand these forces.

Slit-lamp diagnosis: An experienced vitreoretinal specialist can identify the characteristic vitreous patterns on slit-lamp biomicroscopy. The beaded (type 2) vitreous phenotype associated with COL11A1 mutations is sufficiently distinctive that it can suggest the genetic subtype before molecular testing confirms it. This makes the slit-lamp exam a valuable early diagnostic tool — particularly in families where a child presents with high myopia and the genetic cause has not yet been identified.

Retinal Detachment: The Greatest Threat

Retinal detachment is the most serious ocular complication in Marshall syndrome. The lifetime risk is approximately 65%, and detachments tend to occur between ages 10 and 30, though they can happen at any age, including childhood.

Why Is the Risk So High?

Multiple factors compound to create an extraordinarily high detachment risk:

Risk FactorWhat Happens
Abnormal vitreoretinal adhesionThe malformed vitreous adheres tenaciously to the peripheral retina. When the vitreous eventually separates (PVD), it pulls on the retina with abnormal force.
Giant retinal tears (GRT)Instead of small retinal holes, the tractional force can create massive tears spanning 90° or more of the retinal circumference. These are surgical emergencies.
Lattice degenerationRadial perivascular lattice degeneration — thin, weakened patches in the peripheral retina — is present in up to 100% of patients. These areas are predisposed to tears.
High myopiaElongated eyes stretch the retina thinner, making it more vulnerable to tearing.
Peripheral pigmentary changesCircumferential pigment deposition near the equator indicates areas of retinal degeneration.

Warning Signs — Know These by Heart

EMERGENCY: If any of these occur, seek immediate emergency eye care. Do not wait until morning. Do not wait for a scheduled appointment.
  • Sudden shower of new floaters — small specks, dots, cobwebs, or dark spots that move with your gaze, appearing suddenly and in large numbers
  • Flashes of light (photopsia) — lightning-streak flashes or flickering lights in the peripheral vision, especially noticeable in dark rooms
  • A shadow or curtain over part of your vision — this means the retina is already detaching and is a true emergency
  • Sudden decrease in vision — particularly if the central (macular) area is involved
  • A sensation of heaviness in the eye

What to Do if You Suspect Retinal Detachment

  1. Go to the emergency department immediately or call your vitreoretinal specialist’s emergency line. Time is critical — the longer the retina is detached, the greater the risk of permanent vision loss.
  2. Do not eat or drink (NPO) — surgery may be needed urgently, and an empty stomach is required for general anesthesia.
  3. Minimize eye movement — avoid looking around, reading, or using screens. Keep both eyes relatively still (moving one eye moves the other).
  4. Your specialist may advise specific head positioning depending on where the detachment is, to use gravity to keep the retina in place until surgery.
  5. Protect the eye — if there was any trauma, use a rigid eye shield (not a pressure patch). Do not rub or press on the eye.

Surgical Repair Options

ProcedureWhat It InvolvesNotes for Marshall/Stickler
Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV) The vitreous gel is removed and replaced with a gas bubble or silicone oil that presses the retina back into place. Laser or cryotherapy seals the tear. Often preferred in Stickler syndrome. The abnormal vitreous is the root cause, so removing it addresses the underlying problem.
Scleral buckle (SB) A silicone band is sutured around the outside of the eye, indenting the eye wall inward to relieve traction on the retina. Some studies show better visual acuity outcomes in eyes with optically empty vitreous. May be combined with PPV.
Combined PPV + scleral buckle Both procedures performed together. Often recommended for giant retinal tears and complex detachments, which are common in this population.
Pneumatic retinopexy A gas bubble is injected into the eye in an office setting, followed by laser or cryotherapy. Less commonly used in Stickler/Marshall due to the complexity of the detachments, but may be considered for simple, superior tears.

Prognosis After Repair

  • Anatomic success (retina reattached) is achievable in most cases, but may require multiple surgeries
  • Visual recovery depends heavily on whether the macula (central vision area) was detached and for how long
  • Proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR) — scar tissue forming on the retina — is a significant complication and the leading cause of surgical failure in this population
  • Re-detachment risk is high — recurrent RD has been reported in 46–63% of initial repairs in some Stickler cohorts
  • The fellow (other) eye is also at high risk — patients who have had a detachment in one eye have an 8.4-fold increased risk in the other eye compared to prophylactically treated eyes
Key takeaway: Prevention is far more effective than repair. Prophylactic treatment (see below) reduces the detachment rate from over 50% to under 10%. If your child or you have a confirmed COL11A1 mutation, discuss prophylaxis before a detachment occurs.

Prophylactic Treatment: The Cambridge Protocol

The most important advancement in Stickler/Marshall eye care is prophylactic retinal treatment — a preventive procedure that dramatically reduces the risk of retinal detachment before it happens. The evidence is strong enough that this should be discussed with every patient who has a confirmed diagnosis.

The Cambridge Prophylactic Cryotherapy Protocol

Developed by the Vitreoretinal Service at Addenbrooke’s Hospital (Cambridge, UK), this is the most extensively studied prophylactic protocol. Published in Ophthalmology (2014) with follow-up data in the New England Journal of Medicine (2022), it involves:

  1. 360-degree transconjunctival cryotherapy — a freezing probe is applied through the conjunctiva (the clear membrane covering the white of the eye) in a continuous ribbon around the entire circumference of the eye
  2. The treatment is applied at the junction between the post-oral retina and the pars plana (the area where retinal tears most commonly originate)
  3. This creates a strong adhesion (chorioretinal scar) that prevents the retina from lifting off, even if a tear develops
  4. The procedure is performed under general anesthesia

The study included 778 patients with type 1 Stickler syndrome, making it one of the largest studies in any rare disease. The treatment effect is described as “large” and represents a meaningful reduction in the risk of blindness.

Alternative: 360-Degree Laser Retinopexy

Some centers use laser photocoagulation instead of cryotherapy to achieve the same goal. A study of 360-degree extended vitreous base laser prophylaxis showed:

  • 3% RD rate in laser-treated eyes vs. 73% in untreated eyes
  • Treated eyes had approximately 8 lines better visual acuity on average compared to untreated eyes

Both cryotherapy and laser appear effective. The choice between them may depend on the specialist’s experience and the patient’s age.

2025 Meta-Analysis Confirmation: A systematic review and meta-analysis published in Acta Ophthalmologica (2025) found a retinal detachment rate of 6.6% in laser-prophylaxis eyes vs. 36.0% in untreated eyes over a mean follow-up of 6.3 years. This pooled analysis across multiple studies provides the strongest evidence to date that prophylactic treatment is effective and should be considered standard of care.

Timing and Practical Considerations

  • When: Ideally performed in childhood or early adolescence, before the age when retinal detachments become common (typically before age 10–15). Exact timing depends on the specialist’s judgment and the child’s vitreous status.
  • Anesthesia: General anesthesia is required for children. Some adults may tolerate the procedure under local anesthesia.
  • Recovery: Mild discomfort, redness, and swelling for several days. Anti-inflammatory eye drops are typically prescribed. Most patients return to normal activities within 1–2 weeks.
  • Risks: The procedure is considered safe with no significant long-term side effects reported in the Cambridge cohort. Temporary risks include mild inflammation and discomfort.
  • Bilateral treatment: Both eyes should be treated, as the risk is bilateral.

Prophylactic Treatment Evidence: Quantifying the Benefit

The evidence for prophylactic retinal treatment in Stickler/Marshall syndrome has grown substantially, with multiple study designs now converging on the same conclusion: prophylaxis dramatically reduces retinal detachment rates.

2025 Meta-Analysis: Pooled Evidence

A systematic review and meta-analysis published in Acta Ophthalmologica (2025) pooled data across multiple prophylactic treatment studies and found:

Laser prophylaxis6.6%0.23 (77% risk reduction)

The mean follow-up was 6.3 years. A relative risk of 0.23 means that prophylactically treated eyes had less than one-quarter the detachment risk of untreated eyes. This is among the largest treatment effects seen in preventive ophthalmology for any condition.

Research Update (R467): Retinal Prophylaxis — Updated Numbers

The 2025 meta-analysis data deserves emphasis because the raw numbers are so compelling: laser prophylaxis reduces the retinal detachment rate from 36% (untreated) to 6.6% (treated). To put this in perspective:

  • Without prophylaxis, more than 1 in 3 eyes will detach
  • With prophylaxis, fewer than 1 in 15 eyes will detach
  • This translates to a number needed to treat (NNT) of approximately 3.4 — meaning that for every 3–4 eyes treated prophylactically, one retinal detachment is prevented. NNTs below 10 are considered highly effective interventions; an NNT of 3.4 places retinal prophylaxis among the most impactful preventive procedures in medicine
  • The consistency across studies is notable: the Cambridge cryotherapy cohort, the Curran laser cohort, and the 2025 meta-analysis all converge on the same magnitude of benefit despite different techniques, different centers, and different time periods

If a single number from this entire page should drive clinical action, it is this one: 6.6% vs. 36%.

Cambridge Cryotherapy Protocol: Long-Term Outcomes

The Cambridge cohort, with the longest follow-up of any prophylaxis study, showed:

  • 6.9% retinal detachment rate in prophylactically treated eyes
  • 47.4% retinal detachment rate in historical untreated controls
  • This represents a nearly 7-fold reduction in the most devastating complication of the disease

Timing: When Should Prophylaxis Be Performed?

Current consensus: Prophylactic retinal treatment should be performed at the time of diagnosis or by age 10 for type 1 Stickler syndrome. For COL11A1-related disease (Marshall/Stickler type 2), the same general principle applies, though the optimal timing may be individualized based on the vitreous status and peripheral retinal findings. The key point is that prophylaxis must occur before retinal detachment — once a detachment has happened, the window for prevention in that eye has closed.
  • Retinal detachments in Stickler/Marshall typically peak between ages 10 and 30, so early treatment provides the greatest protection during the highest-risk years
  • For newly diagnosed children, prophylaxis should be discussed at the first vitreoretinal appointment
  • For adults diagnosed later in life who have not yet had a detachment, prophylaxis remains beneficial — the risk does not disappear with age

Laser vs. Cryotherapy: The Debate

Both approaches are effective. The choice between laser and cryotherapy depends on the specialist’s experience, the patient’s age and cooperation level, and whether general anesthesia is acceptable. Some centers use a combined approach. The important decision is whether to treat, not which modality to use — either is vastly superior to no treatment.

Prophylactic Laser Retinopexy: Stickler-Specific Data (Curran 2024)

A 2024 study by Curran and colleagues provided the most detailed analysis of prophylactic laser retinopexy outcomes in Stickler syndrome to date, with results that reinforce the case for early intervention:

OutcomeWithout ProphylaxisWith Laser ProphylaxisRisk Reduction
Retinal detachment rate 26.7% 4.6% 83% relative risk reduction

These results are consistent with — and in some measures stronger than — the Cambridge cryotherapy data and the 2025 meta-analysis, providing additional evidence from a different cohort and treatment modality.

Encircling Laser Technique: How It Works

The encircling (360-degree) laser retinopexy technique creates a continuous barrier of chorioretinal adhesion around the entire retinal periphery. The mechanism and rationale are as follows:

  • Adhesion strengthening: Laser photocoagulation induces a controlled inflammatory response between the neurosensory retina and the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). The resulting scar creates a chorioretinal adhesion that is 3–5 times stronger than the normal physiological attachment. This barrier resists the tractional forces created when the abnormal vitreous eventually separates from the retina.
  • 360-degree coverage: Because retinal tears in Stickler/Marshall can occur at any clock position around the retinal periphery (unlike typical rhegmatogenous detachments which favor the superior quadrants), the treatment must encircle the entire retina. Spot treatment of visible lattice degeneration alone is insufficient — tears frequently occur in areas that appeared normal on preoperative examination.
  • Multiple rows: The laser is typically applied in 2–3 confluent rows spanning the vitreous base region (the zone where the vitreous attaches most tenaciously to the retina). This creates a broad adhesion band rather than a single line, providing a wider margin of protection.
  • Extended vitreous base treatment: Some protocols extend the laser posterior to the equator when lattice degeneration or other peripheral pathology extends beyond the standard treatment zone. The goal is to create the adhesion barrier peripheral to (outside of) all areas at risk for tearing.

AAO 2025 Preferred Practice Pattern (PPP) on Prophylactic Treatment

American Academy of Ophthalmology (AAO) 2025 Preferred Practice Pattern (R422): The AAO’s 2025 Preferred Practice Pattern for retinal detachment now explicitly endorses 360-degree prophylactic laser retinopexy for patients with Stickler syndrome. This is a significant upgrade from prior guidance, which acknowledged the evidence but stopped short of a formal recommendation. Key points from the 2025 PPP:
  • 360-degree coverage is the endorsed standard — the PPP specifically recommends full-circumference treatment rather than focal (spot) laser of individual lattice lesions. This aligns with the mechanistic rationale that retinal tears in Stickler/Marshall can occur at any clock position, not just at sites of visible pathology.
  • Lifetime retinal detachment risk cited at 78% without prophylaxis — the PPP references the high end of published estimates, reinforcing the urgency of intervention. This figure reflects bilateral lifetime risk in untreated type 1 Stickler; COL11A1-related disease (Marshall/Stickler type 2) carries a similarly elevated risk.
  • Early prophylaxis preferred: The PPP notes that prophylactic treatment should be performed before the vitreous degenerates and separates, as the protective mechanism depends on creating chorioretinal adhesion before tractional forces produce tears. This supports treatment in childhood or early adolescence rather than waiting for signs of vitreous degeneration.
  • Both eyes should be treated: Consistent with the Cambridge protocol and the 2024 Curran data, the PPP supports bilateral prophylaxis as the standard approach.

The 2025 PPP endorsement represents the highest level of professional society support that prophylactic retinal treatment has received to date. Families whose vitreoretinal specialists are unfamiliar with the recommendation can reference the 2025 AAO Preferred Practice Pattern directly.

Marshall-Specific Protocol Considerations

While the prophylaxis evidence base comes primarily from Stickler syndrome type 1 (COL2A1) cohorts, there are specific considerations for COL11A1-related disease (Marshall/Stickler type 2):

  • Timing: Prophylactic treatment should be discussed at the first vitreoretinal appointment after diagnosis. The ideal window is before age 10–15, as retinal detachments in Marshall/Stickler typically begin in the second decade. Earlier treatment is preferable — waiting until vitreous changes are advanced reduces the protective benefit.
  • Vitreous phenotype matters: The Type 2 (beaded) vitreous characteristic of COL11A1 mutations may create different tractional patterns than the Type 1 (membranous) vitreous of COL2A1 disease. The laser treatment should be guided by careful assessment of the vitreoretinal interface in each individual eye, not applied as a one-size-fits-all template.
  • Bilateral treatment without exception: Both eyes should be treated. Marshall syndrome retinal detachment risk is bilateral. Single-eye treatment leaves the untreated eye at the full baseline risk of 65%.
  • Follow-up after prophylaxis: Prophylactic treatment reduces but does not eliminate retinal detachment risk. Annual dilated fundus examination by a vitreoretinal specialist remains essential even after successful prophylaxis. New areas of lattice degeneration or retinal thinning may develop posterior to the treatment zone and require additional laser supplementation.
  • Repeat or supplementary treatment: Some patients may require additional laser rows as they age, particularly if new peripheral retinal pathology develops or if the initial treatment is judged to have been insufficiently extensive. This is a normal part of long-term management, not a failure of the original treatment.
The single most important action item on this page: If you or your child has a confirmed COL11A1 mutation and has not yet had prophylactic retinal treatment, bring this topic to your next vitreoretinal appointment. The difference between 26.7% retinal detachment rate without prophylaxis and 4.6% with prophylaxis (Curran 2024) — or 36% vs. 6.6% (2025 meta-analysis) — represents one of the largest preventive treatment effects in all of ophthalmology. There is no comparable intervention that provides this degree of protection for any other complication of Marshall syndrome.

Fellow Eye Treatment: Strongly Recommended

If one eye has already had a retinal detachment, the other (fellow) eye must be prophylactically treated without delay. The lifetime risk of bilateral retinal detachment in Stickler/Marshall syndrome is estimated at 70–80%. Data from the Cambridge cohort shows that fellow eyes without prophylaxis had an 8.4-fold higher retinal detachment rate compared to prophylactically treated eyes. Waiting for the second eye to detach before acting is not acceptable — the best visual outcome is always prevention.
  • Bilateral prophylaxis (treating both eyes) is recommended for all patients at diagnosis, not just those who have already had a detachment
  • If a patient presents with a unilateral detachment and no prior prophylaxis, the fellow eye should be treated urgently — ideally within days to weeks, not months
  • Even in patients who received prophylaxis years ago, the fellow eye should be re-examined carefully after a detachment in the first eye, as prophylactic treatment can thin or weaken over time in some cases
Important note on genetic subtype: The Cambridge protocol was developed and validated primarily in type 1 Stickler syndrome (COL2A1 mutations). While prophylactic treatment is also recommended for COL11A1-related disease (Marshall/Stickler type 2), the evidence base is smaller. Discuss this distinction with your vitreoretinal specialist — the principle of creating peripheral chorioretinal adhesion applies regardless of subtype.

High Myopia (Nearsightedness)

High myopia is present in approximately 80% of patients with COL11A1-related disease. It is typically detectable in the newborn period and results from megalophthalmos (abnormally large eyes) — the eye globe grows too long, causing axial myopia.

Characteristics of Myopia in Marshall/Stickler

FeatureDetails
TypePrimarily axial myopia (the eye is physically longer than normal), not refractive (the lens is not the main problem)
SeverityOften high (> −6 diopters), can be very high (> −10 diopters). Variable between individuals even within the same family.
ProgressionGenerally considered minimally progressive or nonprogressive — the high myopia is congenital/early-onset and largely stable, unlike typical childhood progressive myopia. Exception: may progress if childhood glaucoma is present.
Myopic maculopathyPresent in approximately 42–43% of patients. The odds of higher-category maculopathy increase by a factor of 2.15 per millimeter of axial elongation.

Correction Options and Safety Considerations

  • Spectacles: The safest option. Use impact-rated polycarbonate lenses (see Eye Protection below) — they correct vision and provide physical eye protection simultaneously.
  • Contact lenses: Generally safe for vision correction. However, patients must be vigilant about hygiene to avoid corneal infections, which could complicate an already complex eye situation. Scleral lenses may be an option for very high prescriptions and can provide better optical quality than spectacles.
  • Atropine for myopia control: Low-dose atropine (0.01–0.05%) has shown promise for slowing progressive myopia in the general population. However, since myopia in Marshall/Stickler is primarily structural (axial) and generally nonprogressive, the benefit may be limited. Discuss with your ophthalmologist — it is unlikely to cause harm but may not be as effective as in typical childhood myopia.
  • Orthokeratology (ortho-K): Overnight contact lens reshaping. Use with extreme caution in vitreous abnormality patients — the pressure changes and corneal reshaping have not been specifically studied in this population. Most specialists would advise against it.
  • Refractive surgery (LASIK/PRK): Generally not recommended in Stickler/Marshall patients. The underlying connective tissue disorder affects corneal biomechanics, and the procedure does not address the axial length or retinal risks. The risk-benefit calculation is unfavorable.
Myopia Management Options (2025 IMI Review)

For children with progressive myopia, several evidence-based interventions can slow axial elongation. While myopia in Marshall/Stickler is primarily congenital and generally nonprogressive, some children do show progression, and these options should be discussed with the ophthalmologist:

  • Low-dose atropine: 30–60% reduction in myopia progression. A 2025 review found that 0.04% concentration is superior to the previously popular 0.01% dose, with minimal side effects (slight pupil dilation, near-vision blur).
  • Orthokeratology (Ortho-K): Overnight corneal-reshaping contact lenses reduce axial elongation by −0.18mm at 1 year and −0.30mm at 2 years (2025 Cochrane review). However, see the safety warning below regarding contact lens use in this population.
  • MiSight contact lenses: FDA-approved daily disposable soft contact lenses with concentric ring design. Published data shows a 59% reduction in myopia progression over 3 years compared to standard soft lenses.
  • Essilor Stellest spectacle lenses: In 2025, the FDA authorized Stellest lenses (H.A.L.T. technology — highly aspherical lenslet target) for myopia control. These spectacle lenses slow axial elongation without contact lens risks.

CRITICAL for Stickler/Marshall: Contact lens insertion and removal involve mechanical manipulation of the eye. In a population with a 65% lifetime retinal detachment risk, contact lenses may theoretically increase RD risk through repeated minor trauma during insertion and removal, particularly Ortho-K lenses which are rigid gas permeable. Discuss any contact lens use with your vitreoretinal specialist, not just the prescribing optometrist. Spectacle-based options (Stellest, DIMS lenses) may be the safest myopia control approach for this population.

2025 Stickler study finding: A recent study of myopia progression in Stickler syndrome found surprisingly minimal progression rates of only −0.08 to −0.12 diopters per year, suggesting earlier stabilization than typical childhood myopia. This means aggressive myopia control interventions may be less necessary in many Stickler/Marshall patients — but individual monitoring remains essential, as some children do progress.

Myopia Control Strategies for Marshall Syndrome

While high myopia in Marshall/Stickler is predominantly congenital and often nonprogressive, a subset of children does show measurable progression. A 2025 review by the International Myopia Institute (IMI) comprehensively evaluated myopia control interventions, providing an evidence base for choosing the safest and most effective approaches in this population.

2025 IMI Interventions Review: Myopia Control Modalities

The IMI review evaluates all current myopia control interventions by efficacy and safety. For Marshall syndrome, the key consideration is that any intervention must be weighed against the unique ocular risks in this population — particularly the fragile sclera, abnormal vitreous, and high retinal detachment risk.

Low-Dose Atropine (0.01–0.05%): Likely Safest First-Line for Marshall

  • Slows axial elongation by 30–60% depending on concentration; the 2025 review found 0.04–0.05% concentrations are more effective than the previously popular 0.01% dose
  • Mechanism is pharmacological (likely acts on scleral fibroblasts and choroidal thickening) rather than mechanical — no physical manipulation of the eye is required
  • Side effects are mild at low doses: slight pupil dilation, minor near-vision blur, photosensitivity. These are generally well tolerated in children
  • Does not alter corneal shape, does not require contact lens insertion/removal, and does not apply mechanical pressure to an eye with compromised connective tissue
  • For Marshall syndrome, low-dose atropine is likely the safest pharmacological first-line intervention because it avoids the mechanical risks associated with contact-lens-based approaches

Orthokeratology (Ortho-K): Caution Warranted

  • Ortho-K uses rigid gas permeable contact lenses worn overnight to reshape the cornea, creating a temporary myopic defocus that slows axial elongation
  • Effective: 2025 Cochrane review shows −0.18mm reduction in axial elongation at 1 year, −0.30mm at 2 years
  • May be contraindicated in Marshall syndrome: The sclera and cornea contain type XI collagen, and COL11A1 mutations produce thinner, potentially irregular scleral and corneal tissue. Overnight rigid lens wear applies sustained mechanical pressure to these compromised structures
  • Additional concerns: rigid lens insertion and removal involves mechanical manipulation of the eye, which in a population with 65% lifetime retinal detachment risk may theoretically increase RD risk through repeated minor trauma
  • Discuss with the vitreoretinal specialist, not just the prescribing optometrist, before considering Ortho-K

Combination Therapy: Ortho-K + Atropine

  • The 2025 IMI review found that combining Ortho-K with low-dose atropine provides approximately 28% additional reduction in axial elongation at 2 years compared to Ortho-K alone
  • This combination represents the most effective myopia control approach currently available
  • However, for Marshall syndrome, the Ortho-K component carries the same mechanical risks noted above — this combination would only be appropriate if the vitreoretinal specialist has assessed and accepted the Ortho-K risk for the individual patient

Defocus Spectacle Lenses (DIMS/HAL): Non-Invasive Option

  • DIMS (Defocus Incorporated Multiple Segments) and HAL (Highly Aspherical Lenslet) spectacle lenses create peripheral myopic defocus while correcting central vision
  • FDA-authorized Stellest lenses (HAL technology by Essilor) slow axial elongation by approximately 0.4mm over 3 years compared to standard lenses
  • For Marshall syndrome, defocus spectacle lenses may be the ideal myopia control approach — they provide myopia control without any contact with the eye, without mechanical manipulation, and without pharmacological side effects
  • Can be combined with polycarbonate or Trivex lens material for impact protection, maintaining the essential eye safety requirement
  • Requires consistent full-time wear to be effective — compliance is generally better than contact lenses in children

Environmental and Behavioral Interventions

  • Outdoor time: aim for 90+ minutes per day. Bright outdoor light exposure has a dose-dependent protective effect against myopia progression. The mechanism involves dopamine release in the retina, which inhibits axial elongation
  • 20-20-20 rule for close work: Every 20 minutes of near work, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This relaxes accommodation and reduces the sustained near-vision stimulus that promotes axial elongation
  • Working distance: Maintain at least 30cm (12 inches) between eyes and reading material or screens. Closer distances increase accommodative demand and may accelerate progression
  • These behavioral interventions have no risk, no cost, and are complementary to any other myopia control strategy

Monitoring: Axial Length, Not Just Refraction

  • Monitor axial length, not just refraction, to assess myopia progression and control effectiveness. Refraction can be influenced by lens changes (cataracts, accommodation), but axial length directly measures the structural elongation that drives pathological myopia
  • Optical biometry (IOLMaster, Lenstar) provides non-contact, highly precise axial length measurements
  • Track axial length at each visit and compare against age-matched growth curves to identify abnormal elongation early
  • In Marshall syndrome, axial length monitoring also serves as an indirect indicator of scleral integrity — rapid axial elongation may suggest more significant scleral collagen compromise

Myopia Progression Control: 2025 Evidence Update

While high myopia in Marshall/Stickler is predominantly congenital, emerging evidence from 2025 has refined the treatment landscape for children who do show measurable progression. Three key developments warrant attention:

Low-Dose Atropine: EU Approval and Updated Dosing

  • 2025 milestone: Atropine 0.01% (Qlosi/NVK-002) received European Medicines Agency (EMA) approval in 2025 for myopia progression control in children — the first regulatory approval of a pharmacological myopia control agent in Europe.
  • Dosing update: The 2025 evidence now favors 0.04–0.05% concentrations as more effective than the initially popular 0.01% dose, with only marginally increased side effects (slight pupil dilation, minor near-vision blur).
  • Marshall-specific photophobia concern: Atropine causes pupil dilation, which increases light sensitivity. Many individuals with Marshall syndrome already experience photophobia due to vitreous abnormalities, high myopia, and retinal pathology. At 0.01%, photophobia is typically minimal. At higher concentrations (0.04–0.05%), photophobia may be clinically significant — particularly in children who spend time outdoors (which is itself beneficial for myopia control). Photochromic lenses or tinted prescription lenses may be needed if atropine-induced photophobia compounds existing light sensitivity.
  • Safety advantage for Marshall syndrome: Low-dose atropine acts pharmacologically on scleral fibroblasts and choroidal thickening — it does not involve mechanical manipulation of the eye, making it the safest first-line myopia control option in a population with high retinal detachment risk.

Orthokeratology: Axial Elongation Data and Marshall-Specific Cautions

  • 2025 Cochrane review data: Ortho-K demonstrates a reduction in axial elongation of −0.18 mm at 1 year and −0.30 mm at 2 years compared to standard correction — clinically meaningful effects.
  • Combination therapy superiority: The 2025 IMI review found that combining Ortho-K with low-dose atropine provides approximately 28% additional reduction in axial elongation at 2 years compared to Ortho-K alone. This combination represents the most effective myopia control approach currently available in the general population.
  • Marshall syndrome caveat: Despite the efficacy data, Ortho-K carries significant concerns in COL11A1-related disease. The sclera and cornea contain type XI collagen, and nightly rigid lens wear applies sustained mechanical pressure to structurally compromised tissue. Rigid lens insertion and removal also involve mechanical manipulation of an eye with a 65% lifetime retinal detachment risk. Ortho-K should only be considered after explicit assessment and approval by the vitreoretinal specialist, not just the prescribing optometrist.

Scleral Crosslinking: Experimental Frontier

  • Concept: Analogous to corneal crosslinking (CXL) for keratoconus, scleral crosslinking aims to strengthen the scleral wall to resist axial elongation. By inducing covalent cross-links in scleral collagen, the procedure could theoretically compensate for the structural deficit caused by defective collagen XI.
  • Mechanism: Riboflavin (vitamin B2) is applied to the scleral surface as a photosensitizer, then exposed to controlled ultraviolet-A (UVA, 365 nm) irradiation. The UVA energy triggers riboflavin to generate reactive oxygen species that create covalent bonds between adjacent collagen fibrils, increasing tissue stiffness, tensile strength, and resistance to enzymatic degradation.
  • Animal model data: In a guinea pig myopia model, riboflavin/UVA scleral crosslinking significantly increased scleral biomechanical stiffness and reduced axial elongation compared to untreated controls. Rabbit and primate models confirm the mechanical strengthening effect with maintained tissue viability at appropriate energy doses.
  • First human pilot: A clinical pilot study in adults with pathological myopia (>−10 D) has evaluated posterior scleral crosslinking via a subconjunctival approach. While full results are pending, the pilot establishes procedural feasibility and preliminary safety data in human eyes. This represents the first step toward clinical translation.
  • Relevance to COL11A1-deficient sclera: In Marshall syndrome, the sclera contains structurally compromised type XI collagen, leading to reduced biomechanical integrity and progressive axial elongation. Crosslinking could theoretically compensate for this structural deficit by strengthening the inter-fibrillar bonds that the defective collagen fails to provide. However, an important unknown remains: whether COL11A1-deficient scleral tissue responds to crosslinking in the same way as normal scleral tissue. The abnormal fibril diameter and spacing in Marshall syndrome sclera may alter the crosslinking efficiency, density, and distribution.
  • Current status: still experimental. Scleral crosslinking is not available for clinical use. No human trials have been completed in genetic myopia or connective tissue disorder populations. The animal and pilot data are encouraging but not yet sufficient for clinical recommendations.
  • Distinction from corneal CXL: Corneal crosslinking is already clinically available (see the CXL section below) and may be appropriate for Marshall syndrome patients with keratoconus. Scleral crosslinking is a distinct procedure targeting a different tissue with different challenges (scleral access, posterior segment proximity, retinal safety).
Why this matters for Marshall syndrome specifically. Scleral crosslinking is one of the few emerging technologies that could address the root structural problem in Marshall syndrome eyes — not the collagen gene itself, but the mechanical consequence of defective collagen in the scleral wall. If proven safe in COL11A1-deficient tissue, it would be a direct intervention for the progressive axial elongation that drives both extreme myopia and retinal detachment risk. Families should ask their vitreoretinal specialist to monitor the clinical trial landscape for scleral crosslinking studies, particularly any that include genetic myopia or connective tissue disorder cohorts.
Recommended hierarchy for Marshall syndrome myopia control:
  1. First line: Defocus spectacle lenses (DIMS/HAL/Stellest) — no eye contact, no pharmacological side effects, combines with polycarbonate for impact protection
  2. Second line: Low-dose atropine (0.01–0.05%) — pharmacological, no mechanical risk; monitor for photophobia
  3. Third line: Combination atropine + defocus spectacles — additive effect without contact lens risk
  4. With specialist approval only: Ortho-K or combination Ortho-K + atropine — requires explicit vitreoretinal sign-off

In all cases, monitor axial length (not just refraction) at each visit using optical biometry.

Protective Eyewear — Non-Negotiable

Every person with a COL11A1 mutation must wear protective eyewear at all times. This is not optional or situational — it is a fundamental safety measure:

  • Polycarbonate lenses in ALL prescription glasses: Polycarbonate is 10 times more impact-resistant than standard plastic lenses. Every pair of glasses — primary, backup, reading — must use polycarbonate or Trivex lenses. Standard CR-39 plastic is not acceptable.
  • ASTM F803-certified sports goggles: Required for any ball sport or activity with projectile risk (basketball, baseball, tennis, racquetball, badminton). Regular glasses, even with polycarbonate lenses, do not provide adequate coverage for sports.
  • Include in 504 Plan: Protective eyewear requirements should be documented in the child’s 504 Plan or IEP, with a spare pair kept at the school nurse’s office. PE teachers must be informed that the child cannot participate in any sport without certified eye protection.
  • Frame selection: Choose wrap-around frames that prevent objects from entering from the side. All lenses should include UV 400 protection and anti-reflective coating to reduce glare and maximize visual clarity.

Cataracts

Cataracts are more common in COL11A1-related disease than in COL2A1-related Stickler syndrome, reported in approximately 59% of patients with COL11A1 mutations compared to 36% with COL2A1 mutations.

Characteristics

  • Classic pattern: Quadrantic lamellar (cortical) opacity — the cataract affects one quadrant or section of the lens cortex. This is a characteristic finding that can aid diagnosis.
  • May be congenital or juvenile: Unlike age-related cataracts that appear in the 60s–70s, these can be present at birth or develop in childhood/early adulthood.
  • May not involve the visual axis: Some cataracts are located peripherally and may not significantly affect vision initially, though they can progress.
  • Other types: Posterior subcapsular and nuclear cataracts can also occur, sometimes as a consequence of retinal surgery or chronic steroid use.

Surgery Considerations

Cataract surgery carries additional risk in Marshall/Stickler patients. The decision to proceed must carefully weigh the visual benefit against the increased risk of post-operative retinal detachment. This surgery should only be performed by a surgeon experienced with vitreoretinal pathology in connective tissue disorders.
  • Increased retinal detachment risk: Cataract surgery is a known risk factor for retinal detachment in the general population. In patients who already have a 65% baseline RD risk, this compounds the danger significantly.
  • Prophylactic retinal treatment first: Ideally, prophylactic cryotherapy or laser should be completed before cataract surgery to reduce the post-operative RD risk.
  • IOL (intraocular lens) selection: Standard posterior chamber IOL implantation is generally feasible. However, zonular weakness (the fibers that hold the lens in place) may be present due to the connective tissue disorder, potentially affecting lens stability. Long-term IOL dislocation has been reported after vitrectomy.
  • 2025 Case Report — Lens Subluxation & Zonular Loss (PMID 39542031): A landmark case report documented a heterozygous COL11A1 mutation associated with congenital cataract, lens subluxation, AND complete zonular loss — the first documented case linking COL11A1 directly to zonular fiber failure. This means routine dilated exams should specifically assess zonular integrity, not just the retina. If subluxation is detected, capsular tension rings, iris-fixated IOLs, or scleral-fixated IOLs may be needed instead of standard IOL placement.
  • Combined procedures: Some surgeons may combine cataract surgery with vitrectomy in select cases to address both the cataract and the vitreous pathology simultaneously.
  • Close post-operative monitoring: More frequent follow-up than standard cataract surgery is essential, with vigilance for retinal detachment symptoms in the weeks and months following surgery.

Glaucoma

Glaucoma occurs in approximately 10% of patients with Stickler/Marshall syndrome. It results from anterior segment dysgenesis (developmental abnormality of the front part of the eye) and is typically open-angle glaucoma, though acute angle-closure has also been reported.

  • Can occur at any age, including childhood
  • If present in childhood, glaucoma can cause additional progressive myopia (on top of the congenital axial myopia)
  • Requires regular intraocular pressure (IOP) monitoring at every eye exam
  • Treatment follows standard glaucoma protocols (topical medications, laser, or surgery as needed)

Photophobia & Light Sensitivity Management

Photophobia (light sensitivity) is a common but often underaddressed symptom in Marshall syndrome. The combination of high myopia, vitreous abnormalities, and associated migraine susceptibility creates heightened sensitivity to light — particularly in the blue-green wavelength range — that can significantly impact daily function, school performance, and quality of life.

The Neuroscience of Photophobia

Photophobia is not simply “eyes being sensitive to bright light.” It is a neurological phenomenon driven by intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs) that contain melanopsin, a photopigment with peak sensitivity at 480 nm (blue-cyan light). These cells project to the trigeminal nucleus and the thalamic pain pathways, meaning that blue-green light directly activates pain circuitry independently of the visual pathway. In patients with migraine, corneal nerve abnormalities, or structural eye conditions, this pathway is sensitized and responds at lower light thresholds.

FL-41 Tinted Lenses: Targeted Wavelength Filtering

FL-41 lenses are precision-tinted lenses that selectively block light in the 480–520 nm (blue-green) wavelength range — the exact spectrum that activates melanopsin-containing ipRGCs and triggers photophobia-related pain.

  • Pediatric migraine data: A randomized controlled trial in children with migraine found that FL-41 tinted lenses significantly reduced headache frequency, photophobia severity, and light-triggered migraine episodes compared to standard tinted lenses. The effect was not simply light reduction (sunglasses would accomplish that) but selective wavelength filtering that specifically targets the photophobia pathway
  • Mechanism: By filtering 480–520 nm light while transmitting other wavelengths, FL-41 lenses reduce ipRGC activation without substantially dimming overall vision. This allows the child to function in normal lighting environments (classrooms, stores, outdoor activities) without triggering the photophobia-pain cascade
  • Avulux: A newer precision-tinted lens technology (Avulux) has been developed specifically for light-sensitive individuals, with clinical data showing reduction in light-triggered symptoms. Avulux lenses use a multi-band filter to block wavelengths across the pain-triggering spectrum while maintaining more natural color perception than FL-41
Avoid the Dark Adaptation Paradox
A common but counterproductive response to photophobia is to wear dark sunglasses indoors or keep rooms darkened. This creates a paradox: the eyes dark-adapt to the reduced light level, and when the child inevitably encounters normal lighting, the contrast is even more painful than it would have been without the dark adaptation. Over time, progressively darker environments are needed to feel comfortable, creating a cycle of increasing light avoidance and social withdrawal.
  • FL-41 or Avulux lenses are preferred over dark sunglasses because they filter the pain-triggering wavelengths without broadly reducing all light. The eyes do not dark-adapt because overall illumination is maintained
  • If the child currently wears dark sunglasses indoors, transition gradually to FL-41 lenses — do not remove the dark glasses abruptly. The transition may take several weeks as the visual system readjusts
  • Gradual, controlled light exposure (starting with lower-brightness environments and progressively increasing) can recalibrate the photophobia threshold over time

Practical Eye Protection

Because the retina is structurally vulnerable, physical eye protection is a lifelong consideration. Even minor trauma that would be inconsequential to a normal eye can precipitate a retinal detachment in someone with COL11A1 mutations.

Everyday Protective Eyewear

  • Polycarbonate lenses are essential — not just recommended. Polycarbonate is impact-rated and 10x more impact-resistant than standard plastic lenses. All prescription eyeglasses should use polycarbonate or Trivex lenses.
  • UV protection: UV-blocking coatings on all eyewear. UV exposure contributes to cataract progression and may affect the compromised vitreous. Sunglasses should block 99–100% of UVA and UVB.
  • Sports goggles: ASTM F803-rated sports goggles for any permitted athletic activity. Regular glasses are not sufficient protection for sports.

Assistive Technology for Vision

Assistive Technology for Progressive Vision Changes

Progressive vision loss from myopia, cataracts, retinal detachment, or myopic maculopathy may eventually affect daily function. Introducing assistive technology early — before vision decline makes it emergent — normalizes its use and reduces the adjustment burden later.

Low-Vision Aids

  • Handheld magnifiers — portable, inexpensive, useful for reading labels, menus, and fine print
  • Electronic magnifiers (CCTV / video magnifiers) — desktop or portable devices that project enlarged text onto a screen with adjustable contrast and brightness
  • Large-print and adjustable e-readers — Kindle, Kobo, and tablets allow font size, weight, and spacing customization
  • Screen magnification software (ZoomText, Windows Magnifier) — enlarges portions of a computer screen with smooth tracking
  • Text-to-speech (VoiceOver on Apple, JAWS, NVDA on Windows) — reads screen content aloud, reducing reliance on visual reading

Classroom Accommodations

  • Request a copy of teacher slides and notes in advance so the student can follow along without straining to read the board
  • Large-print worksheets and exams — most schools can produce these with minimal effort when included in a 504 plan or IEP
  • Recorded lectures — audio recordings or teacher-provided video allow review without visual fatigue
  • Voice-to-text for note-taking — dictation tools (Google Docs voice typing, Apple Dictation, Microsoft Dictate) let the student capture notes without writing or reading a screen

Smartphone Accessibility

  • Large text and display zoom — built into iOS and Android accessibility settings
  • High-contrast and color inversion modes — reduce eye strain and improve readability
  • Voice assistants (Siri, Google Assistant) — hands-free interaction for texting, calling, navigation, and information lookup
  • Magnifier app — built into iPhone (Accessibility → Magnifier) and available on Android; turns the phone camera into a handheld magnifier with light, zoom, and contrast controls
  • Seeing AI (Microsoft, free) — identifies objects, reads text aloud, describes scenes, and recognizes faces using the phone camera

Start early: Introduce these tools while vision is still functional. A child who is already comfortable with screen readers and magnification software will transition smoothly if vision declines, rather than facing a steep learning curve during an already stressful adjustment.

Corneal Crosslinking (CXL) & Scleral Lenses

Keratoconus — progressive corneal thinning and steepening that causes distorted vision — is associated with connective tissue disorders, including those affecting type XI collagen. The same structural collagen deficiency that weakens the vitreous and sclera also compromises corneal biomechanics, making COL11A1 patients potentially susceptible to keratoconus or corneal ectasia.

Corneal Crosslinking (CXL): Strengthening from Within

CXL is a procedure that strengthens corneal tissue by inducing covalent cross-links between collagen fibrils. The process involves:

  • Riboflavin (vitamin B2) saturation: Riboflavin drops are applied to the cornea, where they absorb into the stromal collagen matrix
  • UV-A light exposure: Controlled UV-A irradiation (370 nm wavelength) activates the riboflavin, generating reactive oxygen species that create new covalent bonds between collagen molecules
  • Result: The treated cornea becomes biomechanically stiffer and more resistant to progressive thinning — essentially compensating for the structural weakness caused by defective collagen

Modified Protocols for Thin Corneas

Standard CXL (the “Dresden protocol”) requires a minimum corneal thickness of 400 µm after epithelial removal. In connective tissue disorders where corneas may already be thinner than average, modified approaches are available:

CXL ProtocolHow It WorksRelevance to Marshall Syndrome
Transepithelial (epi-on) CXL Riboflavin penetrates through intact epithelium using special formulations with enhancers (BAC, EDTA, or trometamol). No epithelial removal required. Preserves the epithelium as a protective barrier — important in connective tissue disorders where wound healing may be compromised. Lower risk of corneal haze and infection.
Accelerated CXL Higher UV-A intensity for a shorter duration (e.g., 9 mW/cm² for 10 minutes instead of 3 mW/cm² for 30 minutes), delivering the same total energy dose. Shorter procedure time reduces anesthesia requirements in children. Comparable efficacy to standard protocol in most studies.
Pulsed CXL UV-A is delivered in on/off cycles rather than continuously, allowing oxygen replenishment in the corneal stroma between pulses. May produce deeper and more uniform crosslinking. The oxygen replenishment cycles are thought to enhance the crosslinking reaction.
Early intervention produces better outcomes. CXL is most effective when performed before significant corneal thinning and steepening have occurred. In patients with connective tissue disorders who show early signs of corneal ectasia or keratoconus, early crosslinking can halt progression before vision is significantly affected. If your ophthalmologist detects corneal steepening on topography, discuss CXL sooner rather than later — waiting for further deterioration reduces the likelihood of a good outcome.
Research Update (R455): 2025 Meta-Analysis of Corneal Collagen Cross-Linking

A 2025 meta-analysis has provided the most comprehensive pooled evidence for CXL efficacy to date, with findings especially relevant to connective tissue disorder populations:

  • 92% retinal detachment risk equivalent: In analogy to prophylactic retinal treatment, CXL halts keratoconus progression in over 92% of treated eyes, effectively converting a progressive blinding condition into a stable one. This makes CXL one of the highest-efficacy preventive procedures in ophthalmology
  • Laser-assisted CXL equally effective: The meta-analysis found that accelerated (higher-intensity, shorter-duration) protocols and femtosecond laser-assisted approaches achieve equivalent stabilization rates to the traditional Dresden protocol, with shorter procedure times that are advantageous for pediatric patients who require general anesthesia
  • Long-term durability confirmed: Studies with 5–10 year follow-up included in the meta-analysis confirm that CXL-induced stabilization persists long-term, with very low rates of late progression requiring retreatment
  • Siena long-term data: The Siena crosslinking study — one of the longest prospective CXL follow-up series — demonstrates 80% corneal stability at 10+ years post-procedure, with the majority of patients maintaining both topographic and visual acuity stability without retreatment. These results confirm that the structural reinforcement from CXL endures well beyond the initial treatment window
  • Connective tissue disorder consideration: While the meta-analysis included primarily idiopathic keratoconus, the subgroup of patients with connective tissue disorders showed similar response rates, suggesting that CXL is effective even when the underlying collagen architecture is constitutively abnormal. The collagen cross-links induced by CXL are covalent bonds that do not depend on normal native collagen structure for their stability

For Marshall syndrome patients showing any corneal topographic changes, this evidence supports early CXL referral rather than a watch-and-wait approach.

CXL Advances Relevant to COL11A1 Corneas

Corneal collagen cross-linking has evolved significantly since the original Dresden protocol, and several advances are particularly relevant to patients with connective tissue disorders:

  • Iontophoresis-assisted CXL (I-CXL): Uses a mild electrical current to drive riboflavin across the intact epithelium more effectively than passive diffusion. This technique achieves deeper stromal penetration while maintaining the transepithelial (epi-on) approach, which preserves the protective epithelium — an important advantage in connective tissue patients with potentially compromised wound healing. Studies show I-CXL halts keratoconus progression in over 95% of cases at 2-year follow-up.
  • Customized (topography-guided) CXL: Rather than treating the entire cornea uniformly, UV-A light is focused on the specific cone region identified by topographic mapping. This concentrates the crosslinking effect where the cornea is thinnest and weakest, while reducing UV exposure to unaffected tissue. Particularly relevant for COL11A1 patients who may have asymmetric or atypical thinning patterns rather than the classic keratoconus cone.
  • CXL combined with photorefractive keratectomy (Athens protocol): In selected cases, CXL can be combined with simultaneous topography-guided PRK to both halt progression and improve visual acuity in a single procedure. This combination is typically considered only in adults and should be evaluated very cautiously in connective tissue disorder patients due to concerns about corneal biomechanics and wound healing.
  • Sub-400 µm cornea solutions: For corneas thinner than the traditional 400 µm minimum, hypo-osmolar riboflavin (0.1% riboflavin in distilled water) can be used to swell the cornea to safe thickness before UV-A exposure. This swelling technique has made CXL accessible to patients who were previously excluded due to thin corneas — a common concern in connective tissue disorders.
Corneal Screening Protocol for Marshall Syndrome
Because COL11A1 mutations affect all collagen-containing ocular structures, not just the vitreous, corneal health monitoring should be integrated into routine ophthalmologic surveillance:
  • Corneal topography (Pentacam or Orbscan) at baseline and annually from age 10+ to detect early steepening or thinning before it becomes clinically apparent
  • Corneal pachymetry (central corneal thickness measurement) at each visit — thinning below 500 µm warrants closer follow-up
  • Corneal hysteresis measurement (Ocular Response Analyzer) if available — this directly measures corneal biomechanical stiffness and may identify at-risk corneas before topographic changes are visible
  • If keratoconus or ectasia is detected early: Refer promptly for CXL evaluation. In connective tissue disorder patients, progression can be faster than in idiopathic keratoconus, making early intervention even more critical

Scleral Lenses for Visual Rehabilitation

For patients with high myopia, corneal irregularity (from keratoconus or after corneal procedures), or poor spectacle tolerance, scleral contact lenses offer a powerful visual rehabilitation option:

  • How they work: Scleral lenses are large-diameter rigid gas permeable lenses that vault over the entire cornea and rest on the sclera (the white of the eye). A fluid reservoir between the lens and the cornea creates a smooth optical surface regardless of corneal irregularity.
  • Optical advantage: By replacing the irregular corneal surface with a perfectly smooth lens surface, scleral lenses can provide dramatically better visual acuity than spectacles or standard contact lenses in eyes with corneal irregularity or very high myopia.
  • Comfort: Because the lens rests on the sclera rather than the cornea, scleral lenses are generally more comfortable than smaller rigid lenses. The fluid reservoir also keeps the cornea hydrated throughout the day.
  • Protection: The lens acts as a physical barrier over the cornea, providing a degree of mechanical protection.
  • Fitting considerations in COL11A1 disorders: The scleral tissue contains type XI collagen, and its shape may differ from the general population. An experienced scleral lens fitter should be consulted, and follow-up should be more frequent initially to ensure proper fit on potentially atypical scleral anatomy.
Contact lens safety reminder: As noted in the Myopia Control section, any contact lens use in Marshall/Stickler syndrome should be discussed with the vitreoretinal specialist, not just the prescribing optometrist. Lens insertion and removal involve mechanical manipulation of the eye. While scleral lenses require less corneal contact than standard rigid lenses, the general precaution about RD risk still applies.

Questions for Your Vitreoretinal Specialist

Bring these questions to your appointment. Not all providers will be familiar with the specifics of COL11A1-related eye disease:

  1. “What is my/my child’s vitreous phenotype, and what does it tell us about the retinal detachment risk profile?” — Understanding the specific vitreous type (beaded vs. membranous) helps assess risk and confirms the genetic subtype.
  2. “Should we pursue prophylactic cryotherapy or laser retinopexy, and if so, when?” — This is the single most important conversation to have. If your specialist is not familiar with the Cambridge protocol, bring published references.
  3. “What specific peripheral retinal findings are present (lattice degeneration, retinal thinning, pigmentary changes), and how do they affect the risk assessment?” — Get a detailed map of what the retinal periphery looks like.
  4. “What is the plan if a retinal detachment occurs — who performs the surgery, and what approach would be used?” — Have an emergency plan in place before it is needed. Know who to call and where to go.
  5. “Are there signs of myopic maculopathy, and is OCT monitoring warranted?” — Myopic maculopathy can cause gradual central vision loss independently of retinal detachment.
  6. “If cataract surgery becomes necessary in the future, how would you approach it given the vitreous abnormality and retinal risk?” — Planning ahead ensures the right surgeon and technique when the time comes.
