VPI & Nasal Speech: Understanding Velopharyngeal Insufficiency

Clinical guidance synthesis. Recommendations on this page are based on published clinical literature and specialist consensus for connective tissue disorders. Marshall-specific evidence is limited due to the rarity of the condition. Always work with your child's medical team.
Velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI) is one of the most functionally significant speech-related challenges in Marshall syndrome. Because COL11A1 mutations affect both palatal structure and the connective tissues that support velopharyngeal closure, children with Marshall syndrome are at elevated risk for persistent VPI — even after cleft palate repair. This page explains the mechanism, diagnosis, and the critical distinction between what speech therapy can and cannot fix.

What Is VPI? Established

Velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI) occurs when the velum (soft palate) fails to make adequate contact with the posterior pharyngeal wall during speech and swallowing. This closure — called velopharyngeal closure — is what separates the oral cavity from the nasal cavity. When it works properly, air and sound are directed entirely through the mouth for most speech sounds. When it fails, air escapes through the nose, producing characteristic speech distortions.

The Velopharyngeal Mechanism

During normal speech, the velum elevates and moves posteriorly to contact the back wall of the pharynx. Simultaneously, the lateral pharyngeal walls may move inward. This creates a seal that prevents air from entering the nasal cavity. The closure pattern varies between individuals — some rely primarily on velar elevation, others on lateral wall movement, and many use a combination.

VPI disrupts this mechanism. The consequences include:

  • Hypernasality: Excessive nasal resonance during vowels and voiced consonants, giving speech a characteristic “nasal” quality
  • Nasal air emission: Audible or inaudible escape of air through the nose during production of pressure consonants
  • Difficulty with pressure consonants: Sounds that require oral pressure buildup — plosives (p, b, t, d, k, g), fricatives (s, z, f, v, sh), and affricates (ch, j) — are weakened or distorted because pressure leaks through the nose
  • Reduced speech intelligibility: Listeners may struggle to understand the speaker, particularly in noisy environments or over the telephone
  • Nasal regurgitation: In more significant VPI, liquids and soft foods may reflux into the nasal cavity during swallowing

Why VPI Is Common in Marshall Syndrome Supported

Several features of Marshall syndrome converge to make VPI significantly more likely than in the general population.

Pierre Robin Sequence Connection

Many children with Marshall syndrome are born with Pierre Robin sequence — the triad of micrognathia (small jaw), glossoptosis (posteriorly positioned tongue), and cleft palate. The cleft palate is the primary driver of VPI risk:

  • Even after surgical repair, the repaired palate may not achieve full velopharyngeal closure. Residual VPI occurs in approximately 20–30% of children following cleft palate repair
  • The palatal muscles (levator veli palatini, tensor veli palatini, musculus uvulae) may have abnormal insertion points after repair, reducing their effectiveness in elevating the velum
  • Scar tissue from the repair can limit palatal mobility

COL11A1 and Palatal Tissue

Type XI collagen is a structural component of the connective tissues in the palate and pharynx. COL11A1 mutations affect VPI risk through multiple pathways:

  • Palatal muscle attachments: The connective tissue framework that anchors palatal muscles may be structurally compromised, reducing the force and range of velar elevation
  • Velum tissue compliance: The velum itself may be more compliant (floppy) due to defective collagen, making it harder to maintain a firm seal against the pharyngeal wall
  • Wound healing: Collagen disorders can affect surgical wound healing, potentially contributing to higher rates of post-surgical VPI

Submucous Cleft Palate

Submucous cleft palate can be occult (hidden). The surface mucosa appears intact, but the underlying muscle and/or bone of the palate has a cleft. This can be missed on routine examination. If a child with Marshall syndrome has hypernasal speech but no visible cleft, submucous cleft palate should be actively investigated. Classic signs include a bifid uvula, a translucent zone in the midline of the soft palate (zona pellucida), and a notch palpable at the posterior hard palate.

Midface Hypoplasia

The characteristic flat midface in Marshall syndrome increases the depth of the nasopharynx. A deeper pharyngeal space means the velum must travel a greater distance to achieve closure — effectively increasing the “gap” that the velopharyngeal mechanism must bridge.

Signs to Watch For

Early recognition of VPI allows earlier intervention. The following signs should prompt referral for a formal speech and velopharyngeal assessment:

Nasal air emission Audible rush of air through the nose during consonants; may be visible as fogging on a mirror held under the nose Noticeable once the child begins producing pressure consonants, age 1.5–3

The mirror test: Hold a small mirror or a cold metal spoon horizontally under the child’s nose while they say words with pressure consonants (“puppy,” “baby,” “cookie”). Fogging on the mirror during these sounds suggests nasal air emission. This is not diagnostic but can help identify when to seek a formal evaluation.

Diagnosis

A comprehensive VPI evaluation involves multiple assessment methods, ideally coordinated through a craniofacial or cleft palate team.

Perceptual Speech Assessment

A speech-language pathologist (SLP) with expertise in resonance disorders conducts a structured evaluation of the child’s speech. This includes:

  • Rating hypernasality severity on standardized scales
  • Assessing nasal air emission (audible vs. inaudible, consistent vs. inconsistent)
  • Identifying compensatory articulation patterns
  • Evaluating overall speech intelligibility in single words and connected speech
  • Distinguishing between phoneme-specific nasal emission (which can be treated with therapy) and structural VPI (which requires surgical correction)

Nasometry

A nasometer measures the ratio of nasal to oral acoustic energy during speech. The child speaks into a device that separates nasal and oral sound output. Results are expressed as a nasalance score — scores above established norms for specific speech samples indicate excessive nasality. This provides an objective, quantifiable measure to track over time.

Videofluoroscopy

A lateral videofluoroscopic study captures real-time X-ray video of the velopharyngeal mechanism during speech. The child speaks standardized phrases while barium contrast outlines the soft palate and pharyngeal walls. This reveals:

  • The gap size between the velum and the posterior pharyngeal wall
  • The pattern of velar movement (how far and how quickly the velum elevates)
  • Lateral pharyngeal wall contribution to closure
  • Presence of a Passavant’s ridge (a compensatory shelf of tissue on the pharyngeal wall)

Nasopharyngoscopy

A flexible fiberoptic endoscope is passed through the nose to directly visualize the velopharyngeal port from above during speech. This is the gold standard for determining the closure pattern and the specific location and size of the gap. It provides information that videofluoroscopy cannot, including the shape of the velopharyngeal opening (circular, sagittal, coronal, or irregular) and the relative contribution of each structure.

When to refer: Any child with Marshall syndrome who has speech concerns should be referred for a velopharyngeal evaluation by age 3. Do not wait for intelligibility to deteriorate significantly — early identification allows intervention before compensatory articulation patterns become deeply ingrained. If the child had a cleft palate repair, VPI assessment should be part of routine post-surgical follow-up regardless of whether concerns are raised.

Speech Therapy

Understanding what speech therapy can and cannot accomplish is one of the most important distinctions for families navigating VPI.

What Speech Therapy CAN Help

  • Compensatory misarticulations: Glottal stops, pharyngeal fricatives, and other learned compensations can be retrained through therapy — teaching the child to produce sounds at the correct place of articulation rather than in the throat
  • Phoneme-specific nasal emission: When nasal air emission occurs only on specific sounds (rather than all pressure consonants), this may be a learned motor pattern rather than structural insufficiency, and therapy can help
  • Articulation errors unrelated to VPI: Children with Marshall syndrome may have articulation delays independent of VPI (due to hearing loss, developmental timing, or oral motor factors), and these respond to standard articulation therapy
  • Oral motor strengthening: Exercises to improve velar movement and oral pressure can provide modest benefit when VPI is mild (small gap)
  • Language and communication: Therapy supports overall language development, pragmatic skills, and communication confidence regardless of VPI status

What Speech Therapy CANNOT Fix

If the velopharyngeal mechanism physically cannot close, no amount of speech therapy will resolve the hypernasality or nasal air emission. This is a critical distinction. Structural VPI — where the velum is too short, too immobile, or the pharyngeal space is too deep for closure to occur — requires surgical correction. Prolonged speech therapy for structural VPI delays necessary surgery, subjects the child to frustration and failure, and allows compensatory patterns to become more entrenched. If an experienced SLP and instrumental assessment (nasopharyngoscopy or videofluoroscopy) confirm structural VPI, surgery should be discussed promptly.

Resonance Therapy Techniques

For cases where VPI is borderline or mild, specialized resonance therapy approaches may be attempted:

  • Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy: Some programs use CPAP to provide resistance against which the velopharyngeal mechanism must work, potentially improving closure strength
  • Visual biofeedback: Nasometry and nasopharyngoscopy can be used as real-time biofeedback tools, allowing the child to see and modify their velopharyngeal closure patterns
  • Oral pressure and airflow direction: Therapy focuses on building awareness of oral vs. nasal airflow and maximizing oral pressure generation
For families: If your child has been in speech therapy for VPI-related hypernasality for 6–12 months without measurable improvement in nasalance scores or perceptual ratings, request a re-evaluation with instrumental assessment. Persistent hypernasality despite therapy strongly suggests structural VPI that needs surgical intervention, not more therapy.

Surgical Management

When structural VPI is confirmed, surgery aims to reduce the size of the velopharyngeal port to allow adequate closure during speech while maintaining a nasal airway for breathing.

Surgical Options

Pharyngeal Flap

A flap of tissue from the posterior pharyngeal wall is raised and attached to the soft palate, creating a permanent bridge between the palate and the pharyngeal wall. Air can still pass through lateral ports on either side of the flap for nasal breathing. This is the most commonly performed procedure for VPI and is particularly effective when the closure pattern shows good lateral wall movement but insufficient velar elevation.

Sphincter Pharyngoplasty

Flaps of tissue from the posterior tonsillar pillars (palatopharyngeus muscles) are rotated and sutured to the posterior pharyngeal wall, creating a dynamic sphincter that narrows the velopharyngeal port. This procedure is preferred when the closure pattern shows poor lateral wall movement.

Furlow Palatoplasty (Revision)

A re-do of the palate repair using the Furlow double-opposing Z-plasty technique, which lengthens the soft palate and repositions the levator veli palatini muscles into a more anatomically correct orientation. This may be appropriate when the original cleft palate repair did not adequately reconstruct the palatal muscle sling.

Timing of Surgery

VPI surgery is typically considered between ages 4 and 6, after:

  • Comprehensive speech assessment confirms structural VPI
  • Instrumental evaluation (nasopharyngoscopy and/or videofluoroscopy) defines the closure pattern and gap characteristics
  • An adequate trial of speech therapy for any compensatory articulation has been completed
  • The child’s adenoid tissue has been evaluated (adenoid hypertrophy can mask VPI; adenoid involution can unmask it)

COL11A1-Specific Surgical Considerations

Collagen disorders affect tissue quality and wound healing. Surgeons operating on children with Marshall syndrome should be aware of the following:
  • Tissue fragility: Type XI collagen abnormalities may make pharyngeal and palatal tissues more friable, requiring careful surgical technique and potentially wider flap margins
  • Healing: Wound healing may be delayed or suboptimal. Closer post-operative follow-up is warranted
  • Re-operation rates: There may be a higher likelihood of VPI recurrence or need for revision surgery compared to non-syndromic cleft populations. Families should be counseled about this possibility
  • Airway considerations: Children with Marshall syndrome may have baseline airway compromise from micrognathia and midface hypoplasia. Pharyngeal flap surgery narrows the airway — obstructive sleep apnea must be screened for before and after surgery

Coordination with the Craniofacial Team

VPI surgery in Marshall syndrome should be performed by a surgeon experienced with both VPI correction and connective tissue disorders. The decision-making process involves:

  • Craniofacial surgeon or plastic surgeon with cleft/craniofacial expertise
  • SLP with resonance specialization (pre- and post-operative assessment)
  • Anesthesiologist aware of potential airway difficulties
  • Geneticist or coordinating physician who understands the COL11A1 context
  • Audiologist (hearing status affects speech assessment and therapy planning)

The Hearing–Speech Connection

In Marshall syndrome, hearing loss and VPI frequently coexist, and their combined effect on speech development is greater than either alone.

Compounded Difficulty

  • Hearing loss affects speech development independently: Children who cannot clearly hear speech sounds have difficulty learning to produce them accurately. High-frequency sensorineural hearing loss (common in Marshall syndrome) particularly affects perception of consonants — the same consonants that VPI makes difficult to produce
  • VPI + hearing loss = compounded challenge: A child who both cannot hear consonants clearly AND cannot produce them with adequate oral pressure faces a dual barrier to intelligible speech
  • Difficulty distinguishing VPI from hearing-related speech errors: Some speech patterns caused by hearing loss (weak fricatives, consonant omissions) overlap with VPI-related patterns, making accurate diagnosis more complex

Optimize Hearing First

Before attributing all speech problems to VPI, ensure hearing is optimally managed. A child whose hearing loss is inadequately aided may appear to have more severe VPI than they actually do. Appropriate amplification (hearing aids, bone conduction devices, or cochlear implants as indicated) should be in place and verified before finalizing VPI severity assessment and surgical planning. See Hearing Management for comprehensive guidance on auditory intervention.

FM Systems in Therapy

For children with both VPI and hearing loss receiving speech therapy:

  • Personal FM/Roger systems should be used during therapy sessions so the child can clearly hear the SLP’s model productions
  • The SLP should position themselves facing the child and within good visual range for speechreading cues
  • Therapy targets should account for the child’s audiogram — targeting sounds in frequency ranges the child cannot hear (even with amplification) will be ineffective
  • Coordinate with the audiologist to ensure hearing aids are programmed for optimal speech perception before evaluating therapy progress

School and Social Impact

VPI-related speech differences affect children beyond the clinical setting. Academic participation, peer relationships, and self-confidence are all impacted.

Speech Intelligibility and Academics

  • Classroom participation: Children with reduced intelligibility may avoid raising their hand, answering questions, or participating in group discussions
  • Oral presentations: Reading aloud and presenting to the class can be anxiety-provoking when speech is difficult for classmates to understand
  • Phonics and reading: VPI can interfere with phonological awareness — the ability to manipulate speech sounds — which is foundational for reading development
  • Peer communication: Difficulty being understood by classmates affects social interaction, group work, and playground participation

IEP/504 Accommodations

Children with VPI-related speech concerns are typically eligible for speech-language services through the school system, and may qualify for additional accommodations:

  • Direct speech-language therapy services (individual or small group)
  • Preferential seating for visual and auditory access
  • Reduced emphasis on oral participation grading when speech intelligibility is significantly affected
  • Additional time for oral responses
  • Alternative methods for demonstrating knowledge (written instead of oral when appropriate)
  • Teacher and staff education about VPI and the child’s specific speech patterns

For detailed guidance on navigating educational accommodations, see 504 Plans & School Accommodations.

Social Confidence

  • Self-awareness: Children typically become aware of their speech differences by age 4–5, and this awareness increases with age
  • Peer reactions: Difficulty being understood can lead to social withdrawal, frustration, or reluctance to communicate
  • Bullying risk: Speech that sounds different may attract unwanted attention from peers. Schools should be proactive about creating supportive environments
  • Building resilience: Speech therapy groups with peers who have similar challenges can normalize the experience and build communication confidence

When to Consider Assistive Communication

Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) is not about giving up on speech — it is about ensuring the child can communicate effectively while speech development continues. If a child’s intelligibility is so reduced that daily communication is significantly impaired, AAC tools (from simple picture boards to speech-generating apps) can bridge the gap, reduce frustration, and support language development. This is particularly relevant during the period between VPI diagnosis and surgical correction, which may span months to years.
