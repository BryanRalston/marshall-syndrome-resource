Doctor Letter

This is a one-page medical summary you can print and hand to any provider who has never heard of Marshall syndrome. Most doctors will not have encountered this condition before. This letter gives them what they need immediately: what Marshall syndrome is, what systems it affects, and — most critically — what to watch out for during procedures and treatment.

When to Use This Letter

  • New specialist appointments — hand it to the doctor or nurse at check-in so they can review it before the visit
  • Emergency room visits — give it to the triage nurse immediately; it flags airway and cervical spine precautions they need to know before any intervention
  • School nurse — keep a copy in your child’s school health file
  • Surgical pre-op — ensure the anesthesiologist and surgical team each receive a copy well in advance
  • Urgent care or walk-in clinics — any setting where providers are seeing your child for the first time

How to Customize It

What Is Marshall Syndrome?

Marshall syndrome is a rare autosomal dominant connective tissue disorder caused by mutations in the COL11A1 gene (chromosome 1p21.1), which encodes the α1 chain of type XI collagen. Type XI collagen is a critical structural component of cartilage, the vitreous humor of the eye, the inner ear, and the intervertebral discs. Marshall syndrome is allelic to Stickler syndrome type II — the same gene is involved, but different mutation types (typically splice-site mutations causing exon skipping in Marshall syndrome) produce a distinct and generally more severe phenotype. Estimated prevalence is fewer than 1 in 1,000,000.

Key Clinical Features

  • Craniofacial: Flat midface (midface hypoplasia), underdeveloped nasal bones, short nose with anteverted nares. Pierre Robin sequence (micrognathia, glossoptosis, cleft palate) occurs in approximately 24% of cases.
  • Eyes: High myopia (often exceeding −8 diopters), early cataracts, vitreous degeneration (membranous or beaded vitreous), and elevated lifetime risk of retinal detachment.
  • Hearing: Early-onset progressive sensorineural hearing loss, often bilateral. Tinnitus is common. Hearing loss may begin in childhood and worsen over time.
  • Skeletal: Spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, early-onset osteoarthritis, joint hypermobility, and short stature relative to family background.
  • Spine: Cervical instability risk (craniocervical junction), accelerated intervertebral disc degeneration, and risk of spinal stenosis at earlier ages than typical.
  • Calvarium: Thickened skull bones (intracranial calcification of the frontal bone) — a distinguishing radiographic finding that helps differentiate Marshall syndrome from Stickler syndrome type II.
  • Cardiac: Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) in 45.6% of Stickler spectrum patients (vs. 2–3% in the general population). COL11A1 has been identified as a novel MVP-related gene (2025 whole-exome sequencing study). Baseline echocardiogram recommended.

Critical Considerations for Any Provider

  1. Anesthesia — Difficult Airway Anticipated. Midface hypoplasia and micrognathia create a potentially difficult airway. Cervical instability may be present. Avoid excessive neck extension during intubation. Video laryngoscopy is recommended. Fiberoptic intubation should be available. Notify the anesthesiologist in advance of any procedure requiring sedation or general anesthesia. Consult OrphanAnesthesia (orphananesthesia.eu) for disorder-specific protocols.
  2. Cervical Spine — Instability Risk. Risk of craniocervical instability due to abnormal collagen in ligaments and intervertebral structures. Obtain flexion-extension cervical spine imaging before any procedure requiring neck manipulation or positioning. Avoid forceful neck extension or rotation. Use cervical precautions during intubation, physical examination, and manual therapy.
  3. Eyes — Retinal Detachment Risk. Retinal detachment can occur with minimal or no trauma due to abnormal vitreous structure. Avoid high-impact activities and Valsalva-inducing procedures when possible. Any sudden increase in floaters, flashes of light, or vision loss constitutes an emergency requiring immediate referral to a vitreoretinal specialist.
  4. Medications to Use with Caution. Ototoxic drugs — aminoglycosides (gentamicin, tobramycin), high-dose loop diuretics (furosemide), and cisplatin — should be avoided or used only when no alternative exists. This patient already has sensorineural hearing loss; ototoxic agents may accelerate irreversible damage. Monitor hearing with audiometry if these medications are unavoidable. Fluoroquinolones may also pose additional risk to collagen-deficient tissues.
  5. Wound Healing & Tissue Integrity. Collagen disorder may affect wound healing, tissue tensile strength, and surgical closure integrity. Dural tears are more likely during spinal procedures. Use appropriate wound closure techniques and plan for longer healing timelines. Easy bruising and skin fragility may be present.
  6. Joint Laxity. Generalized joint hypermobility increases the risk of dislocation and subluxation. Use gentle manipulation during procedures and physical examinations. Avoid forceful joint positioning under anesthesia.
  7. Cardiac Screening. 45.6% of Stickler/Marshall patients have mitral valve prolapse. If echocardiogram has not yet been performed, recommend baseline screening. If MVP is known, monitor for regurgitation progression. MVP symptoms (palpitations, chest pain) may overlap with POTS — evaluate both.

Recommended Surveillance Schedule

  • Ophthalmology: Every 6–12 months — dilated retinal examination with peripheral retinal assessment. Assess for posterior vitreous detachment, lattice degeneration, and retinal breaks.
  • Audiology: Annually — pure-tone audiometry and speech audiometry. Establish baseline early and track progression.
  • Orthopedics / Spine: As clinically indicated. Baseline cervical spine imaging (including flexion-extension views) is recommended. Monitor for scoliosis, joint degeneration, and spinal symptoms.
  • Genetics: Periodic re-evaluation as understanding of COL11A1-related disorders evolves and new therapeutic options emerge.
  • Cardiology: Baseline echocardiogram. If MVP is present, annual echocardiogram to monitor for regurgitation progression. If no MVP on baseline, repeat echocardiogram every 3–5 years.
