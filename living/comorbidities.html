Comorbidity Interactions

Marshall syndrome affects multiple organ systems simultaneously. Decisions about managing one system must account for their effects on the others. This section addresses the most important interactions.

The Eye–Spine Tension

This is the most clinically significant interaction for your child:

The spine needs strengthening — physical therapy, exercise, and core stability work are the primary interventions for spinal pain and degeneration.

The eyes restrict exercise options — retinal detachment is a serious risk in Marshall syndrome, and certain activities that involve jarring, impact, or sudden changes in intraocular pressure are contraindicated:

  • Trampolining (case reports of retinal detachment in Stickler patients)
  • Ball sports with risk of facial/head impact
  • Contact sports
  • Activities involving Valsalva maneuver (heavy straining)

The resolution: Low-impact, non-jarring activities serve both goals simultaneously. Swimming, cycling, controlled resistance training, and aquatic therapy strengthen the spine without risking the eyes. This is why the activity recommendations in this document converge on these specific options.

Prophylactic cryoretinopexy: A procedure called 360-degree prophylactic cryoretinopexy (freezing treatment around the entire retina to create adhesions that prevent detachment) has been shown to reduce retinal detachment risk by a factor of 7.4x in Stickler syndrome. If your child has not had this evaluated, it should be discussed with the vitreoretinal specialist. Successfully reducing detachment risk also broadens the range of safe physical activities.

Hearing–Balance–Spine Interaction

  • Sensorineural hearing loss affects the inner ear, which also houses the vestibular system (balance)
  • Impaired balance compounds spinal instability — the body relies on vestibular input to coordinate postural muscles, and if that input is degraded, the spine bears more uncontrolled loading
  • Proprioception training in physical therapy partially compensates for vestibular deficits
  • Hearing aids should be properly fitted and consistently used — beyond communication, they help the brain orient in space

The Multisystem Planning Principle

Every specialist visit is an opportunity to consider cross-system effects:

  • Before the ophthalmologist restricts activities, ask: "What activities are safe for the spine?"
  • Before the orthopedist prescribes exercises, ask: "Are there retinal restrictions we should account for?"
  • Before the audiologist adjusts hearing aids, mention: "Balance is also a concern for spinal stability."
No single specialist sees the whole picture. You, as the family, are the integrator.
