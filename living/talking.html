Talking About Marshall Syndrome

One of the hardest parts of a rare diagnosis is explaining it — over and over, to different audiences, at different levels of detail. You will have this conversation dozens of times: with new doctors, emergency rooms, teachers, coaches, other parents, and eventually with your child. Each audience needs a different version.

These conversation scripts give you a starting point. They are designed to be read aloud, copied, or screenshotted. Adapt them to your family's situation — change the pronouns, fill in the specifics, make them yours. The goal is not to memorize a speech but to have a framework so that you are not improvising under stress every time someone says, "So what exactly does your child have?"

How to use this page: Each script below is written as a blockquote you can copy directly. Read it once to understand the structure, then adapt it. The key points after each script explain why certain things are included and what you can adjust.

1. To a New Doctor Who Has Never Heard of It

This is the most common conversation you will have. Most physicians have never encountered Marshall syndrome. Your job is not to teach a genetics lecture — it is to give them enough context to understand the clinical picture and know where to look for more.

The Opening

"[Child's name] has Marshall syndrome, caused by a mutation in the COL11A1 gene — type XI collagen. It is very similar to Stickler syndrome type II — same gene, different mutation type. If you have seen Stickler patients, the management is nearly identical."

Why this works: Most doctors have at least heard of Stickler syndrome even if they have never managed it. Anchoring Marshall syndrome to Stickler gives them an immediate reference frame. Naming the gene and the collagen type signals that you know the condition well and that this is a real, defined genetic disorder — not a vague or contested diagnosis.

The Key Systems

"The condition is multi-system. The main areas of involvement are:

Eyes: High myopia, vitreous degeneration, and a lifetime retinal detachment risk of approximately 65%. [Child] sees a retinal specialist every [frequency].

Ears: Progressive sensorineural hearing loss. [Child] currently [wears hearing aids / has mild loss / describe current status]. Monitored with audiograms every [frequency].

Joints and spine: Spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, early disc degeneration, joint hypermobility, and chronic spinal pain. The literature shows 74% endplate abnormalities and 67% chronic back pain in this population.

Face: Midface hypoplasia, which has airway implications — important for any procedure requiring intubation.

Prevalence is estimated at less than 1 in 1,000,000. You may never see another case."

The Handoff

"I have a one-page medical summary and a doctor letter I can share with you. I also have a comprehensive resource site with clinical references if you want to read further. For this visit specifically, the reason we are here is [specific concern]."
Prepare in advance: Bring a printed copy of the Doctor Letter to every new provider visit. Most physicians appreciate having a well-organized reference document rather than having to research a condition they may never have seen. Having it ready signals competence and saves everyone time.

2. To the ER or Urgent Care

In an emergency, you do not have time for a genetics lesson. The ER team needs three things in thirty seconds: what the condition is, what could go wrong during treatment, and what the emergency might actually be. Lead with safety.

The 30-Second Version

"My child has Marshall syndrome — a rare collagen disorder, COL11A1 gene. Three things you need to know right now:

1. Difficult airway. Midface is underdeveloped, jaw is small. If intubation is needed, have a video laryngoscope or fiberoptic scope ready. The cervical spine may be unstable — avoid hyperextension.

2. If we are here for vision changes — flashing lights, sudden floaters, a curtain across the vision — this is a retinal detachment until proven otherwise. They need immediate ophthalmology. Do not wait.

3. Avoid aminoglycoside antibiotics (gentamicin, tobramycin). They already have sensorineural hearing loss, and aminoglycosides are ototoxic. Use alternatives."

If They Push Back or Seem Unfamiliar

"I understand this is rare. It is classified under Stickler syndrome type II in most databases — same gene. I have an emergency card with the key clinical details and our geneticist's phone number."
Always carry the emergency card. In a true emergency, you may not be able to explain anything. An Emergency Medical Card in a wallet, on a phone, and taped inside a school backpack ensures the critical information is available even if you are not. Create one now if you have not already.

If the Visit Involves Sedation or Procedures

"Before any sedation: the airway is anticipated difficult due to midface hypoplasia and micrognathia. Local anesthetics may have reduced effectiveness — approximately 88% of patients with similar collagen disorders report resistance. Please have a difficult airway cart available and consider an anesthesiology consult before sedation."

3. To a Teacher or School Administrator

Schools need practical information, not clinical detail. The goal is to help them understand what the child needs day-to-day without making them feel overwhelmed or afraid. Frame the condition as manageable, the child as capable, and the accommodations as straightforward.

The Overview

"[Child's name] has a genetic condition called Marshall syndrome that affects their connective tissue — basically the collagen that helps hold the body together. It is rare, and you have probably never heard of it. That is completely normal. What matters for the classroom is understanding a few practical things."

The Day-to-Day

"Here is what this means on a daily basis:

Hearing: [Child] has hearing loss and [wears hearing aids / uses an FM system / needs preferential seating near the teacher]. Background noise makes hearing harder. If you can, face [child] when speaking and repeat instructions if asked.

Vision: [Child] is severely nearsighted. They may need to sit closer to the board, have printed materials in larger font, or use a tablet to zoom in on content. If they ever report sudden vision changes — flashing lights, new floaters, or a shadow across their vision — call us immediately. That is a medical emergency.

Physical activity: [Child] should not do contact sports or activities with head impact risk — no dodgeball, football, gymnastics, or trampolining. Swimming, walking, and cycling are great. They can participate in most PE activities with modifications.

Pain: [Child] has chronic spinal pain. They may have days where they need to stand up and move around, take a break, or visit the nurse. They are not being disruptive — they are managing a real structural condition. Sitting still for long periods genuinely hurts.

Absences: [Child] has more medical appointments than most kids. We will try to schedule around school when possible, but some absences are unavoidable."

What Teachers Appreciate Hearing

"[Child] is a [describe your child — curious, creative, determined, funny]. The condition does not affect their intelligence or their ability to learn. They just need a few accommodations to access the same education as everyone else. We are happy to answer any questions, and we appreciate you taking the time to understand."
Formalize the accommodations: A verbal conversation is a good start, but a 504 Plan makes accommodations legally binding. If the school is receptive after this conversation, follow up with a formal 504 request. Bring documentation from the child's physician.

For the School Nurse Specifically

"The three things the school nurse needs to know are:

1. Pain management: [Child] has chronic spinal pain and may need to visit you for medication or a rest break. Here are the approved medications and doses: [list them].

2. Activity restrictions: No trampolining, contact sports, or heavy impact activities in PE. Swimming and cycling are encouraged. They need movement breaks — sitting still for extended periods worsens their pain.

3. Vision emergency: If [child] ever reports sudden vision changes — flashing lights, sudden floaters, a curtain across their vision — call 911 immediately. This could be retinal detachment, which is a surgical emergency. Do not wait to see if it gets better."

For Coaches and PE Teachers

"[Child] has a genetic condition that affects their joints and spine. They absolutely can and should participate in physical activity — it is actually medicine for their condition. Here is what works:

Great activities: Swimming (their best sport), cycling, walking, balance exercises, lightweight resistance bands, modified yoga (strength poses, not extreme flexibility).

Please avoid: Running for distance or speed, trampolining, contact sports, gymnastics, heavy overhead lifting, dodgeball or activities where they could get hit in the head or face (eye injury risk).

They may need: Movement breaks, the ability to sit out when pain flares up (they will tell you), and understanding that they are not being difficult — they are managing a real structural condition. On good days they may be fully active. On bad days they may need to modify. Trust them to know the difference."

4. To Other Parents and Friends

This is the conversation where you do not need to be clinical. The other parent at the birthday party, the neighbor, the friend who asks why your child wears hearing aids — they are asking because they care, not because they need a medical briefing. Keep it warm, keep it honest, and keep it short unless they ask for more.

The Short Version

"[Child] has a genetic condition called Marshall syndrome. It is something they were born with — it affects the connective tissue in their body, so it shows up in a lot of places: their eyes, hearing, joints, and spine. Most people have never heard of it because it is really rare. [Child] is [child] — they just need a few extra accommodations and more doctor visits than most kids."

If They Ask More

"It is a collagen disorder — collagen is the protein that gives structure to a lot of the body. In [child's] case, the collagen does not form quite right, so things that depend on it — like the eyes, the inner ear, the cushions between the vertebrae — need extra monitoring and care. They wear [glasses/hearing aids] because of it, and they see specialists more often than most kids, but they are doing well. It is a marathon, not a crisis."

If Your Child Is Going to Their House

"A couple of things that would be helpful to know: [child] should avoid trampolines and rough physical play — their joints and eyes are more vulnerable than other kids'. They may need to take a break if they are in pain, and that is normal for them. And if they ever say they are seeing flashing lights or something is wrong with their vision, please call me right away — that could be a retinal emergency. Otherwise, they are a regular kid. Just keep an eye out and let them have fun."
You get to decide how much to share. Some families are open books about the diagnosis; others prefer to keep details private. There is no wrong approach. The scripts above are starting points — share as much or as little as feels right for your family. The only non-negotiable is making sure anyone supervising your child knows about the retinal detachment risk and activity restrictions.

5. To the Child Themselves

This may be the most important conversation on this page. How a child understands their own condition shapes their self-image, their willingness to self-advocate, and their relationship with their body for the rest of their life. The goal at every age is honesty without fear, agency without overwhelm.

Ages 5–7: The Building Blocks

"Your body makes something called collagen — it is like the glue that helps hold your body together. Everybody's body makes collagen, but yours makes it a little bit differently. That is why you wear glasses, why you have hearing aids, and why you see more doctors than your friends do.

It does not mean anything is wrong with you. It just means your body needs a little extra help in some places. That is what your glasses do, and your hearing aids, and the exercises we do together.

Lots of people have bodies that work differently in some way. This is your version. And all the people who take care of you — your doctors, your family — we all know exactly what your body needs."

At this age, keep it simple and reassuring. They do not need to know the gene name or the complication list. They need to know that their body is not broken, that help exists, and that the adults around them understand what is going on.

Ages 8–12: More Detail, More Agency

"You know how your body works a bit differently — your eyes, your hearing, your back? There is a reason for all of it, and it is actually kind of interesting.

Inside every cell in your body, there are instructions called genes. You have a gene called COL11A1 that tells your body how to make a specific type of collagen — that is a protein that helps build your eyes, ears, joints, and the cushions in your spine. In your body, that gene has a small change in it, so the collagen it makes is shaped a little differently than most people's.

That one change is why you need glasses (the collagen in your eyes affects how they focus), why your hearing is different (the collagen in your inner ear matters for how sound is processed), and why your back hurts sometimes (the cushions between your vertebrae need collagen to stay healthy).

It is called Marshall syndrome, and it is very rare — most doctors have never even seen it. That does not mean it is scary. It just means your family knows more about it than most doctors do, and part of growing up will be learning how to explain it to people.

You are not fragile. You can do almost everything other kids do. Some things we have to be careful about — like contact sports and trampolines — because of your eyes and spine. But swimming, cycling, all kinds of things are great for you. The goal is to keep your body as strong as possible so your muscles can help support the parts that are built a little differently."

At this age, children can handle the "why." Naming the gene and the protein gives them something concrete to understand, and it gives them language for when other kids ask. Emphasize what they CAN do, not just the restrictions.

Teens: Honest and Direct

"You are old enough for the full picture, so here it is.

You have a mutation in a gene called COL11A1. That gene makes type XI collagen, which is a structural protein found in your eyes, inner ears, cartilage, and intervertebral discs. The mutation means the collagen your body produces is structurally different, and that affects every tissue that depends on it.

The medical name is Marshall syndrome. It is autosomal dominant, which means you have one copy of the changed gene and one normal copy. If you have children someday, there is a 50% chance of passing it on. That is something you will be able to think about when the time comes — genetic counseling exists for exactly that.

The main risks you need to understand are: retinal detachment (this is why you cannot get hit in the head and why you need regular eye exams), progressive hearing loss (this may change over time and your audiologist will track it), and spinal degeneration (your discs are aging faster than a typical person's, which is why exercise and core strength matter so much).

Contact sports are off the table. That is not negotiable, and it is not your parents being overprotective — a retinal detachment is a surgical emergency, and the risk is real. But swimming, cycling, weight training with proper form, hiking, and most other activities are not just allowed, they are medicine.

The most important skill you can develop right now is self-advocacy. You are going to have to explain this condition to doctors, coaches, professors, employers, and partners for the rest of your life. The better you understand it, the better you can advocate for what you need. You are not a patient. You are a person who happens to need a few extra things. Learn to ask for them clearly and without apology."
Let them lead the conversation. Teenagers process medical information differently depending on their personality, maturity, and current emotional state. Some will want all the details immediately; others will absorb it in pieces over months. Offer the information and let them decide when they are ready for more. The Psychological Impact page covers the emotional dimensions of growing up with a rare condition.

6. To a New Specialist

When you see a specialist for the first time, you have about 60 seconds to establish credibility and give them the information relevant to their field. What an audiologist needs to hear is different from what an orthopedist needs to hear. These tailored openings get you past the "I've never heard of that" phase quickly.

To an Audiologist

"[Child] has Marshall syndrome — a COL11A1 collagen disorder on the Stickler spectrum. The hearing involvement is progressive sensorineural loss, typically bilateral and more pronounced in the high frequencies. [Child] was last tested on [date] and showed [describe current hearing levels]. We are monitoring for progression. The loss is caused by abnormal type XI collagen in the inner ear structures — specifically the tectorial membrane, which depends on collagen XI for its mechanical properties.

We need updated audiometry today and a comparison to the last baseline. We are particularly watching for any acceleration in the rate of loss. [Child] currently [uses hearing aids / does not yet use amplification / uses an FM system at school]."

To an Ophthalmologist

"[Child] has Marshall syndrome — COL11A1 mutation, type XI collagenopathy. Same gene as Stickler syndrome type II. The eye involvement includes high myopia (currently [prescription]), vitreous degeneration with [describe: empty vitreous / membranous anomaly / describe current status], and a lifetime retinal detachment risk of approximately 65%.

[Child] [has / has not] had a retinal detachment. We need a dilated fundus exam with attention to the peripheral retina, vitreous status, and any new lattice degeneration or retinal tears. We are here for [routine surveillance / specific symptom]."

To an Orthopedist or Spine Specialist

"[Child] has Marshall syndrome — a COL11A1 collagen disorder. Type XI collagen is a critical component of cartilage and intervertebral discs. The skeletal phenotype includes spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, premature disc degeneration, endplate abnormalities in approximately 74% of patients, and joint hypermobility. The literature reports chronic back pain in 67% of this population.

[Child] is currently experiencing [describe symptoms]. We also need to discuss cervical spine status — craniocervical instability is a known risk in COL11A1 patients, and we [have / have not / need to] obtain flexion-extension imaging."

To an ENT (Otolaryngologist)

"[Child] has Marshall syndrome — COL11A1 collagen disorder with midface hypoplasia, sensorineural hearing loss, and [describe: history of cleft palate / velopharyngeal insufficiency / chronic OME / other ENT-relevant features]. The midface anatomy creates a potentially difficult airway — this is important if any procedures requiring sedation are being considered.

We are here today because [specific concern]. Please note that if ear tubes are being considered, they may extrude earlier than expected due to connective tissue quality, and this may need to be a recurring intervention."

To a Geneticist (Second Opinion or New Provider)

"[Child] was diagnosed with Marshall syndrome based on [clinical features / genetic testing / both]. The confirmed variant is [describe mutation if known] in COL11A1. The phenotype includes [list the specific features present in your child]. We are establishing care with you because [reason: moved, seeking second opinion, need updated counseling].

We would like to discuss: current genotype-phenotype understanding for this specific variant, any updates on management recommendations, and [family planning implications / sibling testing / other specific questions]."
Bring the summary document. Every specialist visit should start with handing over a one-page medical summary. The Doctor Letter page has a template. Update it before each new specialist appointment with recent test results and current medications. A well-prepared parent earns immediate credibility with specialists — they know they are dealing with an informed family and adjust their communication accordingly.

7. To an Anesthesiologist Before Any Procedure

This is a safety-critical conversation. It should happen at a pre-procedure consultation, not on the day of surgery while your child is being prepped. If the surgical team does not offer a pre-procedure anesthesia consult, request one. For the full clinical discussion, see the Pre-Surgery Preparation Checklist.

The Script

"My child has Marshall syndrome — a COL11A1 collagen disorder, also classified as Stickler syndrome type II. I need to flag several anesthesia-specific concerns:

Anticipated difficult airway. [Child] has midface hypoplasia, micrognathia, and a [describe: Mallampati class if known, history of prior intubation difficulties]. Please have a video laryngoscope or fiberoptic scope available, and ideally a difficult airway cart. [Child] [has / has not] been intubated before, and [describe any prior airway difficulties].

Cervical spine instability risk. COL11A1 patients are at risk for craniocervical instability. Avoid hyperextension of the neck during intubation and positioning. Our most recent cervical imaging was [date] and showed [results]. The atlantodental interval was [measurement if known].

Local anesthetic resistance. Approximately 88% of patients with connective tissue disorders similar to this one report reduced effectiveness of local anesthetics. If regional blocks or local infiltration are part of the plan, higher doses or alternative agents may be needed. Please plan accordingly.

Eye precautions. [Child] has a lifetime retinal detachment risk of approximately 65%. Please avoid Valsalva maneuvers during anesthesia, position the head carefully, and avoid prone positioning when possible.

Wound healing. Defective collagen means wounds heal more slowly — expect 50 to 100% longer to reach adequate tensile strength. This is relevant for suture removal timing and post-operative expectations.

I have a pre-surgery letter with all of this documented, including our geneticist's contact information. Here is a copy for the chart."
This conversation saves lives. A difficult airway that is anticipated and prepared for is a manageable clinical challenge. A difficult airway that surprises an unprepared team can be catastrophic. The OrphanAnesthesia guidelines for Stickler syndrome are the gold-standard reference — print a copy and hand it to the anesthesiologist directly.

If They Say "We Do This All the Time"

"I appreciate that, and I am sure you are experienced. But this is not a standard pediatric airway. The combination of midface hypoplasia, potential cervical instability, and connective tissue fragility makes this a specific clinical scenario that requires specific preparation. I would feel much more comfortable knowing that a fiberoptic scope is available and that the cervical spine imaging has been reviewed. Can we confirm those two things?"

You are not being difficult. You are being a responsible advocate for your child. Any anesthesiologist who takes airway safety seriously will appreciate, not resent, a well-prepared parent.

General Advocacy Tips

Regardless of the audience, a few principles make these conversations easier over time:

  • Lead with the gene, not the name. "COL11A1 collagen disorder" communicates more to a medical professional than "Marshall syndrome." The name is rare; the gene is searchable.
  • Anchor to Stickler syndrome. More providers have heard of Stickler than Marshall. Saying "same gene as Stickler type II" gives them an instant lookup path.
  • Carry printed materials. A one-page summary, the emergency card, and the pre-surgery letter cover 90% of scenarios. Keep them in your bag, your car, and your child's school file.
  • Name the systems, not the complications. "It affects the eyes, ears, joints, and spine" is more digestible than leading with "retinal detachment risk, progressive hearing loss, and spinal degeneration."
  • Match the detail to the audience. The ER needs 30 seconds. A new specialist needs 5 minutes. A teacher needs a practical checklist. A friend needs warmth and reassurance. One script does not fit all.
  • Rehearse the hard versions. The ER script and the anesthesiologist script are safety-critical. Practice them out loud so that in a high-stress moment, the words come automatically.
  • Empower your child to speak for themselves. As they grow, shift from speaking for them to speaking with them to letting them speak while you listen. Self-advocacy is a lifelong skill that starts in childhood.
