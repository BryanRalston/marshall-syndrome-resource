Pregnancy & Obstetric Management with Marshall Syndrome

Pregnancy with Marshall syndrome is manageable — but it requires coordinated planning that starts well before conception. The combination of connective tissue fragility, retinal detachment risk, potential spinal involvement, and a 50% inheritance risk for the fetus means that unplanned or under-monitored pregnancies carry avoidable risks. With a prepared, multidisciplinary team, most people with Marshall syndrome have healthy pregnancies and healthy babies.

Pre-Conception Planning

Ideally, individuals with Marshall syndrome considering pregnancy will engage in pre-conception planning with both their obstetrician and their genetics team. Pre-conception planning is not about discouraging pregnancy — it is about going in with a plan rather than discovering problems mid-pregnancy.

Genetic Counseling Review

Marshall syndrome is caused by pathogenic variants in the COL11A1 gene and follows autosomal dominant inheritance. This means:

  • Each pregnancy carries a 50% chance of inheriting the COL11A1 variant from an affected parent, regardless of sex.
  • The severity and specific manifestations of Marshall syndrome vary considerably even within families carrying the same variant — a future child with the mutation might have a milder or more severe phenotype than the affected parent.
  • A small percentage of Marshall syndrome cases arise from de novo mutations, but once established in a family, the 50% transmission rule applies.
  • Genetic counseling provides a framework for understanding these probabilities and for exploring reproductive options without judgment. It is not decision-making on behalf of the patient — it is information provision.
Variability matters: Having Marshall syndrome does not mean a future child will have the same experience. Some individuals with COL11A1 variants have primarily hearing and ocular involvement with little joint disease. Others have significant skeletal involvement. This variability is important context for genetic counseling conversations.

Reproductive Options for Known Carriers

Several reproductive options exist for individuals who wish to minimize the likelihood of transmitting the COL11A1 variant to a child. These are personal choices with no universally "correct" answer:

  • Natural conception and prenatal testing: Conceiving naturally, then using prenatal diagnostic testing to determine whether the fetus has inherited the variant. Testing options include chorionic villus sampling (CVS) at 10–12 weeks gestation, or amniocentesis at 15–18 weeks. Both require knowing the specific family COL11A1 variant sequence in advance. Results are definitive but come after a pregnancy is already established.
  • Preimplantation Genetic Testing for Monogenic disorders (PGT-M): Available through IVF, PGT-M allows embryos to be screened before transfer. Only embryos that did not inherit the COL11A1 variant are selected for transfer. This approach avoids the decision point of prenatal diagnosis mid-pregnancy but requires IVF, which is an intensive process and not always successful. Insurance coverage varies widely; families should consult with a reproductive endocrinologist and their insurance carrier.
  • Donor gametes: Using donor egg or donor sperm from an unaffected individual eliminates the transmission risk on one side of the equation.
  • Adoption or non-genetic paths to parenthood: Valid choices that some families prefer.
  • Natural conception without testing: Also a valid choice. Many adults with Marshall syndrome choose to proceed without prenatal diagnosis, accepting the 50% probability and planning for early evaluation of the newborn. This is not a medical failure — it is a personal decision.
Know the variant first: Any molecular testing approach (PGT-M, CVS, amniocentesis) requires that the specific COL11A1 variant in the family has been identified by prior clinical genetic testing. If you are considering pregnancy and do not yet have a confirmed variant on file, request this information from your genetics team before beginning IVF or conception.

A pre-conception medication review with both the prescribing specialist and the obstetrician is essential. Several categories of medications commonly used in Marshall syndrome require evaluation:

Building the High-Risk Pregnancy Team

Marshall syndrome pregnancy is appropriately classified as high-risk due to the combination of connective tissue fragility, potential cervical incompetence, retinal detachment risk, complex anesthesia considerations, and the possibility of Pierre Robin sequence in an affected fetus. This does not mean the pregnancy will be complicated — it means that the potential for complication is elevated enough to warrant specialist involvement.

Why Marshall Syndrome Is a High-Risk Pregnancy

  • COL11A1 encodes type XI collagen, which is a structural component of the cervix. Cervical insufficiency is a recognized risk in collagen disorders, including Stickler and Marshall syndrome.
  • Pre-existing retinal pathology and vitreous abnormalities create risk around the second stage of labor (pushing), requiring individualized delivery planning.
  • Scoliosis, prior spinal instrumentation, or dural abnormalities (dural ectasia) complicate epidural and spinal anesthesia placement.
  • Midface hypoplasia and micrognathia (present in some Marshall syndrome individuals) may create an airway management challenge under general anesthesia.
  • Joint laxity worsens throughout pregnancy due to relaxin, requiring physiotherapy support and activity modification.
  • If the fetus inherits the COL11A1 mutation, neonatal complications (Pierre Robin sequence, early visual and hearing impairment) require anticipatory neonatology planning.

The Core Team

The following specialists should ideally be identified and briefed before the third trimester:

Request a "team huddle" before 32 weeks: Ask your MFM to facilitate a shared care plan (even if just a written summary circulated among providers, not a physical meeting) that confirms every team member's role and agreed-upon delivery plan before the third trimester. This prevents the situation where labor begins and the anesthesiologist is reading about Marshall syndrome for the first time at 2 a.m.

Cervical Insufficiency & Preterm Birth Risk

Cervical insufficiency is one of the most clinically significant pregnancy risks in connective tissue disorders, and recent evidence from EDS research provides quantitative data that is directly relevant to Marshall syndrome. While Marshall syndrome involves type XI collagen rather than the types I, III, and V collagen affected in most EDS subtypes, the cervix is a collagen-dependent structure where any collagen defect can compromise mechanical integrity.

EDS Obstetric Risk Data — Applicable to Marshall Syndrome

Large population-level studies of Ehlers-Danlos syndrome pregnancies have quantified specific obstetric risks that are driven by collagen deficiency in reproductive tissues. These risks are expected to apply, at least in part, to any collagen disorder affecting cervical and uterine tissue including Marshall syndrome:

  • Cervical insufficiency: 3.11× more likely than in unaffected pregnancies. The cervix depends on organized collagen for mechanical strength; defective collagen reduces the ability of the cervix to remain closed under the increasing weight of the growing uterus.
  • Premature delivery: 1.47× more likely. This is partly a downstream consequence of cervical insufficiency and partly driven by collagen-deficient fetal membranes that are weaker and more prone to premature rupture.
  • Antepartum hemorrhage: 1.71× more likely. Connective tissue integrity in the placental attachment zone and uterine vasculature is compromised, increasing the risk of bleeding before delivery.

Fetal Membrane Fragility

The fetal membranes (chorion and amnion) are collagen-rich structures that must withstand progressive mechanical stress throughout pregnancy. In collagen disorders:

  • Collagen-deficient fetal membranes are structurally weaker and predispose to preterm premature rupture of membranes (PPROM)
  • If the fetus has also inherited the COL11A1 variant (50% probability), the membranes have a double contribution of abnormal collagen — from both maternal tissue and fetal tissue
  • PPROM is one of the leading causes of preterm delivery and carries risks of neonatal infection, pulmonary immaturity, and prolonged NICU admission
  • There is currently no effective intervention to strengthen fetal membranes, making early detection of cervical changes even more critical

Cervical Length Monitoring Protocol

Given the elevated cervical insufficiency risk, proactive cervical monitoring should begin earlier and occur more frequently than in standard pregnancies:

  • Transvaginal ultrasound cervical length measurement starting at 16 weeks — earlier than the 18–20 week starting point used in some standard high-risk protocols
  • Repeat measurements every 2 weeks through 24 weeks, then as clinically indicated
  • Cervical length ≥25 mm at 24 weeks is generally reassuring; <25 mm warrants immediate MFM discussion about intervention
  • Look for funneling (internal os dilation), dynamic cervical shortening, and sludge in the cervical canal as additional warning signs

Cerclage Consideration

If cervical shortening is detected, or if there is a history of second-trimester loss or preterm delivery, cervical cerclage should be discussed promptly:

  • History-indicated cerclage (prophylactic, placed at 12–14 weeks) should be strongly considered in any Marshall syndrome pregnancy with a prior history of cervical insufficiency or preterm birth
  • Ultrasound-indicated cerclage (placed when shortening is detected, typically 16–24 weeks) is the intervention for patients without prior adverse history who develop cervical changes on surveillance
  • The MFM specialist should be aware that connective tissue in the cervix may hold sutures differently than in unaffected tissue — suture technique and material selection should account for this

Wound Closure Considerations for Cesarean Section

If cesarean delivery is required, the surgical team should apply extended wound closure protocols appropriate to connective tissue disorders:

  • Layered closure with attention to fascial and subcutaneous layers where collagen integrity is reduced
  • Non-absorbable sutures for skin closure or extended-retention absorbable sutures — standard absorbable sutures may lose tensile strength before the wound has healed sufficiently in a collagen disorder
  • Suture retention 2–3× longer than standard protocols — see the Wound Healing page for detailed timing recommendations
  • Post-operative wound support with Steri-Strips for 4–6 weeks after suture removal

Postpartum Pelvic Floor Monitoring

The pelvic floor depends on connective tissue for structural support. In Marshall syndrome, where collagen integrity is already compromised, the mechanical demands of pregnancy and delivery can produce more significant pelvic floor changes than in unaffected individuals:

  • Formal pelvic floor assessment by a pelvic floor physiotherapist should be arranged within 6–8 weeks postpartum, regardless of delivery mode
  • Monitor for signs of pelvic organ prolapse (heaviness, pressure, visible bulge) which may develop earlier and more readily in connective tissue disorders
  • A structured pelvic floor rehabilitation program — focused on strengthening rather than stretching — should begin once cleared by the physiotherapist
  • Long-term pelvic floor monitoring is appropriate, as symptoms may develop months to years after delivery rather than immediately postpartum

Trimester-by-Trimester Concerns

First Trimester (Weeks 1–13)

Early Viability Monitoring

No Marshall syndrome-specific concerns alter the standard first-trimester ultrasound schedule, but early establishment of care with the MFM team is important. Confirm gestational dating, fetal cardiac activity, and absence of early structural anomalies.

Cervical Competence Assessment

Cervical insufficiency (inability of the cervix to maintain a pregnancy to term due to structural weakness) is a recognized risk in connective tissue disorders. In the first trimester:

  • Discuss prior cervical history: previous preterm birth, late miscarriage (second trimester), prior cervical procedures (LEEP, cone biopsy), or any prior cervical weakness concerns.
  • Baseline cervical length measurement by transvaginal ultrasound can be obtained in the late first trimester (around 12–14 weeks) to establish a reference point.
  • A history of second-trimester pregnancy loss or preterm labor should prompt discussion of prophylactic cerclage (cervical stitch) with the MFM specialist.

Medication Transition

First trimester is the window to complete medication transitions: stop any contraindicated medications, confirm prenatal vitamin compliance, and adjust pain management protocols to pregnancy-compatible options.

Genetic Testing Decision

If prenatal diagnosis is being pursued, CVS is performed at 10–12 weeks. This requires prior arrangement with the genetics team to ensure the laboratory is set up to analyze the specific family COL11A1 variant. Standard chromosomal microarray (offered routinely in many centers) does not detect COL11A1 point mutations — a targeted molecular test must be ordered.

Standard prenatal cell-free fetal DNA (NIPT) screening does not detect COL11A1 variants. NIPT screens for chromosomal aneuploidy (Down syndrome, trisomy 18, etc.) and some structural variants, but it does not analyze single-gene disorders like Marshall syndrome. Specific molecular testing (CVS or amniocentesis with targeted COL11A1 analysis) is required if fetal genetic status is being assessed.

Second Trimester (Weeks 14–27)

Serial Cervical Length Monitoring

Transvaginal cervical length surveillance is standard in individuals at risk for cervical insufficiency. The typical protocol begins around 16–18 weeks and may continue every 2–4 weeks depending on findings:

  • Cervical length above 25 mm at 24 weeks is generally reassuring.
  • Cervical length below 25 mm at 24 weeks is associated with significantly elevated preterm birth risk and warrants intervention discussion.
  • Funneling (dilation of the internal os) or other cervical shortening signs should be reported promptly to the MFM team.

Cerclage Consideration

Cervical cerclage (a surgical stitch placed around the cervix to reinforce it) may be recommended in individuals with cervical insufficiency or a history of second-trimester loss. There are three main types, and timing matters:

  • History-indicated cerclage: Placed prophylactically at 12–14 weeks based on prior cervical history and connective tissue disorder diagnosis.
  • Ultrasound-indicated cerclage: Placed at 16–24 weeks when cervical shortening is detected on surveillance ultrasound, even without a prior adverse pregnancy history.
  • Physical exam-indicated cerclage: Placed as a rescue procedure when cervical dilation is found on physical examination, typically at 16–24 weeks. The evidence for rescue cerclage in connective tissue disorders is extrapolated largely from general high-risk obstetrics data.

Discuss cerclage candidacy with the MFM specialist at the first prenatal visit — not when cervical shortening is already detected, as that may compress the decision timeline.

Anatomy Scan

The standard anatomy ultrasound at 18–22 weeks evaluates fetal structural development. If fetal COL11A1 status is known or suspected (or prenatal testing was not performed), the anatomy scan can be reviewed with particular attention to:

  • Craniofacial features: midface development, mandible size (micrognathia / Pierre Robin sequence)
  • Cleft palate (secondary cleft palate is a feature of Marshall syndrome; it is not always visible on prenatal ultrasound but severe cases may be detectable)
  • Limb length and proportions
  • Note: many Marshall syndrome features (vitreous abnormalities, sensorineural hearing loss, joint hypermobility) are not detectable prenatally by ultrasound

Ophthalmology Evaluation

A dilated retinal examination in the second trimester provides a mid-pregnancy baseline. Findings at this visit will inform the delivery planning discussion, particularly regarding the safety of Valsalva-heavy pushing. Document the current status of:

  • Retinal tears or lattice degeneration (active vs. previously treated)
  • Vitreous traction patterns
  • Any new floaters, flashes, or visual field changes since the last visit

Progesterone Supplementation

For individuals with a shortened cervix or prior preterm birth, vaginal progesterone supplementation (17-OHPC or vaginal progesterone gel) may be recommended to reduce preterm birth risk. Discuss eligibility with the MFM team.

Joint changes in the second trimester: Relaxin levels rise significantly in the second trimester, and joint laxity increases correspondingly. Many individuals with Marshall syndrome notice a meaningful worsening of joint pain, pelvic girdle instability, and balance changes during this period. Pelvic girdle belts, gait aids, and physical therapy referral are appropriate responses — not signs that something has "gone wrong."

Third Trimester (Weeks 28–40+)

Delivery Planning Conference

A formal delivery planning discussion should occur by 32–34 weeks. This is when the MFM specialist, anesthesiologist, and patient (ideally with supporting documentation from the ophthalmologist) agree on the planned delivery approach. Key decisions to finalize:

  • Vaginal delivery vs. cesarean section — and the rationale
  • Epidural vs. spinal vs. general anesthesia preference and backup plans
  • Whether the patient has scoliosis, spinal instrumentation, or dural abnormalities that affect anesthetic approach
  • Airway management plan (particularly if midface hypoplasia or limited mouth opening is present)
  • Plan for fetal/neonatal team preparation if the baby may be affected

Pre-Delivery Anesthesia Consultation

A formal anesthesia consultation before labor is one of the most important safeguards in a Marshall syndrome pregnancy. Do not leave this to chance. The consultation should include:

  • Review of spine imaging (MRI or X-ray) to evaluate for scoliosis, prior instrumentation levels, or dural ectasia
  • Physical assessment of airway anatomy including mouth opening, jaw mobility, neck range of motion
  • Discussion of epidural vs. spinal vs. combined spinal-epidural options and their technical challenges
  • Written documentation of the agreed approach in the hospital record so it is available when labor begins

Third-Trimester Retinal Examination

A dilated retinal examination in the third trimester (typically 34–36 weeks) provides a final pre-delivery assessment. If new retinal pathology has developed or progressed, delivery planning may be modified. A written ophthalmology letter summarizing retinal status and any recommendations for delivery should be placed in the obstetric record.

Fetal Surveillance

Non-stress tests (NST) or biophysical profiles (BPP) in the third trimester are standard for high-risk pregnancies. The schedule is determined by the MFM team based on individual clinical status.

Delivery Considerations

The delivery room is not the time to explain Marshall syndrome to the team for the first time. Every provider who will be present — OB, anesthesiologist, neonatologist, nursing staff — should have been briefed in advance. A written "Marshall Syndrome Pregnancy Summary" document placed in the hospital record, and brought in hard copy to labor and delivery, serves this function. This document should list the key considerations: retinal risk, anesthesia challenges, airway precautions, and neonatal preparation.

Vaginal Delivery vs. Cesarean Section

Marshall syndrome alone is not an automatic indication for cesarean section. The decision is individualized. Key considerations:

Retinal Detachment Risk and Pushing

The historical concern that Valsalva maneuvers during the second stage of labor (pushing) could precipitate retinal detachment has been evaluated in case reports and case series in Stickler syndrome (the closely related type XI collagenopathy). The current evidence suggests:

  • A history of retinal pathology alone is not a contraindication to vaginal delivery. Individuals with prior retinal tears or detachments that have been treated successfully and remain stable may be appropriate candidates for vaginal delivery.
  • Active, untreated, or unstable retinal pathology is a different matter. Untreated retinal tears, recent retinal detachment repair (less than 6–12 weeks), or active vitreous traction on the retina warrant direct consultation with the vitreoretinal specialist before delivery planning is finalized.
  • Shortened second stage (instrumental delivery with vacuum or forceps) can reduce the duration and intensity of Valsalva, which some centers use as an intermediate option for patients with retinal concerns who are otherwise good vaginal delivery candidates. This approach should be discussed with the MFM team.
  • The vitreoretinal specialist's written assessment of individual retinal risk is the key document informing this decision.
Evidence on Delivery Mode — Vaginal Delivery Generally Safe
  • Limited but reassuring case literature: Only one case report in the literature specifically addresses delivery planning in Marshall/Stickler type I syndrome (Gomez et al. 2020, Molecular Genetics & Genomic Medicine). A multidisciplinary team including obstetrics, ophthalmology, maternal-fetal medicine, and genetics determined there was no contraindication to vaginal delivery based on the patient’s retinal and connective tissue status.
  • Obstetrician survey data: A survey of 185 obstetricians found that 57% would recommend assisted (instrumental) delivery after prior retinal detachment surgery — however, the evidence shows that a history of eye disease alone is not an indication for cesarean section. The decision should be based on current retinal stability, not on the diagnosis itself.
  • Cervical length screening recommended: Given the collagen-dependent structure of the cervix, cervical length screening by transvaginal ultrasound is recommended at the anatomy scan (18–22 weeks) and again at 23 weeks to monitor for cervical insufficiency.
  • Airway preparation is essential: Micrognathia and midface hypoplasia may complicate intubation if general anesthesia becomes necessary. The anesthesia team should be prepared with fiberoptic intubation equipment and video laryngoscopy, and an airway assessment should be documented well before labor.
  • Preconception genetic counseling is essential: Given the 50% transmission risk for COL11A1 variants, genetic counseling should be completed before conception whenever possible — not as an afterthought once pregnancy is established.

Cesarean Section When Indicated

When cesarean section is recommended (whether for obstetric indications, retinal concerns, or other reasons), several Marshall syndrome-specific considerations apply:

  • Collagen disorder affects wound healing. Use of subcutaneous sutures and skin closure that minimizes tension is advisable. See the Wound Healing page for context on collagen-related wound considerations.
  • Post-operative pain management must account for the pre-existing pain condition and prior medication regimen.
  • Mobility in the early postoperative period may be more limited due to baseline joint and spinal issues; physical therapy involvement from day one is recommended.

Anesthesia During Labor and Delivery

Anesthesia management is among the most complex aspects of Marshall syndrome delivery. The challenges are multi-layered and depend on the individual's specific anatomy and history.

Epidural Analgesia

Epidural catheter placement is the standard approach for labor pain management and is preferred over general anesthesia for cesarean section when feasible. Challenges in Marshall syndrome include:

  • Scoliosis: Curvature of the spine alters the landmarks used to identify the epidural space. The anesthesiologist may need to use ultrasound guidance to locate a viable access point between vertebrae. The consent discussion should include the possibility that epidural placement may require more attempts than usual or may not be achievable at the ideal level.
  • Prior spinal instrumentation: Posterior spinal fusion with rods and screws (used to treat scoliosis in Marshall syndrome) may cover multiple lumbar levels, making epidural access at those levels impossible. The anesthesiologist needs to review the surgical report and post-operative imaging to determine which levels, if any, are accessible. In some cases, epidural access below the fusion is possible; in others, alternative strategies are needed.
  • Dural ectasia: Enlargement or thinning of the dural sac is a feature of some collagen disorders. When present, it can alter the anatomy of the epidural space and increase the risk of inadvertent dural puncture (a needle going through the dura into the CSF space, causing a post-dural puncture headache). Discuss whether prior imaging has evaluated for dural ectasia.

Spinal Anesthesia

Spinal anesthesia (a single injection into the CSF space) is often used for cesarean section. In Marshall syndrome:

  • The same anatomical challenges (scoliosis, instrumentation) apply as with epidural placement.
  • If dural ectasia is present, CSF pressure dynamics may be altered, and the spread of spinal anesthesia may be unpredictable.
  • The risk of post-dural puncture headache (PDPH) must be discussed, particularly in the context of dural abnormalities.

General Anesthesia

General anesthesia may become necessary if neuraxial anesthesia (epidural or spinal) fails or is not feasible. Marshall syndrome individuals may present specific airway challenges:

  • Midface hypoplasia: The characteristic flat midface seen in many Marshall syndrome patients alters the fit of a face mask and can make bag-mask ventilation more difficult.
  • Micrognathia: A smaller lower jaw reduces the space available for direct laryngoscopy and intubation. Awake fiberoptic intubation or video laryngoscopy should be available and the anesthesiologist should be prepared to use them.
  • TMJ limitations: Temporomandibular joint involvement (a recognized feature of Marshall syndrome) can limit mouth opening, further complicating intubation. The maximum mouth opening should be assessed at the pre-delivery anesthesia consultation.
  • Cervical spine: If there is cervical spine stenosis, instability, or prior surgery, neck manipulation during intubation must be done with care. In-line stabilization and the avoidance of extreme neck extension are important.
Airway assessment is mandatory before every Marshall syndrome delivery. If the anesthesiologist at the pre-delivery consultation identifies a potentially difficult airway, they should document this assessment in the medical record and notify the on-call anesthesia team. The delivery should, when feasible, occur in a center with access to advanced airway equipment (video laryngoscope, fiberoptic bronchoscope, surgical airway capability).

Postpartum Period

The postpartum period brings its own set of Marshall syndrome-specific considerations that are often overlooked in the focus on delivery.

Joint Instability and Relaxin Persistence

Relaxin, the hormone responsible for softening ligaments during pregnancy, does not disappear immediately after delivery. In non-breastfeeding individuals, relaxin levels typically normalize within 3 months postpartum. In breastfeeding individuals, relaxin levels may remain elevated for the duration of lactation, as prolactin suppresses the recovery of normal ligamentous tone.

For individuals with Marshall syndrome — who already have baseline connective tissue fragility — this means the worsened joint laxity of pregnancy may persist well into the postpartum period. Practical implications:

  • Pelvic girdle pain, sacroiliac joint dysfunction, and generalized joint instability may not resolve as quickly as expected postpartum.
  • Physical therapy should continue postpartum, with emphasis on progressive strengthening rather than passive stretching (hypermobility does not benefit from further stretching).
  • Heavy lifting (including carrying a growing infant) is a significant mechanical load on already-lax joints. Discuss baby-wearing ergonomics and lifting mechanics with the physical therapist.
  • Joint supports (knee braces, wrist supports, sacroiliac belts) may be appropriate during this period.
  • Inform the physical therapist and any new providers that this is a connective tissue disorder, not simple postpartum deconditioning — the approach to rehabilitation differs.

Breastfeeding with Hearing Aids

Breastfeeding is fully compatible with sensorineural hearing loss and hearing aid use, but some practical considerations help:

  • Positioning: Standard breastfeeding positions (cradle hold, football hold) can place the ear with a hearing aid against a pillow or the infant's head, causing feedback (whistling) or dislodging the device. Side-lying positions and positions that keep the hearing aids away from compression points are worth experimenting with. A lactation consultant familiar with disability-related breastfeeding challenges can help.
  • Nighttime feeding: Many individuals with hearing loss remove their devices at night. This means nighttime feeding may occur without hearing aid amplification. Vibrating baby monitors, apps that turn visual alerts into notifications, and co-sleeping safety planning (where appropriate) are options to discuss.
  • Communication with care partners: The person doing nighttime feeding without their devices may miss sounds from other children or household events. A communication plan with a co-parenting partner helps.

Postpartum Depression Screening

Standard postpartum depression (PPD) screening is recommended for all postpartum individuals. For those with Marshall syndrome, there are reasons to be particularly attentive:

  • Chronic illness is an established risk factor for postpartum depression. The combination of chronic pain, functional limitations, fatigue, and the demands of newborn care creates a vulnerable period.
  • Sleep disruption, which is nearly universal with a newborn, is particularly detrimental in the context of a pain condition. Poor sleep worsens pain perception, mood, and cognitive function — creating a self-reinforcing cycle.
  • The experience of parenting a child who may also have Marshall syndrome (if the infant is affected) introduces additional layers of anticipatory grief, guilt, and worry that are distinct from typical new-parent anxiety.
  • Standard PPD screening tools (Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale) are appropriate but do not capture the full range of chronic illness-specific psychological burden. If scores are below threshold but the individual reports significant distress, a referral to a psychologist experienced with chronic illness and perinatal mental health is appropriate regardless.
Proactively establish postpartum mental health support before delivery. Identify a therapist or psychologist (ideally one familiar with chronic illness and perinatal mental health) before the baby arrives, so that access is available immediately if needed rather than requiring a new referral search during a difficult postpartum period. See Psychological Support for further guidance.

Return to Exercise and Activity

Standard postpartum return-to-exercise guidelines (typically beginning gentle activity at 6 weeks postpartum) require modification for Marshall syndrome:

  • Pelvic floor assessment before return to impact: Pelvic floor physiotherapy assessment is recommended for all postpartum individuals regardless of delivery mode. For Marshall syndrome individuals, pelvic floor connective tissue is subject to the same fragility as other connective tissue; pelvic floor prolapse and dysfunction may occur earlier or more severely. Clearance from pelvic floor physiotherapy should precede return to running, jumping, or heavy lifting.
  • Joint loading: High-impact activities (running, HIIT, jumping sports) should be reintroduced gradually and with attention to joint response. Swelling, pain lasting more than 24 hours after activity, or joint instability are signs to slow down.
  • Core and spinal considerations: If there is spinal instrumentation or a history of spinal surgery, return to exercise should be guided in consultation with the spinal surgeon or physiotherapist.
  • Timeline varies widely: Some individuals with Marshall syndrome may be physically capable of light return to exercise at 8–10 weeks postpartum; others, particularly those with significant joint inflammation postpartum, may need a longer recovery. Functional progress, not calendar dates, should guide return to activity.

Postpartum Musculoskeletal Recovery

Postpartum recovery in Marshall syndrome goes beyond standard obstetric recovery. The combination of relaxin-mediated joint laxity on top of baseline connective tissue fragility creates a prolonged vulnerability window that requires structured rehabilitation, not simply “waiting to feel better.”

Pelvic Girdle Pain

Pelvic girdle pain (PGP) — pain in the symphysis pubis, sacroiliac joints, or both — is significantly more common in hypermobile individuals. Research in hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS) populations shows a PGP prevalence of approximately 26%, compared to roughly 7% in the general postpartum population. While Marshall syndrome is a distinct disorder, the shared connective tissue fragility makes this data directly relevant. PGP that develops during pregnancy often persists or worsens postpartum because:

  • The pelvic ligaments (sacrotuberous, sacrospinous, anterior sacroiliac) have been stretched by relaxin on a background of already-lax collagen
  • Abdominal muscle weakening during pregnancy reduces the force-closure mechanism that stabilizes the pelvis
  • Postpartum activities (lifting, carrying, feeding positions) place asymmetric loads on an unstable pelvis
Sacroiliac belt: An SI belt worn at the level of the greater trochanters (not at the waist) provides external force closure to the pelvis and can significantly reduce PGP symptoms during the postpartum recovery period. It is a bridging measure while muscle strength is rebuilt, not a permanent solution.

Diastasis Recti

Diastasis recti abdominis (DRA) — separation of the rectus abdominis muscles along the linea alba — occurs in approximately two-thirds of all pregnancies. In Marshall syndrome and other connective tissue disorders, the recovery of DRA is typically slower and less complete because the linea alba is a fascial structure composed primarily of collagen. When that collagen is inherently weaker, the “bounce-back” that many postpartum individuals experience simply does not occur to the same degree.

  • Assessment: DRA should be formally assessed by a pelvic floor physiotherapist at the 6-week postpartum visit. A separation of more than 2 finger-widths, or a linea alba that does not generate tension under load (it feels soft rather than taut when the patient does a head lift), indicates functional DRA requiring rehabilitation.
  • Rehabilitation sequence: The critical principle is deep core before superficial abdominal work. Begin with transversus abdominis activation (the deepest abdominal layer) and pelvic floor co-contraction before progressing to obliques and finally rectus exercises. Crunches, sit-ups, and planks should be deferred until deep core control is established and the DRA gap is closing — loading the rectus too early can worsen the separation.
  • Timeline: In the general population, DRA often resolves or significantly improves by 6–12 months postpartum. In connective tissue disorders, expect a longer course — 12–18 months of structured rehabilitation is not unusual, and some degree of persistent widening may remain.

Pelvic Floor Rehabilitation

Pelvic floor dysfunction (incontinence, prolapse, dyspareunia, pelvic pressure) is more prevalent in hypermobile populations postpartum. The pelvic floor is a muscular and fascial structure — both components are affected in connective tissue disorders.

  • Timing: Pelvic floor physiotherapy should begin within 6 weeks postpartum and continue for 6–12 months as a structured program, not a single consultation. The extended duration reflects the slower fascial recovery in collagen disorders.
  • Assessment first: Do not assume the pelvic floor needs strengthening — in some hypermobile individuals, the pelvic floor muscles are actually hypertonic (too tight) as a compensatory response to lax ligaments. An assessment by a pelvic floor physiotherapist differentiates between weakness (needs strengthening), hypertonicity (needs down-training and release), and mixed presentation (needs both, sequentially).
  • Progressive loading: Once pelvic floor control is established, gradual exposure to increasing loads — walking, stairs, carrying the infant, and eventually impact activities — should be guided by symptom response. Any return of heaviness, leaking, or pelvic pressure indicates the load has exceeded the current capacity of the pelvic floor.

Relaxin Normalization Timeline

Relaxin, the primary hormone responsible for ligamentous laxity during pregnancy, follows a variable normalization timeline postpartum:

  • In non-breastfeeding individuals, relaxin levels typically return to pre-pregnancy baseline within 3 months
  • In breastfeeding individuals, elevated prolactin may maintain some degree of ligamentous laxity for the duration of lactation
  • Full recovery of joint stability takes 3–6 months after relaxin normalizes — the ligaments must remodel and shorten, which takes time even in normal connective tissue. In Marshall syndrome, this remodeling may be slower and less complete.
  • During this window, joint protection strategies (bracing, ergonomic baby-care setups, limiting repetitive loading) reduce the risk of new injuries or joint subluxation events

Postpartum Depression: Elevated Risk with Chronic Pain

The intersection of chronic pain, sleep disruption, hormonal shifts, and the physical demands of newborn care places individuals with Marshall syndrome at higher risk for postpartum depression and anxiety. This is discussed in the Postpartum Depression Screening section above, but the musculoskeletal recovery context adds another dimension: postpartum pain that worsens or fails to improve can itself drive depressive symptoms. Monitoring mood alongside physical recovery — not as separate tracks — ensures that neither is overlooked.

The recovery sequence matters. Postpartum musculoskeletal rehabilitation in Marshall syndrome should follow this order: (1) pelvic floor assessment and initial activation, (2) deep core recruitment (transversus abdominis, multifidus), (3) DRA closure through progressive loading, (4) superficial abdominal and global strengthening, (5) return to impact activities. Skipping steps — particularly jumping to crunches or planks before deep core control is established — risks worsening both DRA and pelvic floor dysfunction.

Newborn Considerations for Affected Infants

If prenatal genetic testing was not performed, or if testing confirmed fetal inheritance of the COL11A1 variant, the delivery team should be prepared for the possibility of a newborn with Marshall syndrome manifestations. The most acute neonatal concern is Pierre Robin sequence.

Pierre Robin Sequence in the Newborn

Pierre Robin sequence (micrognathia, glossoptosis, and often a U-shaped cleft palate) can cause significant airway compromise at birth. In an infant with a known or suspected COL11A1 mutation:

  • Neonatology should be present at delivery, briefed in advance.
  • If micrognathia was detected on prenatal anatomy ultrasound, airway management equipment for a small jaw airway should be at bedside.
  • Many infants with Pierre Robin sequence can be managed with positioning (prone positioning to prevent the tongue from falling back) without intubation. Severe cases may require nasopharyngeal airway or intubation.
  • Cleft palate affects feeding; early lactation consultant and feeding specialist involvement is important.
  • Most Pierre Robin sequence infants with Marshall syndrome improve as the mandible grows in infancy and early childhood.

Early Screening for COL11A1-Related Features

For newborns known or suspected to carry a COL11A1 variant, early referrals should be arranged before or immediately after discharge:

SpecialtyTimingRationale
Ophthalmology Within first 3 months; ideally within the first month High myopia, vitreous membrane abnormalities, and lens changes should be identified early. Untreated refractive errors in infancy can cause amblyopia (lazy eye). Early baseline evaluation establishes surveillance frequency.
Audiology Newborn hearing screen (universal); formal ABR testing if screen is abnormal Sensorineural hearing loss in Marshall syndrome may not be present at birth or may be mild enough to pass the newborn screen, but early audiological monitoring establishes the baseline. Failing the newborn screen warrants urgent ABR and ENT evaluation.
Genetics Within the first few weeks if not pre-established Confirms diagnosis, counsels on management expectations, coordinates surveillance planning, and establishes the genetic record for the child's lifelong care.
Craniofacial / ENT Within the first weeks if cleft palate or significant micrognathia is present Cleft palate requires a structured team approach (feeding, surgical planning, speech therapy). Craniofacial team programs at children's hospitals provide coordinated care. If no cleft, ENT referral timing is guided by hearing screen results and clinical presentation.
Pediatric orthopedics As clinically indicated; not urgent in the newborn period unless structural concerns are present Baseline joint assessment is useful as the child grows. If there is significant joint laxity or limb alignment concerns noted in infancy, earlier referral is appropriate.
Normal newborn development is possible and common. Not every infant with a COL11A1 mutation will have Pierre Robin sequence or obvious manifestations at birth. Some affected children are clinically quiet in infancy and present later with myopia, hearing loss, or joint hypermobility in toddlerhood or school age. Early surveillance catches these children before complications develop.

Pre-Birth Planning Checklist

Use this checklist to guide conversations with your medical team before your due date. Ideally, all items should be addressed by 34–36 weeks gestation.

With Your MFM / Obstetrician

  • ☐ High-risk pregnancy classification confirmed; MFM team established
  • ☐ Cervical length monitoring schedule established
  • ☐ Cerclage discussed and decision documented (proceed or not)
  • ☐ Vaginal delivery vs. cesarean section plan documented
  • ☐ Second stage management plan documented (normal pushing vs. shortened second stage vs. instrumental delivery)
  • ☐ Fetal surveillance schedule established for third trimester
  • ☐ Neonatology notified if fetal COL11A1 status suggests potential Pierre Robin sequence
  • ☐ Postpartum physiotherapy referral arranged

With Anesthesiology (Pre-Labor Consultation)

  • ☐ Spine imaging reviewed (scoliosis, instrumentation levels, accessible epidural space)
  • ☐ Dural ectasia assessed or ruled out
  • ☐ Airway examination performed (mouth opening, mandible size, neck mobility)
  • ☐ Primary anesthetic plan documented in hospital record
  • ☐ Backup anesthetic plans discussed (if epidural placement fails; if general anesthesia is required)
  • ☐ Advanced airway equipment confirmed to be available at delivery center

With Vitreoretinal Ophthalmologist

  • ☐ Third-trimester dilated retinal examination completed
  • ☐ Written ophthalmology letter summarizing retinal status and delivery recommendations placed in obstetric record
  • ☐ Any active retinal pathology treated or documented with delivery implications explained to OB/MFM

With Genetics / Genetic Counselor

  • ☐ Genetic counseling completed regarding inheritance risk
  • ☐ Prenatal testing decision made (tested, declined, or still pending) and documented
  • ☐ If fetal variant status known, genetics team has briefed neonatology and relevant neonatal specialists
  • ☐ Plan for newborn genetic testing (if not prenatally confirmed) documented

Newborn Referrals to Arrange Before Birth

  • ☐ Neonatology alerted and delivery plan communicated
  • ☐ Pediatric ophthalmology referral arranged for first month of life
  • ☐ Pediatric audiology follow-up plan confirmed (in addition to universal newborn screen)
  • ☐ Pediatric genetics appointment scheduled within first weeks
  • ☐ Craniofacial / ENT team notified if cleft palate or significant Pierre Robin is expected

Postpartum Self-Care Planning

  • ☐ Postpartum pain management plan discussed with OB and pain management team
  • ☐ Pelvic floor physiotherapy referral arranged (first appointment within 6–8 weeks postpartum)
  • ☐ Postpartum mental health support identified (therapist or psychologist contact established before delivery)
  • ☐ Relaxin-related joint instability discussed with physiotherapist; supports available if needed
  • ☐ Breastfeeding and hearing aid positioning discussed with lactation consultant if applicable
