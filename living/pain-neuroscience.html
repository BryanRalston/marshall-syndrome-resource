Pain Neuroscience in Children

Evidence confidence guide

Clinical guidance synthesis. Recommendations on this page are based on published clinical literature and specialist consensus for connective tissue disorders. Marshall-specific evidence is limited due to the rarity of the condition. Always work with your child's medical team.

Pain in Marshall syndrome is not simply tissue damage signals. The developing nervous system processes pain differently, and chronic pain in childhood can create permanent changes in neural circuitry.

BDNF-KCC2 Chloride Shift Established

  • In the developing nervous system, GABA can paradoxically become excitatory
  • BDNF (released during chronic pain) downregulates KCC2 (chloride transporter)
  • Chloride accumulates inside neurons, reversing the chloride gradient
  • GABA binding causes excitation instead of inhibition
  • The nervous system loses its primary brake on pain signaling
Clinical Implication: GABAergic medications (gabapentin, benzodiazepines) may be less effective or counterproductive in children with altered chloride gradients.

Central Sensitization in the Developing Nervous System Established

  • Greater neuroplasticity means pain pathways rewire faster
  • Repeatedly activated pain pathways become hard-wired
  • Descending inhibitory pathways mature later than excitatory pathways
  • Once established in childhood, central sensitization is extremely difficult to reverse

SASP Drives Nerve Ingrowth Hypothesis

In Marshall syndrome discs, SASP creates nerve ingrowth into normally aneural tissue:

  • NGF and Netrin-1 attract sensory nerve fibers deep into the disc
  • Normal discs are aneural in inner regions
  • New nerve fibers are abnormally sensitive (lower activation threshold)
  • Combined with central sensitization, this creates powerful pain amplification

Graded Motor Imagery (GMI) Supported

A neuroscience-based approach effective for pediatric chronic pain:

  1. Left/Right Discrimination: Identify body part images as left or right (activates motor planning without pain)
  2. Imagined Movements: Imagine movements without performing them (normalizes motor cortex)
  3. Mirror Therapy: Visual illusion of pain-free movement retrains the brain's body map

Kinesiophobia — Breaking the Fear-Avoidance Cycle

Kinesiophobia — the irrational and debilitating fear of movement — is increasingly recognized as a major driver of disability in children with chronic pain conditions, including hypermobility disorders like Marshall syndrome.

The Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia (TSK-11), validated for ages 8 and older, measures fear of movement and re-injury. In studies of children with hypermobility disorders, mean TSK-11 scores are significantly elevated: 28.3 vs. 21.1 in healthy controls (p<0.001). A child who fears that movement will cause harm enters a vicious cycle: avoidance leads to deconditioning, which leads to more pain, which reinforces the fear.

A 2025 systematic review found that kinesiophobia is the strongest predictor of disability in pediatric chronic pain — stronger than pain intensity itself. This means a child’s beliefs about movement matter more for functional outcomes than how much pain they actually experience.

Graded Exposure Therapy

The evidence-based treatment for kinesiophobia is graded exposure therapy: a structured, 12-session protocol conducted with a physiotherapist. The child builds a “fear hierarchy” of avoided movements and progressively works through them, starting with the least threatening. Each successful exposure weakens the fear-pain association.

The key principle is “hurt vs. harm” — movement may hurt, but it does not damage joints when done correctly. Pain during controlled exercise is expected and safe; it is not a signal of injury. Teaching this distinction to both child and parent is foundational.

VR-Based Graded Exposure

A 2025 pilot study (n=32, ages 10–16) tested virtual reality graded exposure for pediatric kinesiophobia. Gamification — turning feared movements into game mechanics — significantly increased engagement and completion rates compared to traditional graded exposure. The immersive environment appears to reduce the perceived threat of movement by shifting attentional focus.

For Marshall syndrome specifically: Controlled exercise is not just safe — it is therapeutic. Strengthening periarticular muscles provides the joint stability that lax ligaments cannot. The child’s collagen is structurally weak; the muscles around each joint must compensate. Avoiding exercise is the worst possible outcome, because it leaves hypermobile joints entirely unsupported. See Exercise as Medicine for specific protocols.

Early Intervention Prevents Permanent Rewiring

The Window: The period between pain onset and central sensitization may be as short as 3–6 months in children. After establishment, treatment becomes significantly harder.

Recommended early interventions:

  • Adequate pain control from the start — do not wait and see
  • CBT — reduces central sensitization via coping strategies
  • Exercise — activates descending inhibitory pathways
  • Sleep optimization — poor sleep worsens central sensitization
  • Pain neuroscience education — age-appropriate explanation reduces fear-avoidance
  • Graded Motor Imagery
  • Aquatic therapy

See also: Seven-Mechanism Pain Model and Urgency: Early Pain Management.

The HOPE Program: Evidence-Based Online Pain Education (2025)

HOPE (Hypermobile Online Pain managemEnt) — a 2025 pilot RCT published in the European Journal of Pain tested an 8-week, 12-module asynchronous online program for people with hypermobility disorders. Results showed significant improvement in worst pain intensity (Cohen’s d = 0.63, moderate effect) and impact of hypermobility (d = 0.32). Feasibility scored 81–91%. While developed for adults with hEDS/HSD, the framework is directly adaptable to Marshall syndrome.

Key HOPE components applicable to Marshall syndrome families:

  • Pain neuroscience education — understanding that pain involves nervous system changes, not just tissue damage
  • Condition-specific education — collagen biology adapted for the audience
  • Activity pacing — recognizing and breaking the boom-bust cycle
  • Movement reframing — exercise as therapy, not threat (see Exercise as Medicine)
  • Self-management strategies — diaphragmatic breathing, progressive muscle relaxation, sleep hygiene

Age-Appropriate Metaphors

How you explain pain neuroscience depends on the child’s age:

  • Ages 5–8 (“Alarm System”): “Your body has an alarm system. Sometimes the alarm gets too sensitive — it goes off even when there’s no real danger. We can help it calm down.”
  • Ages 9–12 (“Volume Knob”): “Your nervous system has a volume knob for pain. Right now it’s turned up too high. We can learn ways to turn it back down.”
  • Ages 13+ (Neuroscience): More detailed explanation of central sensitization, descending inhibition, and the difference between acute and chronic pain processing.
Parent co-participation is essential. Children model their pain beliefs and behaviors from their parents. If a parent catastrophizes (“this will never get better”) or avoids activities out of fear, the child will too. Parents should participate in all pain education alongside their child.

What Pain Neuroscience Education Is NOT

  • It is NOT telling the child their pain isn’t real. Pain in Marshall syndrome IS real — it has structural origins in damaged collagen, disc degeneration, and joint instability.
  • It is NOT dismissing the pathology. It acknowledges that structural damage exists AND that the nervous system amplifies pain signals on top of that structural component.
  • It is NOT a replacement for medical treatment. It is an additional tool that addresses the nervous system component while medical treatment addresses the structural component.
  • It IS evidence-based. A 2025 study confirmed central sensitization is measurable in adolescents with hypermobility spectrum disorders — pain amplification is not imaginary, and it responds to neuroscience-informed interventions.

Parent Catastrophizing & the Interpersonal Fear-Avoidance Model

Pain in children does not happen in isolation — it happens within a family system. A growing body of research demonstrates that parental responses to a child’s pain significantly influence the child’s disability, sometimes more than the pain itself.

The Interpersonal Fear-Avoidance Model

The traditional fear-avoidance model (pain → catastrophizing → fear → avoidance → disability) operates within the child. The interpersonal extension recognizes that parents have their own parallel cycle:

  • Parent catastrophizing (“This pain means something terrible is happening”) directly amplifies the child’s disability — even when the child’s own catastrophizing is controlled for
  • Parent protective behaviors (keeping the child home from school, carrying their backpack, discouraging activity) increase child avoidance. The parent’s well-intentioned protection sends the message that movement is dangerous.
  • The child learns pain-related fear not just from their own experience but from observing and absorbing the parent’s fear response
This is not about blame. Marshall syndrome pain IS real. It has documented structural origins in damaged collagen, degenerating discs, and hypermobile joints. Parent concern is rational and appropriate. The problem arises when rational concern becomes catastrophic thinking (“any pain means permanent damage”) that drives excessive protective behavior. The goal is to validate real pain while reducing fear-based avoidance.

The “Hurt vs. Harm” Distinction for Parents

This is the single most important concept for parents of children with Marshall syndrome to internalize:

  • Hurt = temporary discomfort during or after activity. Expected. Safe. Normal.
  • Harm = actual tissue damage (dislocation, retinal detachment, fracture). Requires medical attention.
  • In Marshall syndrome, controlled exercise hurts but does not harm — and the consequences of avoiding exercise (deconditioning, worse joint instability, increased pain) are more harmful than the temporary discomfort of activity
  • Parents can learn to differentiate: sharp, sudden, specific pain in a joint (possible harm — stop and assess) vs. diffuse muscle soreness and joint aching after exercise (hurt — expected and safe)

Assessment: Fear of Pain Questionnaire (FOPQ)

The Fear of Pain Questionnaire (FOPQ) is a validated tool for assessing pain-related fear in both children and parents:

  • FOPQ-Child — measures the child’s fear of pain and avoidance of activity
  • FOPQ-Parent — measures the parent’s fear about their child’s pain and their tendency toward protective behavior
  • High parent FOPQ scores predict child disability independent of the child’s own scores — this is the interpersonal effect
  • Ask your psychologist or pain team about using the FOPQ as part of the comprehensive pain assessment

Family-Based CBT: Treating Both Parent and Child

Standard CBT for pediatric pain addresses the child’s cognitions. Family CBT extends this to include the parent’s cognitions and behaviors:

  • Parent cognitive restructuring — identifying and challenging catastrophic thoughts (“If she plays at recess, she’ll dislocate something” → “Controlled activity strengthens her joints; the risk of deconditioning from inactivity is greater”)
  • Reducing protective behaviors — gradually stepping back from accommodations that reinforce avoidance (e.g., letting the child carry their own bag, encouraging school attendance despite pain)
  • Joint parent-child exposure — parent and child together practice the feared activities, with the parent modeling calm confidence rather than anxious monitoring
  • Communication coaching — how to validate pain without reinforcing catastrophizing: “I can see that hurts. What do you think would help right now?” rather than “Oh no, you’re in pain again — let’s skip PE today.”
For Marshall syndrome families specifically: The challenge is unique because the child’s pain IS structural and some activities ARE genuinely dangerous (see Sports Clearance). Family CBT must acknowledge real medical restrictions (RED activities are prohibited for real medical reasons) while preventing those restrictions from generalizing into total avoidance of all physical activity. The parent’s job is to enforce the RED boundaries firmly while actively encouraging GREEN activities with confidence.

Cincinnati Children’s Evidence-Based Guideline

The American Physical Therapy Association (APTA) has endorsed a guideline from Cincinnati Children’s Hospital for therapeutic management of pediatric hypermobility spectrum disorder. It covers:

  • Therapeutic exercise recommendations and frequency
  • Caregiver education priorities
  • Activity modification strategies
  • Orthotic recommendations
  • Therapy session frequency and duration

Ask your physical therapist if they are familiar with this guideline. It provides the closest available evidence-based framework for managing Marshall syndrome’s musculoskeletal symptoms.
