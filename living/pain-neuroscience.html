Pain Neuroscience in Children

Pain in Marshall syndrome is not simply tissue damage signals. The developing nervous system processes pain differently, and chronic pain in childhood can create permanent changes in neural circuitry.

BDNF-KCC2 Chloride Shift

  • In the developing nervous system, GABA can paradoxically become excitatory
  • BDNF (released during chronic pain) downregulates KCC2 (chloride transporter)
  • Chloride accumulates inside neurons, reversing the chloride gradient
  • GABA binding causes excitation instead of inhibition
  • The nervous system loses its primary brake on pain signaling
Clinical Implication: GABAergic medications (gabapentin, benzodiazepines) may be less effective or counterproductive in children with altered chloride gradients.

Central Sensitization in the Developing Nervous System

  • Greater neuroplasticity means pain pathways rewire faster
  • Repeatedly activated pain pathways become hard-wired
  • Descending inhibitory pathways mature later than excitatory pathways
  • Once established in childhood, central sensitization is extremely difficult to reverse

SASP Drives Nerve Ingrowth

In Marshall syndrome discs, SASP creates nerve ingrowth into normally aneural tissue:

  • NGF and Netrin-1 attract sensory nerve fibers deep into the disc
  • Normal discs are aneural in inner regions
  • New nerve fibers are abnormally sensitive (lower activation threshold)
  • Combined with central sensitization, this creates powerful pain amplification

Graded Motor Imagery (GMI)

A neuroscience-based approach effective for pediatric chronic pain:

  1. Left/Right Discrimination: Identify body part images as left or right (activates motor planning without pain)
  2. Imagined Movements: Imagine movements without performing them (normalizes motor cortex)
  3. Mirror Therapy: Visual illusion of pain-free movement retrains the brain's body map

Early Intervention Prevents Permanent Rewiring

The Window: The period between pain onset and central sensitization may be as short as 3–6 months in children. After establishment, treatment becomes significantly harder.

Recommended early interventions:

  • Adequate pain control from the start — do not wait and see
  • CBT — reduces central sensitization via coping strategies
  • Exercise — activates descending inhibitory pathways
  • Sleep optimization — poor sleep worsens central sensitization
  • Pain neuroscience education — age-appropriate explanation reduces fear-avoidance
  • Graded Motor Imagery
  • Aquatic therapy
