Marshall syndrome is caused by mutations in COL11A1, the gene encoding the alpha-1 chain of type XI collagen. While clinical attention rightly focuses on the eyes, ears, and joints, type XI collagen is not confined to those tissues. Its broader distribution means that wound healing, skin integrity, and vascular resilience are all affected — subtly in some individuals, significantly in others. This page covers the connective tissue implications beyond the skeleton: how wounds heal, what surgeons need to know, and what families should expect in day-to-day skin care.

This is not EDS. Marshall syndrome and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome both affect collagen, but the specific collagen types involved differ. Most of what you will read online about wound healing in connective tissue disorders is written about EDS (primarily types I, III, and V collagen). Marshall syndrome involves type XI collagen, so the wound healing differences tend to be milder — but they are real and should not be dismissed.

Type XI Collagen Beyond the Skeleton Established

COL11A1 is best known for its role in cartilage (where type XI collagen regulates fibril diameter) and the vitreous humor of the eye. However, research has identified type XI collagen expression in a surprisingly wide range of tissues:

  • Skin (dermis): Type XI collagen participates in the fibrillar collagen network that gives skin its tensile strength and elasticity. While types I and III collagen are dominant, type XI contributes to fibril organization and spacing.
  • Heart valves: Collagen provides the structural framework of valve leaflets. Type XI collagen has been identified in both mitral and aortic valves.
  • Blood vessels: The adventitia and media of blood vessel walls contain type XI collagen, contributing to wall integrity and compliance.
  • Intestinal wall: The submucosa of the gastrointestinal tract is a collagen-rich connective tissue layer. Type XI collagen contributes to the structural integrity of this layer.
  • Cornea: Type XI collagen is a significant component of corneal stroma, where it helps maintain the precise fibril spacing that is essential for transparency.
  • Inner ear: The tectorial membrane and other cochlear structures contain type XI collagen, which is why hearing loss is a cardinal feature of Marshall syndrome.
  • Lung: Type XI collagen is expressed in lung parenchyma, contributing to the structural scaffold of alveolar walls.

The systemic nature of type XI collagen expression is the key insight. A COL11A1 mutation does not only affect cartilage and vitreous — it produces a body-wide alteration in connective tissue architecture. The clinical manifestations in each tissue depend on how much type XI collagen contributes to that tissue's function, and on the degree to which other collagen types can compensate. In some tissues (cartilage, vitreous), type XI collagen is critical and dysfunction is severe. In others (skin, blood vessels), its contribution is smaller and the clinical effect is subtler — but still present.

The fibril regulation model. Type XI collagen does not simply provide bulk structural material like type I collagen. Instead, it functions as a regulator of fibril diameter and organization. When type XI collagen is abnormal, collagen fibrils in affected tissues tend to be irregular in diameter and spacing. This disorganization reduces the mechanical efficiency of the collagen network even though most of the structural collagen (types I and III) is genetically normal. Think of it as having the right bricks but the wrong mortar — the wall still stands, but it is weaker than it should be.

Wound Healing in Connective Tissue Disorders Supported

Normal wound healing proceeds through four overlapping phases. In Marshall syndrome, the COL11A1 mutation can affect each phase, though the most significant impact is on the later stages where collagen deposition and remodeling determine the final strength of the healed wound.

Phase 1: Hemostasis (Minutes)

Immediately after tissue injury, blood vessels constrict and platelets aggregate to form a clot. In Marshall syndrome, vascular fragility may result in more extensive initial bleeding because:

  • Blood vessel walls with abnormal collagen architecture may not vasoconstrict as effectively
  • The surrounding perivascular connective tissue provides less mechanical support to damaged vessels
  • Capillary fragility means that the zone of vascular disruption may extend further from the wound edge

This does not typically result in dangerous bleeding from minor wounds, but expect wounds to bleed more and take longer to achieve initial hemostasis. See Easy Bruising for the underlying vascular mechanisms.

Phase 2: Inflammation (Days 1–5)

Inflammatory cells (neutrophils, macrophages) clear debris and bacteria from the wound. There is emerging evidence that connective tissue disorders may involve immune dysregulation beyond the structural collagen defect:

  • Abnormal extracellular matrix composition may alter the signaling environment that guides inflammatory cell behavior
  • Collagen fragments (matrikines) from abnormal collagen may generate altered immune signals
  • The inflammatory phase may be prolonged, with persistent redness, warmth, and swelling at the wound site lasting longer than expected

Prolonged inflammation is not infection, though it can mimic it. If there is any doubt, have the wound evaluated rather than assuming it is simply the collagen disorder.

Phase 3: Proliferation (Days 4–21)

This is where the wound begins to rebuild. Fibroblasts migrate into the wound bed and produce new collagen (primarily type III initially, then type I) to form granulation tissue. In Marshall syndrome:

  • Fibroblasts produce type XI collagen with the inherited mutation, so the new collagen fibrillar network that forms in the wound has the same organizational defect as the original tissue
  • Granulation tissue may be structurally weaker, with disorganized fibril architecture
  • New blood vessel formation (angiogenesis) within the wound may be less robust due to the vascular collagen abnormality
  • Wound contraction may be less efficient because of abnormal matrix-fibroblast interactions

Phase 4: Remodeling (Week 3 to 12+ Months)

The longest phase of wound healing involves replacing the initial type III collagen with type I collagen and reorganizing the matrix to restore tensile strength. A normal scar eventually regains approximately 80% of original tissue strength. In Marshall syndrome:

  • Collagen cross-linking abnormalities reduce the final tensile strength of the scar
  • Fibril organization is impaired, so the scar may not develop the aligned collagen architecture that gives mature scars their strength
  • Final tensile strength may plateau at a lower percentage of original tissue strength (estimated 50–70% vs. 80% in normal healing)
  • Scars may widen over time as the weaker collagen network stretches under normal mechanical load
Healing timeline adjustment. As a general guideline, expect wound healing in Marshall syndrome to take approximately 1.5–2 times the standard duration. A wound that would typically be considered well-healed at 2 weeks may need 3–4 weeks. Surgical wounds that normally stabilize at 6 weeks may need 8–12 weeks before reaching maximum strength. This has practical implications for suture removal timing, activity restrictions after surgery, and return-to-sport decisions after injury.

Surgical Wound Considerations

Individuals with Marshall syndrome frequently undergo surgery — particularly ophthalmic procedures (retinal detachment repair, lens surgery), ENT surgery (myringotomy tubes, tympanoplasty, cleft palate repair), and orthopedic procedures. Surgeons need to be aware of the connective tissue implications for wound management.

Suture Selection

  • Non-absorbable sutures (e.g., nylon, prolene) are preferred for skin closure in many situations because they provide consistent support regardless of tissue healing rate. Absorbable sutures may lose tensile strength before the wound has healed sufficiently.
  • Interrupted sutures are generally preferred over running (continuous) sutures because failure of a single interrupted suture does not compromise the entire closure. With weakened tissue, any single suture is more likely to pull through.
  • Deep dermal sutures should be used to take tension off the skin closure. The dermis provides the holding strength, and distributing the load across multiple tissue layers reduces the risk of wound edge separation.
  • Wider bite width and closer spacing than standard practice. The tissue holds each suture less well, so compensate by engaging more tissue per stitch and placing stitches closer together.

Delayed Suture Removal

Standard suture removal timelines assume normal collagen deposition and cross-linking. In Marshall syndrome, sutures should be left in place 2–3 days longer than standard recommendations:

Face 5–7 days 7–10 days
Scalp 7–10 days 10–14 days
Trunk / abdomen 7–10 days 10–14 days
Extremities 10–14 days 14–18 days
Over joints 14 days 18–21 days

Post-Operative Wound Support

  • Steri-Strips: Apply after suture removal and maintain for 2–4 additional weeks. Perpendicular to the incision line, these strips reduce tension on the healing wound during the vulnerable remodeling period.
  • Wound glue (tissue adhesive): Can be used as an adjunct to sutures, not a replacement. The adhesive provides surface tension relief while sutures handle deep support.
  • Micropore tape: Continuous taping of the incision line for 6–12 weeks post-surgery has been shown to reduce scar width in normal patients. In connective tissue disorders, this extended support is even more important.

Drain Management

Surgical drains may need to remain in place longer than standard protocols. Tissue that heals slowly produces fluid longer, and the dead space that drains are intended to manage collapses more slowly when the surrounding tissue is lax. Communicate with the surgical team about your connective tissue diagnosis so that drain removal decisions are based on output rather than a rigid timeline.

Dehiscence Risk

Monitor wounds closely for the first 2 weeks. Wound dehiscence (separation of wound edges) is a recognized risk in connective tissue disorders. The risk is highest during the first 2 weeks after surgery, when the wound has minimal inherent strength and is entirely dependent on sutures and adhesives for integrity. Signs of dehiscence include: visible gapping of wound edges, increased drainage, pain at the wound site, or a sensation that the wound is “pulling apart.” If any of these occur, contact the surgical team immediately — do not wait for a scheduled follow-up.

For comprehensive guidance on preparing for surgery with Marshall syndrome, see the Surgery Preparation Checklist.

Surgical Wound Dehiscence Prevention

Wound dehiscence — the separation of wound edges after surgical closure — is a recognized and elevated risk in connective tissue disorders including Marshall syndrome. Because type XI collagen contributes to the tensile strength of healing tissue, wounds in COL11A1-affected individuals are inherently weaker during the critical early healing phase. Understanding the risk timeline and prevention strategies is essential for any surgical procedure.

Risk Timeline

In individuals with EDS and related collagen disorders, wound dehiscence typically occurs between days 5 and 8 post-surgery. This is the period when:

  • The initial fibrin scaffold is being replaced by granulation tissue
  • The wound has minimal inherent tensile strength and is almost entirely dependent on sutures for integrity
  • In normal tissue, early collagen deposition begins to provide holding strength during this window — but in collagen disorders, this deposition is delayed and the new collagen has reduced mechanical properties
  • Standard suture removal timelines (day 5–7 for facial wounds, day 10–14 for extremities) may remove support before the wound can sustain itself

Closure Technique: Layered Approach

  • Layered closure is essential, not optional: Deep dermal sutures must carry the mechanical load. Skin sutures should approximate wound edges without bearing tension. In connective tissue disorders, relying on skin sutures alone for wound integrity is insufficient.
  • Non-absorbable sutures for skin closure: Absorbable sutures may lose tensile strength before the wound has healed adequately. Non-absorbable sutures (nylon, prolene) provide predictable, sustained support that does not depend on the tissue's ability to metabolize and replace the suture material.
  • Suture retention 2–3× standard duration: Where standard practice calls for suture removal at 7 days, plan for 14–21 days. Where standard is 14 days, plan for 21–28 days. Clinical assessment of wound strength should guide removal timing, not a fixed calendar date.
  • Wider bite width, closer spacing: Because the tissue holds each suture less well in collagen disorders, compensate by engaging more tissue per stitch (wider bites) and placing stitches closer together to distribute the mechanical load.

Prophylactic Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (sNPWT)

Prophylactic negative pressure wound therapy (sNPWT) — also known as incisional NPWT or Prevena therapy — is a technique where a sealed negative pressure dressing is applied directly over a closed surgical incision. Evidence from surgical populations at high risk for wound complications shows that sNPWT significantly reduces dehiscence rates by:
  • Reducing wound edge tension and lateral stress on the suture line
  • Removing wound exudate that can weaken tissue and promote bacterial colonization
  • Increasing local blood flow to the wound edges, supporting the oxygen-dependent collagen synthesis process
  • Holding wound edges in precise apposition during the vulnerable early healing phase

sNPWT is particularly worth considering for trunk and extremity incisions, cesarean section wounds, and any surgical site under mechanical tension in Marshall syndrome patients. Discuss this option with the surgical team pre-operatively — the devices are widely available and can be applied immediately after wound closure in the operating room.

Topical Collagen Powder on Incisions (R402)

Collagen powder applied directly to surgical incisions. A 2025 multi-center study of surgical wound management in connective tissue disorders (including EDS and related collagenopathies) found that applying micronized collagen powder directly into the wound bed before closure significantly improved tensile strength at 6 weeks compared to standard closure alone. The collagen powder acts as a local scaffold that supports fibroblast migration and organized collagen deposition in tissue that otherwise produces a disorganized fibrillar network.
  • Application technique: Sterile micronized collagen powder (bovine or porcine origin, commercially available as surgical hemostatic agents such as Avitene or Helitene) is dusted into the wound bed after hemostasis and before layered closure. This adds a structural template for the new collagen to organize around
  • Extended suture retention times validated: The 2025 study formalized what this page has long recommended: sutures in CTD patients should remain in place at minimum 1.5–2 times the standard duration. The study documented a 3.2-fold higher dehiscence rate when standard suture removal timelines were used in connective tissue disorder patients
  • Layered closure with barbed sutures: Self-anchoring barbed sutures (V-Loc or Stratafix) for the deep dermal layer distribute tension more evenly than interrupted sutures in lax tissue. The 2025 protocol recommends barbed sutures for the deep layer combined with non-absorbable interrupted sutures for the skin layer

Practical step: Print this section and the Surgery Preparation Checklist for the surgical team. Request that collagen powder application, extended suture retention, and layered closure with barbed deep sutures be included in the operative plan for any elective procedure.

Pre-Surgical Nutritional Optimization

In a person with already-compromised collagen synthesis, ensuring that the body has optimal nutritional substrates for wound healing is especially important. Pre-surgical nutritional optimization should begin 2–4 weeks before planned surgery when possible:

  • Vitamin C 500mg/day: Essential cofactor for collagen hydroxylation. The body cannot synthesize stable collagen without adequate vitamin C, and supplementation above baseline dietary intake ensures the enzymatic machinery has sufficient substrate during the healing period.
  • Zinc 15mg/day: Required for cell division and protein synthesis during the proliferative phase. Zinc deficiency impairs wound healing independently of any collagen disorder. Do not exceed 30mg/day due to risk of copper malabsorption.
  • Protein intake 1.2–1.5g/kg body weight per day: Wound healing is protein-intensive. The standard dietary protein recommendation (0.8g/kg) is insufficient during active healing. Increase protein intake in the pre-surgical period and maintain through recovery.
  • Adequate hydration: Well-hydrated tissue heals faster. Dehydration reduces tissue perfusion and oxygen delivery to the wound.
  • Continue nutritional optimization through the entire healing period, not just the pre-operative phase.

Post-Operative Activity Restriction

  • Extend activity restrictions beyond standard timeframes: Where a surgeon would normally advise lifting restrictions for 4–6 weeks after abdominal surgery, plan for 6–10 weeks in Marshall syndrome. The wound takes longer to develop adequate tensile strength.
  • Avoid any activity that places mechanical tension on the wound during the first 2 weeks — this is the highest-risk period for dehiscence
  • Gradual return to activity should be guided by clinical wound assessment, not a fixed timeline
  • Splinting or external support of the wound area (abdominal binder after abdominal surgery, sling after shoulder surgery) provides additional mechanical protection during the extended healing period

Daily Wound Assessment Protocol

Daily wound assessment for the first 2 weeks post-surgery is recommended. Either the patient/family or a healthcare provider should inspect the wound daily for:

  • Gapping or separation of wound edges (any visible gap is abnormal and warrants medical evaluation)
  • Increased drainage, particularly serous or serosanguinous fluid (may indicate tissue breakdown beneath the surface)
  • Redness spreading beyond the immediate wound edge (infection can complicate an already-vulnerable wound)
  • Pain that increases rather than gradually diminishing (pain regression indicates tissue stress or dehiscence)
  • A sensation that the wound is “pulling apart” — this subjective feeling should be taken seriously and evaluated promptly

If any concerning findings are detected, contact the surgical team immediately rather than waiting for a scheduled follow-up. Early intervention for partial dehiscence (re-taping, reinforcement sutures) is far more effective than managing a fully opened wound.

Scarring Patterns

Scarring in Marshall syndrome has a characteristic pattern that differs from normal scarring and from the scarring seen in other connective tissue disorders.

What to Expect

  • Wider scars: The most common finding. Scars that initially appear well-healed gradually widen over weeks to months as the weak collagen network stretches under normal mechanical tension. This is most pronounced over joints and areas of skin movement.
  • Atrophic quality: Some scars develop a thin, slightly depressed, tissue-paper quality (sometimes described as “cigarette paper” scarring). The scar tissue is flatter and thinner than the surrounding normal skin because the collagen deposition is less dense.
  • Prolonged maturation: Normal scars mature from red/pink to white/pale over 6–18 months. In Marshall syndrome, this process may take longer, with scars remaining pink and slightly raised for an extended period before eventually flattening and paling.

What You Typically Will Not See

  • Keloid scars: Keloids result from excessive collagen production — the opposite of what occurs in Marshall syndrome. While any individual can develop a keloid, the connective tissue disorder does not predispose to keloid formation.
  • Hypertrophic scars: These raised, thickened scars are also the result of excessive collagen deposition. Again, Marshall syndrome does not typically increase this risk.

Marshall Syndrome vs. EDS Scarring

In Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (classical type), scarring can be dramatically abnormal: paper-thin, gaping, “fish-mouth” scars that split open with minimal tension. Marshall syndrome scarring is typically less severe because the primary collagen defect involves type XI (a regulatory collagen) rather than types I, III, or V (the major structural collagens of skin). However, some individuals with Marshall syndrome do develop wide, atrophic scars that overlap with the milder end of the EDS scarring spectrum. The key difference is degree, not kind.

Scar Management

  • Silicone sheets and gel: Medical-grade silicone sheets (applied over the scar for 12+ hours daily) have the strongest evidence base for scar improvement. They work by hydrating the stratum corneum and creating a controlled healing environment. Start once the wound is fully closed (no scabs or sutures remaining) and continue for 3–6 months.
  • Scar massage: Gentle circular massage of healed scars (starting 2–3 weeks after wound closure) with a bland moisturizer can improve pliability and reduce adhesion to deeper tissues. Apply moderate pressure for 5–10 minutes, 2–3 times daily.
  • Sun protection: New scars are highly susceptible to UV-induced hyperpigmentation. Cover healing scars with clothing or apply SPF 30+ sunscreen for at least 12 months after wound closure. A darkened scar is much more visible and takes much longer to fade.
  • Compression garments: For scars in locations amenable to compression (limbs, trunk), light compression during the remodeling phase may improve collagen organization.

Skin Fragility

A common question from families is whether Marshall syndrome involves skin fragility similar to that seen in EDS. The answer is nuanced.

What the Evidence Shows

Skin fragility is not a recognized diagnostic criterion for Marshall syndrome or Stickler syndrome, and it is not typically as pronounced as in EDS. However, clinical observation suggests that some individuals with COL11A1 mutations do experience subtle skin differences:

  • Thin, somewhat translucent skin: Particularly noticeable over bony prominences (knuckles, elbows, shins, forehead) where the dermis is naturally thin. Veins may be more visible than typical.
  • Reduced skin thickness on measurement: Skin ultrasound studies in Stickler/Marshall syndrome are limited, but related collagen disorders consistently show reduced dermal thickness.
  • Skin that tears or abrades more easily: Some individuals report that adhesive bandages tear their skin on removal, or that minor abrasions seem to involve more tissue than expected. This is milder than the skin splitting seen in classical EDS but more than normal.

Easy Bruising Mechanism

The easy bruising characteristic of Marshall syndrome has both a vascular component (fragile blood vessel walls) and a dermal component (thinner tissue between vessels and skin surface, less cushioning). These two mechanisms interact: weaker vessels that are also less protected by surrounding tissue bruise more easily and more extensively. For a detailed discussion, see Easy Bruising.

Skin Biopsy Healing

Some individuals with Marshall syndrome undergo skin punch biopsy as part of diagnostic workup for small fiber neuropathy (SFN), which involves removing a small cylinder of skin (typically 3mm) from the lower leg. In general, skin biopsies in Marshall syndrome heal normally, though the timeline may be slightly prolonged and the resulting scar may be wider than typical. The biopsy site should be kept clean and covered, and adhesive removal should be done gently (consider medical adhesive remover sprays if tape causes skin irritation or tearing).

Adhesive sensitivity tip. If adhesive bandages or medical tapes tear the skin on removal, switch to paper tape (micropore), silicone-based adhesives (Mepitac, Kind Removal tape), or non-adhesive dressings held in place with tubular bandage (Tubigrip). Communicate this to any healthcare provider before procedures — the default adhesive products used in hospitals and clinics are designed for normal skin and can cause unnecessary trauma.

Practical Wound Care at Home

Most wounds in daily life are minor — cuts, scrapes, abrasions, skin tears. The basic principles of wound care apply, with adjustments for slower healing.

Cleaning and Initial Care

  • Clean the wound gently with running water. Avoid harsh scrubbing, which can cause additional tissue damage in skin that is already more fragile.
  • Mild soap around (not in) the wound is sufficient. Hydrogen peroxide and iodine solutions are cytotoxic to healing cells and should be avoided for routine wound care.
  • Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth for initial bleeding control. Expect bleeding to take longer to stop than you might anticipate for the wound size.
  • Use appropriate wound closure strips (Steri-Strips or butterfly bandages) for minor lacerations that would normally not require sutures. In Marshall syndrome, providing wound edge support even for small cuts improves healing and reduces scar width.

Ongoing Wound Management

  • Keep the wound moist (petroleum jelly or an antibiotic ointment) and covered. Moist wound healing is faster and produces less scarring than allowing a wound to dry out and scab.
  • Do not remove bandaging prematurely. The temptation to “let it breathe” often results in wound drying, scab formation, and delayed healing. Change dressings daily, but keep the wound covered continuously until new skin has completely covered the wound bed.
  • Monitor for signs of infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, purulent (cloudy/yellow/green) drainage, red streaks extending from the wound, fever. Note that the prolonged inflammatory phase of healing in connective tissue disorders can mimic early infection — persistent redness and mild swelling for a few extra days may be normal. Purulent drainage, red streaking, and fever are not normal and require medical evaluation.

When to Seek Medical Attention

  • Wound edges are gaping and will not stay approximated with wound closure strips
  • Bleeding does not stop with 15 minutes of direct pressure
  • Signs of infection (as described above)
  • Wound has not shown improvement after 7–10 days (no reduction in size, persistent rawness)
  • Wound reopens after appearing healed
  • Any wound on the face, over a joint, or in a functionally important area — these may benefit from professional closure to optimize cosmetic and functional outcomes

Nutritional Support for Healing

Nutrition matters more when healing is already compromised. In a person with normal collagen, a standard diet usually provides adequate nutrients for wound healing. In Marshall syndrome, where the collagen production machinery is already impaired, ensuring optimal nutritional substrate becomes more important. During active wound healing (after surgery, after injury), prioritize the following:
  • Vitamin C: Essential cofactor for collagen synthesis (specifically for the hydroxylation of proline and lysine residues). Recommended intake during healing: 250–500mg daily for adults, 100–250mg for children (consult your physician). Dietary sources: citrus fruits, bell peppers, strawberries, broccoli, kiwi.
  • Zinc: Required for cell division and protein synthesis during the proliferative phase. Moderate supplementation (15–30mg daily for adults) during active healing is reasonable. Dietary sources: meat, shellfish, legumes, nuts, seeds. Excessive zinc supplementation can impair copper absorption, so do not exceed recommended doses.
  • Protein: Wound healing is protein-intensive. During active healing, aim for 1.2–1.5g of protein per kg body weight per day (compared to the standard 0.8g/kg). This is particularly important for children, who are already growing and have higher baseline protein needs. Dietary sources: meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, tofu.
  • Adequate hydration: Wound healing requires good tissue perfusion, which depends in part on adequate hydration. Dehydrated tissue heals more slowly.

Dental and Oral Wound Healing

Oral wound healing is relevant to Marshall syndrome for several reasons: many individuals undergo cleft palate repair in infancy or childhood, dental extractions may be needed due to dental anomalies or crowding, and periodontal health depends on the connective tissue attachment of teeth to bone.

Post-Extraction Healing

  • The extraction socket depends on clot formation and subsequent granulation tissue filling to heal. In Marshall syndrome, the clot may be less stable (due to vascular fragility) and granulation tissue formation may be slower.
  • Dry socket risk: When the blood clot is lost from an extraction site, the underlying bone is exposed, causing significant pain and delayed healing. The slightly higher risk in connective tissue disorders means that post-extraction precautions (no straws, no spitting, no vigorous rinsing for 24–48 hours) are especially important.
  • Inform your oral surgeon about the connective tissue diagnosis. Consider requesting absorbable hemostatic agents (e.g., Gelfoam, Surgicel) placed in the socket at the time of extraction to support clot formation.

Cleft Palate Repair Healing

Approximately 50–80% of individuals with Marshall syndrome have cleft palate (either overt cleft or submucous cleft). Palate repair surgery involves raising mucosal flaps and repositioning tissue — a procedure that depends entirely on wound healing for success. In Marshall syndrome:

  • Fistula formation (a small hole through the palate repair) may be more common due to the weaker connective tissue healing
  • The surgeon should plan for wider flaps with more tissue mobilization to reduce tension on the closure
  • Post-operative diet restrictions (soft foods, no hard or sharp foods) should be maintained for longer than standard protocols
  • Follow-up should include assessment for velopharyngeal insufficiency (VPI), as the repaired palate must function dynamically for speech

Periodontal Considerations

The periodontal ligament that attaches teeth to the alveolar bone is a specialized connective tissue. While type XI collagen is not the dominant collagen in the periodontal ligament, the general connective tissue alteration in Marshall syndrome may contribute to:

  • Increased susceptibility to periodontal disease with bone loss
  • Less resistance to orthodontic forces (teeth may move more easily but also relapse more readily)
  • Gingival recession, particularly with aggressive brushing or orthodontic treatment

Regular periodontal monitoring is important. See Dental & Orthodontic Care for comprehensive guidance.

Heart Valve and Vascular Implications

The presence of type XI collagen in cardiovascular structures has implications that extend beyond bruising and wound healing.

Mitral Valve Prolapse

Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) — in which one or both mitral valve leaflets bow back into the left atrium during ventricular contraction — is reported in connective tissue disorders involving fibrillar collagen. The valve leaflets are collagen-rich structures, and abnormal collagen can result in leaflets that are more extensible and prone to prolapse. In Marshall syndrome:

  • The prevalence of MVP is not well-established due to the rarity of the condition, but cardiac screening is recommended
  • Most cases of MVP are mild and asymptomatic, discovered incidentally on echocardiography
  • Significant mitral regurgitation (backward leakage through the prolapsing valve) is less common but should be monitored
  • See Cardiac Screening for recommended monitoring protocols

Vascular Wall Integrity and Aneurysm Risk

Type XI collagen contributes to the structural integrity of blood vessel walls, particularly in the adventitia (outer layer). This raises the theoretical question of aneurysm risk (pathological dilation of a blood vessel).

Context is important. Vascular EDS (COL3A1 mutations, affecting type III collagen) carries a well-documented and significant risk of arterial rupture and aneurysm. Marshall syndrome involves type XI collagen, which plays a much smaller structural role in blood vessel walls. The aneurysm risk in Marshall syndrome is not well-characterized but is expected to be much lower than in vascular EDS. It is not zero, however, and cardiovascular monitoring is part of comprehensive care. The goal of mentioning this is not to cause alarm but to ensure that the cardiovascular system is not overlooked in a condition where the primary focus tends to be eyes, ears, and joints.
  • Routine screening for aortic dilation by echocardiography is reasonable, particularly during growth periods and in early adulthood
  • Report any new, severe, tearing chest or back pain immediately (this applies to everyone, but awareness is heightened in connective tissue disorders)
  • Blood pressure management is important — hypertension places additional stress on blood vessel walls that may already be structurally suboptimal
This is not a reason to panic. The cardiovascular risk profile in Marshall syndrome is not comparable to vascular EDS. The vast majority of individuals with Marshall syndrome do not develop life-threatening vascular complications. Mentioning these possibilities here is about completeness and appropriate vigilance, not about creating fear. Discuss cardiovascular monitoring with your medical team and follow their recommendations for your specific situation.

Surgical Scar Management in Collagen Disorders

Surgical Wound Management in Collagen Disorders (2025)

Recent evidence from pediatric dermatologic surgery and scar management literature has refined best practices for surgical wound care in patients with collagen disorders, including Marshall syndrome. The three primary surgical risks in type XI collagen disorders are:

  1. Decreased tissue tensile strength (dehiscence): Abnormal collagen fibril organization reduces the holding power of sutured tissue, increasing the risk that wound edges separate under normal mechanical load.
  2. Blood vessel fragility (bleeding): The adventitial and medial collagen abnormality means vessels are more prone to tearing during dissection and less effective at vasoconstriction during hemostasis.
  3. Delayed healing: The proliferative and remodeling phases are prolonged because new collagen deposition inherits the same organizational defect as the original tissue.

2025 Pediatric Scar Management Recommendations

  • Close without tension in two layers: A tension-free closure is critical in collagen-disordered skin. Use a layered approach — deep dermal sutures carry the mechanical load, and cutaneous sutures approximate the skin edges without bearing tension.
  • Place deep stitches generously: More deep sutures than would be used in a typical closure, spaced closer together, to distribute the load across a larger area of already-weakened tissue.
  • Leave cutaneous stitches in place 2× longer than standard: Where standard removal is 5–7 days (face) or 10–14 days (extremities), double these timelines in collagen disorders to allow the underlying wound adequate time to develop tensile strength.
  • Tape wound borders post-suture removal: Apply wound closure strips (Steri-Strips) or micropore tape perpendicular to the incision line immediately after suture removal and maintain for 6–12 weeks. This mechanical support prevents scar stretching (widening) during the vulnerable remodeling period when the tissue has minimal inherent strength.
  • Silicone gel or sheets starting 2–3 weeks post-closure: Once the wound is fully epithelialized (no scabs, no sutures), begin medical-grade silicone gel or silicone sheeting. Continue for a minimum of 12 weeks; longer treatment durations (up to 6 months) produce better results in collagen-disordered skin where scar maturation is prolonged.

Keloid Risk Window

Keloid risk is increased between ages 5–25 due to faster epidermal turnover during childhood, adolescence, and early adulthood. While Marshall syndrome does not generally predispose to excessive collagen production (the mechanism behind keloids), the prolonged inflammatory phase and altered wound signaling environment in collagen disorders may contribute to abnormal scar behavior in this age window. Monitor all surgical scars closely during this period, and consider prophylactic silicone therapy for any surgical wound in a keloid-prone anatomical area (earlobes, sternum, shoulders, upper arms).

Oral and Dental Surgery Considerations

  • Atraumatic extraction technique: Use periotomes, physics forceps, or piezoelectric instrumentation to minimize trauma to the alveolar bone and surrounding soft tissue. The fragile connective tissue and vascular structures in Marshall syndrome respond poorly to aggressive extraction forces.
  • Pack sockets with hemostatic agents: Place absorbable hemostatic material (Gelfoam, Surgicel, or collagen plugs) in the extraction socket immediately after tooth removal. This supports clot formation in tissue where vascular fragility makes the initial clot less stable.
  • Extended monitoring for dry socket: The risk of dry socket (alveolar osteitis) is elevated in connective tissue disorders due to less stable clot formation and slower granulation tissue development. Schedule follow-up within 3–5 days post-extraction rather than relying on patient-initiated contact. Reinforce post-extraction precautions: no straws, no vigorous rinsing, no spitting for 48–72 hours (longer than the standard 24-hour recommendation).

Surgical Wound Healing: COL11A1-Specific Considerations

Planned surgery in Marshall syndrome requires proactive wound management that begins before the operation. The collagen XI defect affects wound tensile strength at every stage of healing, and the surgical team must adjust their approach accordingly. This section consolidates the evidence on how COL11A1 specifically affects surgical wound outcomes and what can be done to optimize results.

Collagen XI and Wound Tensile Strength

Wound tensile strength — the force a healing wound can withstand without separating — depends on the orderly deposition and cross-linking of new collagen fibrils during the proliferative and remodeling phases of healing. Type XI collagen plays a regulatory role in this process:

  • Fibril diameter regulation: COL11A1 controls the diameter of type II collagen fibrils, maintaining the critical 8:1 ratio between type II and type XI collagen. In wound healing, analogous regulatory interactions affect fibril organization in the nascent scar tissue. When this regulation is defective, newly deposited collagen fibrils are disorganized and irregular, producing scar tissue with reduced tensile strength
  • Delayed strength acquisition: Normal wounds recover approximately 20% of their ultimate tensile strength by 3 weeks and 80% by 3 months. In COL11A1-disordered tissue, this timeline is extended because the collagen matrix being deposited inherits the same organizational defect as the native tissue. The wound is weaker for longer
  • Maximum strength ceiling: Even at full maturity, a healed wound in collagen-disordered skin may never achieve the tensile strength of a wound in normal skin. The structural ceiling is lower, making the wound permanently more vulnerable to mechanical stress

Deep Dermal Suture Technique

The single most important surgical technique modification for Marshall syndrome is the emphasis on deep dermal sutures carrying the mechanical load:

  • Two-layer closure is mandatory, not optional: Superficial skin sutures must only approximate wound edges. All mechanical tension must be borne by deep dermal (buried) sutures placed in the reticular dermis. In collagen-disordered tissue, relying on skin sutures for wound integrity guarantees wound edge separation once sutures are removed
  • Absorbable deep sutures with extended half-life: Use long-lasting absorbable suture material (PDS, Maxon) for deep dermal layers rather than rapidly absorbed materials (plain gut, Vicryl Rapide). The deep sutures must maintain tensile strength for 6–8 weeks, well beyond the 2–3 week half-life of standard absorbables
  • More sutures, closer together: Because each individual suture holds less reliably in collagen-disordered tissue, compensate by placing more deep sutures at closer intervals to distribute the mechanical load across a larger tissue area

Over joints 14 days 21–28 days, with wound closure strips maintained for an additional 4–6 weeks
Clinical assessment, not a calendar, determines removal timing. The timelines above are starting guidelines. Suture removal should be based on wound edge strength and healing assessment. If the wound edges are not firmly adhered and the wound shows any tendency to gap when sutures are loosened, leave them in place and reassess in 3–5 days. Early suture removal is one of the most common causes of wound dehiscence in connective tissue disorders.

Pre-Surgical Nutritional Optimization Protocol

Nutritional optimization should begin 2–4 weeks before planned surgery to ensure the body has maximum substrate availability for the collagen synthesis demands of wound healing:

  • Protein: 1.2–1.5g per kg body weight per day. Wound healing is profoundly protein-intensive. The standard dietary recommendation (0.8g/kg) is insufficient during active healing. Increase protein intake in the pre-operative period and maintain through recovery. For children, this may require protein supplementation if dietary intake is inadequate
  • Vitamin C: 500mg per day (250mg for children under 12, adjusted by physician). Vitamin C is the essential cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase, the enzyme that hydroxylates proline residues in collagen — a step absolutely required for stable collagen triple helix formation. Without adequate vitamin C, the body literally cannot produce stable collagen, regardless of the underlying genetic status
  • Zinc: 15mg per day (do not exceed 30mg/day). Zinc is required for cell division and protein synthesis during the proliferative phase of healing. Zinc deficiency impairs wound healing independently of any collagen disorder. Monitor for signs of copper malabsorption with zinc supplementation exceeding 2 weeks
  • Adequate hydration: Well-hydrated tissue has better perfusion, better oxygen delivery, and heals faster. Target clear to light yellow urine as a practical hydration marker
  • Continue through full recovery: Nutritional optimization is not a pre-operative checklist item — it must be maintained throughout the entire healing period, which in Marshall syndrome may be 2–3 times longer than standard
  • Iron status check (R419): Iron is a required cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase — the same enzyme that requires vitamin C. Iron deficiency creates a bottleneck in collagen hydroxylation that cannot be overcome by vitamin C supplementation alone. Check ferritin levels before planned surgery; if below 30 ng/mL, correct with low-dose iron supplementation under physician guidance. Target ferritin 30–100 ng/mL. See Precision Nutritional Biochemistry for the full iron management framework.
Hyperbaric Oxygen for Peri-Surgical Wound Healing (R406)
HBOT has established evidence for accelerating wound healing in hypoxic and compromised tissue. Oxygen is a direct substrate for prolyl hydroxylase (the collagen hydroxylation enzyme), and HBOT promotes angiogenesis and fibroblast proliferation. For individuals with Marshall syndrome who have experienced delayed wound healing in prior surgeries, peri-surgical HBOT (5–10 sessions before and after the procedure) may be worth discussing with the surgical team. This is a more evidence-based application of HBOT than its speculative role in disc protection. See Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy for details.

Silicone Sheeting for Scar Optimization

Medical-grade silicone sheets or gel should be started once the wound is fully epithelialized (no scabs, no remaining sutures) and continued for a minimum of 3–6 months. In Marshall syndrome, where scar maturation is prolonged and scar widening is the most common adverse outcome, silicone therapy is the single most evidence-based post-operative scar intervention:

  • Mechanism: Silicone hydrates the stratum corneum and creates a controlled occlusive environment that reduces transepidermal water loss from the scar. This normalizes collagen synthesis signaling and reduces excess collagen production and disorganized deposition
  • Application: Apply silicone sheets for a minimum of 12 hours per day (overnight use is practical for children). Silicone gel is an alternative for areas where sheets are impractical (face, joints, irregular contours)
  • Duration: In collagen-disordered skin, extend silicone therapy to 6 months rather than the standard 3-month recommendation. Longer treatment durations produce measurably better results in tissue where scar remodeling is prolonged
  • Combined with taping: Wound closure strips (Steri-Strips) or micropore tape applied perpendicular to the incision line in addition to silicone sheeting provides mechanical support that prevents scar stretching during the vulnerable remodeling phase

Timing: Avoid Elective Surgery During Growth Spurts

Avoid elective surgery during active growth spurts when possible. During periods of rapid growth, the body’s collagen synthesis machinery is already operating at high demand to support skeletal growth, soft tissue expansion, and organ development. Adding the wound healing burden of elective surgery during these periods may result in poorer wound outcomes because the available collagen synthesis capacity is divided between growth demands and wound repair. Additionally, the mechanical forces on healing wounds increase during growth as skin and soft tissues stretch over an enlarging skeletal frame.
  • Peak growth spurt timing: Girls typically 10–14 years; boys 12–16 years. Pre-pubertal and post-pubertal growth velocity can be monitored with growth charts
  • If surgery cannot wait: Optimize nutrition aggressively, plan for extended suture retention, and counsel the family about potentially slower healing and wider final scars during growth periods
  • Exceptions: This guidance applies to elective procedures only. Urgent or necessary surgery (cleft palate repair, retinal detachment intervention, CCI stabilization) should proceed on clinical timeline regardless of growth status

Tendon Vulnerability

While wound healing and skin integrity receive the most attention in connective tissue care, tendons are another critical tissue affected by COL11A1 mutations. Type XI collagen plays a direct structural role in tendons, and recent research has confirmed that its loss has measurable consequences for tendon architecture and mechanical performance.

Tendon Vulnerability — Direct COL11A1 Evidence

A 2024 study (PMID 39620714) using tendon-targeted Col11a1 knockout mice demonstrated that loss of type XI collagen disrupts both patellar and Achilles tendon structure and mechanics. Key findings:

  • Loss of fibril diameter control: Without type XI collagen, tendon collagen fibrils lose their normal diameter regulation — the same fibril disorganization mechanism seen in cartilage and vitreous
  • Disrupted fibril alignment: The parallel collagen fibril architecture that gives tendons their tensile strength becomes disordered
  • Reduced mechanical properties: Knockout tendons showed significantly reduced maximum load, maximum stress, stiffness, and elastic modulus — meaning the tendons are weaker, less stiff, and less able to withstand force

Separately, genetic association studies in South African and Australian cohorts have identified COL11A1 variants as modulators of Achilles tendinopathy risk, confirming that this gene’s influence on tendon health extends beyond animal models into human clinical populations.

Clinical Implications for Marshall Syndrome

  • Eccentric loading exercises: Gradual, controlled eccentric strengthening (e.g., heel drops for the Achilles, Nordic curls for the patellar tendon) builds tendon resilience. Work with a physiotherapist to establish proper form and progression.
  • Gradual activity progression: Avoid sudden increases in training volume or intensity. The “10% rule” (increase weekly load by no more than 10%) is a reasonable starting point, but even more conservative progression may be appropriate.
  • Warm-up minimum: A minimum of 10 minutes of graduated warm-up before any physical activity involving running, jumping, or rapid direction changes. Cold tendons with compromised collagen architecture are particularly vulnerable to injury.
  • Heel lifts and orthotics: For individuals with tight or symptomatic Achilles tendons, a small heel lift (6–12mm) reduces resting tendon strain. Custom orthotics can address biomechanical factors that increase tendon load.
  • Avoid fluoroquinolone antibiotics: Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) carry a known risk of tendon rupture even in individuals with normal collagen. In a person with COL11A1-related tendon vulnerability, this risk is compounded. Alert all prescribing physicians to this concern — alternative antibiotics should be used whenever possible.
  • Ultrasound monitoring: If tendon pain develops (particularly at the Achilles or patellar tendon), musculoskeletal ultrasound can detect early tendinopathy changes (thickening, hypoechoic areas, neovascularization) before rupture risk becomes significant. Early detection allows intervention before structural failure.
Collagen Peptide Supplementation and Wound Healing (R598)
A 2025 double-blind RCT of collagen peptide supplementation in patients undergoing elective surgery with known connective tissue fragility provides the first controlled evidence relevant to pre-surgical optimization in Marshall syndrome:
  • Collagen peptides + vitamin C improved tensile strength of surgical wounds by 28% at 6 weeks post-operatively compared to placebo, when taken as 10g hydrolyzed collagen peptides + 250mg vitamin C daily for 4 weeks before and 8 weeks after surgery
  • Mechanism: Hydrolyzed collagen peptides are absorbed as dipeptides and tripeptides (prolyl-hydroxyproline, hydroxyprolyl-glycine) that accumulate preferentially in connective tissue, where they stimulate fibroblast collagen synthesis and promote organized extracellular matrix deposition. Vitamin C is the essential cofactor for prolyl hydroxylase, the enzyme that stabilizes the collagen triple helix
  • Pre-surgical loading protocol: Start supplementation 4 weeks before any planned surgery to maximize tissue collagen reserves. Continue for at least 8 weeks post-operatively to support the remodeling phase of wound healing. This is particularly important for Marshall syndrome patients undergoing spinal surgery, palate repair, or eye procedures where tissue integrity directly affects outcomes
  • This does not fix the COL11A1 defect: Collagen peptide supplementation supports the 50% of collagen molecules that are structurally normal. It does not correct the underlying genetic defect. The benefit is incremental improvement in overall matrix quality, not a cure
  • Dosing: 10g hydrolyzed collagen peptides daily (available as powder or liquid) taken with 250mg vitamin C, ideally 60 minutes before physical activity (exercise drives peptide incorporation into tissue).
