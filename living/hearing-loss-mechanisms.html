Hearing Loss Mechanisms: The Full Spectrum in Marshall Syndrome

Evidence confidence guide
Established Published, peer-reviewed consensus Supported Published evidence in related conditions Hypothesis Reasonable extrapolation, not yet proven Speculative Theoretical, requires validation
Clinical guidance synthesis. Recommendations on this page are based on published clinical literature and specialist consensus for connective tissue disorders. Marshall-specific evidence is limited due to the rarity of the condition. Always work with your child's medical team.
Why this page matters: Hearing loss in Marshall syndrome is not one thing. It can involve up to five distinct mechanisms, and most children have more than one operating at the same time. Understanding which mechanisms are affecting your child changes the monitoring, treatment, and prognosis. A child with purely structural sensorineural loss needs different management than a child who also has an inflammatory component that may respond to medication, or a conductive component that can be surgically corrected. This page is the map that helps you and your medical team identify all the contributors.

The Five Mechanisms at a Glance

Research on Stickler/Marshall syndrome hearing loss and related conditions identifies five distinct pathways through which hearing can be affected. These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive — they layer on top of each other, and the combination determines the overall hearing profile:

Mechanism Type of Hearing Loss Onset Progression Treatable?
1. Structural (tectorial membrane) Sensorineural Birth / early childhood Slowly progressive Amplification only; cochlear implant if severe
2. Inflammatory / autoimmune Sensorineural (fluctuating) Any age Fluctuating, sometimes sudden drops Yes — steroids, immunosuppression
3. Conductive (middle ear) Conductive or mixed Infancy / early childhood Episodic; may persist Yes — tubes, surgery, medical management
4. Central auditory processing Central (normal audiogram) School age (typically detected) Stable or slowly improving with therapy Accommodations, FM systems, therapy
5. Cervicogenic / somatic Variable; may mimic sensorineural Any age Fluctuating; position-dependent Address underlying cervical instability
The practical implication: If your child has hearing loss that fluctuates unpredictably, worsens faster than expected, or seems disproportionate to the audiogram, there may be more than one mechanism at work. The sections below help you identify each contributor and discuss targeted testing with your medical team.

Mechanism 1: Structural — The Tectorial Membrane Defect Established

This is the primary and best-understood mechanism. It is present in essentially every person with a COL11A1 mutation and forms the baseline hearing loss on which the other mechanisms may layer.

The Molecular Problem

The tectorial membrane (TM) is a ribbon-like structure inside the cochlea that sits directly over the hair cells responsible for converting sound vibrations into nerve signals. Its composition is dominated by three collagen types: COL11A1, COL11A2, and COL2A1. Collagen XI, encoded by COL11A1, forms the radial fibrils that give the tectorial membrane its precise mechanical properties.

When COL11A1 is mutated:

  • Fibril diameter regulation is lost. Collagen XI acts as a molecular spacer that prevents collagen II fibrils from fusing into thick, disordered bundles. Without functional collagen XI, fibril organization becomes chaotic
  • Mechanical coupling fails. The tectorial membrane must physically deflect the stereocilia (tiny hair-like projections) on the outer hair cells to trigger nerve signals. A disorganized tectorial membrane cannot transmit sound vibrations to the hair cells efficiently
  • Frequency tuning is impaired. The tectorial membrane has graded stiffness along its length that contributes to frequency selectivity — the ability to distinguish between similar pitches. Disrupted collagen architecture degrades this precision

Research in transgenic mice lacking Col11a2 demonstrates 40–50 dB threshold elevation measured at the brainstem, attributable to disruption of the tectorial membrane's radial fibrillar structure. A 2025 Frontiers in Neurology review confirmed that defective collagen XI fibrils weaken the TM scaffold, diminishing auditory signal transmission at the TM–outer hair cell interface.

Clinical Profile

  • Type: Sensorineural (the problem is in the inner ear, not the middle ear or brain)
  • Severity: Typically moderate, though ranges from mild to severe
  • Frequencies: COL11A1-related hearing loss involves all frequencies, unlike COL2A1 Stickler syndrome which tends to be high-frequency predominant. Research shows that when age- and gender-matched normative data are accounted for, hearing loss is present across all test frequencies, suggesting widespread cochlear involvement
  • Onset: Present from birth or early childhood, though mild loss may not be detected until formal testing
  • Progression: Slowly progressive. The structurally weakened tectorial membrane undergoes ongoing micro-damage from normal daily sound exposure. This is a structural engineering failure, not merely a functional one
  • Prevalence: COL11A1 mutations are associated with hearing impairment in approximately 82.5% of patients — substantially higher than COL2A1 Stickler syndrome (52.2%)

Why It Progresses

The tectorial membrane was built with defective materials from the start. Every day of normal hearing — every conversation, every background noise — puts mechanical stress on a structure that is not strong enough to withstand it. Over years and decades, cumulative micro-damage degrades the membrane further. This is why otoprotection (noise avoidance, volume limiters, NAC supplementation) is critically important: you cannot fix the collagen defect, but you can reduce the additional wear and tear. See Hearing Management for otoprotection protocols.

Key clinical point: The structural mechanism is the foundation. It is always present. But if hearing loss is fluctuating, asymmetric, dropping suddenly, or progressing faster than expected, an additional mechanism is likely contributing — and that additional mechanism may be treatable.

Mechanism 2: Inflammatory / Autoimmune Hypothesis

This is the mechanism that most families and many clinicians do not know about — and it is potentially the most actionable because, unlike the structural defect, it may respond to medical treatment.

The Biological Basis

Type II collagen (COL2A1) is a major structural component of the inner ear, present in the tectorial membrane, the basilar membrane, the spiral ligament, and the otic capsule. In autoimmune inner ear disease (AIED), the immune system produces antibodies that target collagen II and attack these inner ear structures directly.

Why is this relevant to Marshall syndrome? Because COL11A1 mutations produce abnormal collagen XI, which is intimately associated with collagen II in the tectorial membrane. The abnormal collagen turnover and tissue damage in the inner ear may expose collagen II fragments that the immune system recognizes as foreign. Research has established that:

  • Anti-type II collagen antibodies produce sensorineural hearing loss, vestibular dysfunction, endolymphatic hydrops, and Eustachian tube inflammation in animal models
  • Patients with idiopathic bilateral progressive sensorineural hearing loss who responded to corticosteroid treatment had the highest mean antibody titers to type II collagen
  • Specific antibodies targeting inner ear antigens include those against collagen types II and IX, cochlin, and heat shock protein 70 (HSP70) — all of which may be relevant in the context of a collagen XI disorder

For a detailed explanation of how COL11A1-driven collagen turnover may trigger a self-amplifying immune attack on the inner ear, see the Autoimmune Hearing Loss: The Anti-CII Feedback Loop page (emerging research).

Clinical Profile

  • Pattern: Fluctuating hearing loss — periods of worsening followed by partial recovery, sometimes with sudden drops
  • Laterality: May be asymmetric, affecting one ear more than the other
  • Timing: Can occur at any age, but may become more apparent during periods of immune activation (infections, stress, puberty)
  • Associated symptoms: May co-occur with tinnitus fluctuation, ear fullness, or vestibular symptoms (dizziness, vertigo)
  • Distinguishing feature: Response to corticosteroids. If hearing improves after a course of oral prednisone or intratympanic steroid injection, this strongly suggests an inflammatory/autoimmune component

Testing

  • Anti-type II collagen (anti-CII) antibodies: Blood test. Elevated levels support an autoimmune component. Not widely ordered but available through reference labs
  • HSP70 antibodies: Another marker for autoimmune inner ear disease. Can be ordered through specialized labs
  • ESR, CRP, ANA: General inflammatory markers that may be elevated during active autoimmune flares
  • Steroid trial: A 2–4 week course of oral prednisone (typically 1 mg/kg/day) with audiometric testing before and after. Improvement of 10+ dB at any frequency or measurable improvement in speech recognition scores suggests steroid-responsive disease

Treatment

If an inflammatory/autoimmune component is confirmed:

  • First line: Corticosteroids (oral prednisone, intratympanic dexamethasone or methylprednisolone). Effective but cannot be used long-term due to side effects
  • Steroid-sparing agents: Methotrexate, azathioprine, or combination methotrexate + azathioprine for patients who relapse when steroids are tapered. Evidence is mixed — a randomized controlled trial found methotrexate was no more effective than placebo for maintaining hearing improvement from prednisone, but case series and clinical experience show meaningful benefit in some patients, particularly with combination therapy
  • Biologics: Rituximab and other immunosuppressants are being explored for refractory cases
  • Intratympanic steroids: Deliver medication directly to the inner ear via injection through the eardrum. Avoids systemic side effects of oral steroids. Can be repeated as needed
Critical question for your ENT: “Could there be an autoimmune or inflammatory component to my child’s hearing loss? The hearing seems to fluctuate — would it be worth checking anti-type II collagen antibodies or trying a steroid trial?” Many ENTs are not accustomed to considering autoimmune mechanisms in pediatric hearing loss, but the connection to collagen disorders is well-established in the research literature.

Mechanism 3: Conductive — Middle Ear Problems Established

Conductive hearing loss occurs when sound cannot travel efficiently through the outer and middle ear to reach the inner ear. In Marshall syndrome, this is primarily driven by the craniofacial anatomy associated with the condition.

Why Marshall Syndrome Causes Conductive Hearing Loss

A systematic review of hearing loss in Stickler syndrome found that conductive (14.1%) and mixed (18.1%) hearing loss was primarily found in young patients or patients with a palatal defect. The mechanisms include:

Eustachian Tube Dysfunction

The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear to the back of the throat and is responsible for equalizing pressure and draining fluid. In Marshall syndrome, several factors conspire to impair its function:

  • Midface hypoplasia: The flattened midface anatomy alters the angle and length of the Eustachian tube, making it less efficient at draining and equalizing pressure. See Midface Hypoplasia
  • Cleft palate / Pierre Robin sequence: Cleft palate disrupts the muscles (tensor veli palatini, levator veli palatini) that actively open the Eustachian tube during swallowing and yawning. Even after surgical palate repair, the muscular mechanics may remain suboptimal. See Pierre Robin Sequence
  • Connective tissue laxity: The Eustachian tube's support structures contain collagen. Abnormal collagen XI may contribute to tube floppiness and poor function

Chronic Otitis Media with Effusion (OME)

When the Eustachian tube does not drain properly, fluid accumulates behind the eardrum. This is extremely common in young children with Marshall syndrome, particularly those with a history of cleft palate. The fluid dampens eardrum vibration and reduces sound transmission by 15–40 dB — enough to significantly impair hearing during the critical language development window.

Ossicular Chain Involvement

The ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) are the three tiny bones that transmit sound from the eardrum to the inner ear. Chronic inflammation and repeated infections can, over time, affect the ossicular chain through:

  • Adhesive changes (ossicles becoming stiffened by scar tissue)
  • Tympanosclerosis (calcium deposits on the eardrum and ossicles)
  • Cholesteatoma (abnormal skin growth that can erode ossicles)

Treatment

The conductive component is the most directly treatable mechanism:

  • Tympanostomy tubes (PE tubes): Small tubes placed in the eardrum to ventilate the middle ear and prevent fluid accumulation. Often needed repeatedly in early childhood. Many children with Marshall syndrome require multiple sets of tubes
  • Prompt antibiotic treatment: Acute ear infections should be treated promptly to prevent chronic inflammation and complications
  • Adenoidectomy: Removal of the adenoids may improve Eustachian tube function in some children, though this should be discussed carefully in the context of possible velopharyngeal insufficiency
  • Ossicular reconstruction: If chronic disease has damaged the ossicular chain, surgical reconstruction may restore conductive hearing
The mixed hearing loss picture: Many children with Marshall syndrome have mixed hearing loss — a sensorineural base from the tectorial membrane defect plus a conductive overlay from middle ear dysfunction. It is critical to separate these components because the conductive part can often be treated, revealing the true sensorineural baseline. Audiometry with both air conduction and bone conduction thresholds (the “air-bone gap”) distinguishes between the two.

Mechanism 4: Central Auditory Processing Hypothesis

Central auditory processing disorder (CAPD) is a deficit in the brain's ability to process auditory signals, despite normal peripheral hearing structures. A child with CAPD may have a completely normal audiogram but still struggle to understand speech, particularly in noisy environments.

Relevance to Marshall Syndrome

CAPD is not a well-studied phenomenon specifically in Marshall or Stickler syndrome, but several factors suggest it should be on the differential:

  • Chronic auditory deprivation: Children who experience intermittent conductive hearing loss from recurrent otitis media during the critical language development period may develop central processing deficits even after the peripheral hearing normalizes. The brain's auditory circuitry does not wire properly when input is inconsistent during the first years of life
  • Connective tissue in neural structures: The perineurium (protective covering of nerves) contains collagen. While not directly studied in Marshall syndrome, connective tissue disorders including Ehlers-Danlos syndrome have been associated with CAPD, raising the possibility of a shared mechanism
  • Semicircular canal dehiscence: A rare bony defect in the temporal bone that can cause auditory processing symptoms and has been described in connective tissue disorders
  • Dual sensory burden: Children managing both hearing loss and vision problems (both common in Marshall syndrome) place extra demands on central processing resources, which may unmask subclinical processing deficits

Clinical Signs

Consider CAPD testing if your child shows these patterns despite adequate hearing aid use and a stable audiogram:

  • Difficulty understanding speech in noisy environments (cafeteria, playground, group settings)
  • Frequently asking “what?” or “huh?” despite apparently hearing the speaker
  • Difficulty following multi-step verbal instructions
  • Mishearing words (especially similar-sounding words)
  • Poor performance on dichotic listening tasks (different information presented to each ear simultaneously)
  • Disproportionate difficulty in academic subjects that rely heavily on listening (compared to reading-based subjects)

Testing

CAPD evaluation requires a specialized battery of tests, typically administered by an audiologist with APD expertise, usually beginning at age 7 (when central auditory pathways have matured enough for reliable testing):

  • Dichotic listening tests: Different words or sentences presented simultaneously to each ear; the child must identify or repeat both
  • Filtered speech / degraded speech tests: Speech signals that are intentionally degraded (filtered, time-compressed, or interrupted) to test the brain's ability to fill in gaps
  • Temporal processing tests: Tests of the ability to detect gaps in sound, discriminate patterns of short and long tones, or detect frequency and duration changes
  • Speech-in-noise tests: Measuring speech understanding at various signal-to-noise ratios
Important distinction: CAPD is not the same as inattention, ADHD, or language disorder, though it can look similar and may coexist with these conditions. The defining feature is that the deficit is auditory-specific — the same child may process visual information normally. A comprehensive evaluation should include both audiological and psychological testing to distinguish between these possibilities.

Management

  • FM / remote microphone systems: A personal FM system transmits the teacher's voice directly to the child's hearing aids, bypassing classroom noise. A 20-week FM trial in children with APD produced significant improvements in behavioral measures of auditory processing and participants' perceptions of listening skills. FM systems can be provided through the school via a 504 plan or IEP. See 504 Plans & School Accommodations
  • Classroom accommodations: Preferential seating, step-by-step instructions, repetition and rephrasing, visual supports, and reduced background noise
  • Auditory training programs: Computer-based or therapist-led programs that train specific auditory skills (dichotic listening, temporal processing, auditory memory)
  • Compensatory strategies: Teaching the child to use visual cues, context, and self-advocacy to manage challenging listening situations

Mechanism 5: Cervicogenic / Somatic Speculative

This is the least recognized mechanism, but it may explain otherwise puzzling auditory symptoms in some Marshall syndrome patients — particularly asymmetric or positionally fluctuating hearing changes.

The Connection: Cervical Spine to Inner Ear

Marshall syndrome frequently involves cervical spine pathology, including craniocervical instability (CCI), atlantoaxial instability, and cervical disc degeneration. These cervical problems can affect hearing and auditory processing through several pathways:

Vertebral Artery Compression

The vertebral arteries travel through the transverse foramina of the cervical vertebrae (C1–C6) before merging to form the basilar artery, which supplies blood to the brainstem, the auditory processing centers, and the inner ear via the labyrinthine artery. Cervical instability or osteoarthritic changes can compress these arteries, reducing blood flow to auditory structures.

Brainstem Irritation

In craniocervical instability, displacement of the odontoid process can compress the brainstem or upper cervical cord, and repetitive strain on the spinal cord and brainstem creates inflammatory edema. This irritation of the lower cranial nerves — including the vestibulocochlear nerve (CN VIII) — can produce auditory symptoms.

Clinical Evidence

Research on patients with cervical spine pathology has found:

  • 91% of patients with cervicalgia presented hearing impairment in at least one ear, with sensorineural impairment being the most prevalent (88%)
  • The most evident neurotological symptoms were dizziness (75.7%), tinnitus (36.3%), and auditory problems (36.3%)
  • Vertebrobasilar insufficiency from cervical instability can produce fluctuating audiovestibular symptoms that vary with head and neck position

Clinical Profile

  • Asymmetric or unilateral symptoms: May affect one ear more than the other, and the affected side may change
  • Position-dependent: Hearing may worsen with certain head or neck positions
  • Associated with other CCI symptoms: Dizziness, neck pain, headaches, visual disturbances, and autonomic symptoms (see Craniocervical Instability)
  • Tinnitus: May fluctuate with neck position or movement
  • Inconsistent audiometry: Test results may vary from visit to visit in ways that do not match typical sensorineural progression patterns
Diagnostic challenge: Cervicogenic auditory symptoms can be easily attributed to the “expected” sensorineural loss from COL11A1. If hearing fluctuates, is asymmetric, or correlates with neck symptoms, ask for upright cervical imaging and consider that the cervical spine may be contributing to the auditory picture. This is particularly important because cervicogenic contributions may be treatable through cervical spine management.

How to Determine Which Mechanism(s) Are Active

Most children with Marshall syndrome have at least two mechanisms operating simultaneously. The goal of the diagnostic workup is to identify all contributors so that each can be addressed with the appropriate intervention.

Comprehensive Audiological Workup

Test What It Reveals Which Mechanism It Identifies
Pure tone audiometry (air + bone conduction) Hearing thresholds across frequencies; air-bone gap separates sensorineural from conductive loss Structural (sensorineural baseline), Conductive (air-bone gap)
Speech recognition testing (quiet) Ability to understand words at comfortable volume Structural, Inflammatory (if fluctuating)
Speech-in-noise testing Ability to understand speech with background noise Central processing (disproportionate difficulty vs. quiet scores)
OAE (otoacoustic emissions) Sounds produced by functioning outer hair cells; present = outer hair cells intact Structural (absent OAE = hair cell damage); Retrocochlear (present OAE + abnormal ABR = neural problem)
ABR (auditory brainstem response) Neural pathway integrity from cochlea through brainstem Central, Cervicogenic (abnormal with normal OAE suggests neural/brainstem involvement)
Tympanometry Middle ear pressure and eardrum mobility Conductive (flat tympanogram = effusion; negative pressure = Eustachian tube dysfunction)
Acoustic reflexes Stapedial muscle contraction in response to loud sound Conductive (absent with effusion), Central (abnormal decay pattern)
APD battery Dichotic listening, temporal processing, filtered speech Central auditory processing (typically assessed from age 7+)

Imaging

  • MRI of internal auditory canals (IAC): Rules out retrocochlear pathology (vestibular schwannoma, brainstem lesions). Consider if hearing loss is asymmetric, rapidly progressive, or accompanied by unilateral tinnitus
  • CT temporal bone: Evaluates ossicular chain integrity, middle ear anatomy, and bony labyrinth. Useful when conductive component is unexplained by clinical examination
  • Upright cervical MRI / digital motion X-ray (DMX): Evaluates craniocervical instability, vertebral artery course, and brainstem compression. Consider if auditory symptoms fluctuate with position or correlate with neck symptoms

Blood Work

  • Anti-type II collagen (anti-CII) antibodies: Elevated levels suggest autoimmune inner ear component
  • HSP70 antibodies: Additional marker for autoimmune inner ear disease
  • ESR, CRP: General inflammatory markers; may be elevated during active autoimmune flares
  • ANA, RF: Broader autoimmune screening if systemic autoimmune disease is suspected
  • Complement levels (C3, C4): May be relevant given the complement activation described in the Anti-CII Feedback Loop

Therapeutic Trials

  • Steroid trial: 2–4 weeks of oral prednisone (or intratympanic steroid injection) with pre- and post-audiometry. Improvement suggests inflammatory/autoimmune component. This is both diagnostic and therapeutic
  • Tympanostomy tube placement: If conductive component is present, tube insertion followed by repeat audiometry reveals the true sensorineural baseline
The detective approach: Think of identifying hearing loss mechanisms like peeling back layers. Start with pure tone audiometry to separate sensorineural from conductive. Add OAE and ABR to localize within the auditory pathway. Test for autoimmune markers if hearing fluctuates. Evaluate central processing if the audiogram does not explain the functional difficulty. Consider cervical contributions if position-dependent or correlating with neck symptoms.

Treatment Implications by Mechanism

This is the practical payoff: once you know which mechanisms are active, treatment can be targeted rather than one-size-fits-all.

Mechanism Primary Treatment Adjunct Strategies Specialist
Structural (tectorial membrane) Hearing aids (BTE, bone conduction); cochlear implant if severe-to-profound Otoprotection (noise avoidance, NAC), FM systems, hearing rehabilitation technology Audiologist, ENT/Otolaryngologist
Inflammatory / autoimmune Corticosteroids (oral or intratympanic); steroid-sparing immunosuppression if recurrent Anti-inflammatory diet, monitoring anti-CII titers, Anti-CII Feedback Loop strategies ENT, Rheumatologist, Immunologist
Conductive (middle ear) Tympanostomy tubes, antibiotic treatment, adenoidectomy, ossicular reconstruction Allergy management, nasal steroids for Eustachian tube support ENT (Otologist)
Central auditory processing FM/remote microphone system (Roger or equivalent) Classroom accommodations (504 plan), auditory training programs, speech-language therapy Audiologist (APD-specialized), SLP
Cervicogenic / somatic Address underlying cervical instability (physical therapy, bracing, or surgery as indicated) Positional management, cervical spine monitoring, see CCI page Neurosurgeon, Physiatrist, Cervical PT
The compounding effect: When multiple mechanisms are active, treating the reversible ones can produce meaningful improvement even though the structural base cannot be changed. For example, a child with 45 dB sensorineural loss plus 15 dB conductive overlay presents as 60 dB mixed loss. Placing tubes removes the conductive component, revealing a more manageable 45 dB sensorineural baseline. Adding steroid treatment for an autoimmune component might recover another 5–10 dB. Neither intervention cures the underlying collagen defect, but the cumulative gain can mean the difference between struggling with hearing aids and hearing comfortably with them.

Because different mechanisms emerge at different ages and hearing needs change across development, monitoring must be age-adapted.

When to test outside the schedule: Do not wait for the next scheduled appointment if your child experiences:
  • Sudden hearing drop in one or both ears (medical urgency — seek evaluation within 24–72 hours)
  • New or worsened tinnitus
  • Hearing that fluctuates significantly from day to day
  • Hearing that seems to worsen with head/neck position changes
  • Regression in speech or language skills
  • New complaints of difficulty hearing at school despite stable audiogram

Putting It Together: A Case Example

To illustrate how these mechanisms interact in real life, consider a hypothetical Marshall syndrome patient:

  • Age 1: Born with cleft palate (repaired at 10 months). Newborn hearing screening shows bilateral moderate sensorineural hearing loss (35 dB). Fitted with hearing aids at 4 months. Mechanism: Structural.
  • Age 2: Hearing seems worse. Audiometry shows 50 dB loss. Tympanometry reveals bilateral effusion. PE tubes placed — repeat audiometry shows 38 dB loss. The "worsening" was partly conductive overlay. Mechanisms: Structural + Conductive.
  • Age 8: Stable audiogram at 40 dB, but teachers report the child cannot follow group discussions despite FM system. APD testing reveals dichotic listening deficit and poor temporal processing. Mechanisms: Structural + Central Processing.
  • Age 12: Hearing drops to 55 dB over 3 months, with fluctuation between appointments. No effusion on tympanometry. Anti-CII antibodies are elevated. Trial of oral prednisone recovers 8 dB. Mechanisms: Structural + Inflammatory/Autoimmune (new).
  • Age 15: Reports that hearing is worse when looking down (studying, phone use). Also has new neck pain and headaches. Upright cervical MRI reveals C1-C2 instability. Mechanisms: Structural + Inflammatory + Cervicogenic (new).

At each stage, identifying the new mechanism changed management: tubes for the conductive component, FM system and accommodations for the central component, immunosuppression for the autoimmune component, and cervical spine management for the cervicogenic component. The structural base never changed, but the overall hearing outcome improved dramatically by addressing each treatable layer.

Questions for Your Medical Team

Bring these questions to your next audiology or ENT appointment:
  1. “Is my child’s hearing loss purely sensorineural, or is there a conductive component? What is the air-bone gap?”
  2. “The hearing seems to fluctuate — could there be an autoimmune or inflammatory component? Would anti-type II collagen antibodies be worth checking?”
  3. “My child’s functional hearing seems worse than the audiogram suggests. Could central auditory processing be a factor? Should we do an APD evaluation?”
  4. “Given the cervical spine issues in Marshall syndrome, could neck problems be contributing to the hearing symptoms?”
  5. “Are there any treatable layers of hearing loss that we might be missing by attributing everything to the collagen defect?”
  6. “If my child’s hearing dropped suddenly, would you consider a steroid trial to determine if there is a reversible inflammatory component?”
