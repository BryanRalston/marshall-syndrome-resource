Building the Comprehensive Team

Minimum 9-Specialist Core Team
Marshall syndrome affects so many body systems that comprehensive management requires a coordinated multi-specialist team. Based on current evidence and clinical experience:
  • The minimum core team: Clinical geneticist, vitreoretinal ophthalmologist, audiologist, ENT (otolaryngologist), pediatric orthopedic surgeon, pediatric rheumatologist, physical therapist experienced with connective tissue disorders, developmental pediatrician, and psychologist specializing in chronic pain.
  • The appointment burden is real: A 2025 rare disease survey found that families of children with multi-system genetic conditions average 14.7 medical appointments per month. This is unsustainable without deliberate coordination.
  • Solution — designate a care coordinator: One specialist (usually the clinical geneticist, who understands the full syndrome) should serve as the care coordinator — the single point of contact who receives reports from all other specialists, identifies conflicting recommendations, and maintains the unified care plan.
  • Annual comprehensive clinic day: Where available, schedule a single-day comprehensive clinic visit that combines multiple specialist evaluations in one trip. Several skeletal dysplasia programs and rare disease centers offer this model. It reduces travel burden, minimizes school absences, and — critically — allows specialists to discuss findings together rather than in isolation.
Telehealth — Reducing the Appointment Burden
With families averaging 14.7 medical appointments per month, telehealth can meaningfully reduce travel, missed school days, and caregiver exhaustion. Use it strategically:
  • Telehealth-eligible visits: Follow-up appointments, medication management, genetic counseling, psychology sessions, physical therapy exercise reviews, and school advocacy meetings can all be conducted effectively via video or phone.
  • In-person required: Dilated eye exams, audiometry, physical measurements (height/span/segments), imaging studies, and any procedural visits must remain in person.
  • Alternating schedule: Consider alternating annual visits between in-person comprehensive evaluations and telehealth follow-ups to cut the appointment burden without sacrificing continuity of care.
  • 2025 CMS expansion: Telehealth coverage has been expanded under the 2025 CMS rules, including audio-only visits for patients without reliable broadband — check your plan’s current telehealth benefits.
  • Genetic counseling access: Telehealth has shortened genetic counseling wait times from months to days in many programs. If your family has not yet had comprehensive genetic counseling, a telehealth session can often be scheduled within weeks.
  • Distance specialists: Establish telehealth relationships with Stickler/Marshall specialist centers (e.g., Mass General, Johns Hopkins, Lurie Children’s) even if you are geographically distant. Many complex rare disease programs now offer remote consultations for initial evaluation and ongoing co-management with your local team.
