Sports Clearance & Physical Activity Guide

A printable resource for PE teachers, coaches, camp counselors, and families. This page provides evidence-based activity classifications for children with Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutation, type XI collagen disorder), covering what is safe, what needs modification, and what must be avoided.

Print this page: This guide is designed to be printed and handed to PE teachers, coaches, and camp counselors. Use the print button in the sample letter section below, or press Ctrl+P at any time.

The Activity Paradox

Children with Marshall syndrome need physical activity. Exercise is not optional wellness advice—it is molecular medicine. At the cellular level:

  • NRF2 pathway activation — exercise triggers the master antioxidant response, reducing the oxidative stress that accelerates connective tissue degradation
  • AMPK signaling — the cellular energy sensor that, when activated by exercise, promotes mitochondrial biogenesis and reduces chronic inflammation
  • Irisin release — this myokine (muscle-derived hormone) promotes bone density, reduces fat-driven inflammation, and supports cartilage homeostasis—all directly relevant to a collagen disorder
  • Muscle as a stabilizer — in connective tissue disorders, ligaments and joint capsules are inherently lax. Strong muscles compensate for what collagen cannot provide. Deconditioning is the enemy.

But some activities carry genuinely elevated risk specific to Marshall syndrome: retinal detachment, cervical spine injury, and joint damage from hypermobility. The consequences of retinal detachment or cervical instability are severe and sometimes irreversible.

The goal is maximum safe activity, NOT maximum restriction. An overly restricted child becomes deconditioned, which worsens joint instability, pain, fatigue, and bone density—creating a vicious cycle. Every "no" should come with an alternative "yes."

Why Each RED Activity Is Prohibited

Each prohibited activity maps to specific, well-documented risks in connective tissue disorders. These are not theoretical—they reflect the known complications of COL11A1-related collagen deficiency.

Activity Specific Risks in Marshall Syndrome
Trampolining Retinal detachment (Valsalva maneuver + jarring vertical forces increase intraocular pressure and vitreous traction); cervical spine injury from uncontrolled landing; joint hyperextension at knees, ankles, and hips on rebound
Gymnastics Cervical hyperextension (bridges, walkovers, handstands); joint hyperextension in lax ligaments under load; high-impact landings on degenerated cartilage; risk of vertebral subluxation
Contact sports (football, rugby) Cervical instability makes tackles and collisions dangerous (risk of cord compression); direct blows to the head/face risk retinal detachment; joint dislocations in hypermobile joints under contact forces
Diving (pool/platform) Cervical hyperextension on water entry; increased intraocular pressure on impact; retinal detachment risk from sudden deceleration; risk of vertebral compression
Wrestling Cervical manipulation by opponent; joint locks against hypermobile joints risk dislocation; sustained Valsalva increases intraocular pressure
Boxing / martial arts sparring Direct facial/head impact is the single highest risk factor for retinal detachment in myopic eyes; cervical whiplash from blows; cumulative joint trauma
Bungee jumping Extreme deceleration forces on the cervical spine and retina; massive intraocular pressure spike; uncontrolled joint hyperextension
Roller coasters High g-forces on cervical spine (especially lateral); vitreous traction from rapid acceleration/deceleration; jarring not controllable by the participant
Heading soccer balls Repetitive cervical loading; direct orbital/facial impact risk; cumulative retinal traction
Heavy overhead lifting Disc compression in already-degenerated vertebral discs; Valsalva maneuver increases intraocular pressure; cervical strain under load; risk of vertebral endplate fracture
Competitive cheerleading stunts Flyer position: falling risk with cervical instability; tumbling: same risks as gymnastics; base position: heavy overhead loading on cervical spine

Eye-Specific Activity Restrictions

Retinal detachment is one of the most serious acute risks in Marshall syndrome. The vitreous abnormalities (optically empty vitreous, vitreoretinal degeneration) combined with high myopia create a baseline elevated risk. Activity restrictions must account for the child's current retinal status.

Key Questions for Risk Assessment

  • Has the child had prophylactic cryoretinopexy or laser retinopexy? If yes, the retina has been reinforced at its weakest points. This reduces but does not eliminate risk. Some YELLOW activities may become safer after successful prophylaxis, but RED activities remain prohibited.
  • What is the most recent retinal exam status? Look for: lattice degeneration, retinal holes, posterior vitreous detachment, retinal tears, or prior detachment history. Any of these findings should shift YELLOW activities toward RED.
  • Current refraction? Higher myopia (> -6 diopters) correlates with higher retinal detachment risk and should lower the threshold for restriction.

Activities That Increase Intraocular Pressure

The following activities produce transient spikes in intraocular pressure through Valsalva maneuver, inversion, or straining. These spikes increase vitreous traction on an already vulnerable retina:

  • Heavy lifting with breath-holding (Valsalva) — prohibited; light weights with continuous breathing are acceptable
  • Inverted positions (headstands, handstands, inverted yoga poses) — prohibited
  • Straining exercises (maximal effort lifts, intense isometrics) — prohibited
  • High-altitude activities — discuss with ophthalmologist if above 8,000 feet
Emergency awareness: Any sudden onset of flashing lights, a shower of new floaters, or a curtain/shadow across vision is a retinal emergency. Stop all activity immediately and go to the nearest emergency room or retinal specialist. Time matters—outcomes are dramatically better within 24 hours.

Cervical Spine Considerations

Type XI collagen is a critical structural component of intervertebral discs and the ligaments that stabilize the craniocervical junction. COL11A1 mutations can compromise both, making cervical spine clearance essential before any contact or high-impact activity.

Before Participation: CCI Assessment

  • Craniocervical instability (CCI) must be evaluated before clearing any contact activity, competitive sport, or activity with fall risk
  • Evaluation: upright MRI or flexion-extension imaging of the craniocervical junction
  • If CCI is identified: contact activities move from YELLOW/RED to absolute prohibition; even some GREEN activities may require a cervical collar
  • If CCI is ruled out: YELLOW activities can proceed with stated modifications

Activity-Specific Cervical Precautions

  • No heading in soccer — repetitive cervical axial loading is contraindicated regardless of CCI status
  • No tackling in football — cervical compression and lateral flexion under force
  • No inverted positions — headstands, handstands, and inverted yoga poses load the cervical spine and increase intraocular pressure simultaneously
  • Cervical collar consideration — if CCI is present, discuss with the spine specialist whether a soft collar should be worn during specific activities (e.g., cycling, horseback riding)

For detailed information on craniocervical instability, see Craniocervical Instability.

Cardiac Clearance

While cardiac involvement in Marshall syndrome is less common than in Stickler syndrome type I or Marfan syndrome, mitral valve prolapse (MVP) and occasional aortic root dilation have been reported. Cardiac clearance should be part of the annual sports physical.

When Cardiac Clearance Is Required

  • Annual echocardiogram before sports season if MVP or any valvular abnormality has been identified
  • Aortic root measurement — if the aortic root z-score exceeds +2, competitive sports should be restricted per AHA/ACC guidelines for connective tissue disorders
  • Moderate-severe valvular regurgitation — limits high-intensity sustained aerobic activity and competitive sports; recreational GREEN activities remain appropriate
  • No cardiac findings — standard participation in GREEN and YELLOW activities; repeat echo every 2–3 years as surveillance
Key point: Most children with Marshall syndrome will have normal cardiac function. Cardiac screening is a precaution based on the connective tissue overlap, not an expectation of pathology.

For cardiac screening protocols, see Cardiac Screening.

Sample Letter for PE Teacher / Coach

Use this template to communicate activity restrictions clearly to school staff. Fill in the blanks, print, and provide to the PE teacher, coach, or camp counselor alongside any medical documentation.

Date: _______________

To: PE Teacher / Coach / Activity Supervisor
From: Parent/Guardian of _______________
Re: Physical Activity Modifications for Marshall Syndrome

Our child has been diagnosed with Marshall syndrome, a connective tissue disorder caused by a mutation in the COL11A1 gene (type XI collagen). This affects the structural integrity of joints, eyes, spine, and other connective tissues. Physical activity is important and encouraged, but certain activities carry specific medical risks that require modification or avoidance.

CAN Participate In (GREEN — Encouraged)

  • Swimming (no diving)
  • Cycling / stationary bike
  • Walking / hiking
  • Recumbent bike / elliptical
  • Modified yoga (strength-focused, no inverted poses)
  • Pilates and core stability work
  • Archery, golf, tai chi

Modified Participation (YELLOW — With Specific Adjustments)

  • Running — soft surfaces only (grass, track), limit to short distances
  • Basketball — drills and skills only, no competitive/contact play
  • Soccer — absolutely no heading; limited body contact
  • Dance — no positions that exploit joint hypermobility, no drops or lifts
  • Rock climbing — belayed, low walls only, with spotter
  • Tennis / badminton — avoid aggressive overhead serves initially

CANNOT Participate In (RED — Medically Prohibited)

  • Trampolining
  • Gymnastics (all forms)
  • Diving (pool/platform/springboard)
  • Contact football, rugby, wrestling
  • Boxing or martial arts sparring
  • Bungee jumping, high g-force rides
  • Heading soccer balls
  • Heavy overhead lifting
  • Competitive cheerleading stunts

Emergency Signs — Stop Activity and Contact Parent Immediately

  • Vision changes: sudden flashing lights, shower of floaters, curtain or shadow across vision → this is a retinal emergency, call 911
  • Neck pain or neurological symptoms: numbness, tingling, weakness in arms/legs after any impact or fall
  • Joint dislocation: a joint that "pops out" and does not return to normal position
  • Severe or unusual pain: not typical exercise soreness but sharp, sudden, or worsening pain

Summer Camp & Outdoor Activities

Summer camps, field trips, and outdoor programs require advance preparation. The camp counselor or trip leader should receive the sample letter above, ideally with a phone conversation to answer questions.

Camp Counselor Preparation

  • Provide the traffic light classification chart (print this page)
  • Explain the three emergency signs: vision changes, neck symptoms, joint dislocation
  • Ensure the child is never pressured to participate in RED activities "just this once"
  • Designate a GREEN alternative for every RED activity on the camp schedule

Water Activities

  • Swimming: YES — swimming is one of the best activities for Marshall syndrome; buoyancy unloads joints and spine while building strength and cardiovascular fitness
  • Diving: NO — no diving from boards or platforms; entering the pool by jumping feet-first from the edge is acceptable
  • Water slides: CAUTION — gentle slides are fine; high-speed or multi-drop slides carry cervical whiplash risk
  • Kayaking / canoeing: YES — low-impact upper body exercise with life vest; avoid whitewater

Field Trips and Playgrounds

  • Playground equipment: Swings and slides are fine; avoid monkey bars if shoulder instability is present; no trampolines at trampoline parks
  • Amusement parks: Avoid roller coasters and high g-force rides; gentle rides are acceptable
  • Hiking: Appropriate with even terrain and reasonable distance; ensure adequate hydration and rest breaks

Heat Sensitivity

If autonomic dysfunction is present: Some children with Marshall syndrome experience temperature dysregulation and heat intolerance. Ensure access to shade, hydration, and cooling breaks. Know the signs of heat exhaustion: excessive fatigue, dizziness, nausea, headache, or unusually flushed skin. See Autonomic Dysfunction for more detail.

Age-Appropriate Progression

Activity recommendations evolve as the child grows. Younger children need guardrails set by parents and teachers; older children and teens need honest conversations about why restrictions exist so they can self-advocate.

Ages 4–7: Free Play with Guardrails

  • Encourage active free play: running, climbing (low structures), swimming, bike riding with training wheels
  • No trampolines — this is the single most important restriction to enforce early, because trampolines are ubiquitous at birthday parties and playdates
  • Playground supervision to redirect away from high-risk activities
  • Swimming lessons are ideal at this age—builds a lifelong safe activity skill
  • Avoid competitive or organized contact sports

Ages 8–12: Organized Sports with Modifications

  • Swim team is strongly encouraged — competitive but safe; builds cardiovascular fitness, strength, and social belonging
  • YELLOW sports can be attempted with modifications and coach awareness
  • This is when school PE becomes structured—provide the PE letter at the start of each school year
  • Begin teaching the child to self-advocate: "I can do X instead of Y"
  • Introduce the concept of joint-protective movement: proper form matters more for them than for peers

Ages 13–18: Fitness Focus and Self-Advocacy

  • Shift from "sports" to "fitness" — the goal is lifelong physical health, not competitive athletics
  • Resistance training with proper form: light weights, high repetitions (15–20 reps), continuous breathing (no Valsalva), focus on controlled movements. A few sessions with a physical therapist or certified trainer who understands connective tissue disorders is worth the investment.
  • Discuss limitations honestly — teens deserve to understand the "why" behind restrictions. "Your collagen doesn't have the same tensile strength, so ligaments can't absorb the forces that your peers' ligaments can. We build muscle instead."
  • Peer pressure peaks in these years—role-play responses: "I have a joint condition, so I do cycling instead of football. Want to come ride sometime?"
  • Consider adaptive sports programs if available in the community
Long-term perspective: The children who maintain consistent, safe exercise through adolescence enter adulthood with better joint stability, bone density, and pain management than those who become sedentary due to over-restriction. The investment in finding the right activities pays dividends for decades.
