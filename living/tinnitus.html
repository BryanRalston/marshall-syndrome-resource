Tinnitus Management in Marshall Syndrome

Key Point: Tinnitus — the perception of sound without an external source — is a common but under-recognized feature of Marshall syndrome. The same tectorial membrane collagen defect that causes sensorineural hearing loss also creates the conditions for chronic tinnitus. Because many Marshall patients already use hearing aids, tinnitus masking features can often be integrated into existing amplification devices, making management more accessible than in the general tinnitus population.

Why Tinnitus Is Common in Marshall Syndrome

Tinnitus is not a disease itself but a symptom of underlying auditory system dysfunction. In the general population, approximately 10–15% of adults experience chronic tinnitus. In individuals with sensorineural hearing loss — which affects over 80% of people with Marshall syndrome — tinnitus prevalence rises to 70–85%. Marshall syndrome creates multiple overlapping mechanisms that make tinnitus particularly likely.

The Tectorial Membrane Connection

The primary mechanism begins with the same COL11A1 mutation that causes hearing loss. As described on the Hearing Management page, defective type XI collagen disrupts the tectorial membrane’s radial collagen fibrils, weakening the coupling between the membrane and the outer hair cell (OHC) stereocilia. This structural defect reduces the cochlear amplifier’s gain, resulting in diminished neural input from the cochlea to the brain.

The brain responds to this reduced input through a process called central gain enhancement: the central auditory system increases its sensitivity to compensate for the weaker peripheral signal. This compensatory hyperactivity — essentially the brain “turning up the volume” on a weakened signal — is the dominant neurophysiological model of tinnitus generation. Research published in Scientific Reports (2023) confirmed that individuals with chronic tinnitus show reduced cochlear nerve responses alongside hyperactivity in central auditory pathways — precisely the pattern expected in Marshall syndrome.

Cochlear Damage Beyond the Tectorial Membrane

The tinnitus-generating damage in Marshall syndrome extends beyond the tectorial membrane alone:

  • Outer hair cell dysfunction: When the defective TM fails to properly couple to OHC stereocilia, the hair cells may sustain cumulative mechanical stress and damage over time, further reducing cochlear output and amplifying central hyperactivity
  • Cochlear synaptopathy: Recent NIH research has identified that tinnitus is associated with damage to the synapses between inner hair cells and spiral ganglion neurons — so-called “hidden hearing loss.” The structurally compromised cochlea in Marshall syndrome may be more vulnerable to this synaptic damage
  • Spiral ligament involvement: Type XI collagen is also present in the spiral ligament, which maintains the ionic environment essential for OHC function. COL11A1 mutations may compromise this structure, adding a metabolic insult to the mechanical one

The Autoimmune Amplifier

As described on the Autoimmune Hearing Loss page, there is growing evidence that immune-mediated mechanisms may contribute to inner ear damage in connective tissue disorders. Autoantibodies directed against type II collagen — which shares structural epitopes with type XI collagen — have been shown to produce sensorineural hearing loss, vestibular dysfunction, and endolymphatic hydrops in animal models. As many as 25–50% of patients with autoimmune inner ear disease (AIED) experience tinnitus and aural fullness. If an autoimmune component is active in some Marshall patients, it may create inflammatory damage that worsens tinnitus beyond what structural defects alone would produce.

Chronic Otitis Media and Inflammatory Spillover

The recurrent middle ear infections common in Marshall syndrome (due to midface hypoplasia and Eustachian tube dysfunction) may worsen tinnitus through inflammatory mediator spillover. Bacterial endotoxins and cytokines from chronic otitis media can cross the round window membrane into the cochlea, damaging inner ear structures and potentially triggering or intensifying tinnitus independently of the sensorineural component.

Types of Tinnitus in Marshall Syndrome

Not all tinnitus is the same. Marshall syndrome creates conditions for multiple distinct tinnitus subtypes, each with different mechanisms and treatment implications. A single patient may experience more than one type simultaneously.

Type Character Mechanism in Marshall Key Features
Cochlear (sensorineural) High-pitched ringing, hissing, or whistling; often constant Tectorial membrane defect → reduced cochlear output → central gain enhancement → phantom sound perception The most common type in Marshall. Pitch typically matches the frequency region of greatest hearing loss (usually high-frequency). Worsens as hearing loss progresses
Somatic / cervicogenic Variable tone; may change with head/neck position, jaw movement, or muscle tension Cervical spine pathology (disc degeneration, CCI) → abnormal somatosensory input to dorsal cochlear nucleus → modulation of tinnitus signal Can be made louder or softer by turning the head, clenching the jaw, or pressing on neck muscles. May fluctuate with cervical spine flares. A 2025 study found somatic tinnitus is associated with greater distress and quality-of-life impact
TMJ-related Low-pitched roaring, clicking, or buzzing; often unilateral TMJ dysfunction (COL11A1 affects TMJ disc collagen) → tensor tympani and tensor veli palatini muscle spasm → middle ear pressure changes and direct auditory pathway modulation People with TMJ disorders are approximately 4 times more likely to have tinnitus. Often worsens with chewing, yawning, or jaw clenching. May improve with TMJ treatment (splints, physical therapy, botulinum toxin)
Pulsatile Rhythmic whooshing or throbbing synchronized with heartbeat Cervical spine pathology or connective tissue laxity may alter blood vessel geometry near the cochlea; Eustachian tube dysfunction can make normal vascular sounds audible Less common than other types but clinically important because it can indicate vascular pathology. Always warrants investigation (see “When to Escalate” below)
Clinical Pearl: When a Marshall syndrome patient reports tinnitus, ask them to demonstrate whether they can change its loudness or pitch by moving their head, clenching their jaw, or pressing on their neck muscles. If yes, a somatic or TMJ component is likely present — and these components are often the most treatable.

Assessment: Evaluating Tinnitus in Marshall Syndrome

Thorough assessment is the foundation of effective management. Because Marshall patients typically already have audiological follow-up for hearing loss, tinnitus assessment can often be integrated into existing appointments.

Audiological Workup

  • Comprehensive audiometry: Pure-tone air and bone conduction thresholds, speech recognition testing, and tympanometry. The audiogram pattern helps predict tinnitus frequency (tinnitus pitch usually corresponds to the region of maximum hearing loss)
  • Otoacoustic emissions (OAE): Absent or reduced OAEs confirm OHC dysfunction, supporting a cochlear origin for tinnitus
  • Tinnitus pitch matching: The audiologist presents tones at different frequencies and the patient identifies which one most closely matches their tinnitus. This is essential for notched sound therapy (see below)
  • Tinnitus loudness matching: The patient adjusts a tone to match the perceived loudness of their tinnitus. Most tinnitus is surprisingly quiet when matched objectively (typically only 5–10 dB above threshold), which can itself be reassuring to patients
  • Minimum masking level (MML): The lowest level of broadband noise needed to just cover the tinnitus. A low MML predicts good response to sound therapy
  • Residual inhibition testing: After masking the tinnitus with noise for 60 seconds, the tinnitus may temporarily decrease or disappear. Positive residual inhibition is a favorable prognostic sign for sound-based treatments

Standardized Questionnaires

Imaging: When and Why

Routine imaging is not needed for typical cochlear tinnitus in the context of known Marshall syndrome hearing loss. However, imaging is indicated when:

  • Pulsatile tinnitus: MRA/CTA to evaluate for vascular causes (dural arteriovenous fistula, carotid dissection, glomus tumors, venous sinus anomalies). Connective tissue disorders increase the theoretical risk of vascular abnormalities
  • Unilateral tinnitus with asymmetric hearing loss: MRI with gadolinium to rule out retrocochlear pathology (vestibular schwannoma/acoustic neuroma)
  • Sudden onset or rapidly worsening tinnitus: Urgent MRI to evaluate for retrocochlear lesion, labyrinthine hemorrhage, or endolymphatic hydrops
  • Suspected cervicogenic component: Cervical spine MRI to evaluate disc disease, instability, or CCI that may be contributing to somatic tinnitus

Management Strategies

There is no cure for tinnitus. The goal of management is to reduce its perceived loudness, minimize emotional distress, improve sleep, and prevent the tinnitus from dominating the patient’s attention. The most effective approach combines multiple strategies tailored to the individual.

1. Sound Therapy

Sound therapy uses external sound to reduce the contrast between tinnitus and the auditory environment, facilitating habituation. It is the foundation of most tinnitus management programs.

Approach How It Works Evidence Practical Notes
Background enrichment Low-level continuous sound (white noise, nature sounds, rain, fans) reduces the silence that makes tinnitus most noticeable Well-established; universally recommended as a first-line strategy Sound machines at bedside, smartphone apps (myNoise, White Noise), open windows, low-volume music. The goal is to partially mask, not completely cover, the tinnitus — total masking prevents habituation
Notched sound therapy Music or noise with a “notch” (frequency band removed) centered on the tinnitus pitch. This selectively reduces cortical hyperactivity at the tinnitus frequency via lateral inhibition Okamoto et al. (PNAS 2010): 12 months of notched music reduced subjective tinnitus loudness and auditory cortex activity in the tinnitus frequency region. A 2022 RCT confirmed reorganization in emotional processing centers. Effective for tinnitus frequencies ≤8 kHz; limited evidence above 8 kHz Requires accurate tinnitus pitch matching first. Available through apps (AudioNotch, Tinnitracks) that filter the user’s own music library. Recommended listening: 1–2 hours daily for at least 3–6 months
Fractal tones Algorithmically generated, non-repeating tonal patterns (used in Widex Zen hearing aids). Unpredictable enough to engage attention but pleasant enough to promote relaxation Moderate evidence from manufacturer-sponsored studies showing reduced tinnitus distress when combined with counseling Particularly useful for patients who find white noise unpleasant. Can be programmed into compatible hearing aids as an additional program

2. Hearing Aids with Tinnitus Masking Features

This is where Marshall syndrome patients have a significant advantage. Because most already use hearing aids for sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus management can be integrated into their existing devices at little or no additional cost.

  • Amplification alone helps tinnitus: By restoring auditory input that the brain has been compensating for with hyperactivity, hearing aids directly address the central gain mechanism that generates tinnitus. Studies show that 60–70% of hearing aid users report tinnitus improvement from amplification alone
  • Built-in tinnitus sound generators: Most major hearing aid manufacturers (Phonak, Oticon, Widex, ReSound, Signia, Starkey) now include tinnitus sound generator programs in their premium and mid-tier devices. These can produce white noise, pink noise, ocean sounds, or fractal tones that blend with the amplified sound
  • App-controlled customization: Modern hearing aids allow the user (or parent) to adjust tinnitus masking volume, frequency shape, and sound type through a smartphone app. This means the child or adult can increase masking in quiet environments (bedtime, studying) and reduce it in noisy ones
  • Bluetooth streaming: Sound therapy apps, relaxation audio, and notched sound therapy can be streamed directly to hearing aids via Bluetooth LE Audio, eliminating the need for separate earbuds or speakers
For parents: If your child already wears hearing aids and reports ringing or buzzing, ask the audiologist about activating the tinnitus masking program at the next appointment. This is typically a software adjustment to the existing devices — no new hardware required.

3. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) for Tinnitus

CBT is the most evidence-based psychological treatment for tinnitus. It does not eliminate the tinnitus signal, but it fundamentally changes the brain’s emotional and attentional response to it, reducing suffering even when the sound persists.

The 2025 Tonndorf Lecture at the World Tinnitus Congress, published in Brain Sciences, confirmed that both psychologist-delivered and audiologist-delivered CBT approaches produce significant improvements in tinnitus-related quality of life. Guided internet-based CBT (iCBT) also demonstrates positive outcomes, making it accessible for families without local tinnitus specialists.

Core CBT components for tinnitus:

  • Cognitive restructuring: Identifying and challenging catastrophic thoughts about tinnitus (“This will never stop,” “I’m going deaf,” “Something is seriously wrong”) and replacing them with accurate, balanced appraisals
  • Attention retraining: Exercises that practice shifting focus away from the tinnitus signal toward other sensory inputs or mental tasks, strengthening the brain’s ability to filter the tinnitus into the background
  • Behavioral activation: Ensuring the patient remains engaged in valued activities despite tinnitus, preventing the withdrawal and isolation that worsen distress
  • Relaxation training: Progressive muscle relaxation, diaphragmatic breathing, and guided imagery to reduce the autonomic arousal (muscle tension, elevated heart rate) that often accompanies tinnitus distress
  • Sleep-specific strategies: Stimulus control, sleep restriction, and cognitive strategies targeting tinnitus-related sleep anxiety (see “Sleep Hygiene” below)
Evidence Summary: A 2025 bibliometric analysis published in Frontiers in Neuroscience found that research into CBT for tinnitus has accelerated rapidly, with structured counseling and CBT performing as well as or better than sound therapy alone in head-to-head comparisons. Crucially, a 2025 Frontiers in Audiology and Otology review found that the combination of counseling plus sound therapy produced greater benefits than either intervention alone — supporting a multimodal approach.

4. Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT)

Developed by Pawel Jastreboff based on his neurophysiological model of tinnitus, TRT combines two components:

  1. Directive counseling: Education about tinnitus mechanisms, emphasizing that tinnitus itself is not dangerous and that the brain can learn to reclassify it as a neutral signal (like the hum of a refrigerator) rather than a threat
  2. Sound therapy: Continuous low-level broadband noise delivered through ear-level sound generators (or hearing aids with sound generators), set below the level of the tinnitus. The goal is not to mask the tinnitus but to reduce the contrast between tinnitus and silence, promoting neural habituation over 12–24 months

A meta-analysis of 13 randomized controlled trials (1,345 patients) found that TRT combined with standard care produced higher response rates and greater THI score improvements than standard care alone. However, the evidence base remains limited by small studies and high risk of bias. TRT requires a trained TRT clinician and a commitment of 12–24 months for full effect.

CBT vs. TRT — Which to Choose? Both approaches are valid and not mutually exclusive. CBT has a stronger overall evidence base and typically shows faster results (8–12 weeks of structured sessions). TRT may be preferred when the patient has strong emotional reactions to silence or when sound sensitivity (hyperacusis) coexists with tinnitus. Many audiologists now combine elements of both approaches.

5. Neuromodulation: Vagus Nerve Stimulation

Transcutaneous auricular vagus nerve stimulation (taVNS) targets the same auricular branch of the vagus nerve described on the Vagus Nerve Stimulation page. The rationale for tinnitus is that pairing vagus nerve stimulation with sound exposure promotes plasticity in the auditory cortex, helping to “reprogram” the hyperactive neural circuits generating tinnitus.

A 2024 narrative review in Frontiers in Neuroscience found that while subjective and objective tinnitus measures did not consistently improve with taVNS compared to sham controls, EEG studies showed significant increases in theta band activity — suggesting real cerebral effects even when symptom improvement was modest. Some patients reported improvements on quality-of-life questionnaires even without changes in tinnitus loudness.

Marshall-specific considerations:

  • Patients already using taVNS for pain management or autonomic dysfunction may gain a secondary tinnitus benefit at no additional cost or effort
  • Standardized therapeutic protocols for tinnitus-specific taVNS are still being developed; current evidence does not support taVNS as a standalone tinnitus treatment
  • taVNS appears safe and well-tolerated, with the most common adverse events being localized skin irritation, headache, and, paradoxically, temporary worsening of tinnitus in some patients

6. Sleep Hygiene for Tinnitus-Related Insomnia

Tinnitus is most distressing at bedtime, when reduced environmental sound makes the internal signal most prominent. Sleep disruption then worsens tinnitus perception the next day, creating a vicious cycle. The iTICQ pediatric questionnaire identified “Sleep and Feeling Tired” as one of the six primary domains affected by tinnitus in children.

  • Bedside sound enrichment: A sound machine, fan, or smartphone app playing low-level continuous noise (rain, waves, pink noise) is the single most effective intervention for tinnitus-related sleep difficulty. Place the sound source at a consistent distance; set volume just below the tinnitus level, not above it
  • Pillow speakers: For patients who share a room (siblings, partners) and cannot use a loud bedside sound source, under-pillow speakers deliver sound only to the tinnitus sufferer
  • Avoid silence: Total silence amplifies tinnitus perception. Even a quietly ticking clock or slightly open window provides partial masking
  • Consistent sleep schedule: Regular sleep and wake times stabilize circadian rhythms. Sleep deprivation independently worsens tinnitus through increased cortisol and reduced inhibitory neurotransmission
  • Screen curfew: Blue light from screens suppresses melatonin. For Marshall patients already managing sleep architecture, this is doubly important since poor sleep amplifies both pain and tinnitus
  • Cognitive defusion for bedtime anxiety: Teach the patient (or help a child practice) acknowledging the tinnitus without fighting it: “I notice the ringing. It is there. It is not dangerous. My brain will filter it out as I relax.” This reduces the arousal response that prevents sleep onset

7. Medications to Avoid: Ototoxic Drugs

CRITICAL — Medications That Can Worsen Tinnitus
Individuals with Marshall syndrome already have a structurally compromised cochlea. Ototoxic medications that would cause only mild, temporary effects in a healthy ear can cause permanent worsening of hearing loss and tinnitus in an ear with pre-existing collagen defects. Every prescriber who treats a Marshall syndrome patient should be informed of the baseline hearing loss and tinnitus.
Drug Class Examples Ototoxicity Mechanism Risk Level / Notes
Aminoglycoside antibiotics Gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin, streptomycin, neomycin Direct destruction of cochlear and vestibular hair cells through reactive oxygen species (ROS) and mitochondrial toxicity HIGH RISK. Can cause permanent sensorineural hearing loss. Gentamicin preferentially damages vestibular hair cells; amikacin preferentially damages cochlear cells. In Marshall patients with already-compromised hair cells, even a single course may cause irreversible worsening. Use only when no alternative antibiotic exists, and monitor with serial audiometry
Loop diuretics Furosemide (Lasix), bumetanide, ethacrynic acid Disrupts the endocochlear potential by affecting the stria vascularis ion transport MODERATE RISK. Ototoxicity occurs in approximately 6–7% of users. Usually reversible upon discontinuation, but potentiated by concurrent aminoglycosides. Avoid combination with aminoglycosides in Marshall patients
Platinum chemotherapy Cisplatin, carboplatin Destroys OHCs through ROS and DNA damage; irreversible HIGH RISK. Cisplatin causes hearing loss in up to 80% of treated patients at high cumulative doses. If chemotherapy is ever needed, request audiological monitoring before, during, and after treatment. Otoprotective agents (sodium thiosulfate) should be discussed with the oncologist

Practical step: Add “sensorineural hearing loss — avoid ototoxic medications” to the patient’s allergy/alert list in their medical record. This creates a system-level safeguard that triggers pharmacist review before ototoxic drugs are dispensed. Include this information on the Emergency Medical Card.

Pediatric Considerations: Tinnitus in Children with Marshall Syndrome

Children often do not report tinnitus unprompted. Studies consistently show that when children with hearing loss are directly asked about ringing, buzzing, or other head noises, prevalence rates of 25–60% are found. When not asked, almost none volunteer the information. Many children assume the noise is normal because they have had it their entire lives. Audiologists and parents should proactively ask about tinnitus at every hearing evaluation.

Why Children Stay Silent

  • Lack of reference: A child born with sensorineural hearing loss may have had tinnitus from their earliest memories and may not realize that other people do not hear a constant noise
  • Limited vocabulary: Young children may not have the words to describe what they are experiencing. They may say “my ears are noisy” or “I hear a bug” rather than “I have ringing in my ears”
  • Fear of consequences: Older children who already deal with hearing aids, frequent doctor visits, and feeling “different” may not want to report another symptom that could lead to more appointments
  • Normalization: If a parent or sibling with Marshall syndrome also has tinnitus but has never discussed it, the child assumes it is a universal experience

Behavioral Signs to Watch For

Even when children do not verbally report tinnitus, behavioral clues may indicate its presence:

  • Sleep difficulty beyond expected: Trouble falling asleep despite adequate sleep hygiene, particularly if the child is distressed in quiet rooms but sleeps better with background noise
  • Concentration problems at school: Tinnitus competes with speech for auditory attention. A child whose classroom performance is worse than their audiogram would predict may be dealing with tinnitus interference
  • Covering ears or head-banging (young children): Infants and toddlers cannot describe internal sounds but may react to them with distress behaviors
  • Preference for background noise: Always wanting the TV, radio, or music on — not for entertainment but for masking
  • Emotional changes: Increased irritability, anxiety, or sadness that cannot be attributed to other causes

Age-Appropriate Assessment

  • Ages 3–5: Use the “tinnitus thermometer” visual analogue scale. Ask the child to point to how loud and how bothersome the noise is. Use simple, direct questions: “When it’s really quiet, do you hear any sounds inside your head or ears?”
  • Ages 6–10: The child can usually participate in basic tinnitus pitch matching with guidance. Use drawings or emoji-based rating scales for distress
  • Ages 11–14: The Children’s Tinnitus Questionnaire (CTQ) is validated for this age range. Tinnitus pitch and loudness matching, minimum masking level, and residual inhibition testing can all be performed reliably
  • Ages 15+: Adult-level assessment tools (THI) and full tinnitus evaluation protocols are appropriate

Management Adaptations for Children

  • Sound enrichment at bedtime: The single most impactful intervention for pediatric tinnitus. A sound machine in the child’s bedroom should be considered standard practice for any child with Marshall syndrome and hearing loss, whether or not they have reported tinnitus
  • Hearing aid tinnitus programs: If the child already wears hearing aids, activating the tinnitus masker program provides help with no additional hardware or compliance burden
  • Age-appropriate CBT: CBT can be adapted for children as young as 7–8. For younger children, the techniques overlap with those described on the Pain Neuroscience in Children page — using metaphors (“your brain has a volume knob that’s turned up too high”), relaxation stories, and breathing games
  • School accommodations: Add tinnitus to the 504 Plan: preferential seating, permission to use low-level background noise (earbuds with white noise) during quiet testing, extended time for tasks that require intense auditory concentration
  • Parental reassurance: One of the most effective pediatric tinnitus interventions is simply explaining to the child (and parents) that the tinnitus is not dangerous, is not a sign of going deaf, and that most people’s brains learn to filter it out over time. This reduces fear, which reduces the limbic activation that amplifies tinnitus distress

Connections to Other Marshall Syndrome Features

Tinnitus in Marshall syndrome does not exist in isolation. It intersects with multiple other disease features, and managing these connections is essential for comprehensive care.

Related Feature Connection to Tinnitus Management Implication
Hearing Loss Sensorineural hearing loss is the primary driver of cochlear tinnitus via central gain enhancement. Worsening hearing loss typically worsens tinnitus Optimize hearing aid fitting. Any improvement in hearing thresholds (even small gains from upgrading technology) may reduce tinnitus. Cochlear implant candidates: CI recipients often report significant tinnitus reduction
Central Sensitization Central sensitization amplifies all sensory processing, including auditory. Patients with central sensitization may perceive tinnitus as louder and more distressing than their audiometric profile would predict Address central sensitization first (or in parallel). Treatments that reduce central sensitization (amitriptyline, LDN, graded exercise, sleep optimization) may independently reduce tinnitus severity
TMJ Dysfunction TMJ disorders cause somatic tinnitus through tensor tympani spasm, middle ear pressure changes, and trigeminal-auditory pathway crossover. Prevalence of tinnitus in TMJ patients is approximately 4x baseline TMJ treatment (stabilization splints, physical therapy, botulinum toxin for muscle spasm) may reduce or eliminate the TMJ-related tinnitus component
Vestibular Dysfunction The cochlear and vestibular systems share anatomy and blood supply. Vestibular damage (documented in Marshall patients by Griffith 2000) suggests widespread inner ear involvement that could contribute to tinnitus Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) addresses balance but does not directly treat tinnitus. However, reducing vestibular-related anxiety may indirectly reduce tinnitus distress

Corticosteroid responsive tinnitus suggests an immune component

When to Escalate: Red Flags

URGENT ENT REFERRAL REQUIRED for any of the following tinnitus presentations. These may indicate serious pathology beyond the expected Marshall syndrome tinnitus:
  • Sudden onset of new tinnitus (or dramatic change in existing tinnitus) with concurrent hearing loss — this is a medical emergency. Sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) requires treatment with corticosteroids within 72 hours for best recovery outcomes. In Marshall patients, SSNHL could indicate autoimmune flare, vascular event, or retrocochlear pathology
  • Pulsatile tinnitus — must be evaluated with vascular imaging (MRA, CTA) to rule out dural arteriovenous fistula, carotid dissection (connective tissue disorders increase risk), glomus tumors, or idiopathic intracranial hypertension
  • Unilateral tinnitus with progressive asymmetric hearing loss — requires MRI with gadolinium to rule out vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma). Although rare in children, it must not be missed
  • Tinnitus accompanied by facial weakness, vertigo, otorrhea, or otalgia — may indicate cholesteatoma, mastoiditis, or other middle ear pathology requiring surgical evaluation
  • Tinnitus with severe psychological distress — if tinnitus is causing significant depression, anxiety, panic attacks, or suicidal ideation, immediate psychological/psychiatric referral is warranted alongside audiological management. Do not wait for the tinnitus to resolve before addressing mental health

Practical Management Summary

The following stepped approach integrates the evidence above into a practical framework for Marshall syndrome patients and their care teams:

The goal of tinnitus management is not silence — it is indifference. With proper sound enrichment, hearing aid optimization, education, and when needed, CBT or TRT, most patients reach a point where they are aware of their tinnitus only when they deliberately listen for it. In Marshall syndrome, where so many other symptoms demand attention, helping the brain move tinnitus from “alarm signal” to “background noise” frees cognitive and emotional resources for everything else.
