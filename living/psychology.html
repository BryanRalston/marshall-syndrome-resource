The Reality of Chronic Pain in Childhood Supported

  • 92% of adults with Stickler/Marshall syndrome report chronic pain — these patterns likely begin in childhood
  • Pain in childhood is harder to manage because children have less experience with coping strategies, less control over their environment, and less ability to self-advocate
  • Greater pain intensity and multiple pain sites correlate with increased anxiety, depression, and functional disability

The Invisible Disability Paradox

Children with Marshall syndrome often look normal to casual observers. They don't use wheelchairs, may not have visible braces or assistive devices, and can appear fully capable. This creates a painful paradox:

  • Teachers, coaches, and peers may not believe the child is in pain
  • Accommodations may be questioned ("But you look fine")
  • The child may push through pain to avoid appearing different, worsening their condition
  • Social isolation can result from inability to participate in activities that peers enjoy

School Accommodations

Only 35% of children with pain-related hypermobility syndromes attend school full-time. Proactive accommodation planning prevents crisis-driven absences.

In the United States, your child may qualify for:

  • IEP (Individualized Education Program) — if the condition affects educational performance
  • 504 Plan — if the condition affects access to education but the child doesn't need specialized instruction

Stickler Involved People (SIP) provides guidance on school accommodation planning specifically for Stickler/Marshall families.

Common accommodations to request:

  • Permission to stand, stretch, or change position during class
  • Extra time between classes (avoiding rushing on stairs, through crowds)
  • Reduced or modified physical education requirements
  • Access to elevator if stairs are painful
  • Ergonomic seating (supportive chair, footrest)
  • Permission to carry and use a supportive cushion
  • Excused absences for medical appointments and pain flares without academic penalty
  • Access to a quiet space during pain episodes
  • Modified homework load during flare periods

Psychological Support Established

CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) is specifically recommended in Stickler syndrome pain management guidelines. It is not about "thinking away" the pain — it's about developing concrete skills:

  • Identifying and interrupting pain catastrophizing (the spiral of "this will never get better")
  • Pacing activities to avoid boom-bust cycles (overdoing on good days → crashing on bad days)
  • Relaxation and mindfulness techniques with evidence for pain reduction
  • Building self-advocacy skills for medical appointments, school, and social situations

Building Identity Beyond the Diagnosis

A child who can't do many of the physical activities their peers enjoy needs to find sources of competence and identity elsewhere:

  • Swimming can be both therapeutic and a competitive sport
  • Creative pursuits — art, music, writing, coding
  • Academic achievement — the condition does not affect cognitive ability
  • Music — particularly valuable if hearing is preserved or well-aided
  • Help the child find what they're good at, not just what they're restricted from

Social Dimensions

Don't underestimate the cumulative social impact of:

  • Wearing hearing aids and thick glasses in elementary/middle school
  • Being unable to participate in recess activities, sports teams, field trips
  • Frequent absences for medical appointments
  • Having to explain an invisible condition to skeptical peers and adults
  • Being shorter than peers (short stature is common in Marshall syndrome)

Proactive conversations with teachers, school counselors, and close friends' parents can significantly reduce the social burden.
