Marshall Syndrome — Emergency Protocol Rare connective tissue disorder • COL11A1 gene • Type XI collagen deficiency
CRITICAL — Tell the ER Team Immediately

1 DIFFICULT AIRWAY

  • Marshall syndrome causes midface hypoplasia and possible micrognathia — anticipate difficult mask ventilation and intubation
  • Cervical spine may be unstable — do NOT hyperextend neck for intubation
  • Video laryngoscopy recommended as first-line approach
  • Have difficult airway cart at bedside before any airway intervention
  • If Pierre Robin sequence is present: especially high risk — consider early call to anesthesiology

2 CERVICAL SPINE PRECAUTIONS

  • Risk of craniocervical instability (CCI) due to ligamentous laxity from collagen disorder
  • Apply C-collar if any neck trauma suspected
  • Obtain flexion-extension imaging before any neck manipulation
  • Symptoms of CCI: severe headache with neck movement, arm/leg weakness, electric shock sensation down spine (Lhermitte sign)

3 RETINAL DETACHMENT RISK

  • If patient reports sudden flashes, new floaters, or curtain/shadow over vision → EMERGENCY vitreoretinal consult
  • Do NOT wait — retinal detachment in high myopes progresses rapidly and delay risks permanent vision loss
  • This patient likely has very high myopia (often > −8 D) and vitreous degeneration from abnormal type XI collagen
IMPORTANT — Medication & Procedural Cautions

4 OTOTOXIC DRUGS — USE WITH CAUTION

  • Patient has sensorineural hearing loss — protect remaining hearing
  • AVOID if possible: gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin (aminoglycosides)
  • CAUTION: high-dose furosemide, vancomycin (monitor trough levels closely)
  • If aminoglycoside absolutely required: use once-daily dosing, monitor peak/trough levels, limit duration to shortest effective course

5 CARDIAC — MITRAL VALVE PROLAPSE

  • 45.6% of Stickler/Marshall patients have mitral valve prolapse — COL11A1 is expressed in heart valve development
  • If cardiac symptoms present (palpitations, chest pain, exercise intolerance), request echocardiogram
  • MVP can cause palpitations, chest pain, and exercise intolerance — overlaps with POTS symptoms
  • If significant MVP is known: use caution with stimulants and medications that increase cardiac demand

6 WOUND HEALING

  • Collagen disorder — wounds may heal slowly or with poor tensile strength
  • Use extra care with wound closure: consider longer retention of sutures/staples, lower tension techniques
  • Dural tears more likely if spinal procedure or lumbar puncture is needed — use atraumatic (pencil-point) needle for LP

7 JOINT LAXITY

  • Joints are hypermobile and prone to subluxation/dislocation
  • Gentle handling during positioning, transfers, and procedures
  • Shoulder, hip, and knee most commonly affected
  • Check for associated fracture with dislocations — osteopenia may be present
Common Marshall Syndrome ER Scenarios
Scenario Key Actions
Head / neck trauma C-spine precautions. CT head + cervical spine. Flexion-extension imaging when safe. Neurological exam for cord signs (Lhermitte, weakness, hyperreflexia).
Sudden vision changes Immediate ophthalmology / vitreoretinal consult. Do not delay for other workup. Dilated fundus exam urgently.
Severe headache + neck pain Evaluate for CCI, CSF leak, Chiari malformation. Ask about positional component (worse upright = CSF leak; worse supine/Valsalva = Chiari). MRI brain + cervical spine with sagittal views.
Joint dislocation Gentle reduction. Obtain X-ray to check for associated fracture (osteopenia possible). Immobilize and refer to orthopedics for follow-up.
Respiratory distress Anticipate difficult airway. Have video laryngoscope, LMA, and difficult airway cart ready. Early anesthesiology involvement. Avoid neck hyperextension.
Post-surgical fever Standard fever workup. If recent spinal procedure: evaluate for CSF leak (positional headache, clear rhinorrhea/otorrhea, wound drainage). Dural tears are more common in collagen disorders.
Palpitations / chest pain Do NOT dismiss as anxiety. Evaluate for MVP (echocardiogram) and POTS (tilt table). 45.6% of Stickler/Marshall patients have mitral valve prolapse.
