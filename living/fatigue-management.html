Fatigue: Understanding and Managing Energy Depletion

Key Understanding
Fatigue is one of the most disabling yet least visible symptoms of Marshall syndrome. It has at least seven biological sources — not one. Understanding which sources are active guides both investigation and management.

Fatigue Is Not Laziness

When a child with Marshall syndrome says they are too tired to do something, they are not being dramatic, defiant, or lazy. Their fatigue has biological causes — multiple, simultaneous, measurable biological causes — that would exhaust anyone.

Normal tiredness resolves with rest. The fatigue of Marshall syndrome often does not. A child may sleep 10–12 hours and wake feeling unrefreshed. They may crash after activities that their peers handle effortlessly. This is not a character flaw. It is a predictable consequence of a body that is fighting on multiple fronts simultaneously: managing chronic pain, compensating for unstable joints, recovering from fragmented sleep, and regulating a dysautonomic cardiovascular system — all at once, all day, every day.

The Spoon Theory

The spoon theory, originally described by Christine Miserandino for lupus, is useful for Marshall syndrome families. Imagine starting each day with a limited number of “spoons” — units of energy. Healthy people have so many spoons that they never think about them. A child with Marshall syndrome might start with 12 spoons where peers have 50. Every activity costs spoons:

  • Getting dressed with stiff, painful joints: 1–2 spoons
  • Walking to class on hypermobile ankles: 1 spoon
  • Concentrating through brain fog in math class: 2 spoons
  • Eating lunch (TMJ pain, fatigue from chewing): 1 spoon
  • Gym class: 3–4 spoons
  • Managing sensory overload in a noisy cafeteria: 1–2 spoons

By mid-afternoon, the spoons may be gone. The child is not choosing to stop participating — they have hit a biological wall.

The Energy Envelope

The energy envelope concept extends spoon theory into a management strategy. Every person has an energy envelope — a range of activity they can sustain without triggering a crash. The goal is to stay inside the envelope consistently, rather than swinging between overactivity on good days and collapse on bad days.

  • Below the envelope: Too much rest causes deconditioning, worsening long-term fatigue
  • Inside the envelope: Activity matched to capacity, sustainable day to day
  • Above the envelope: Overexertion triggers post-exertional malaise and multi-day crashes

Finding a child’s envelope requires careful observation over weeks, not guesswork. Activity diaries, heart rate monitoring, and symptom tracking all help define it. See Symptom Tracker for tools.

The Seven Sources of Fatigue

Marshall syndrome fatigue is almost never single-cause. Most children have three to five of these sources operating simultaneously. Addressing only one will produce incomplete improvement. The goal is to identify and address as many as possible. While we title this section “seven sources,” the reality is that COL11A1 creates at least nine distinct fatigue pathways — and they interact.

Source Mechanism What to Investigate

Alpha-delta intrusion — alpha (waking) brainwaves intruding into delta (deep) sleep, preventing restorative sleep. The brain devotes processing resources to pain suppression that would otherwise support cognition, mood regulation, and motor planning. This consumes resources that would otherwise support attention, learning, and social engagement. Audiometry, speech-in-noise testing, hearing aid evaluation. Classroom FM system assessment. Visual Strain High myopia (common in Marshall syndrome) requires constant accommodative effort. Vitreous abnormalities, retinal pathology, and cataracts further increase the visual processing load. Reading, screen work, and any visually demanding task costs more energy than for a child with normal vision. Compensatory head positioning for best visual angle adds musculoskeletal fatigue. This amplification consumes enormous cognitive resources. The brain is processing a firehose of sensation that would normally be filtered out, resulting in cognitive fatigue and sensory overwhelm even in “quiet” environments. Clinical Pearl: When a child’s fatigue is not improving despite addressing sleep and pain, look harder at autonomic dysfunction and central sensitization. These are the two most frequently missed sources in pediatric connective tissue disorders.

Morning Fatigue vs. Evening Crashes

The pattern of fatigue matters as much as its severity. Morning fatigue and evening crashes have different underlying causes and require different interventions.

Morning Fatigue: “I Can’t Wake Up”

When a child consistently wakes exhausted despite adequate sleep duration, the problem is almost always sleep quality, not quantity:

  • Obstructive sleep apnea: Midface hypoplasia, floppy airways from connective tissue laxity, and enlarged tonsils/adenoids (common in Marshall syndrome) cause repeated airway obstruction. The child may not wake fully but cycles out of deep sleep dozens of times per night. Even mild OSA (AHI 1–5) causes significant morning fatigue in children.
  • Alpha-delta intrusion: Alpha brainwaves (normally associated with relaxed wakefulness) intrude into delta sleep stages. The child spends hours in bed but never achieves truly restorative deep sleep. This pattern is common in chronic pain conditions and fibromyalgia-spectrum disorders.
  • Delayed sleep phase: The circadian clock shifts later, especially during adolescence. The child cannot fall asleep until 1–2 AM and then cannot wake for school at 7 AM. This is not insomnia — it is a clock problem. Melatonin timing (low dose, 0.3–0.5 mg, 4–5 hours before desired bedtime) can help reset the clock.
  • Nocturnal pain: Joint pain, muscle spasms, and positional discomfort cause micro-awakenings throughout the night. The child may not remember waking but their sleep architecture is fragmented.

Evening Crashes: “I Hit a Wall”

When a child functions reasonably in the morning but crashes dramatically in the afternoon or evening, consider:

  • Autonomic decompensation: Children with POTS or orthostatic intolerance gradually deplete their compensatory reserves throughout the day. Each hour of upright posture drains the autonomic system further. By afternoon, the system can no longer maintain adequate cerebral perfusion, and the child crashes. Lying down provides immediate relief because gravity is no longer working against blood return.
  • Cervical myelopathy fatigue: In children with cervical spine involvement (common in Marshall syndrome), cord compression during upright hours can cause progressive fatigue. The sustained axial load and dynamic compression with head movement accumulate over the day. This type of fatigue may be accompanied by clumsiness, gait changes, or hand weakness that also worsen through the day.
  • Medication wearing off: If morning medications provide energy or pain relief, their effect may wear off by afternoon. Conversely, if sedating medications are taken in the morning, their peak effect may align with afternoon fatigue.
  • Glycemic instability: Autonomic dysfunction can impair glucose regulation. Reactive hypoglycemia after lunch can mimic or worsen afternoon fatigue.

Post-Exertional Malaise (PEM)

Post-exertional malaise is a hallmark feature that distinguishes pathological fatigue from normal tiredness. After activity, a healthy child feels tired but recovers quickly. A child with PEM experiences disproportionate fatigue, pain, and cognitive dysfunction hours to days after activity that would be trivial for peers.

Recognizing PEM

  • Delayed onset: Symptoms typically peak 12–48 hours after the triggering activity, not during or immediately after
  • Disproportionate severity: Walking around a store might produce 2 days of exhaustion. A field trip might cause a week of recovery
  • Multi-system symptoms: Not just tiredness but increased pain, cognitive fog, headache, nausea, sore throat, swollen glands, and temperature dysregulation
  • Not improved by rest alone: Unlike normal post-exercise soreness, PEM does not simply resolve with a good night’s sleep

The Boom-Bust Cycle

The most common pattern in children with PEM:

  1. BOOM: Child has a “good day” and, feeling temporarily better, does everything they have been missing — plays with friends, catches up on schoolwork, attends activities
  2. BUST: 24–72 hours later, the child crashes severely and may be unable to function for days
  3. GUILT: Child feels guilty about “wasting” the good day and “causing” the crash
  4. REPEAT: The next good day, desperation to feel normal drives the same overexertion

This cycle is deeply destructive — it prevents any baseline stability and teaches the child that feeling good is actually dangerous, which creates psychological harm on top of the physical consequences.

Breaking the Cycle: Pacing

  • Activity capping: On good days, do 70–80% of what feels possible, not 100%. Save the reserve.
  • Pre-scheduled rest: Build rest periods into the daily schedule before exhaustion hits, not after
  • Heart rate monitoring: Use a heart rate monitor to set an anaerobic threshold. For many children with autonomic dysfunction, staying below 60–70% of age-predicted maximum heart rate prevents PEM. When the monitor alerts, the child stops and rests regardless of how they feel in the moment.
  • Activity logging: Track what was done and what happened 24–48 hours later. Over weeks, patterns emerge that reveal the child’s actual activity threshold.
  • Gradual expansion: Once a stable baseline is established (2–4 weeks without a crash), increase activity by no more than 10% per week
Important Distinction: Pacing is not the same as avoidance. The goal is not to do nothing — it is to do a consistent, sustainable amount every day. Doing a little every day produces better outcomes than boom-bust cycles, even if the total weekly activity is less.

Heart Rate-Guided Pacing: The Objective Envelope

Research on energy envelope management in chronic fatigue conditions has increasingly validated heart rate monitoring as the most objective method for identifying and staying within a child’s safe activity threshold. Subjective “how do you feel?” assessments are unreliable — children with chronic fatigue frequently feel fine during activity and crash 24–48 hours later. Heart rate provides a real-time physiological signal that catches overexertion before the child feels it.

The HR-Based Pacing Protocol
  • Establish the anaerobic threshold (AT): For most children with autonomic dysfunction, a conservative AT estimate is 60–70% of age-predicted maximum heart rate (220 minus age). Some clinicians use 55% for severely deconditioned patients. A formal cardiopulmonary exercise test (CPET) provides a precise AT but is not always available.
  • Wearable HR monitor: Use a chest strap or wrist-based monitor with an alarm function. Set the alarm to the child’s AT. When the alarm sounds, the child stops the current activity and rests until heart rate returns below the threshold — regardless of how they feel in the moment.
  • Activity logging with HR data: Track which activities push heart rate above the AT and which stay below it. Over 2–4 weeks, patterns emerge: walking may be safe at a slow pace but not a brisk pace; one flight of stairs may be below AT but two flights above it. This data makes the energy envelope visible and measurable.
  • Gradual expansion: Once the child maintains 2–4 weeks at their current HR ceiling without post-exertional crashes, the threshold can be raised by no more than 5 beats per minute. This is a slower expansion than the 10% activity increase rule, but it is anchored to physiology rather than subjective assessment.
  • Marshall-specific consideration: Autonomic dysfunction (POTS, orthostatic intolerance) is common in Marshall syndrome and produces exaggerated heart rate responses to standing and mild exertion. The HR ceiling for a child with POTS may be lower than peers, and the alarm will sound during activities that appear trivially easy — standing in line, walking to class, climbing a single flight of stairs. This is not the child being dramatic; it is their cardiovascular system objectively working harder than normal.

Cognitive Fatigue (Brain Fog)

Cognitive fatigue — difficulty concentrating, word-finding problems, slow processing speed, working memory deficits, and mental exhaustion after cognitive tasks — is one of the most school-disabling symptoms of Marshall syndrome. It is not “laziness” or “not trying.” It has specific, identifiable biological mechanisms.

Mechanisms of Brain Fog

  • Sleep deprivation: Even one night of fragmented sleep impairs prefrontal cortex function — the brain region responsible for attention, working memory, and executive function. Chronic sleep disruption from OSA or pain creates cumulative cognitive debt that cannot be repaid on weekends.
  • Autonomic-mediated cerebral hypoperfusion: In POTS and orthostatic intolerance, blood pools in the lower extremities when upright. Cerebral blood flow decreases, particularly to the prefrontal cortex. The child is literally thinking with a brain that is receiving inadequate blood supply. Symptoms worsen with prolonged upright posture (sitting in class) and improve when lying down.
  • Medication effects: Gabapentin impairs word-finding and processing speed. Amitriptyline causes next-day cognitive dulling. Antihistamines impair attention. These effects are dose-dependent and often additive.
  • Chronic pain’s cognitive load: Pain processing competes with cognitive processing for shared neural resources. A child in constant pain has fewer cognitive resources available for schoolwork — not because they are not trying, but because their brain is already at capacity.
  • Central sensitization: When the CNS amplifies all sensory input, the brain is constantly processing a flood of irrelevant signals — sounds, lights, textures, temperatures — that would normally be filtered out. This leaves fewer resources for intentional cognitive work.

Impact on School Performance

Cognitive fatigue in Marshall syndrome typically presents as:

  • Inconsistent performance — good work on some days, poor on others, leading teachers to conclude the child “could do it if they tried”
  • Performance declining through the school day as cognitive resources deplete
  • Difficulty with timed tests (processing speed is reduced, not intelligence)
  • Trouble following multi-step instructions
  • Word-finding difficulties that may be mistaken for inattention
  • Need to re-read material multiple times
  • Inability to sustain attention for age-appropriate durations

Accommodations for Cognitive Fatigue

  • Front-load cognitively demanding subjects in the morning when brain fog is typically least severe
  • Allow brain breaks every 20–30 minutes (stand, stretch, walk to water fountain)
  • Provide written instructions rather than relying on verbal multi-step directions
  • Reduce background noise and visual clutter in the learning environment
  • Allow use of noise-canceling headphones or earplugs during independent work
  • Permit lying down or reclining during class if autonomic dysfunction is contributing (this genuinely improves cerebral perfusion)
  • See School & 504 Plans for formal accommodation strategies

Energy Management Strategies

While investigation and treatment of underlying causes are essential, daily energy management strategies can substantially improve quality of life right now.

Prioritize by Energy Cost

Not all activities cost the same amount of energy. Rank daily activities into high, medium, and low energy cost categories. Schedule no more than one or two high-cost activities per day, and always buffer them with low-cost recovery periods.

Schedule Rest Before Exhaustion

Rest is far more effective as prevention than as treatment. A 15-minute rest break before the child is exhausted preserves more energy than an hour of rest after a crash. Build rest periods into the daily schedule at fixed times, not as a response to symptoms.

Front-Load Important Tasks

Most children with Marshall syndrome have their best energy and clearest thinking in the late morning (after any morning stiffness resolves but before afternoon depletion). Schedule the most demanding tasks — homework, medical appointments, social activities — during this window when possible.

Break Activities into Chunks

Instead of completing a task in one sustained effort, break it into smaller segments with rest between them. Homework in 15–20 minute blocks with 5–10 minute breaks. Household tasks in segments. This “chunking” approach stays inside the energy envelope while still accomplishing the task over time.

The Energy Envelope — Practical Pacing System
  • Evidence base: Children with heritable connective tissue disorders show significantly higher fatigue T-scores compared to healthy peers. Research demonstrates that patients who consistently exceed their energy envelope experience 40% more disability than those who pace within it.
  • Spoon adaptation for children: Assign “spoons” (energy units) to daily activities to make the energy budget concrete and visible. Example: showering = 2 spoons, one school class = 1 spoon, PE class = 3 spoons, homework session = 2 spoons. When the spoons are gone, the child must rest — this is not optional. Start by tracking actual spoon usage for a week before setting the daily budget.
  • OT-guided 4-step approach:
    1. Activity diary for 2 weeks: Log every activity, its duration, and the fatigue response 2–4 hours later. This reveals the child’s actual energy envelope, which is often much smaller than parents or teachers expect.
    2. Identify energy drains and energy givers: Some activities cost more energy than expected (e.g., noisy cafeteria lunch). Others are restorative (e.g., quiet drawing time). Map these explicitly.
    3. Build a pacing schedule with mandatory rest breaks: Structure the day so that high-cost activities are separated by scheduled rest. Rest breaks are built into the timetable, not earned by exhaustion.
    4. Teach the ‘3 Ps’ — Planning, Prioritizing, Positioning: Planning means preparing for activities in advance to reduce unnecessary energy expenditure. Prioritizing means choosing what matters most when energy is limited. Positioning means using body mechanics that conserve energy (sitting instead of standing, using ergonomic tools).
  • Marshall-specific compounding: Joint pain, hearing effort, and visual strain compound fatigue in ways that are unique to Marshall syndrome. The energy budget must account for sensory processing load — a child who spends the day straining to hear and see is burning energy that peers do not. Listening in a noisy classroom may cost 2 spoons for a child with hearing loss but 0 for a child with normal hearing.
  • School IHP recommendation: The child’s Individual Health Plan (IHP) should include scheduled rest periods as a standing accommodation, not just “as-needed breaks.” As-needed breaks require the child to recognize and advocate for their own exhaustion in real time, which children with chronic fatigue are notoriously poor at until they crash. Scheduled rest prevents the crash.

OT-Led Energy Conservation & Pacing

Occupational therapy (OT) provides the most structured, evidence-based approach to energy conservation in children with chronic fatigue. An OT with experience in connective tissue disorders or chronic fatigue conditions can formalize what families often discover informally — and prevent the patterns that lead to repeated crashes.

The Energy Envelope Concept

The energy envelope is the total amount of physical, cognitive, and emotional energy available on a given day. It is not fixed — it fluctuates with sleep quality, pain levels, illness, weather, hormonal cycles, and stress. The core principle is simple: stay inside the envelope. Children who consistently exceed their energy envelope experience approximately 40% more disability than those who learn to pace within it. An OT measures the envelope through structured activity diaries, then builds a daily plan that respects its boundaries.

The 3 Ps: Plan, Prioritize, Pace

This framework, taught by occupational therapists specializing in energy management, gives children and families a repeatable decision-making system:

  • Plan: Prepare for activities in advance to eliminate unnecessary energy expenditure. Gather materials before starting, organize the workspace, lay out clothes the night before. Planning converts unstructured chaos into predictable sequences, which cost less energy.
  • Prioritize: When energy is limited, not everything can be done. Each morning, identify the 2–3 most important tasks for the day and accept that lower-priority items may not happen. This is not failure — it is resource allocation. Help the child rank by importance rather than urgency.
  • Pace: Alternate high-energy and low-energy activities throughout the day. Never stack multiple high-cost tasks back-to-back. Build scheduled rest breaks into activities, not only between them. A 20-minute homework block followed by a 10-minute rest break is pacing; a 90-minute homework marathon followed by a collapse is boom-bust.

Breaking the Boom-Bust Cycle

The boom-bust cycle is the single most destructive pattern in chronic fatigue management. It works like this:

  1. Good day (boom): The child feels relatively well, so they try to “catch up” on everything they missed during bad days — schoolwork, social activities, chores, sports
  2. Crash (bust): The overexertion depletes reserves far beyond the energy envelope, triggering 1–3 days of severe fatigue, increased pain, brain fog, and inability to function
  3. Recovery and repeat: After recovering, the child again feels “good” relative to the crash, and the cycle repeats

Over time, the boom-bust cycle actually shrinks the energy envelope — each crash lowers the baseline. Breaking the cycle requires a counterintuitive discipline: doing less on good days in order to have more consistent energy across all days. This is one of the hardest concepts for families to accept, but it is the foundation of successful energy management.

The boom-bust trap in school: Children with Marshall syndrome are particularly vulnerable to the boom-bust cycle around school. After missing several days due to fatigue, the child returns and tries to “make up” everything at once — late assignments, missed tests, social obligations. This almost always triggers another crash. Schools must be educated that graded re-entry with reduced workload after fatigue-related absences is essential, not optional.

School Pacing Modifications

Effective school pacing requires formal accommodations that are built into the child’s IHP (Individual Health Plan) or 504 plan, not left to the child’s discretion:

  • Scheduled rest breaks: 10–15 minutes of quiet rest in the nurse’s office or a designated space, scheduled at fixed times (e.g., between second and third period), not “as-needed.” As-needed breaks require self-advocacy during exhaustion, which children with chronic fatigue are poor at until they crash.
  • Reduced class transitions: Where possible, minimize the number of times the child must move between classrooms. Walking long distances between classes with a heavy backpack is a significant energy drain.
  • Modified PE participation: Replace high-intensity PE with supervised low-intensity alternatives (walking, gentle stretching, aquatic exercise) that maintain some physical activity without triggering post-exertional malaise.
  • Front-loaded scheduling: Place the most cognitively demanding subjects in the morning when energy is typically highest. Afternoons should be reserved for less demanding activities.
  • Assignment flexibility: Extended deadlines and the option to complete work in shorter segments across multiple days rather than in single sittings.
  • Sensory load reduction: Preferential seating away from noise sources, use of FM systems for hearing loss (to reduce listening effort), and permission to use sunglasses or tinted lenses if photosensitivity is present.

Assistive Devices for Energy Conservation

Simple devices and modifications can save surprising amounts of energy across a day. An OT can assess which are most relevant for the individual child:

Use Assistive Devices to Conserve Energy

  • Shower chair or bath bench: Eliminates the energy cost of standing during bathing
  • Lightweight backpack on wheels: Removes the postural energy cost of carrying weight
  • Ergonomic writing tools: Reduce grip fatigue from hypermobile finger joints
  • Voice-to-text software: Eliminates handwriting energy cost for longer assignments
  • Elevator access: Preserves energy that stairs would consume
  • Compression garments: Support venous return in autonomic dysfunction, reducing the energy cost of standing

Environmental Modifications

  • Sit for tasks normally done standing (cooking, brushing teeth, waiting in line)
  • Keep frequently used items at waist height to avoid bending and reaching
  • Maintain cool ambient temperature — heat worsens autonomic dysfunction and increases energy expenditure
  • Use task lighting rather than overhead fluorescents (reduces sensory processing load from glare)
  • Reduce background noise to lower the cognitive cost of filtering irrelevant stimuli

The Exercise Paradox

Exercise reduces fatigue long-term but causes it short-term. This paradox is one of the most difficult aspects of fatigue management in Marshall syndrome. The child is exhausted and you are telling them to exercise — it seems cruel and counterintuitive. But the evidence is clear: carefully graduated exercise is one of the most effective long-term fatigue interventions available.

Why Exercise Helps

  • Improves cardiovascular fitness, reducing the metabolic cost of all daily activities
  • Increases mitochondrial density and efficiency in skeletal muscle
  • Improves autonomic function and orthostatic tolerance (critical for POTS-related fatigue)
  • Strengthens muscles that stabilize hypermobile joints, reducing pain and the energy cost of postural compensation
  • Improves sleep quality through natural circadian entrainment
  • Activates NRF2 and other protective pathways (see NRF2 Convergence)

How to Start When Already Exhausted

  1. Begin in water: Aquatic exercise in a warm pool (30–34°C / 86–93°F) eliminates gravity, supports joints, and provides hydrostatic pressure that improves venous return. Start with 5-minute sessions. See Aquatic Therapy.
  2. Recumbent before upright: If pool access is not available, start with recumbent exercises (recumbent bike, floor exercises, rowing machine) that do not challenge the autonomic system with upright posture.
  3. Micro-dosing: Five minutes of exercise twice a day is better than 10 minutes once a day for children with PEM. Shorter bouts are less likely to trigger crashes.
  4. Increase by time, not intensity: Add 1–2 minutes per session per week. Do not increase intensity until duration reaches 20–30 minutes without triggering PEM.
  5. Monitor 48-hour response: The test of whether an exercise session was appropriate is not how the child feels during or immediately after, but how they feel 24–48 hours later. If they crash, the session was too much — reduce by 50% and try again.

Avoid the “Good Day Trap”

On a good day, the child feels well enough to exercise more. Do not let them. Exceeding the planned exercise dose on a good day is the single most common trigger for PEM crashes. The exercise plan should be the same on good days and bad days. Consistency is more important than any single session.

For detailed exercise protocols, see Exercise Guidelines and Exercise Pathways.

School Accommodations for Fatigue

Fatigue-specific accommodations are among the most important for children with Marshall syndrome but are often the hardest to obtain because fatigue is invisible. Formal documentation through a 504 Plan or IEP provides legal protection for these accommodations.

Schedule Modifications

  • Late start: Beginning the school day 1–2 hours later allows for morning stiffness to resolve and sleep needs to be met. This may be the single most impactful accommodation for children with sleep-related fatigue.
  • Shortened school day: Attending core academic classes only, with electives provided through alternative formats
  • Flexible attendance: Excused absences for crash days without academic penalty, with a plan for making up missed content
  • Homebound instruction: For periods of severe exacerbation, home-based instruction preserves educational continuity

During-School Accommodations

  • Scheduled rest periods: A quiet space to lie down for 15–20 minutes at midday can prevent afternoon crashes
  • Preferential seating: Near the door (easy exit for rest breaks) and near the teacher (reduces the cognitive effort of processing distant speech)
  • Permission to recline: Allowing the child to put their head down or recline improves cerebral perfusion in autonomic dysfunction
  • Drink and snack access: Hydration and frequent small meals support autonomic function and glycemic stability
  • Elevator pass: Conserves energy for academic activities rather than stair climbing

Academic Accommodations

  • Reduced homework load: Quality over quantity — demonstrating mastery with fewer problems rather than completing every assigned item
  • Extended time on tests: Slow processing speed from fatigue and medication effects requires additional time, not because the child cannot do the work but because they process more slowly
  • Testing breaks: Allow breaks during tests (the clock stops during breaks) to prevent cognitive fatigue from invalidating results
  • Alternative formats: Oral instead of written responses, typed instead of handwritten, recorded lectures for later review
  • Priority seating for hearing: If hearing loss is present (common in Marshall syndrome), front-row seating and FM system use reduce the cognitive effort of listening

For comprehensive 504 plan guidance, see School & 504 Plans. For navigating the transition from pediatric to adult care — where fatigue accommodations change significantly — see Transition to Adult Care.

Mitochondrial Dysfunction & Energy Metabolism

Emerging research identifies mitochondrial dysfunction as a biologically distinct fatigue driver in connective tissue disorders — separate from, and compounding, the seven sources described above. In Marshall syndrome, where chronic inflammation, deconditioning, and autonomic instability all converge on cellular energy production, mitochondrial impairment may explain fatigue that persists even after sleep, pain, and autonomic dysfunction are optimally managed.

How COL11A1 Connects to Mitochondrial Dysfunction

The connection between collagen disorders and mitochondrial impairment operates through several converging pathways:

  • Chronic NF-κB activation: The persistent low-grade inflammation characteristic of Marshall syndrome drives sustained NF-κB signaling, which directly suppresses mitochondrial biogenesis (the creation of new mitochondria) and impairs the electron transport chain. This means cells produce less ATP (energy) per unit of fuel consumed.
  • Oxidative stress accumulation: Intermittent hypoxia from sleep apnea, ferroptosis-related iron dysregulation, and exercise-induced reactive oxygen species (ROS) all damage mitochondrial membranes and mitochondrial DNA. Damaged mitochondria produce even more ROS, creating a self-amplifying cycle.
  • Deconditioning reduces mitochondrial density: Skeletal muscle mitochondrial content is directly proportional to exercise level. The deconditioning spiral that affects many children with Marshall syndrome reduces mitochondrial density in muscle, meaning fewer mitochondria must meet the same energy demands — explaining why trivial activities feel exhausting.
  • CoQ10 depletion: Coenzyme Q10 (ubiquinone) is essential for electron transport chain function and is also the body’s primary lipid-soluble antioxidant. In states of chronic oxidative stress, CoQ10 is consumed faster than it is synthesized, creating a functional deficiency even when dietary intake is adequate.

CoQ10 Supplementation: Evidence and Practical Guidance

Research Context (R424): Multiple randomized controlled trials in chronic fatigue, fibromyalgia, and mitochondrial myopathy populations have demonstrated that CoQ10 supplementation can improve subjective fatigue scores, exercise tolerance, and cellular energy markers. While no trial has been conducted specifically in Marshall syndrome, the mechanistic rationale is strong given the convergence of inflammation, oxidative stress, and deconditioning on mitochondrial function.
ParameterDetails

Useful tests include plasma CoQ10 levels, organic acids (urine), lactate/pyruvate ratio, and carnitine profile. A metabolic medicine or mitochondrial disease specialist can guide this workup.

Brain Fog: Assessment and Cognitive Accommodations

Building on the mechanisms described above in Cognitive Fatigue (Brain Fog), this section covers the emerging research on brain fog as a distinct phenomenon, how to formally assess it, and specific cognitive accommodations that go beyond the general fatigue accommodations in the preceding section.

Brain Fog Is Real — and Complex

A 2025 systematic review established that brain fog is dissociable from objective cognitive performance — a person can report severe brain fog while performing adequately on standardized tests, or vice versa. This means brain fog is not simply “poor cognition” but a distinct subjective state in which fatigue and affect (mood, emotional state) mediate the perceived deficits. In practical terms: a child may pass their cognitive tests at a neuropsychological evaluation while still experiencing debilitating fog in daily life. Both the subjective experience and the objective data are important, and neither should be dismissed.

Multiple Contributors in Marshall Syndrome

Cognitive fatigue in Marshall syndrome has several converging sources, which is why it can be so persistent and difficult to treat with a single intervention:

  • Chronic pain: Constant nociceptive input diverts neural processing resources away from cognitive tasks. The brain is literally using its bandwidth for pain suppression rather than thinking. See Seven-Mechanism Pain Model.
  • Sleep disruption: Non-restorative sleep from obstructive sleep apnea, alpha-delta intrusion, or pain-fragmented sleep impairs overnight memory consolidation and next-day attentional capacity. Even one night of poor sleep measurably reduces working memory and processing speed.
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Reduced cerebral perfusion from orthostatic intolerance or POTS directly impairs cognitive function. Brain fog that worsens with upright posture and improves when lying down is a hallmark of autonomic-mediated cognitive impairment. See Autonomic Dysfunction.
  • Medication effects: Many medications used in Marshall syndrome — including anticonvulsants for pain, antihistamines, muscle relaxants, opioids, and sedating antidepressants — have cognitive side effects. These may be individually subtle but can compound when multiple sedating medications are used simultaneously.
  • Central sensitization: When the nervous system is in a heightened state of sensitization, sensory processing demands increase across the board, leaving fewer cognitive resources available for deliberate thinking. See Central Sensitization.

Neuropsychological Evaluation: The First Step

When cognitive fatigue is significantly impacting school performance or daily function, a formal neuropsychological evaluation is the most valuable first step. This comprehensive assessment (typically 4–8 hours over one or two sessions) maps specific cognitive strengths and weaknesses and produces a report with concrete, individualized recommendations for the school.

  • What it tests: IQ, processing speed, working memory, attention and concentration, visual-spatial reasoning, verbal fluency, executive function, academic achievement, and emotional functioning
  • Why it matters: The results provide objective evidence that schools are legally required to consider when developing accommodations. A neuropsychologist’s report carries more weight than a parent’s description of difficulties.
  • Timing considerations: Schedule the evaluation during a period representative of the child’s typical functioning — not during a flare (which would overestimate deficits) and not during the child’s best period (which would underestimate them). If the child has significant variability, note this for the evaluator.
  • Request school-specific recommendations: Ask the neuropsychologist to include a dedicated section on school accommodations in their report. Many evaluators will do this routinely, but it helps to request it explicitly.

Cognitive Accommodations for School

Beyond the general fatigue accommodations described above, cognitive fatigue specifically requires accommodations that reduce cognitive load:

  • Reduced homework during flares: When fatigue is elevated (during illness, pain flares, or medication changes), temporarily reducing homework volume prevents the child from spending their entire evening on work that would take a healthy peer 30 minutes. The goal is to demonstrate mastery, not endurance.
  • Extended test time: Slow processing speed from fatigue means the child knows the material but cannot retrieve and express it at the pace of untimed performance. Extended time (typically 1.5x to 2x standard) is one of the most evidence-supported accommodations for processing speed deficits.
  • Break cards: A laminated card that the child can place on their desk to signal the teacher that they need a cognitive break. No verbal request needed, no drawing attention from peers. The child steps out for 3–5 minutes, returns, and resumes. This simple tool reduces the social cost of needing breaks.
  • Note-taking support: Listening to a lecture while simultaneously taking notes is a divided-attention task that taxes working memory heavily. Providing a note-taking buddy, teacher-provided outlines, or permission to record lectures frees cognitive resources for comprehension rather than transcription.
  • Modified assignment length: Completing 10 math problems instead of 30 when the child has demonstrated understanding of the concept. The additional 20 problems test endurance, not knowledge, and penalize the child for their disability rather than their ability.
  • Chunked instructions: Multi-step verbal instructions overload working memory. Providing instructions in writing, one step at a time, with visual checklists allows the child to process at their own pace.
  • Preferential scheduling: Place cognitively demanding subjects (math, science, foreign language) in the morning when cognitive energy is typically highest, and less demanding activities (art, music, study hall) in the afternoon when fog tends to worsen.
Practical Tip: Brain fog often fluctuates throughout the day and between days. Teaching the child to recognize their own cognitive energy level — and to communicate it simply (e.g., a 1–5 “brain battery” scale) — empowers them to advocate for their needs and helps parents and teachers understand when to push and when to accommodate.

Sleep Optimization

Sleep is the single most modifiable contributor to fatigue. When sleep quality improves, every other fatigue source becomes more manageable. When sleep is poor, no amount of pacing or medication fully compensates.

When to Refer for a Sleep Study

A formal polysomnography (PSG) referral is indicated when any of the following are present:

  • Snoring, gasping, or observed apneas during sleep
  • Waking unrefreshed despite adequate sleep duration (9–12 hours for school-age children)
  • Mouth breathing during sleep (common with midface hypoplasia)
  • Excessive daytime sleepiness inappropriate for age
  • Morning headaches (suggestive of nocturnal hypercapnia from airway obstruction)
  • Behavioral problems that worsen with poor sleep (ADHD-like symptoms may be sleep apnea in disguise)

Given the high prevalence of airway abnormalities in Marshall syndrome (midface hypoplasia, Pierre Robin sequence, floppy airways from connective tissue laxity), a baseline PSG should be considered for all children with the diagnosis, even if symptoms seem mild. See Sleep Apnea and Sleep Architecture for detailed guidance.

CPAP and BiPAP for Obstructive Sleep Apnea

When PSG confirms obstructive sleep apnea, continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) or bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP) may be prescribed. These devices deliver pressurized air through a nasal or full-face mask to splint the airway open during sleep.

  • CPAP: Delivers a single pressure level. Effective for most mild-to-moderate OSA.
  • BiPAP: Delivers higher pressure on inhalation and lower on exhalation. May be better tolerated in children with central apnea or those who find exhaling against CPAP pressure uncomfortable.
  • Adherence challenges in children: Mask fit, sensory sensitivity (common in Marshall syndrome), and fear of the device are common barriers. Behavioral desensitization — gradually introducing the mask during waking hours, positive reinforcement — improves compliance. Pediatric sleep labs often have behavioral support programs.
  • Dramatic improvement when tolerated: Children who consistently use PAP therapy often experience transformative improvement in daytime energy, mood, cognition, and even pain perception within 2–4 weeks.

Sleep Hygiene for Pain Patients

Standard sleep hygiene advice applies but must be adapted for chronic pain:

  • Consistent sleep-wake times: The same bedtime and wake time every day, including weekends. This anchors the circadian clock. Allowing weekend sleep-ins feels restorative but actually disrupts circadian rhythm (“social jet lag”).
  • Pre-sleep pain management: Time analgesic medications so that peak effect coincides with the first 3–4 hours of sleep (when deep sleep occurs). Evening stretching, gentle heat application, and topical analgesics can reduce nocturnal pain without systemic sedation.
  • Cool, dark environment: 65–68°F (18–20°C) is optimal. Darkness promotes melatonin secretion. Blackout curtains are particularly helpful if a late-start school schedule is in place.
  • Screen curfew: Blue light from devices suppresses melatonin. Screens off 60–90 minutes before bed. If screens cannot be avoided, blue-light-blocking glasses provide partial mitigation.
  • Bedding and positioning: Supportive mattress (medium-firm), body pillows for joint support, cervical pillow to maintain neutral spine alignment. Children with Pierre Robin sequence or airway involvement should avoid supine (face-up) sleeping; side-sleeping or slight elevation of the head of bed (10–15 degrees) can improve airway patency.

Melatonin

Melatonin serves two distinct functions depending on dose and timing:

  • Circadian phase-shifting (low dose): 0.3–0.5 mg taken 4–5 hours before desired bedtime advances the circadian clock. This is the appropriate use for delayed sleep phase, which is common in adolescents with Marshall syndrome.
  • Sleep-onset aid (higher dose): 1–3 mg taken 30–60 minutes before bed has a mild soporific effect. Higher doses (5–10 mg) are sometimes used but do not produce proportionally better sleep and may cause morning grogginess.
  • Beyond sleep: Melatonin is also a potent antioxidant and NRF2 activator (see NRF2 Convergence). Its protective effects on cartilage and anti-inflammatory properties may provide additional benefit in Marshall syndrome beyond sleep improvement.

Positional Sleeping for Cervical Spine

Children with cervical spine involvement in Marshall syndrome need careful attention to sleep positioning:

  • Cervical pillow: A contoured cervical pillow maintains neutral alignment and prevents excessive flexion or extension during sleep. Memory foam or water-filled pillows allow individual adjustment.
  • Avoid prone sleeping: Sleeping face-down requires maximal cervical rotation, which can compress the vertebral arteries and aggravate cervical instability.
  • Side-sleeping with support: A pillow between the knees reduces lumbar rotation. A body pillow prevents rolling to prone. The cervical pillow should fill the space between the shoulder and ear to keep the cervical spine neutral.
Practical Tip: When a child’s fatigue is worst upon waking, optimize sleep first. It is the foundation on which all other fatigue interventions are built. A child who sleeps well will respond better to exercise programs, tolerate more school, and need less pain medication.

When Fatigue Signals Something New

Fatigue in Marshall syndrome is usually chronic and slowly evolving. When the pattern changes suddenly, it may signal a new and potentially serious complication. These red flags warrant urgent medical evaluation — do not wait for the next scheduled appointment.

Red Flags — Seek Prompt Medical Evaluation
Any sudden, unexplained change in fatigue pattern in a child with Marshall syndrome should be evaluated urgently. The conditions below are treatable but can cause permanent harm if missed.

Sudden Fatigue Worsening + Positional Headache

Consider: CSF leak (spontaneous intracranial hypotension)

Connective tissue disorders predispose to dural weakness. A spontaneous cerebrospinal fluid leak causes headache and profound fatigue that is characteristically worse when upright and better when lying flat. The headache may be the dominant symptom, but sometimes fatigue is more prominent. If a child with Marshall syndrome develops new positional headache with worsening fatigue, brain MRI with gadolinium and/or CT myelography should be considered. See Headaches for detailed guidance on CSF leak evaluation.

Fatigue + Exertional Breathlessness

Consider: Cardiac involvement

While cardiac complications are less common in Marshall/Stickler syndrome type I (COL11A1) than in type II (COL11A2) or Marfan syndrome, mitral valve prolapse and other valvular abnormalities can occur in connective tissue disorders. New fatigue combined with breathlessness on exertion, palpitations, or exercise intolerance that is disproportionate to the child’s fitness level warrants echocardiography and cardiac evaluation. See Screening Tests for cardiac screening recommendations.

Fatigue + Progressive Weakness or Clumsiness

Consider: Cervical myelopathy

Cervical spine involvement in Marshall syndrome can progress to spinal cord compression (myelopathy). The presentation is often subtle: progressive fatigue, declining coordination, new clumsiness, difficulty with fine motor tasks (buttons, handwriting), gait changes, or new urinary symptoms. Because the child already has baseline fatigue from other sources, the myelopathic component may be attributed to “just their usual fatigue” until it becomes severe. Any progressive neurological decline requires urgent cervical MRI. See Cervical Myelopathy.

Fatigue + Rapid Weight Change

Consider: Endocrine dysfunction

New-onset hypothyroidism (fatigue + weight gain + cold intolerance), adrenal insufficiency (fatigue + weight loss + low blood pressure), or growth hormone deficiency (fatigue + growth deceleration) should be evaluated with appropriate hormonal testing. See Endocrine Panel.

Fatigue After a Medication Change

Consider: Medication side effect

This is the most common — and most easily reversible — cause of sudden fatigue worsening. New medications, dose increases, or drug interactions can all cause fatigue. Review all medications, including over-the-counter supplements, with the prescribing physician.

Key Principle: In Marshall syndrome, never attribute a change in fatigue to “that’s just how this condition is” without first ruling out treatable new causes. Diagnostic complacency is the enemy of good care in complex chronic conditions.

Questions for Your Doctor

Bring These Questions
  1. “Could my child’s fatigue have an autonomic component?” — Ask specifically about testing for POTS or orthostatic intolerance. Many pediatricians do not routinely screen for these conditions, but they are common in connective tissue disorders and are treatable.
  2. “Has a full sleep study been done, and did it assess alpha-delta intrusion?” — Standard pediatric PSG reports may not comment on alpha-delta intrusion unless specifically requested. This EEG pattern is a major cause of non-restorative sleep in chronic pain conditions.
  3. “Which of my child’s medications could be contributing to fatigue, and can we adjust timing or dosing?” — Medication-related fatigue is the most immediately addressable source. Moving sedating medications to bedtime, reducing doses, or switching to less sedating alternatives can produce rapid improvement.
  4. “Can we check a complete iron panel, not just ferritin?” — Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant that may be falsely normal with chronic inflammation. Transferrin saturation and TIBC provide a more accurate picture of functional iron status.
  5. “Is graded exercise safe for my child, and can you refer us to a physical therapist who understands hypermobility and pacing?” — Not all physical therapists understand pacing or post-exertional malaise. A therapist experienced with hypermobility spectrum disorders or chronic fatigue will design a program that builds fitness without triggering crashes.
