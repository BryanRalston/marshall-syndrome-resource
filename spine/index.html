Why the Spine is Affected

Type XI Collagen in the Spine

Type XI collagen is found in:

  • The nucleus pulposus — the gel-like center of each intervertebral disc
  • The annulus fibrosus — the tough outer ring of each disc
  • Vertebral growth plates — where bones grow during childhood
  • Articular cartilage — the smooth surfaces of joints, including facet joints in the spine

A landmark genetic study (Mio et al., 2007) demonstrated that:

  • COL11A1 is expressed at higher levels in intervertebral discs than in most other tissues
  • Reduced COL11A1 expression directly correlates with worse disc degeneration
  • COL11A1 variants are significantly associated with lumbar disc herniation

In animal models (Col11a1-deficient mice), researchers observed:

  • Incompletely formed vertebral bodies
  • Spinal curvatures
  • Decreased separation between vertebrae (compressed disc spaces)
  • Altered vertebral shape
  • Incomplete bone mineralization
The takeaway: COL11A1 is not optional for spinal health — it is required for normal vertebral development, disc integrity, and spinal structure. A child with a COL11A1 mutation has structurally compromised spinal tissues from birth, and these problems become symptomatic as the spine bears more load during growth.

COL11A1 and Disc Herniation Susceptibility

Disc degeneration in Marshall syndrome is genetically driven, not simply wear-and-tear. Research on the COL11A1 variant rs1676486 has established a direct genetic link between COL11A1 and lumbar disc herniation susceptibility:

  • SNP rs1676486 (a single nucleotide polymorphism in COL11A1) is significantly associated with lumbar disc herniation in multiple population studies
  • COL11A1 expression is inversely correlated with degeneration severity — lower COL11A1 expression means worse disc breakdown. In Marshall syndrome, where COL11A1 is constitutively reduced by mutation, this relationship predicts accelerated degeneration from the outset.
  • Both disc compartments are affected: the nucleus pulposus (gel center) and the annulus fibrosus (tough outer ring) both depend on type XI collagen for structural integrity. When COL11A1 is deficient, neither compartment functions normally, making herniation more likely under lower mechanical loads than would affect an unaffected disc.
  • This means that the disc disease seen in Marshall syndrome is a primary genetic pathology, not a secondary consequence of activity, posture, or “overuse.” Clinicians should not attribute early disc herniation in Marshall patients to lifestyle factors alone.
Clinical implication: When a child or adolescent with Marshall syndrome presents with disc herniation, it should be understood as a direct consequence of the underlying collagen defect — not as an unusual or unexplained finding. Early imaging and proactive spine monitoring are justified by this genetic susceptibility.

Scoliosis Management: Bracing & Growing Rods

Scoliosis — abnormal lateral curvature of the spine — occurs at higher rates in collagen disorders because the structural scaffolding that maintains vertebral alignment is compromised from birth. In Marshall syndrome, where COL11A1 affects both vertebral development and intervertebral disc integrity, scoliosis can develop during growth spurts when the spine is under the greatest mechanical demand from the weakest structural support.

Bracing: Who Benefits and When

Bracing is the first-line non-surgical intervention for progressive scoliosis in growing children. Evidence from major orthopedic trials demonstrates that bracing is effective in 28–51% of patients, depending on curve magnitude at initiation, skeletal maturity, and compliance. The key findings that guide bracing decisions:

  • Most effective for curves between 25–45 degrees (Cobb angle) in skeletally immature patients. Below 25 degrees, observation may be sufficient; above 45 degrees, bracing is unlikely to prevent surgical intervention.
  • Compliance is the strongest predictor of success: Studies show a dose-response relationship — more hours of brace wear per day correlates directly with better outcomes. The target is typically 16–23 hours per day depending on the brace type and curve severity.
  • Risser sign 0–2 (indicating significant remaining growth) is the window where bracing has the greatest impact. Once skeletal maturity approaches (Risser 3+), the benefit diminishes.
Marshall-Specific Bracing Concerns
Standard bracing protocols must be modified for Marshall syndrome patients due to two factors that orthopedic teams may not anticipate:
  • Skin fragility: COL11A1 mutations can affect skin collagen, making the skin more vulnerable to pressure injuries from brace contact points. Brace fitting must include extra padding at pressure points, more frequent skin checks (daily initially), and a lower threshold for brace modification if redness or breakdown occurs. Families should be taught to inspect skin at every brace removal.
  • Altered bone quality: Reduced bone mineral density, which is common in Marshall syndrome (see Bone Density Monitoring), can affect how the spine responds to corrective forces. Overly aggressive correction may risk vertebral compression in osteopenic bone. The orthopedic team should have access to the child’s most recent DEXA results before designing a bracing protocol.

Growing Rods: When Curves Exceed Bracing Capacity

For children with progressive scoliosis where the Cobb angle exceeds 60–65 degrees, or where bracing has failed to halt progression, growing rod systems allow spinal correction while preserving growth potential. The goal is to control the curve during the remaining growth years and delay definitive spinal fusion until the spine has reached or approached mature length.

  • Traditional growing rods (TGR): Surgically implanted rods that are anchored above and below the curve. The rods must be surgically lengthened every 6–8 months to keep pace with spinal growth. Each lengthening is a separate procedure under general anesthesia, which creates a cumulative surgical burden.
  • Magnetically controlled growing rods (MCGR): An emerging approach that represents a significant advance over traditional growing rods. MCGRs use an internal magnetic mechanism that allows non-invasive lengthening in the outpatient clinic using an external magnetic controller. This eliminates the need for repeated surgical lengthenings, reducing anesthetic exposure, infection risk, and the psychological burden of repeated operations on the child.
  • VEPTR (Vertical Expandable Prosthetic Titanium Rib): An alternative for severe early-onset scoliosis with thoracic insufficiency. Expands the thoracic cavity while controlling the curve. Used in younger children (typically under age 8) where the primary concern is lung development as much as spinal alignment.
Timing matters: Research demonstrates that postponing definitive spinal fusion surgery by even 1 year reduces complication rates by approximately 12% in growing children. Each additional year of growth allows more spinal length, more thoracic volume for lung development, and more skeletal maturity for the fusion to hold. Growing rods — particularly MCGRs — are the bridge that makes this delay possible while still controlling the curve.

Marshall-Specific Surgical Considerations

Any spinal instrumentation in Marshall syndrome carries risks that differ from the general scoliosis population:

  • Bone quality and screw purchase: Reduced bone mineral density may compromise the ability of pedicle screws and hooks to maintain purchase in osteopenic vertebrae. Pre-operative DEXA assessment is essential, and the surgical team should plan for augmented fixation (e.g., cement-augmented screws) if bone density is significantly reduced.
  • Wound healing: Collagen-dependent wound healing is slower and may be less robust. Extended wound care protocols, longer suture retention, and delayed return to activity should be anticipated. See Wound Healing for detailed guidance.
  • Anesthetic considerations: The child’s airway may be affected by micrognathia or other craniofacial features of Marshall syndrome. Difficult intubation should be anticipated and planned for. See Anesthesia Considerations.
  • Post-operative pain management: Children with Marshall syndrome may have altered pain processing. Post-operative pain protocols should account for the possibility of both increased pain sensitivity and existing chronic pain medication use.
Questions for the orthopedic team: Before consenting to any scoliosis intervention, ask: (1) What is the current Cobb angle and rate of progression? (2) What is my child’s Risser sign and estimated remaining growth? (3) Has bone density been assessed, and does it affect the surgical plan? (4) For growing rods, is MCGR available and appropriate? (5) What is the expected number of procedures, and what is the plan for transitioning to definitive fusion?
