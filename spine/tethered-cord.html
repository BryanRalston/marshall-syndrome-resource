Tethered Cord Syndrome: When the Spinal Cord Can’t Move Freely

Growth Spurt Alert
New lower extremity weakness, bladder changes, or worsening back pain during a growth spurt in a child with Marshall syndrome should prompt full-spine MRI evaluation — not just cervical imaging. Tethered cord syndrome can produce progressive neurological damage if not identified and treated.

What Is Tethered Cord?

Tethered cord syndrome occurs when the spinal cord is abnormally anchored at its lower end, restricting its normal movement within the spinal canal. The cord is normally attached at its caudal end by the filum terminale — a thin, fibrous strand that connects the conus medullaris (the tapered lower end of the spinal cord) to the coccyx. When the filum is abnormally thickened, shortened, or inelastic, it holds the cord under tension, particularly during spinal growth or flexion.

In normal development, the spinal cord ascends relative to the vertebral column as the spine grows faster than the cord. By approximately two months of age, the conus medullaris reaches its adult position at the L1–L2 vertebral level. When the cord is tethered, it cannot ascend normally, and the resulting traction produces progressive neurological, urological, and orthopaedic symptoms.

The COL11A1 Connection

The relationship between Marshall syndrome and tethered cord has not been directly studied, but there are compelling biological reasons to consider this connection:

  • Collagen in the filum terminale: The filum terminale is a connective tissue structure composed of collagen fibers, glial tissue, and ependymal remnants. Type XI collagen, encoded by COL11A1, plays a role in organizing collagen fibril diameter and spacing across multiple tissue types. A mutation that disrupts fibril architecture in cartilage, vitreous, and intervertebral discs could plausibly affect the structural properties of the filum — potentially altering its elasticity or thickness.
  • Dural attachments: The dura mater and its attachments throughout the spinal canal contain collagen as a primary structural component. Abnormal collagen in these attachments could create areas of abnormal tethering or adhesion that are not present in the general population.
  • The Ehlers-Danlos parallel: Tethered cord is increasingly recognized in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS), another heritable connective tissue disorder. Multiple case series have documented occult tethered cord in EDS patients, sometimes presenting with symptoms that were initially attributed to the underlying connective tissue disorder itself. The shared biology between EDS and Marshall syndrome (both involve collagen mutations affecting structural tissues) makes this parallel clinically relevant.

Occult Tethered Cord

A particularly important concept is occult tethered cord syndrome — a condition where the conus medullaris is in a normal position (at or above L1–L2) but the cord is still functionally tethered by an abnormally thickened or inelastic filum terminale. Standard MRI may appear normal, yet the patient experiences classic tethered cord symptoms. This entity is increasingly recognized in the EDS community and may be relevant to Marshall syndrome, where abnormal collagen could produce a filum that looks anatomically normal on imaging but is biomechanically abnormal.

EDS Prevalence Data Underscores the Risk
A 2025 study of hypermobile EDS (hEDS) patients found that 17.9% had tethered cord syndrome, often occult on standard imaging. Filum terminale abnormalities — including thickening, fatty infiltration, and decreased elasticity — were frequently identified only after clinical suspicion prompted advanced evaluation. Because Marshall syndrome shares a connective tissue collagen defect with EDS, this prevalence figure should raise clinical awareness: tethered cord is not a rare coincidence in heritable connective tissue disorders — it is a plausible co-occurrence that warrants systematic screening.

Why Occult Tethering Is Common in Connective Tissue Disorders

The filum terminale is composed of collagen, glial tissue, and ependymal remnants. In connective tissue disorders, the collagen component may be structurally abnormal in ways that do not produce obvious MRI findings (such as a low conus or visibly thickened filum) but do alter the biomechanical properties of the filum — making it less elastic and more prone to transmitting traction forces to the spinal cord. This explains why standard imaging criteria (conus position, filum diameter) may appear normal while the patient experiences genuine tethered cord symptoms. The 17.9% prevalence in hEDS suggests that relying solely on imaging findings to exclude tethered cord in connective tissue disorder patients will miss a significant number of affected individuals.

Speculative but Plausible: While no published studies have specifically examined tethered cord in Marshall or Stickler syndrome, the biological rationale is sound and the EDS prevalence data provides indirect evidence. The filum terminale is a collagen-containing structure; COL11A1 mutations affect collagen throughout the body; and tethered cord is documented at nearly 18% prevalence in another heritable connective tissue disorder (hEDS). Clinicians managing Marshall syndrome patients should be aware of this potential association, particularly when symptoms do not fit the expected pattern of cervical spine pathology alone.

Why It Matters in Marshall Syndrome

Tethered cord syndrome is particularly important in Marshall syndrome for several reasons:

Growth Spurts Stretch the Cord

During periods of rapid spinal growth — particularly the pubertal growth spurt — the vertebral column elongates significantly. A tethered cord cannot ascend normally with this growth, and the resulting traction on the lower spinal cord and nerve roots produces or worsens neurological symptoms. Children with Marshall syndrome who develop new neurological complaints during a growth spurt need evaluation that includes the entire spine, not just the cervical region.

Symptom Overlap Creates Diagnostic Confusion

Many tethered cord symptoms overlap with other spinal conditions already recognized in Marshall syndrome:

  • Lhermitte’s sign — electric shock sensations with neck flexion can occur with both cervical pathology and tethered cord (traction on the cord from either end)
  • Cervical myelopathy — limb weakness and sensory changes can originate from cervical cord compression or from tethering at the caudal end
  • Craniocervical instability (CCI) — headaches, neck pain, and neurological symptoms can mimic or coexist with tethered cord
The Diagnostic Challenge: When a child with Marshall syndrome has neurological symptoms that could be explained by cervical spine pathology but cervical imaging does not fully account for the clinical picture, consider tethered cord. The key is to think about the entire spinal cord, not just the segment closest to the most obvious pathology.

Bladder Symptoms Are the Red Flag: Bladder and bowel dysfunction is the single most important distinguishing feature of tethered cord compared to cervical spine pathology. Any child with Marshall syndrome who develops new urinary symptoms — urgency, frequency, daytime wetting, or difficulty emptying — should be evaluated for tethered cord, even if other spinal conditions are already known. These symptoms are easily overlooked or attributed to other causes, especially in younger children.

Diagnosis

MRI of the Full Spine

The essential imaging study is MRI of the entire spine — not just the cervical region. Key findings include:

  • Conus medullaris position: A conus below L2 in a child older than two months is considered abnormally low and suggests tethering. However, a normal conus position does not exclude occult tethered cord.
  • Filum terminale thickness: A filum terminale measuring greater than 2 mm in diameter is considered abnormally thickened. A thickened filum, particularly if it contains fatty infiltration (visible on MRI), strongly suggests tethering.
  • Associated findings: Fatty filum, lipomas, split cord malformations, syringomyelia (fluid-filled cavity within the cord), or evidence of prior inflammatory/surgical scarring.
Prone vs. Supine Positioning: Standard MRI is performed with the patient lying supine (face up). Some centres advocate prone positioning for suspected tethered cord, as the gravitational shift can unmask subtle tethering that is not apparent in the supine position. If initial supine imaging is inconclusive but clinical suspicion remains, requesting a prone MRI study may be worthwhile.

When MRI Is Normal but Suspicion Remains

Occult tethered cord — where the conus is in a normal position and the filum appears normal on imaging — presents a diagnostic challenge. Additional investigations may include:

  • Urodynamic testing: Formal assessment of bladder function can reveal neurogenic bladder patterns (detrusor overactivity, poor compliance, incomplete emptying) that support the diagnosis of tethered cord even when imaging is unremarkable. This is particularly valuable because bladder dysfunction may precede other neurological findings.
  • Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEP): Electrical stimulation of peripheral nerves with recording at the spinal cord and cortex can identify conduction abnormalities in the lower spinal pathways, supporting the diagnosis of cord tethering even when anatomic imaging is normal.
  • Clinical assessment over time: Serial neurological examinations documenting progressive lower extremity changes, particularly in correlation with growth, can build a cumulative case for tethered cord when no single test is definitive.
The Full Picture: In a child with Marshall syndrome who already has cervical spine pathology, it is tempting to attribute all neurological symptoms to the known problem. Ordering a full-spine MRI rather than just a cervical MRI ensures that a coexisting tethered cord is not missed. The additional imaging time is minimal; the diagnostic value can be significant.

Treatment

Neurosurgical Untethering

The definitive treatment for tethered cord syndrome is surgical untethering — a neurosurgical procedure to release the spinal cord from its abnormal attachment:

  • Procedure: A laminectomy (removal of a small section of vertebral bone) provides access to the filum terminale, which is divided (sectioned) to release the cord. In occult tethered cord, the filum may appear grossly normal but is sectioned based on clinical findings and supporting investigations.
  • Outcomes: Surgery typically stabilizes or improves neurological symptoms. Pain and motor symptoms tend to respond best; bladder dysfunction may improve but recovery is less predictable, particularly if the dysfunction has been present for a prolonged period.
  • Intraoperative monitoring: Electrophysiological monitoring (SSEP, motor evoked potentials, EMG) is used during surgery to protect neural structures and confirm identification of the filum.

Timing Considerations in Children

  • Earlier is generally better: Neurological deficits from tethered cord may become irreversible if tethering is prolonged. Early surgical intervention, before significant neurological damage has occurred, provides the best chance of preventing permanent deficits.
  • Pre-emptive surgery in high-risk children: In children with connective tissue disorders who have imaging evidence of a thickened or fatty filum but are not yet symptomatic, the decision to operate is individualized. Some centres advocate prophylactic untethering to prevent neurological deterioration during upcoming growth spurts.
  • Growth spurt timing: If surgery is planned in a child approaching puberty, completing the procedure before the pubertal growth spurt is generally preferable to reduce the risk of neurological deterioration during rapid growth.

Recurrence Risk with Connective Tissue Disorders

Retethering Risk: Retethering — re-formation of adhesions between the cord and surrounding tissues after surgical release — is a known complication of untethering procedures. In connective tissue disorders, where abnormal collagen affects wound healing and scar formation, the risk of retethering may be elevated. This necessitates:
  • Long-term post-surgical monitoring with serial MRI and clinical examination
  • Vigilance for recurrence of symptoms, particularly during subsequent growth spurts
  • Low threshold for repeat imaging if new symptoms develop
  • Coordination between neurosurgery and genetics to understand the patient’s specific connective tissue biology

Post-Surgical Monitoring

  • Regular neurological assessments focusing on lower extremity strength, sensation, and gait
  • Periodic urodynamic testing if bladder symptoms were present pre-operatively
  • Follow-up MRI to assess for retethering, typically at intervals determined by the neurosurgeon (often annually during growth years)
  • Awareness that new symptoms during growth spurts may indicate retethering even if recent imaging appeared stable

The Growth Spurt Red Flag

The most critical practical takeaway regarding tethered cord in Marshall syndrome relates to the pubertal growth spurt:

Full-Spine Evaluation Imperative: When a child with Marshall syndrome develops new neurological symptoms during a growth spurt — particularly lower extremity weakness, bladder changes, worsening back pain, or progressive scoliosis — the evaluation must include the entire spine, not just the cervical region. Focusing exclusively on known cervical pathology risks missing a tethered cord that is producing symptoms from below.

The growth spurt is the period of highest risk because:

  • The vertebral column elongates rapidly, increasing traction on a tethered cord
  • Symptoms may appear suddenly as the cord reaches the limit of its available movement
  • The window for intervention is time-sensitive — prolonged tethering during rapid growth can cause irreversible neurological damage
  • Multiple spinal pathologies may be simultaneously worsening, each requiring different management

For detailed guidance on monitoring during growth periods, see Growth Spurt & Spinal Risk and The Puberty Window.

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  1. “When ordering spinal MRI, can we image the full spine rather than just the cervical region?” — This ensures a tethered cord is not missed. The additional imaging adds modest time but can reveal pathology that changes the entire management plan.
  2. “Is the filum terminale thickness measured on this MRI? Is the conus in a normal position?” — These specific measurements should be part of the radiologist’s report. A filum greater than 2 mm or a conus below L2 warrants further evaluation.
  3. “Could bladder symptoms be related to a spinal cord problem rather than a primary urological issue?” — Neurogenic bladder from tethered cord is commonly misdiagnosed as a primary bladder condition. If urinary symptoms are present alongside any neurological findings, spinal cord tethering should be considered.
  4. “Given our child’s connective tissue disorder, is there a higher risk of retethering after surgery? What does the monitoring plan look like?” — Ensures the surgical team accounts for abnormal collagen biology in their post-operative planning.
  5. “If imaging looks normal but symptoms suggest tethered cord, should we pursue urodynamic testing or SSEP?” — Occult tethered cord can exist with normal imaging. Functional testing may provide the diagnostic evidence needed.
