Lhermitte's Sign: The Convergent Cascade

Research-Informed Hypotheses — Important Disclaimer
This page synthesizes peer-reviewed research into novel hypotheses for how COL11A1 mutations may produce Lhermitte's sign. These are research-informed hypotheses, not proven diagnoses. Always consult qualified medical professionals.

Lhermitte's sign — an electric shock sensation radiating down the spine or limbs upon neck flexion — results from mechanical stimulation of hyperexcitable or damaged dorsal column axons. The cervical cord stretches approximately 10 mm during flexion (roughly 10% elongation). In Marshall syndrome, at least 12 distinct mechanisms could produce this sign, many operating simultaneously in a convergent cascade.

What Happens During Neck Flexion

When the neck bends forward, a precise series of biomechanical events unfolds inside the spinal canal:

  • Cord elongation: The cervical spinal cord elongates 10–12 mm, with dorsal (posterior) structures stretching more than ventral
  • Anterior shift: The cord moves forward in the canal, pressing closer to the vertebral bodies and discs
  • Dorsal column stretch: The posterior columns — which carry fine touch and vibration signals — experience the greatest mechanical strain
  • Dentate ligament tightening: These lateral tethers pull taut, transmitting additional tension to the cord parenchyma
  • CSF cushion narrowing: The posterior subarachnoid space narrows, reducing the fluid buffer that normally protects the cord from bone contact

In a child with Marshall syndrome cervical abnormalities — platyspondyly, premature disc degeneration, irregular endplates — the mechanical stretch concentrates at the C3–C5 segments and the craniocervical junction, where structural abnormalities are most pronounced. A 9-year-old has a tighter canal-to-cord ratio than an adult and greater ligamentous laxity, meaning less space and less structural restraint. What might be harmless flexion in a normal spine becomes concentrated mechanical stress at vulnerable points.

The Novel Finding: Ephaptic Transmission

This is perhaps the most important mechanism on the list — not because it is the most likely in isolation, but because it explains how chronic inflammation in Marshall syndrome could directly produce Lhermitte's sign through a cascade that has not been previously described in the connective tissue disorder literature.

The cascade unfolds as follows:

  1. COL11A1 mutation produces abnormal intervertebral discs and endplates from birth
  2. Abnormal discs generate chronic local inflammation — NF-κB activation, complement activation, and iron dysregulation
  3. Iron accumulation in the local spinal environment drives Fenton chemistry (iron + hydrogen peroxide → hydroxyl radicals)
  4. Ferroptosis — iron-dependent, lipid peroxidation-mediated cell death — selectively kills oligodendrocytes, the cells that produce myelin in the central nervous system
  5. Focal demyelination of dorsal column axons at the levels of worst disc disease (C3–C5)
  6. Exposed axons become mechanosensitive — they fire in response to physical stretch that they would normally ignore
  7. Neck flexion stretches the cord, triggering ectopic firing in these exposed axons
  8. Ephaptic cross-talk — the electrical signal jumps between adjacent demyelinated axons (ephaptic transmission), amplifying the signal into the characteristic electric shock sensation
Key supporting evidence:
  • Ferroptosis mediates oligodendrocyte loss in CNS demyelinating conditions (Jhelum et al., J Neuroscience 2020; 40:48)
  • Ferroptosis and CNS demyelination: molecular mechanisms and therapeutic potential (J Neurochemistry)
  • Ephaptic transmission in damaged nerves as the basis for mechanically-triggered neuropathic pain (Smith & McDonald, Neurology 1980; 29:7)
  • Complement–ferroptosis convergence provides the upstream inflammatory trigger

The critical insight is that you do not need a large area of demyelination. Even a small patch of demyelinated dorsal column axons at C4 — sitting in the inflammatory penumbra of a degenerating Marshall disc — could produce Lhermitte's sign on neck flexion. Standard MRI may not detect this focal demyelination, which is why the sign can occur even when imaging appears “normal.”

The Radiation-Like Mechanism

Post-radiation Lhermitte's sign is the best-understood non-MS cause of this symptom. It occurs in 3–13% of patients receiving cervical radiation and typically appears 2–4 months after treatment. The parallels with Marshall syndrome are striking:

Radiation Effect on CordMarshall Syndrome Equivalent
Direct oligodendrocyte killingFerroptosis from iron accumulation + lipid peroxidation
Vascular endothelial damageComplement-mediated microvascular injury
Cytokine disruption of repairChronic NF-κB activation from disc inflammation
Free radical damage to myelinFenton reaction (iron + H₂O₂ → hydroxyl radicals)
Impaired remyelinationChronic inflammation prevents oligodendrocyte precursor cell (OPC) maturation

The key difference: Post-radiation Lhermitte's is almost always transient, resolving within 3–6 months as oligodendrocytes remyelinate. But the “radiation” in Marshall syndrome is not a one-time insult — if the inflammatory milieu from chronic disc degeneration is sustained, the demyelination may persist or recur. The cord never gets the recovery window that radiation patients get.

Clinical implication: If a child with Marshall syndrome has Lhermitte's sign with a “normal” MRI, the radiation-analogy model suggests considering the same diagnostic approach used in radiation myelopathy: somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) can detect dorsal column conduction delays that MRI misses.

Chiari Malformation & Syringomyelia

Acquired Chiari I malformation occurs when cranial settling — downward displacement of the skull base due to ligamentous laxity — pushes the cerebellar tonsils below the foramen magnum. This is well-documented in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome: one study found that 12.7% of Chiari patients met criteria for a hereditary connective tissue disorder. In Marshall syndrome, the same mechanism applies:

  1. COL11A1 mutation produces lax craniocervical ligaments
  2. Craniocervical instability allows progressive cranial settling
  3. Acquired Chiari I develops as tonsils are pushed downward
  4. CSF flow obstruction at the foramen magnum
  5. Syringomyelia — a fluid-filled cavity forms within the cord from CSF pressure dynamics

A syrinx expanding within the dorsal cord directly damages the dorsal columns, producing Lhermitte's sign. Importantly, a syrinx can also produce bizarre, seemingly unrelated symptoms (numbness patterns that don’t follow dermatomes, dissociated sensory loss) that may be dismissed as psychosomatic in a child.

Vascular Mechanisms

The posterior spinal arteries supply the dorsal columns — the exact structures that produce Lhermitte's sign when damaged. During neck flexion:

  • The vertebral arteries can be compressed or kinked, especially at the C1–C2 level where they pass through the transverse foramina and loop around the atlas
  • In a child with craniocervical instability, this compression may be dynamic and positional
  • Bow Hunter syndrome — vertebral artery compression during neck rotation or flexion — is a recognized entity in hypermobile patients
  • Even transient posterior spinal artery insufficiency during flexion could produce a momentary “brownout” in dorsal column function, experienced as the electric shock

Distinguishing feature: Vascular Lhermitte's may co-occur with brief dizziness, visual disturbance, or a feeling of lightheadedness during the same neck flexion that triggers the shock. This combination suggests vertebral artery compromise and warrants CT angiography.

The Zinc–Copper Trap

Actionable Warning for Parents: If your child takes zinc supplements and has Lhermitte's sign, request urgent copper and ceruloplasmin testing. Zinc-induced copper deficiency myelopathy is reversible if caught early but can cause permanent spinal cord damage if missed.

This mechanism is critically important because it is entirely preventable and reversible:

  • B12 deficiency causes subacute combined degeneration — demyelination of the dorsal columns and corticospinal tracts — which produces Lhermitte's sign
  • Copper deficiency produces an identical myelopathy, clinically indistinguishable from B12 deficiency
  • Excess zinc antagonizes copper absorption. Zinc competes with copper for intestinal uptake via metallothionein binding. Doses as low as 50 mg/day of supplemental zinc can deplete copper over weeks to months
  • Children with Marshall syndrome may be given zinc supplements for immune support (frequent infections are common), or zinc may be present in multivitamins, cold remedies, or dietary supplements
  • The result: an iatrogenic, reversible myelopathy that perfectly mimics other causes of Lhermitte's sign in Marshall syndrome
Practical steps:
  • If zinc supplementation is used, always co-monitor copper and ceruloplasmin levels at least every 6 months
  • Consider copper-containing multivitamins rather than isolated zinc
  • If copper is low: stop zinc, supplement copper (2 mg/day oral copper gluconate is typical), and recheck in 4–6 weeks
  • Neurological improvement from copper repletion can begin within weeks if caught before axonal damage occurs

The Convergence Model

The critical insight of this page is that these 12 mechanisms are not a differential diagnosis list where you pick one. In Marshall syndrome, they are synergistic layers that compound each other:

  • Structural narrowing (abnormal vertebrae, disc degeneration) reduces the canal space available
  • Ligamentous laxity adds dynamic instability on top of the static narrowing
  • Chronic inflammation from disc degeneration creates a toxic microenvironment around the cord
  • Ferroptosis / complement / NF-κB damage oligodendrocytes in the dorsal columns
  • Demyelinated axons become mechanosensitive to the very stretch that the structural problems are creating
  • Neck flexion in a narrowed, inflamed, partially demyelinated cord → the electric shock

Each layer alone might be insufficient to produce symptoms. Together, they cross the threshold. This explains why Lhermitte's sign can occur in Marshall syndrome even when no single finding on imaging is dramatic enough to “explain” it.

Recommended Workup

For a child with Marshall syndrome who reports “neck zaps,” “electric shocks,” or “lightning going down my back” when looking down:

Imaging

  • MRI cervical spine with flexion-extension views — standard supine MRI misses dynamic compression that only occurs during flexion. Upright or kinematic MRI is ideal if available
  • MRI brain with craniocervical junction focus — evaluate for acquired Chiari, basilar invagination, and cranial settling
  • MRI with syrinx protocol — thin-cut sagittal and axial T2 through the entire cord, specifically looking for intramedullary cavities
  • Flexion-extension lateral X-rays — measure atlantodental interval (ADI) and clivo-axial angle in both positions to assess C1–C2 instability
  • CT angiography of vertebral arteries — if MRI is negative or if dizziness accompanies the shocks, evaluate for dynamic vertebral artery compression

If Initial Workup Is Negative

  • Somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) — can detect dorsal column conduction delays that MRI cannot visualize
  • Upright or weight-bearing MRI — if supine MRI was normal, gravitational loading may reveal compression only present when upright
  • If zinc-copper imbalance is found: correct immediately — stop zinc supplementation, begin copper repletion, and recheck in 4–6 weeks

Questions for Doctors

Bring these questions to your child's neurologist or neurosurgeon:
  1. “Given my child's connective tissue disorder, could there be dynamic cervical instability that only shows up on flexion-extension imaging? Should we get upright or kinematic MRI rather than just standard supine MRI?”
  2. “Could my child have acquired Chiari malformation from craniocervical settling? A standard brain MRI was read as normal, but I'd like the craniocervical junction specifically evaluated with measurements.”
  3. “My child takes zinc supplements. Could this be causing copper deficiency myelopathy? Can we check copper, ceruloplasmin, and zinc levels?”
  4. “If the MRI looks normal, could the Lhermitte's sign be from focal demyelination too small to see on imaging? Would somatosensory evoked potentials (SSEPs) be useful to check dorsal column function?”
  5. “Is there any reason to evaluate the vertebral arteries with CT angiography? My child sometimes feels dizzy with the neck shocks.”
