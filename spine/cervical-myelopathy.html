Cervical Myelopathy: When “Fatigue” Is Really Cord Compression

URGENT — If your child has electric shock sensations when bending their neck (Lhermitte’s sign) combined with leg weakness or legs giving out, this may indicate cervical spinal cord compression. Request an MRI of the cervical spine promptly — within days, not weeks.

What Is Cervical Myelopathy?

The spinal cord — the main cable carrying all nerve signals between your brain and body — passes through the bones of the neck (the cervical spine). In Marshall syndrome, the COL11A1 mutation produces structurally abnormal collagen that affects intervertebral discs and vertebral development. These abnormal discs and vertebrae can compress the spinal cord, causing a condition called cervical myelopathy.

This is not theoretical. It has happened in patients with the same underlying gene:

  • Cervical myelopathy with Brown-Séquard syndrome has been reported in Stickler syndrome (J Med Genet 1992) — the same collagen disorder family as Marshall syndrome
  • Thoracic disc herniation causing paraplegia has been documented in Stickler syndrome patients
  • A COL11A1 functional polymorphism has been significantly associated with disc herniation susceptibility in the general population (Am J Hum Genet 2007), confirming that COL11A1 variants directly increase herniation risk

Why “Fatigue” May Not Be Fatigue

Critical Insight: Published case series (Johns Hopkins, J Translational Medicine 2018) have documented patients diagnosed with ME/CFS (chronic fatigue syndrome) whose symptoms — profound fatigue, exercise intolerance, orthostatic dysfunction — completely resolved after cervical decompression surgery. The “fatigue” was cord compression all along.

There is a phenomenon called subjective lower limb weakness (SLLW): “a subjective sense of bilateral lower limb weakness without any objective decrease in lower limb muscle power elicited by manual tests.” In plain language, a child’s legs can feel heavy and weak even though standard bedside strength testing shows normal power. This means a doctor can test your child’s leg strength with manual resistance, find it “normal,” and miss real corticospinal tract dysfunction.

A child saying “my legs feel heavy” or “I’m just tired” may actually have genuine neurological compromise from cord compression. The mechanisms include:

  • Reduced motor efficiency: Compressed corticospinal tracts require more neural effort for the same movement
  • Autonomic dysfunction: Compression affects sympathetic pathways, causing blood pressure instability and exercise intolerance
  • Central fatigue: Neuroinflammation at the compression site generates fatigue signals independent of peripheral muscle function
  • Impaired proprioception: Dorsal column compression degrades position sense, making movement feel effortful and uncertain

Why Legs Are Affected by a NECK Problem

This is the most counterintuitive part for many parents: how can a neck problem cause leg symptoms?

All nerve signals traveling to the legs pass through the cervical spinal cord. The cord is like a highway — everything heading south to the legs must transit through the neck first. Compression in the neck can therefore knock out leg function while leaving the arms relatively normal, because the nerve fibers serving the legs run through the center and lateral portions of the cord where compression tends to be most severe.

Stair climbing is especially revealing because it requires greater motor recruitment than flat walking, demands precise proprioceptive feedback for foot placement, and involves eccentric muscle control (controlled lowering) that is particularly sensitive to corticospinal tract dysfunction.

The evening timing matters: Gravity loads the spine throughout the day. In a child with ligamentous laxity from COL11A1 (as in Marshall syndrome), the stabilizing muscles fatigue more quickly. The discs lose height under sustained load. Inflammation accumulates. The result is worsening compression as the day progresses — a pattern that looks exactly like “fatigue” but has a completely different cause and treatment.

Tests the Doctor Should Perform

Simple bedside tests that can detect myelopathy:
  • Hoffmann sign: Flick the middle fingernail — watch for reflex flexion of thumb and index finger. Present in 46% of mild myelopathy cases and more sensitive than the Babinski sign
  • Babinski sign: Stroke the sole of the foot — extension of the great toe with fanning of the other toes. Always pathological after infancy
  • Reflexes: Check knees and ankles — hyperreflexia (abnormally brisk reflexes) suggests an upper motor neuron problem
  • Ankle clonus: Dorsiflex the foot sharply — sustained rhythmic beating is abnormal
  • Tandem gait: Heel-to-toe walking reveals subtle coordination problems that ordinary walking conceals
  • Grip-and-release test: Can the child open and close fists rapidly 20 or more times in 10 seconds? Inability to do so suggests myelopathy
  • Running: Often brings out subtle gait abnormalities — stumbling, asymmetry, stiffness — that walking conceals

The MRI: What to Request

Provide this exact wording to the ordering physician:

Suggested MRI order language:

“MRI cervical spine with and without contrast, to include sagittal and axial T1, T2, STIR sequences through the entire cervical spine and craniocervical junction. Include screening of posterior fossa for Chiari malformation. Flexion-extension views if tolerated. Clinical concern: cervical myelopathy in the setting of COL11A1 collagen disorder (Marshall syndrome), with progressive lower extremity weakness, Lhermitte’s sign, and limb heaviness. Evaluate for cord compression, myelomalacia, syrinx, atlantoaxial instability, and ligamentous integrity.”

What the radiologist should look for:

  • T2 hyperintensity in the cord: Indicates edema or damage. Poorly circumscribed (fuzzy) signal is potentially reversible. Sharply demarcated signal suggests chronic, established damage
  • T1 hypointensity: Represents irreversible neural tissue loss — carries the worst prognosis
  • Disc herniations at all cervical levels — not just the most obvious one
  • Chiari malformation: Tonsillar herniation greater than 5mm below the foramen magnum
  • Syrinx: A fluid-filled cavity within the cord itself
  • Flexion-extension views: 22.5% of significant compression is invisible on neutral-position MRI alone. Dynamic imaging is critical
  • Atlantodental interval: Should be less than 3mm in children — greater distance suggests atlantoaxial instability
  • Ligamentous integrity: Evaluate the transverse and alar ligaments, which may be lax due to COL11A1 collagen dysfunction

The Progression Pattern

How cervical myelopathy typically progresses:
  • 75% of cases follow a stepwise pattern — periods of apparent stability interrupted by sudden worsening episodes
  • 20% show slow, continuous decline
  • 5% progress rapidly

The 18-month window: Outcomes deteriorate significantly when intervention is delayed beyond 18 months from symptom onset. T2 signal changes that appear diffuse and edematous are still potentially reversible with timely decompression surgery. However, once myelomalacia (actual cord tissue destruction) is established on imaging, lost function is generally permanent.

This is why urgency matters. The stepwise pattern can create a false sense of security during stable periods, but each “step down” may not be recoverable.

Activity Restrictions While Awaiting Evaluation

Until the MRI is done and reviewed by a specialist:
  • No contact sports, no trampolines, no roughhousing
  • Avoid repetitive neck flexion (looking down at screens for prolonged periods)
  • No diving or jumping into water
  • Avoid heavy backpacks — use a rolling bag instead
  • No gymnastics, no overhead lifting
  • If symptoms acutely worsen — go to the emergency room

The Autonomic Connection

Cervical cord compression does not only affect motor and sensory pathways. It also impacts the autonomic nerve pathways that pass through the neck, potentially causing a wide range of symptoms that may seem unrelated:

  • Dizziness and lightheadedness from disrupted autonomic blood pressure regulation
  • Exercise intolerance from impaired cardiovascular autonomic responses
  • Temperature dysregulation — episodes of inappropriate sweating or inability to thermoregulate
  • Gastrointestinal symptoms from vagal pathway involvement
  • Red hot ears (Red Ear Syndrome) and flushing — these can result from cervical sympathetic chain irritation, particularly at the C2-C3 level

The key insight is that all of these apparently “separate” symptoms may have one upstream cause in the cervical spine. A child presenting with fatigue, leg weakness, dizziness, exercise intolerance, and red ears might be seeing multiple specialists for what is actually a single problem.

Key Takeaway

Bottom line: The combination of Lhermitte’s sign and leg weakness in a child with Marshall syndrome is cervical myelopathy until proven otherwise. Early intervention — before cord damage becomes permanent — produces the best outcomes. Request the MRI. See the neurosurgeon. Time matters.
