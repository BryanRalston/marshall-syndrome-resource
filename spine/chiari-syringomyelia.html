Chiari Malformation & Syringomyelia: When the Brain Descends

Critical Distinction
In Marshall syndrome and related connective tissue disorders, Chiari malformation is frequently acquired rather than congenital — driven by craniocervical instability (CCI) causing structural settling of the skull on the cervical spine. This distinction determines treatment: CCI-driven Chiari may resolve with craniocervical fusion, making decompression surgery unnecessary. Treating the wrong root cause can lead to failed or incomplete recovery.

What Is Chiari Malformation?

Chiari malformation is a structural abnormality in which the cerebellar tonsils — the lowermost portions of the cerebellum — descend through the foramen magnum, the large opening at the base of the skull through which the brainstem passes into the spinal canal. When the cerebellar tonsils herniate into or below the foramen magnum, they can compress neural structures and obstruct the normal flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) at the craniocervical junction.

The consequences of this herniation vary widely. Some individuals have measurable tonsillar descent but minimal or no symptoms; others develop a constellation of neurological problems that significantly affect quality of life. The degree of herniation does not perfectly correlate with symptom severity, which is one reason Chiari remains a challenging diagnosis.

The Critical Distinction: Acquired vs. Congenital Chiari

This is the most important concept on this page for families navigating Marshall syndrome. Chiari malformation is not a single disease — it is a radiological finding that can arise from fundamentally different mechanisms, and those mechanisms require different treatments.

Congenital Chiari I: A Structural Problem with the Posterior Fossa

In classic congenital Chiari I, the primary problem is posterior fossa hypoplasia — the bony compartment at the back of the skull that houses the cerebellum is too small. The cerebellum is crowded by a small container and is pushed downward through the foramen magnum as a result. This is a structural skeletal issue present from birth, and the treatment — when symptoms are significant — is typically posterior fossa decompression surgery, which expands the bony compartment to give the cerebellum more room.

Acquired Chiari in Connective Tissue Disorders: A Mechanical Problem at the Craniocervical Junction

In individuals with connective tissue disorders such as Marshall syndrome, Chiari malformation often develops through an entirely different mechanism. The sequence is:

  1. Ligamentous laxity at the craniocervical junction. COL11A1 mutations produce structurally abnormal collagen throughout the body, including the alar ligaments, transverse ligament, and tectorial membrane that stabilize the skull on the atlas (C1). When these ligaments are insufficiently strong, the craniocervical junction becomes unstable.
  2. Craniocervical instability (CCI). The skull settles downward relative to the upper cervical spine, reducing the effective vertical height of the craniocervical junction. See Craniocervical Instability for detailed discussion of CCI mechanisms and diagnosis.
  3. Posterior fossa volume reduction. As the skull settles, the effective volume of the posterior fossa is dynamically reduced — not because the bone is malformed, but because the structural geometry has changed. The cerebellum has less room as a result of the settling.
  4. Tonsillar herniation as a consequence. With reduced posterior fossa volume, the cerebellar tonsils are pushed downward through the foramen magnum. The Chiari malformation is real and measurable on MRI — but it is a downstream consequence of CCI, not a primary structural abnormality of the skull.
Why This Matters for Treatment: If acquired Chiari is treated with posterior fossa decompression — the standard surgical approach for congenital Chiari — the bony compartment is enlarged but the underlying CCI is not addressed. The craniocervical junction continues to be unstable, the settling continues, and the Chiari may recur or symptoms may persist. In contrast, if the CCI is treated with craniocervical fusion, the settling is arrested, the posterior fossa geometry is restored, and the cerebellar tonsils often ascend back toward their normal position over time — without any decompression surgery at all.

The EDS-Chiari-CCI Triad and Its Relevance to Marshall Syndrome

The association between heritable connective tissue disorders, craniocervical instability, and acquired Chiari malformation is well-documented in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS). Multiple published case series and expert discussions have described this triad in EDS, and dedicated centers now approach Chiari differently in hypermobile patients than in the general population. The same biological logic applies to Marshall syndrome:

  • Both EDS and Marshall syndrome involve mutations in collagen genes that affect ligamentous and structural integrity
  • COL11A1 — the gene mutated in Marshall syndrome — contributes to type XI collagen, which plays a structural role in cartilage, ligaments, and connective tissue throughout the body, including at the craniocervical junction
  • The ligaments that stabilize the skull on the cervical spine are collagen-dependent, and abnormal collagen in Marshall syndrome could plausibly produce the same CCI mechanism seen in EDS
  • The radiological appearance of Chiari in an individual with Marshall syndrome should therefore prompt investigation for underlying CCI before any surgical planning for the Chiari itself
Clinical Question to Ask: When a Chiari malformation is identified in someone with Marshall syndrome, the first question should not be “should we decompress?” but rather “is there craniocervical instability driving this?” CCI evaluation — including upright and dynamic imaging of the craniocervical junction — should precede any discussion of Chiari surgery.

Symptoms

Chiari malformation produces symptoms through two main mechanisms: direct compression of the brainstem and cerebellum at the foramen magnum, and obstruction of CSF flow at the craniocervical junction. The symptoms of CCI-driven Chiari overlap substantially with those of CCI alone, which further underscores why distinguishing the root cause matters.

Headaches

Occipital headaches worsened by Valsalva maneuvers are the hallmark symptom of Chiari malformation. These headaches:

  • Are located at the back of the head and upper neck
  • Are characteristically triggered or worsened by coughing, sneezing, laughing, straining during a bowel movement, or any action that transiently raises intracranial pressure
  • May be brief (seconds to minutes) or prolonged
  • Are distinct from typical tension headaches or migraines, which are not typically triggered by Valsalva

Lhermitte’s Sign

An electric shock sensation that travels down the spine and into the limbs when the neck is flexed. While Lhermitte’s sign is most commonly associated with cervical cord pathology, it can also occur with Chiari malformation due to cord compression at the foramen magnum. See Lhermitte’s Sign for detailed discussion.

Balance and Coordination

  • Unsteady gait, difficulty walking in a straight line
  • Clumsiness with fine motor tasks
  • Truncal ataxia (difficulty maintaining balance while seated or standing)
  • These symptoms reflect cerebellar involvement and may be worsened by the dynamic compression of the cerebellar tonsils

Swallowing and Sleep

  • Dysphagia (difficulty swallowing) from brainstem compression at the foramen magnum
  • Sleep apnea — both central (from brainstem compression) and obstructive types are more common in Chiari; may manifest as snoring, witnessed apnea episodes, or daytime sleepiness
  • Vocal cord paresis (hoarseness) in more severe cases

Upper Extremity Symptoms

  • Hand weakness and reduced grip strength
  • Numbness, tingling, or pain in the hands and arms
  • Symptoms may mimic carpal tunnel syndrome or peripheral neuropathy and are sometimes misdiagnosed as such
  • When a syrinx (see below) develops in the cervical spinal cord, upper extremity symptoms often become more prominent

Overlap with Cervical Myelopathy

Many Chiari symptoms overlap significantly with those of cervical myelopathy — the syndrome produced by spinal cord compression in the cervical spine. Both can cause hand weakness, numbness, gait disturbance, and loss of fine motor control. When both conditions are present, attributing symptoms to the correct source is important for surgical planning. Comprehensive neurological evaluation and careful imaging interpretation are essential when both Chiari and cervical cord compression are identified.

Syringomyelia: When the Spinal Cord Fills with Fluid

What Is a Syrinx?

A syrinx is a fluid-filled cavity within the spinal cord itself. Unlike the normal CSF spaces around the cord, a syrinx is an abnormal cavity within the cord parenchyma — the actual neural tissue. Over time, the expanding fluid cavity damages the nerve fibers within the cord, producing progressive neurological deficits that reflect the cord levels affected.

Syringomyelia most commonly develops as a consequence of Chiari malformation. When tonsillar herniation obstructs the normal pulsatile flow of CSF at the foramen magnum, pressure dynamics within the spinal canal are disrupted. One widely accepted theory holds that obstructed CSF flow drives fluid into the spinal cord under abnormal pressure gradients, gradually forming and expanding the syrinx cavity. Treating the Chiari malformation — whether by decompression or fusion — often stabilizes or reduces the syrinx over time.

Symptoms of Syringomyelia

The pattern of neurological deficits produced by a syrinx reflects the anatomy of the spinal cord. The syrinx typically develops in the central portion of the cord, which contains the fibers that cross between sides to carry pain and temperature sensation. The distinctive symptom pattern that results is:

  • Cape-like dissociated sensory loss: Loss of pain and temperature sensation across both shoulders, the upper back, and the backs of the arms — in a pattern that resembles a cape draped over the shoulders — while touch sensation and proprioception are relatively preserved. This distribution reflects central cord destruction.
  • Progressive weakness: Upper extremity weakness first (the hands and forearms), with lower extremity involvement as the syrinx expands. The weakness may be asymmetric.
  • Wasting of hand muscles: Chronic compression of anterior horn cells produces atrophy of the small muscles of the hands.
  • Scoliosis progression: Syringomyelia is a well-recognized cause of progressive scoliosis. The asymmetric muscle weakness and sensory loss from the syrinx can produce or accelerate spinal curvature.

Why Syringomyelia Matters in Marshall Syndrome: The Scoliosis Intersection

Evaluate Before Surgery: In a child with Marshall syndrome who has both scoliosis and a syrinx, the scoliosis surgery should not proceed until the Chiari malformation has been addressed. Scoliosis surgery with an untreated syrinx carries a significantly elevated risk of neurological complications. After the Chiari is treated, the syrinx may stabilize or regress, which can also affect the progression of the scoliosis itself. Spinal surgeons and neurosurgeons must coordinate closely when both conditions are present.

Marshall syndrome is already associated with scoliosis through multiple mechanisms (see Scoliosis & Kyphosis). When a syrinx is identified in a child with Marshall syndrome and scoliosis, evaluating the full picture — Chiari, CCI, and syrinx — is essential before proceeding with any scoliosis intervention. This sequence is not optional; it is a patient safety requirement.

Diagnostic Imaging

Imaging for Chiari malformation and syringomyelia requires careful protocol selection. Standard imaging approaches used for other spinal conditions may miss or underestimate the severity of CCI-driven Chiari.

Standard Supine MRI: Important Limitations

Most MRI studies are performed with the patient lying supine (flat on their back). In supine positioning, gravitational effects on the craniocervical junction are eliminated — meaning that a Chiari malformation driven by CCI may appear minimal or absent when the patient is lying down. The same applies to CCI itself: supine imaging may show apparently normal craniocervical alignment even when the patient has clinically significant instability in the upright position.

The Supine Problem: A normal or borderline supine MRI does not exclude clinically significant Chiari malformation or CCI in someone with Marshall syndrome. If symptoms are consistent with Chiari — Valsalva-provoked occipital headaches, balance problems, dysphagia, or progressive hand weakness — additional imaging beyond a standard supine MRI is indicated.

Upright and Dynamic MRI

Upright or seated MRI, performed on open-bore MRI systems that allow imaging in the vertical position, can reveal tonsillar herniation or craniocervical instability that is not apparent on supine imaging. Flexion-extension sequences — imaging the craniocervical junction in both flexed and extended positions — can demonstrate dynamic instability and dynamic worsening of tonsillar herniation during movement. These studies are available at specialized centers and may be essential for accurate diagnosis in suspected CCI-driven Chiari.

Cine-Flow MRI (CSF Flow Studies)

Phase-contrast cine MRI is a specialized sequence that visualizes the pulsatile flow of CSF through the foramen magnum during the cardiac cycle. In normal individuals, CSF flows freely both upward and downward with each heartbeat. When the foramen magnum is obstructed by herniated cerebellar tonsils, this flow is impaired or absent. Cine-flow MRI:

  • Provides functional information about the degree of CSF flow obstruction, complementing the structural measurement of tonsillar descent
  • Is particularly important in Chiari 0, where tonsillar herniation is borderline but CSF flow obstruction may be the predominant finding
  • Helps guide surgical planning: significant flow obstruction generally supports intervention, while preserved flow suggests observation is appropriate

CT with 3D Reconstruction of the Craniocervical Junction

CT imaging provides superior bony detail compared to MRI and is essential for assessing the craniocervical junction when CCI is suspected. Specific measurements that inform CCI diagnosis — including the clivo-axial angle, the Harris measurement, and the grabb-oakes measurement — require clear visualization of bony landmarks that CT provides. 3D reconstructions allow surgeons to plan craniocervical fusion with precise understanding of the local anatomy. CT and MRI are complementary; both are typically needed when CCI-driven Chiari is under evaluation.

Treatment Options

Treatment decisions for Chiari malformation in Marshall syndrome must account for the underlying mechanism. The following options are not mutually exclusive, and specialist centers experienced in connective tissue disorders are best positioned to guide decision-making.

Observation

For individuals with measurable tonsillar herniation but stable, mild, or absent symptoms, and without evidence of syringomyelia or significant CSF flow obstruction, observation with serial imaging and clinical monitoring is appropriate. The key is that observation is a active surveillance strategy — not a passive one. Specific triggers that should prompt re-evaluation include new or worsening headaches, any new neurological symptoms, or interval development of a syrinx on follow-up imaging.

Posterior Fossa Decompression (for Congenital Chiari)

Posterior fossa decompression is the established surgical treatment for congenital Chiari I when symptoms are significant and attributable to the Chiari. The procedure removes a small portion of the occipital bone (and sometimes the posterior arch of C1) to enlarge the bony compartment at the base of the skull, relieving pressure on the cerebellar tonsils. Duraplasty — opening and expanding the dura to create additional space — is performed in many cases. This surgery is appropriate when:

  • The Chiari is structural (posterior fossa hypoplasia), not CCI-driven
  • Symptoms are clearly attributable to Chiari (particularly Valsalva-provoked headaches and evidence of CSF flow obstruction)
  • CCI has been evaluated and excluded as the primary driver

When applied to CCI-driven Chiari, decompression addresses the herniation without treating the instability. Outcomes are less predictable, symptoms may persist, and craniocervical instability may worsen if the decompression procedure destabilizes the craniocervical junction further.

Craniocervical Fusion (for CCI-Driven Chiari)

When CCI is the root cause of the Chiari malformation, craniocervical fusion — a surgery that stabilizes the skull-to-spine junction — addresses the underlying mechanism. By preventing further craniocervical settling, fusion restores posterior fossa geometry. Over time, the cerebellar tonsils often ascend toward their normal position as the compressive forces driving the herniation are removed. In published case series from centers specializing in connective tissue disorders, this approach has produced significant symptom improvement and syrinx stabilization or regression in patients with CCI-driven Chiari.

Craniocervical fusion is a major surgery with significant implications, and the decision requires evaluation at a center experienced in both craniocervical stabilization and connective tissue disorders. It is not appropriate for all patients, and the full risk-benefit discussion must occur with a surgical team familiar with CCI in this context.

Syrinx Shunting

Direct shunting of a syrinx — placing a drainage catheter into the syrinx cavity to divert fluid into the subarachnoid space or the pleural/peritoneal cavity — is rarely the primary approach to syringomyelia associated with Chiari. Treating the Chiari (whether by decompression or fusion) is almost always attempted first, as addressing the underlying CSF flow obstruction frequently leads to syrinx stabilization or regression without direct syrinx surgery. Syrinx shunting may be considered when:

  • The syrinx continues to expand despite adequate Chiari treatment
  • Neurological deterioration is rapid and the syrinx is large
  • There is an independent cause for the syrinx (such as arachnoiditis) that is not amenable to Chiari surgery

Syrinx shunts carry risks of shunt malfunction and the need for revision surgery, and the decision is made case by case.

Red Flags Requiring Urgent Neurological Evaluation
The following symptoms in a child or adult with Marshall syndrome and known or suspected Chiari malformation warrant urgent medical evaluation — not a scheduled appointment:
  • Sudden severe headache at the occiput (“thunderclap” or the worst headache of the patient’s life), particularly if triggered by coughing or straining
  • Acute onset of swallowing difficulty or change in voice quality, suggesting new or worsening brainstem compression
  • Rapidly progressive weakness or numbness in any limb, particularly if ascending or descending over hours to days
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control (new onset), which may indicate rapid syrinx expansion or acute cord compromise
  • Respiratory irregularity, apnea episodes, or new difficulty breathing — these can reflect brainstem compression affecting respiratory centers
  • Loss of consciousness or near-syncope with coughing or Valsalva, suggesting transient herniation or acute intracranial pressure change
  • Rapid scoliosis progression over weeks in a child with a known syrinx, suggesting active syrinx expansion
Research Update (R499): High Rate of Failed Chiari Decompressions in Connective Tissue Disorders

Recent surgical outcome data has reinforced a critical point: up to 50% of Chiari decompression surgeries in patients with underlying connective tissue disorders fail to provide sustained relief. The primary reason is unrecognized CCI — the Chiari is a consequence of craniocervical instability, not a primary posterior fossa problem, and decompression without addressing the instability leaves the root cause untreated.

  • Screen with upright MRI before Chiari surgery: Standard supine MRI can miss CCI-driven Chiari because lying flat reduces the gravitational settling that causes the herniation. Upright MRI or dynamic imaging reveals the instability that supine imaging conceals
  • Repeat imaging if symptoms recur post-decompression: Patients who improve initially after Chiari decompression but relapse should be evaluated for progressive CCI. The instability may have worsened, or may have been subclinical at the time of the initial surgery
  • Marshall syndrome context: Given the COL11A1-mediated ligamentous laxity at C0-C1-C2, any Marshall syndrome patient diagnosed with Chiari I should have CCI formally excluded before proceeding to decompression surgery. CCI-driven Chiari may resolve with craniocervical fusion alone, sparing the patient an unnecessary posterior fossa decompression

Questions to Ask Your Doctor

  1. “Is this Chiari congenital or could it be acquired through craniocervical instability?” — This is the essential first question. The answer shapes the entire treatment pathway. If the team is not familiar with CCI-driven Chiari in connective tissue disorders, seeking an additional opinion at a center with this expertise is appropriate.
  2. “Has craniocervical instability been evaluated with upright and dynamic imaging, not just supine MRI?” — Standard supine MRI is insufficient to exclude CCI. Dynamic imaging studies may be needed before the true picture is clear.
  3. “Should we obtain cine-flow MRI to assess CSF flow at the foramen magnum?” — CSF flow studies provide functional information that complements structural measurements. They are particularly important when tonsillar herniation is borderline but symptoms are significant.
  4. “Is there a syrinx? If so, what is its extent and is it stable or growing?” — Syrinx size and stability directly influence the urgency of intervention. Serial MRI is needed to track progression.
  5. “If scoliosis surgery is being considered, is it safe to proceed with an untreated Chiari or syrinx?” — The answer, in virtually all cases, is that Chiari and syrinx should be addressed before scoliosis surgery is undertaken. Confirm that the surgical team is aware of and has accounted for all three conditions.
  6. “What would trigger a reassessment if we are taking an observation approach?” — Establishes clear criteria so that new symptoms are not dismissed as unrelated to the Chiari. Families should know exactly when to call, when to go to the emergency department, and what imaging would be needed at that point.
