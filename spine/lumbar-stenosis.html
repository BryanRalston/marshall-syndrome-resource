Lumbar Spinal Stenosis: Early-Onset Narrowing in COL11A1

In the general population, lumbar spinal stenosis is a condition of aging — typically appearing after age 50. In Marshall syndrome, premature disc degeneration, platyspondyly, and endplate changes can narrow the spinal canal decades earlier. Symptomatic stenosis in the 20s and 30s is a real possibility that clinicians unfamiliar with COL11A1 may not consider.

What Is Lumbar Spinal Stenosis?

Lumbar spinal stenosis is the narrowing of the spaces within the lower spine through which the spinal cord and nerve roots travel. There are three distinct types, each compressing neural structures in a different location:

  • Central canal stenosis: Narrowing of the main spinal canal itself, where the cauda equina (the bundle of nerve roots extending below the spinal cord) passes. This is the most common type and produces the classic symptom of neurogenic claudication.
  • Lateral recess stenosis: Narrowing of the lateral recesses — the channels where nerve roots exit the central canal and enter the neural foramina. This compresses individual nerve roots as they begin their exit path, often causing radiculopathy in a specific dermatomal pattern.
  • Foraminal stenosis: Narrowing of the neural foramina — the bony tunnels through which nerve roots exit the spine entirely. Disc collapse, osteophyte formation, and facet hypertrophy all contribute to foraminal narrowing.

In practice, many patients have a combination of all three types, particularly when the underlying cause is generalized disc and facet degeneration — as is the case in Marshall syndrome.

Why this matters in Marshall syndrome: The typical degenerative cascade that produces stenosis — disc desiccation, disc bulging, facet hypertrophy, ligament thickening, osteophyte formation — depends on decades of accumulated wear on initially normal structures. In COL11A1 patients, the structures are abnormal from birth. The degenerative cascade starts earlier, progresses faster, and occurs in a canal that may already be narrowed by platyspondyly and endplate irregularities.

Platyspondyly adds an additional factor: flattened vertebral bodies alter the baseline geometry of the spinal canal. Even mild degenerative changes superimposed on a canal that is already geometrically compromised can push the available space below the symptomatic threshold.

Symptoms

Neurogenic Claudication

The hallmark symptom of lumbar spinal stenosis is neurogenic claudication — pain, heaviness, numbness, or weakness in the legs that develops with walking or prolonged standing and is relieved by sitting down or bending forward. The mechanism is straightforward: standing and walking extend the lumbar spine, which further narrows an already compromised canal. Sitting and forward flexion open the canal, relieving nerve compression.

Key characteristics:

  • Walking distance limited: Patients can typically walk a predictable distance before symptoms force them to stop
  • Relieved by sitting or bending forward: Not just by stopping — the position matters. Sitting on a bench provides relief; standing still does not
  • Bilateral and often diffuse: Symptoms commonly affect both legs, though one side may be worse
  • Progressive over months to years: Walking tolerance gradually decreases as stenosis worsens
  • Worse going downhill or on flat surfaces: Uphill walking and cycling involve forward flexion, which opens the canal — patients often tolerate these better than flat walking
The shopping cart sign: A classic clinical clue for neurogenic claudication. Patients report that they can walk much farther when leaning on a shopping cart (which flexes the lumbar spine) than when walking upright. If you notice this pattern — better tolerance for activities involving forward lean — mention it to the spine specialist.

Distinguishing from Vascular Claudication

Vascular claudication (from peripheral artery disease) also causes leg pain with walking but has important differences:

Radiculopathy

When stenosis compresses a specific nerve root (particularly in lateral recess or foraminal stenosis), the result is radiculopathy — pain radiating along the distribution of that nerve root. In the lumbar spine, this typically presents as sciatica: pain radiating from the buttock down the back or side of the leg, potentially into the foot. Associated findings may include numbness, tingling, or weakness in the muscles supplied by the affected nerve root.

Cauda Equina Syndrome: Red Flags

Cauda equina syndrome is a surgical emergency. Severe central canal stenosis can compress the cauda equina sufficiently to cause: bladder or bowel dysfunction (retention, incontinence, or loss of awareness of fullness), saddle anesthesia (numbness in the perineal region — the area that would contact a bicycle seat), and progressive bilateral leg weakness. If any of these symptoms develop, seek emergency medical evaluation immediately. Delayed surgical decompression can result in permanent neurological damage.

When It Happens Early

In the typical population, lumbar spinal stenosis is overwhelmingly a condition of the second half of life. The average age of presentation is 60–65, with most patients becoming symptomatic after age 50. The degenerative process that produces stenosis requires decades of cumulative wear on initially normal spinal structures.

In Marshall syndrome, the timeline is compressed:

  • Childhood: Disc degeneration is already underway. MRI studies of children with Stickler/Marshall syndrome show disc desiccation (dark discs on T2-weighted imaging) and early endplate changes that would not be expected until middle age in unaffected individuals.
  • Teens and 20s: Disc height loss, facet loading changes, and early osteophyte formation may be visible on imaging. Most individuals remain asymptomatic during this stage, though some may experience low back pain (see spinal pain).
  • 20s and 30s: The degenerative cascade has had sufficient time to produce meaningful canal narrowing. Symptomatic stenosis — neurogenic claudication, radiculopathy — becomes a realistic possibility. This is two to three decades earlier than typical.
  • 40s and beyond: The severity of stenosis may parallel what is typically seen in the 60s and 70s in the general population, potentially requiring surgical intervention at an age when most people have not yet developed the condition.
The diagnostic challenge: When a 28-year-old presents with leg pain that worsens with walking and improves with sitting, lumbar stenosis is rarely at the top of the differential diagnosis. Clinicians may consider disc herniation, hip pathology, or even functional pain before considering stenosis — because stenosis “doesn’t happen at that age.” For individuals with Marshall syndrome, it absolutely can. Sharing the diagnosis and its known spinal effects with the evaluating physician is essential.

Diagnosis

MRI: The Primary Imaging Study

MRI is the gold standard for evaluating lumbar spinal stenosis. It provides excellent visualization of soft tissue structures (discs, ligaments, nerve roots, spinal cord) without radiation exposure. A standard lumbar MRI protocol for stenosis evaluation includes:

  • T1-weighted sagittal and axial sequences: Excellent anatomic detail; fat appears bright, helping delineate bony margins and foraminal contents
  • T2-weighted sagittal and axial sequences: Cerebrospinal fluid appears bright, making it easy to assess canal patency. Disc degeneration appears as dark (desiccated) discs against the normally bright signal of healthy discs
  • Axial images through the disc levels: Critical for measuring canal dimensions and identifying lateral recess and foraminal stenosis
Request the right study: A generic “lumbar MRI” order may not include all the sequences needed for stenosis evaluation. Ensure the ordering physician specifies evaluation for spinal stenosis so the radiologist protocols the study appropriately, including thin-cut axial images through the disc levels of interest.

CT Myelography

When MRI is insufficient — due to artifact from prior instrumentation, claustrophobia, implanted devices, or when MRI findings do not correlate with clinical symptoms — CT myelography provides an alternative. This involves injecting contrast dye into the spinal canal via lumbar puncture, then performing a CT scan. The contrast outlines the nerve roots and canal, providing detailed assessment of stenosis severity. CT myelography is particularly useful for:

  • Post-surgical spines with metal artifact
  • Dynamic stenosis suspected but not confirmed on standard MRI
  • Foraminal stenosis, which MRI sometimes underestimates

Dynamic Stenosis: Standing and Flexion-Extension Views

Standard MRI is performed with the patient lying down. In this position, the spine is unloaded, and stenosis may appear less severe than it is when the patient is upright and bearing weight. Dynamic stenosis refers to narrowing that worsens significantly with certain positions — particularly extension (arching backward) and axial loading (standing).

When clinical symptoms are more severe than the supine MRI suggests, consider:

  • Upright or axial-loaded MRI: Some centers offer MRI performed in a standing or seated position, or with an axial loading device that simulates weight-bearing. These studies can reveal stenosis that is occult on supine imaging.
  • Flexion-extension lateral X-rays: Dynamic X-rays taken while the patient bends forward and backward can reveal instability (spondylolisthesis) that is not apparent on static imaging. This is particularly relevant in COL11A1 patients, where ligamentous laxity may allow more translation between segments than expected.

Conservative Management

The majority of patients with lumbar spinal stenosis — even moderate stenosis — can be managed conservatively, at least initially. The natural history is variable: some patients remain stable for years, some improve, and some gradually worsen. Conservative management aims to maximize function and minimize symptoms while monitoring for progression.

Physical Therapy: Flexion-Based Approach

Because lumbar flexion opens the spinal canal (and extension narrows it), physical therapy for stenosis emphasizes flexion-based exercises — the opposite of the extension-based approaches used for many other spine conditions. For broader PT principles in Marshall syndrome, see physical therapy overview.

The Williams flexion protocol is the traditional flexion-based exercise program for stenosis. Core components include:

  • Posterior pelvic tilts: Flatten the lumbar lordosis against the floor, opening the canal
  • Knee-to-chest stretches: Single and double knee pulls to flex the lumbar spine
  • Partial sit-ups / curl-ups: Strengthen abdominal muscles that support lumbar flexion posture
  • Hamstring stretches: Tight hamstrings contribute to increased lumbar lordosis
  • Hip flexor stretches: Tight hip flexors pull the pelvis into anterior tilt, increasing lordosis
  • Squat exercises: Functional strengthening in a flexed lumbar posture
Cycling over walking: Cycling is an excellent aerobic exercise for people with lumbar stenosis. The forward-leaning posture on a bicycle naturally flexes the lumbar spine, opening the canal. Many stenosis patients who can only walk a few hundred meters can cycle for miles without symptoms. Recumbent bikes and stationary bikes are equally effective. Conversely, prolonged upright walking — especially on flat or downhill terrain — may provoke symptoms.

Epidural Steroid Injections

Epidural steroid injections deliver anti-inflammatory medication directly to the area of nerve compression. They can provide temporary symptom relief, typically lasting weeks to months:

  • Interlaminar approach: Steroid deposited in the posterior epidural space; useful for central stenosis
  • Transforaminal approach: Steroid delivered to a specific nerve root at the foramen; useful for radiculopathy from foraminal stenosis
  • Caudal approach: Entry through the sacral hiatus; an alternative when interlaminar access is difficult
  • Typically limited to 3–4 injections per year to minimize systemic steroid effects
  • Not curative — they reduce inflammation and pain but do not reverse the structural narrowing

Activity Modification

Practical adjustments that can meaningfully improve daily function:

  • Use a shopping cart or rolling walker for longer walks (flexed posture)
  • Choose cycling over walking for aerobic exercise
  • Avoid prolonged standing; sit with a slight forward lean when possible
  • Sleep in a position that maintains lumbar flexion (side-lying with knees drawn up)
  • Avoid activities that hyperextend the lumbar spine (overhead reaching, sleeping prone)
  • When walking, break long distances into shorter segments with seated rest breaks

Surgical Options

Surgery is considered when conservative management fails to provide adequate symptom relief, when neurological deficits are progressing, or when cauda equina syndrome is present. For Marshall syndrome patients, the decision is complicated by the earlier age of onset and the likelihood of needing the surgical result to last for decades. For general surgical considerations in COL11A1, see surgical overview.

Fusion Considerations

Decompression alone (laminectomy without fusion) is the standard approach for stenosis without instability. However, fusion may be added when:

  • Spondylolisthesis is present (vertebral slippage creating instability)
  • Significant scoliosis creates asymmetric stenosis requiring extensive decompression that would destabilize the spine
  • Decompression requires removing so much bone and ligament that postoperative instability is likely
  • Recurrent stenosis after prior decompression suggests underlying instability

Adding fusion increases operative time, blood loss, complication risk, and recovery time — but prevents postoperative instability that could cause worse symptoms than the original stenosis.

Relationship to Other Spine Issues

Scoliosis Creating Asymmetric Stenosis

Scoliosis in Marshall syndrome can produce asymmetric spinal canal narrowing. The concavity of a scoliotic curve compresses the canal and foramina on one side while relatively sparing the other. This creates a pattern of unilateral or asymmetric symptoms that can be confusing diagnostically. In adults with degenerative scoliosis superimposed on COL11A1-driven curves, the combination of rotational deformity and degenerative stenosis may produce multilevel, asymmetric compression requiring careful surgical planning.

Cervical Stenosis Overlap

COL11A1 affects the entire spine. Patients with lumbar stenosis from Marshall syndrome may also have — or may later develop — cervical stenosis and myelopathy. Tandem stenosis (simultaneous cervical and lumbar stenosis) is an important diagnostic consideration because cervical myelopathy can produce leg symptoms (spasticity, gait disturbance) that overlap with lumbar stenosis symptoms. When a patient with known lumbar stenosis fails to improve after adequate lumbar decompression, cervical stenosis should be evaluated.

Tethered Cord Mimicry

In some cases, the symptom pattern of lumbar stenosis — particularly progressive leg weakness with gait difficulty and bladder symptoms — can mimic a tethered spinal cord. While tethered cord is not a recognized feature of Marshall syndrome, it is important to differentiate because the treatments are entirely different. MRI of the full spine (not just lumbar) helps distinguish these entities. A tethered cord shows the conus medullaris (the terminal end of the spinal cord) positioned abnormally low (below L2 in adults), while stenosis shows canal narrowing with a normally positioned conus.

Spondylolisthesis in Connective Tissue Disorders (R597)

Spondylolisthesis occurs earlier and progresses faster in COL11A1 disorders. A 2025 retrospective study of spondylolisthesis in pediatric connective tissue disorder (CTD) populations found clinically important differences from idiopathic spondylolisthesis:
  • Earlier age of presentation: Mean age at diagnosis was 11.2 years in CTD children vs. 15.4 years in idiopathic cases. The combination of ligamentous laxity, facet joint incompetence, and disc degeneration creates earlier mechanical failure at the pars interarticularis and facet joints
  • Higher progression rate: CTD children showed a 2.3x higher rate of slip progression compared to idiopathic spondylolisthesis over 3 years of follow-up. Defective collagen in the pars and ligamentous structures impairs the body’s ability to stabilize the slip through natural healing
  • L5-S1 predominance but multi-level risk: While L5-S1 remains the most common level (as in the general population), CTD children had a significantly higher rate of multi-level involvement — particularly L4-L5 in combination with L5-S1
  • Bracing may be less effective: Standard TLSO bracing for spondylolisthesis assumes normal tissue healing response. In COL11A1 disorders, the pars defect may not heal reliably even with bracing, shifting the risk-benefit calculation toward earlier surgical stabilization for progressive slips
  • Screening recommendation: Standing lateral X-rays with flexion-extension views should be obtained at baseline and during the pubertal growth spurt in any Marshall syndrome child with low back pain, hamstring tightness, or gait change. The growth spurt is the highest-risk period for slip initiation and progression
The multi-level spine: Marshall syndrome does not affect just one part of the spine. When evaluating any spinal complaint, consider the entire spine — cervical instability, thoracic kyphosis, lumbar stenosis, and scoliosis can all coexist and interact. A comprehensive spinal assessment at baseline, with periodic reassessment, provides the best framework for management across the lifespan.
