Craniocervical Instability (CCI): The Hidden Structural Risk

CLINICAL ALERT — If your child has Lhermitte’s sign (electric shocks with neck flexion), progressive leg weakness, or difficulty walking, CCI must be evaluated urgently. Standard supine MRI may miss dynamic instability — flexion-extension imaging is required.

What Is CCI and Why It Matters in Marshall Syndrome

The craniocervical junction — where the skull meets the top two vertebrae (C1 and C2) — is stabilized almost entirely by ligaments, not bone. Unlike most of the spine, this region depends on soft tissue to keep the brainstem and upper spinal cord safe. When those ligaments are abnormal, the consequences can be severe.

The transverse ligament of the atlas is the most critical stabilizer at C1-C2. Its central fibrocartilaginous portion contains type II collagen and aggrecan — the same molecules affected by COL11A1 mutations. COL11A1 regulates type II collagen fibril diameter, maintaining the critical 8:1 ratio between type II and type XI collagen fibrils. When COL11A1 is mutated, this ratio is disrupted, producing disorganized, irregular fibrils with reduced structural integrity.

This creates a direct mechanistic pathway:

  • COL11A1 mutation → abnormal type XI collagen → dysregulated type II collagen fibril assembly
  • Dysregulated fibrils → weakened transverse ligament → ligamentous laxity at C0-C1-C2
  • Ligamentous laxity → craniocervical instability → potential brainstem and cord compression

This is not theoretical speculation. The evidence from related collagenopathies is striking:

  • 100% of spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia (SED) congenita patients had craniovertebral junction instability in one study — SED congenita is caused by COL2A1 mutations affecting the same type II collagen that COL11A1 regulates
  • A 2025 study in the Journal of Pediatric Orthopaedics now recommends regular lateral flexion-extension neck X-rays in all children with type II collagenopathies, including Marshall and Stickler syndromes

CCI Can Explain Multiple Symptoms Simultaneously

One of the most important things to understand about CCI is that a single anatomical lesion at the craniocervical junction can produce a wide array of seemingly unrelated symptoms:

SymptomMechanism at Craniocervical Junction
Lhermitte’s sign (electric shocks with neck flexion) Dorsal cord compression at C1-C2
Leg weakness / legs giving out Corticospinal tract compression (legs are affected before arms due to tract somatotopy)
Red hot ears C2-C3 nerve root irritation or brainstem trigeminal autonomic pathway disruption
Evening worsening fatigue Dynamic mechanical compression accumulates throughout the day under gravitational load
Autonomic instability (dizziness, temperature dysregulation, GI symptoms) Brainstem compression → “cervical medullary syndrome”
Key insight: All of these symptoms can originate from a single anatomical lesion at the craniocervical junction. A child seeing multiple specialists for “separate” problems may have one upstream cause.

The Chiari Connection

CCI does not just coexist with Chiari malformation — it can cause it. A landmark 2007 paper by Milhorat and colleagues demonstrated that craniocervical instability can produce acquired Chiari malformation through the following pathway:

  • Ligamentous laxity at the craniocervical junction allows abnormal movement and settling
  • Cranial settling (the skull sinks relative to the spine) pushes the cerebellar tonsils downward
  • Tonsils are forced through the foramen magnum (the opening at the skull base)
  • This obstructs CSF flow, potentially leading to syringomyelia (fluid cavities within the cord)

This is the well-documented EDS-Chiari-CCI triad, extensively studied in Ehlers-Danlos syndrome. The same mechanism applies to COL11A1 disorders like Marshall syndrome, because the underlying problem — ligamentous laxity from abnormal collagen — is the same.

Important: Acquired Chiari from CCI may only be visible on upright imaging. When the patient lies down for a standard supine MRI, gravity is eliminated, the cranial settling reverses, and the tonsils may retract to a normal position. The Chiari effectively “disappears” on the scan.

The Diagnostic Gap — Why Standard MRI Can Give False Reassurance

CCI is a dynamic condition. It manifests under gravitational load and with movement. This creates a fundamental problem with standard imaging:

  • A supine MRI eliminates gravity, decompressing the spine
  • Lying flat reverses cranial settling
  • The patient is motionless, so dynamic instability is not provoked
A normal supine MRI does NOT rule out CCI. Dynamic instability can only be detected with imaging that incorporates movement or gravitational load.

Imaging options ranked by sensitivity for dynamic CCI:

  1. Digital Motion X-ray (DMX) — Captures at 30 frames per second during active neck movement. The most accurate modality for detecting dynamic instability. Limited availability.
  2. Upright / weight-bearing MRI — Imaging under gravitational load reveals functional instability and cranial settling that disappears when supine.
  3. Flexion-extension MRI — Can be performed on a standard scanner. The patient flexes and extends the neck for separate image sequences.
  4. Flexion-extension plain X-rays — The most widely available first-line dynamic assessment. Low cost, readily accessible. A reasonable starting point.
  5. CT with 3D reconstruction — Best for bony detail (odontoid morphology, os odontoideum), but provides no dynamic information.

Key Measurements to Request

If your child undergoes craniocervical imaging, ensure these specific measurements are reported:

MeasurementNormalConcerningWhat It Shows
Clivo-Axial Angle (CXA) >150° <135° (severe <130°) Ventral brainstem compression
Grabb-Oakes (pB-C2) Pediatric <4.5mm ≥6mm Ventral brainstem compression
Atlantodental Interval (ADI) Pediatric CT <2.6mm (97.5th percentile) ≥8mm needs surgical consideration C1-C2 stability
Basion-Dens Interval (BDI) Pediatric <10.5mm (CT) >12mm Occipitocervical relationship
Powers Ratio <1.0 ≥1.0 Atlanto-occipital dislocation screening
Exact wording to provide to your radiologist:

“Please measure and report: clivo-axial angle (CXA), Grabb-Oakes (pB-C2) measurement, basion-dens interval (BDI), basion-axial interval (BAI), atlantodental interval (ADI), Powers ratio, and atlantooccipital interval. If dynamic imaging, measure CXA and ADI in both flexion and extension. Clinical context: child with COL11A1 collagen disorder (Marshall syndrome) presenting with neurological symptoms. Evaluate for craniocervical instability, atlantoaxial instability, odontoid hypoplasia, cranial settling, and ventral brainstem compression.”

Treatment if CCI Is Confirmed

Conservative Management

  • Cervical collar: Limited use — approximately 3 times daily for 20–30 minutes. Prolonged collar use causes muscle atrophy, which worsens instability. The collar is a bridge, not a treatment.
  • Physical therapy: Cervical stabilization exercises to strengthen the deep neck flexors and surrounding musculature. The goal is to build muscular support to compensate for ligamentous laxity.
CRITICAL: No chiropractic neck manipulation under any circumstances. Even “gentle” upper cervical adjustments can worsen instability or cause acute neurological injury in a child with ligamentous laxity. This applies to all forms of cervical manipulation — traditional chiropractic, atlas orthogonal, NUCCA, and similar techniques.

Surgical Treatment: Occipitocervical Fusion

When conservative management is insufficient or neurological symptoms are progressing, occipitocervical fusion surgically stabilizes the skull-to-spine junction. Key considerations:

  • 100% fusion was achieved in one study of 24 children with skeletal dysplasia — demonstrating that fusion is reliably attainable even in patients with abnormal bone
  • Complication rates are significantly higher in children under age 10 (50% vs 18% in older children) — timing decisions must weigh progression risk against surgical risk
  • Hardware failure risk is higher in patients with collagen disorders due to abnormal bone quality
  • Despite these risks, delayed treatment in the presence of myelopathy leads to worse outcomes — neurological damage that occurs while waiting may be permanent

Recommended Next Steps

  1. Request flexion-extension cervical X-rays — widely available, low cost, and the appropriate first-line dynamic assessment
  2. If abnormal or clinical suspicion persists → pursue upright MRI or digital motion X-ray (DMX) for definitive dynamic evaluation
  3. Ensure any existing MRI is reviewed with the specific CCI measurement parameters listed above (CXA, Grabb-Oakes, ADI, BDI, Powers ratio)
  4. Referral to pediatric neurosurgery experienced in craniocervical disorders — general neurosurgery or orthopedics may not have sufficient expertise in this specific area
  5. Implement activity restrictions now while awaiting evaluation (see Cervical Myelopathy for the full restriction list)
  6. No chiropractic neck manipulation — not now, not ever, in the setting of potential craniocervical instability
