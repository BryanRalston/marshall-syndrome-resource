Scoliosis & Kyphosis: COL11A1-Specific Management

34% of individuals with Marshall syndrome develop scoliosis, and 43% show platyspondyly or Scheuermann-like kyphosis. Unlike idiopathic scoliosis, COL11A1-driven curves arise from defective disc and ligament collagen — which changes both the monitoring approach and the treatment decisions.

Prevalence & Curve Pattern

Scoliosis in Marshall syndrome is classified as syndromic scoliosis — a category distinct from both adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS) and neuromuscular scoliosis. It shares characteristics with scoliosis seen in other heritable connective tissue disorders such as Marfan syndrome (~60% prevalence) and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (~30% prevalence), where the underlying collagen defect directly compromises spinal structural integrity.

Key findings from radiographic studies of Stickler/Marshall cohorts include:

  • Scoliosis in 34% of patients
  • Endplate abnormalities in 74%
  • Schmorl’s nodes in 64%
  • Platyspondyly in 43%
  • Scheuermann-like kyphosis in 43%
How this differs from AIS: Adolescent idiopathic scoliosis occurs in structurally normal spines where the cause of asymmetric growth remains unknown. In COL11A1-related scoliosis, the cause is identifiable: defective type XI collagen in the discs, ligaments, and vertebral endplates creates asymmetric mechanical environments that drive progressive curvature. This distinction matters because the underlying pathology is ongoing, meaning curves may behave less predictably than typical AIS and may progress even with smaller initial Cobb angles.

Typical Curve Characteristics

While large cohort studies specific to Marshall syndrome curves are limited, connective tissue scoliosis generally presents with:

  • Thoracic or thoracolumbar curve patterns (similar distribution to AIS)
  • Both single and double curves reported, without a clear predominant pattern
  • Greater ligamentous laxity producing more flexible curves initially — which can be deceptive, as flexible curves in hypermobile patients may still progress rapidly
  • Associated kyphosis more commonly than in AIS (the 43% Scheuermann-like kyphosis rate is far higher than the general population)
  • Earlier onset possible due to endplate abnormalities present from birth

The COL11A1 Mechanism: Why Scoliosis Develops

Type XI collagen, encoded by COL11A1, is a quantitatively minor but functionally critical component of cartilage collagen fibrils. It is essential for anchoring proteoglycan aggregates to the collagen network — effectively acting as the molecular glue that maintains disc and cartilage structural integrity. In Marshall syndrome, defective type XI collagen sets up a cascade of spinal pathology:

Step 1: Structural Weakness in Discs and Ligaments

Type XI collagen is present in both the annulus fibrosus and nucleus pulposus of intervertebral discs, as well as in spinal ligaments and vertebral endplates. COL11A1 mutations produce abnormal collagen that weakens all three structures simultaneously:

  • Disc integrity: Defective proteoglycan anchoring reduces the disc’s ability to distribute load evenly across vertebral endplates
  • Ligament laxity: Spinal ligaments with abnormal collagen provide less passive stabilization, allowing more vertebral motion than normal
  • Endplate vulnerability: The 74% rate of endplate abnormalities reflects the direct effect of collagen XI deficiency on the disc-vertebral junction (see endplate calcification)

Step 2: Asymmetric Loading

With weakened discs and ligaments, the spine becomes vulnerable to asymmetric mechanical loading. Even small postural asymmetries — which would be corrected by normal connective tissue — can create uneven pressure distribution across vertebral growth plates.

Step 3: The Hueter-Volkmann Vicious Cycle

The Hueter-Volkmann principle describes how mechanical forces modulate bone growth: compression slows growth plate activity, while reduced compression (tension) accelerates it. In a spine with initial asymmetry:

  1. The concave side of the curve bears more compression → growth slows on that side
  2. The convex side bears less compression → growth accelerates on that side
  3. This differential growth creates vertebral wedging, increasing the curve
  4. The increased curve creates more asymmetric loading
  5. The cycle repeats, driving progressive deformity
The COL11A1 amplifier: In normal spines, healthy discs and ligaments resist this vicious cycle by distributing loads more evenly. In Marshall syndrome, weakened discs and ligaments amplify the cycle — loads are distributed less evenly from the start, the threshold for initiating asymmetric growth is lower, and the corrective forces that might slow progression are weaker. This is why the 34% scoliosis rate is significantly higher than the ~3% rate in the general adolescent population.

The PAX1-COL11A1-MMP3 Axis

Recent genetic research (eLife, 2024) has identified a specific molecular pathway linking COL11A1 to scoliosis susceptibility. In the intervertebral disc-vertebral junction region:

  • PAX1 and collagen XI both localize to the growth plate region where the disc inserts into the vertebral body (the ring apophysis)
  • Wild-type COL11A1 normally suppresses expression of PAX1 and MMP3 (a matrix-degrading enzyme)
  • Mutant COL11A1 fails to suppress these genes, leading to excessive MMP3 activity
  • Increased MMP3 degrades the extracellular matrix at the disc-vertebral junction
  • This weakens the ring apophysis — the very structure that anchors the disc to the vertebral body

Lagging maturity of the ring apophysis, combined with mechanical forces across the disc, has been proposed as an initiating factor for rotatory decompensation — the hallmark three-dimensional deformity of scoliosis.

Platyspondyly & Scheuermann-like Kyphosis

What Is Platyspondyly?

Platyspondyly is a reduction in vertebral body height — the vertebrae appear flattened on imaging, with decreased distance between the upper and lower endplates. In Marshall syndrome, this occurs because type XI collagen is critical for normal vertebral body growth plate function. Without adequate collagen XI, the endochondral ossification process that builds vertebral height is impaired.

On X-ray, platyspondyly in Marshall syndrome typically shows:

  • Generalized reduction in vertebral body height (not limited to one or two vertebrae)
  • Relatively preserved disc height initially (unlike degenerative conditions)
  • Irregular endplates — the 74% endplate abnormality rate reflects this
  • Possible Schmorl’s nodes (64%) where disc material herniates through weakened endplates
Clinical significance: Platyspondyly contributes to the short stature characteristic of Marshall syndrome. It also alters spinal biomechanics — flattened vertebrae change the leverage arm for paraspinal muscles and shift the distribution of loads across the spine, potentially contributing to both scoliosis and kyphosis development.

Scoliosis Plus Kyphosis: Combined Deformity

Many individuals with Marshall syndrome have both scoliosis and kyphosis (kyphoscoliosis). This combined three-dimensional deformity is more complex to manage than either alone and requires careful assessment of both the coronal plane curve (scoliosis) and the sagittal plane curve (kyphosis) when planning treatment.

Growth Spurt Risk: The Critical Window

Scoliosis progresses most rapidly during the adolescent growth spurt, when peak height velocity (PHV) creates the maximum differential between growth rates on the concave and convex sides of a curve. For children with Marshall syndrome, this period requires intensified monitoring.

Skeletal Maturity Assessment

Two systems are used to assess how much growth remains — and therefore how much scoliosis progression risk exists:

SystemWhat It MeasuresScaleKey Thresholds
Risser Sign Ossification of the iliac apophysis (pelvic bone cap), visible on spinal X-rays 0 to 5 Risser 0-1 = high risk (significant growth remaining); Risser 4-5 = low risk (near/at skeletal maturity)
Sanders Classification Hand/wrist X-ray: digital phalanx ossification stages 1 to 8 Sanders 1-3 = highest risk (at or before PHV); Sanders 7-8 = low risk (growth complete)
Sanders is more precise: Research shows the Sanders classification correlates more strongly with scoliosis progression than the Risser sign. Using Risser alone leads to mistreatment in approximately 1 in 4 patients, with the majority being undertreated — meaning growth (and curve progression risk) was underestimated. If your child’s spine specialist only uses Risser staging, ask whether a hand/wrist X-ray for Sanders staging would add useful information.

Peak Height Velocity Timing

PHV typically occurs at:

  • Girls: Around age 11–12 (before menarche)
  • Boys: Around age 13–14

The highest-risk period for curve progression is the 18-month window centered on PHV. For children with Marshall syndrome, the puberty window discussion is particularly important because this period also represents the time of maximum disc vulnerability.

Progression Risk: COL11A1 vs. Idiopathic

While no large prospective studies specifically compare progression rates in COL11A1 scoliosis versus AIS, several factors suggest COL11A1-related curves may carry higher progression risk:

  • The underlying collagen defect is constant — it does not improve with maturity
  • Disc degeneration begins earlier and progresses throughout life
  • Ligamentous laxity reduces passive spinal stability
  • Platyspondyly alters vertebral growth plate mechanics
  • The Hueter-Volkmann vicious cycle operates on a spine with less ability to resist asymmetric loading
Practical implication: Standard AIS progression prediction models may underestimate risk in COL11A1 patients. More conservative monitoring thresholds — checking more frequently and considering intervention at lower Cobb angles — may be appropriate. Discuss this directly with the treating spine specialist.

Monitoring: What, When, How

Home Screening: Adams Forward Bend Test

The Adams forward bend test is a simple screening tool that parents can perform at home between clinic visits:

  1. Have the child stand with feet together, knees straight, arms hanging freely
  2. Ask them to bend forward slowly at the waist until the back is roughly horizontal, palms together
  3. Stand behind and look along the horizontal plane of the back
  4. Look for: one side of the ribcage or lumbar area sitting higher than the other (a “rib hump” or “lumbar prominence”)

A scoliometer (an inexpensive inclinometer placed on the back during the forward bend) can quantify the degree of trunk rotation. A scoliometer reading of 7° or more warrants clinical evaluation. The Adams test should be added to regular checks starting at age 9–10 for girls and 11–12 for boys — but for children with Marshall syndrome, earlier screening (from age 5–6) is reasonable given the known risk.

Important limitation: The Adams test detects trunk rotation but can miss purely coronal plane curves. It is a screening tool, not a diagnostic test. A positive result requires standing X-ray confirmation, and a negative result does not rule out scoliosis — regular clinical assessment remains essential.

Cobb Angle: What the Numbers Mean

The Cobb angle is the standard measurement of scoliosis severity, measured on a standing anteroposterior (front-to-back) spinal X-ray. Lines are drawn along the most tilted vertebrae at the top and bottom of the curve, and the angle between these lines defines the Cobb angle.

Imaging Schedule

  • Baseline: Full spine standing X-ray at diagnosis or when scoliosis first detected
  • Growth period (Risser 0–2 or Sanders 1–4): Every 4–6 months (consider every 3–4 months for COL11A1)
  • Late growth (Risser 3–4 or Sanders 5–6): Every 6–12 months
  • Skeletal maturity (Risser 5 or Sanders 7–8): Annual or as needed
  • Adults with curves >30°: Every 1–2 years to monitor for degenerative progression

EOS Imaging: Reducing Radiation Exposure

Children with scoliosis require serial X-rays throughout growth — potentially 16 or more full-spine films over a childhood. The cumulative radiation exposure is a real concern. The EOS imaging system offers significant advantages (see spinal imaging):

  • 2–3x less radiation than standard computed radiography X-rays
  • 20x less radiation than CT scans
  • EOS micro-dose mode reduces exposure by an additional two-thirds
  • Captures simultaneous frontal and lateral full-body views in a single 20-second scan
  • Images taken in natural standing position for accurate Cobb angle measurement
  • 3D reconstruction capability for surgical planning
Cumulative radiation comparison: Over 16 full-spine studies (typical lifetime count for a scoliosis patient), standard radiography delivers approximately 10.9 mSv total radiation, while EOS delivers only 2.02 mSv — an 80% reduction. For a child who will need lifelong spine monitoring due to COL11A1, this difference is clinically meaningful. Ask whether your imaging center has EOS capabilities.

Bracing

Bracing works by applying corrective forces that counteract the Hueter-Volkmann vicious cycle: by shifting loads toward the convex side of the curve, the brace restores more symmetric growth plate loading and slows or halts progression. In AIS, bracing has strong evidence of effectiveness. In connective tissue scoliosis, the evidence is more limited but generally supports bracing as a first-line intervention.

COL11A1-Specific Bracing Considerations

  • Skin vulnerability: Collagen defects affect skin as well as skeletal tissue. Children with Marshall syndrome may be more prone to pressure injuries, skin breakdown, and irritation from brace contact points. Regular skin inspection under brace pads is essential, and the orthotist should be informed of the collagen disorder.
  • Ligamentous laxity: More flexible spines may achieve greater in-brace correction (which can look encouraging) but may also lose more correction when the brace is removed. The spine specialist should assess both in-brace and out-of-brace Cobb angles.
  • Variable effectiveness: In connective tissue disorders, bracing success may be less predictable than in AIS. Some patients show excellent response; others progress despite good compliance. This does not mean bracing is futile — it may slow progression enough to delay or avoid surgery even if it does not fully halt the curve.
  • Earlier initiation: Given the higher progression risk, some specialists advocate beginning bracing at lower Cobb angles (20–25° rather than the standard 25–30° threshold) in connective tissue scoliosis.
Scoliosis Bracing in Hypermobile Patients (2025)

A 2025 study examining bracing outcomes in patients with joint hypermobility found a potentially positive effect on bracing outcomes — more flexible spines respond better to the corrective forces applied by the brace, achieving greater in-brace correction. This is encouraging for Marshall syndrome patients, whose ligamentous laxity produces the spinal flexibility that allows braces to work effectively.

However, realizing this benefit requires strict attention to compliance and brace management:

  • Compliance is critical: A minimum of 16 hours per day of wearing time is required for effective treatment. Studies with compliance monitors consistently show that outcomes correlate directly with hours worn — partial compliance produces partial results.
  • Challenges in connective tissue disorders: Skin sensitivity (thinner, more fragile skin from the collagen defect), heat intolerance (braces trap body heat, particularly problematic in warm climates or during physical activity), and rapid fit changes (growth spurts and soft tissue laxity can alter brace fit faster than in typical patients) all require proactive management.

Brace Type Selection

  • Rigo-Chêneau (3D corrective): Emphasizes three-dimensional de-rotation; custom-molded; may offer superior correction for the complex curve patterns seen in connective tissue scoliosis
  • Boston (posterior opening): The most widely studied brace; CAD/CAM designed; good evidence base for thoracic and thoracolumbar curves
  • Providence (nighttime only): For patients who cannot tolerate or comply with full-time bracing; significant over-correction built into supine design; much better adherence rates

Strategies for Success

  • Experienced orthotist: Work with an orthotist who has experience fitting braces for patients with connective tissue disorders. The skin fragility, rapid fit changes, and need for modified padding are not standard considerations.
  • Undershirt protection: A seamless, moisture-wicking undershirt worn beneath the brace reduces skin friction and irritation. Cotton is acceptable; synthetic moisture-wicking fabrics may be better in warm climates.
  • Gradual wear-time increase: Start with 2–4 hours per day and increase by 1–2 hours daily over 1–2 weeks until target wear time is reached. This allows the skin and the patient to adapt, improving long-term compliance.
  • Every-3-month fit checks: Schedule orthotist appointments at least every 3 months (more frequently during growth spurts) to assess fit, adjust padding, and ensure the brace is still delivering effective correction. In connective tissue patients, brace fit can change faster than the standard 6-month check interval accommodates.
  • Complement with PSSE: Physiotherapeutic scoliosis-specific exercises (Schroth, SEAS) should be used alongside bracing, not as an alternative. PSSE teaches active self-correction and trunk muscle activation that reinforces the brace’s corrective forces and maintains gains during brace-free hours.
3D-Printed Custom TLSO Braces (R571)

Emerging brace technology using 3D printing (additive manufacturing) offers significant advantages for children with Marshall syndrome who require scoliosis bracing:

  • 25–30% lighter than traditional braces: 3D-printed TLSOs use optimized lattice structures that provide equivalent corrective force with substantially less weight. For a child already dealing with joint pain and fatigue, every gram matters.
  • Superior ventilation: The lattice design allows significantly better airflow compared to solid-shell braces, reducing heat retention and skin irritation — both major compliance barriers in children with collagen-fragile skin.
  • Precision fit from body scanning: 3D body scanning captures the torso geometry with millimeter accuracy, eliminating the mess and imprecision of plaster casting. The digital model can be adjusted and reprinted if the child grows or the fit changes, without requiring a full re-casting appointment.
  • Improved compliance: Studies show that the combination of lighter weight, better ventilation, and improved aesthetics leads to measurably better wear-time compliance in adolescents — the single most important factor in bracing success.

Availability: 3D-printed braces are offered at an increasing number of orthotic centers and children’s hospitals. Ask your orthotist whether 3D-printed options are available. Insurance coverage is typically equivalent to traditional bracing. The technology is no longer experimental — it is an evidence-based manufacturing improvement.

Compliance: The Biggest Challenge

Full-time bracing asks a child to wear a rigid device for 16–23 hours per day over several years of adolescence. Non-compliance — from outright refusal to inconsistent wear — is the most common reason bracing fails. Modern braces include built-in compliance monitors (temperature sensors) that objectively track wear time.

Smart Scoliosis Brace Monitoring (R508)

Next-generation scoliosis braces are integrating AI-powered sensor systems that go far beyond simple temperature-based compliance logging. These systems provide real-time data on brace effectiveness and wearing behavior, addressing the two biggest problems in brace management: compliance tracking and corrective force optimization.

  • Integrated pressure sensors: Force-sensing arrays embedded in the brace pads measure the actual corrective forces being applied to the spine in real time. This data allows the orthotist to verify that the brace is producing adequate correction — not just that it is being worn, but that it is working. For Marshall syndrome, where skin fragility limits tolerable pressure, this feedback can help optimize the balance between correction and tissue safety
  • AI-driven compliance analytics: Machine learning algorithms analyze wearing patterns (when the brace is worn, for how long, and whether it is worn correctly) and present the data to families and clinicians via mobile apps. Some systems send gentle reminders when wearing time is below target and provide positive reinforcement when goals are met — approaches shown to improve compliance by 15–25% compared to unmonitored bracing
  • Mobile app integration: Companion apps display daily and weekly compliance data, corrective force trends, and progress visualization. Adolescents who can see their own data tend to engage more actively with treatment. Some apps include gamification elements (streaks, goals, achievement badges) that improve compliance in the 12–16 age group
  • Remote orthotist monitoring: Sensor data can be transmitted to the orthotist between office visits, allowing early detection of problems (brace not fitting after a growth spurt, declining compliance, inadequate correction) without waiting for the next scheduled appointment. This is particularly valuable for families who travel long distances for specialist care

Current availability: Smart brace systems are increasingly available at major scoliosis centers but are not yet standard of care everywhere. Ask your orthotist whether sensor-equipped options (e.g., ScoliSMART, iOrthotics, or Brace Monitor systems) are available. Some systems are compatible with existing brace designs and can be retrofitted. Insurance coverage varies — document the compliance data as medically necessary for treatment optimization.

Nighttime bracing option: For children who struggle with full-time brace compliance, nighttime-only braces (Providence, Charleston) offer a compromise. While full-time bracing generally shows better outcomes for larger curves, a nighttime brace worn consistently is more effective than a full-time brace worn inconsistently. Discuss this option with the spine specialist if compliance is a barrier.
Nighttime Bracing & Schroth Integration for Hypermobile Patients (R395)

A 2025 review of scoliosis management in hypermobile patients highlights three strategies with particular relevance to COL11A1-driven curves:

  • Night bracing as first choice: For moderate curves (25–35°) in hypermobile patients, nighttime bracing (Providence or Charleston) is increasingly favored as a first-line approach rather than a fallback for non-compliance. The supine position combined with hypermobile spine flexibility allows aggressive over-correction that achieves comparable correction to full-time bracing — without the daytime compliance burden that derails many treatment courses. This is particularly relevant for Marshall syndrome, where skin fragility limits tolerable brace-wearing hours.
  • Schroth method as mandatory complement: The 2025 evidence strengthens the case that scoliosis-specific exercises (Schroth or SEAS) are not optional additions but essential co-interventions alongside bracing in connective tissue patients. In hypermobile spines, the active muscle correction patterns taught by Schroth provide dynamic stabilization that compensates for ligamentous laxity — the correction force that disappears when the brace comes off. Families should insist on referral to a Schroth-certified therapist, not generic PT.
  • Skin fragility padding protocol: Connective tissue patients require modified brace padding that accounts for thinner, more fragile skin. The updated recommendation calls for silicone gel pads at all bony pressure points, closed-cell foam replacing standard felt padding, and a two-layer interface (moisture-wicking seamless undershirt plus the brace padding). Inspect skin at every brace-wearing session for the first 4 weeks and at least weekly thereafter. Any skin breakdown warrants immediate orthotist consultation for pressure redistribution.

Physical Therapy: Scoliosis-Specific Exercises

Scoliosis-specific exercise programs are distinct from general physical therapy. They use three-dimensional, curve-pattern-specific corrective movements rather than generic core strengthening (see physical therapy overview).

Schroth Method

The Schroth method is the best-studied scoliosis-specific exercise approach, using a combination of:

  • Three-dimensional correction: Exercises target the specific curve pattern (not generic)
  • Corrective breathing (rotational angular breathing): Directional breathing expands the collapsed side of the ribcage
  • Postural awareness: Active self-correction maintained during daily activities
  • Muscle activation: Targeted strengthening of the elongated/weakened side muscles

Evidence summary: A systematic review and meta-analysis found that the Schroth method is effective for reducing Cobb angle and trunk rotation angle and improving quality of life in adolescents aged 10–18 with AIS compared to no intervention or other conservative therapies. Six months of Schroth exercises added to standard care produced significantly better Cobb angle outcomes than standard care alone. However, the average improvement did not exceed the minimum clinically important difference in all studies, suggesting Schroth is most valuable as part of a comprehensive management plan rather than a standalone treatment.

SEAS (Scientific Exercise Approach to Scoliosis)

SEAS is another evidence-based approach that emphasizes active self-correction — teaching the patient to voluntarily correct their posture and hold it during task-oriented exercises. SEAS has shown Cobb angle improvements of approximately 5.3° at skeletal maturity versus stable curves with traditional exercises.

Core Stabilization vs. Curve-Specific Exercises

General core stabilization exercises (planks, bridges, etc.) strengthen trunk muscles but do not address the specific three-dimensional deformity of scoliosis. Evidence shows that curve-specific approaches like Schroth and SEAS produce better outcomes than generic core exercises for Cobb angle improvement. However, core stabilization remains valuable for overall spinal support and can complement scoliosis-specific programs.

Swimming: The Controversy

Swimming has historically been recommended for scoliosis, based on the idea that eliminating gravity would reduce spinal loading. Current evidence challenges this:

  • Swimming works back and abdominal muscles symmetrically, which provides general benefit
  • However, competitive swimming involves arching and flattening the thoracic spine, which may stimulate progression in some patients
  • Swimming is not a treatment for scoliosis — it will neither cure nor prevent curve progression
  • Recreational swimming is generally safe and provides good cardiovascular exercise
  • It should not replace scoliosis-specific exercises (Schroth, SEAS)
For COL11A1 patients: Aquatic therapy in warm water may offer additional benefits for joint pain and stiffness (see aquatic therapy), but this is separate from scoliosis management. The key exercise prescription for scoliosis is a scoliosis-specific program (Schroth or SEAS) supervised by a certified therapist, not generic swimming.

Surgical Options

Surgery is considered when curves progress beyond the threshold where bracing can control them — generally 40–50° in adolescents, or when curves cause significant functional limitation or pain (see surgical considerations).

Posterior Spinal Fusion and Instrumentation (PSF)

The traditional surgical approach for scoliosis:

  • Metal rods and screws (pedicle screws) are attached to the spine to correct and hold the curve
  • Bone graft is placed to create a solid fusion across the instrumented segments
  • The fused segments no longer move independently — the curve is permanently corrected and stabilized
  • Typical correction: 50–70% of the Cobb angle
  • Recovery: 4–6 months for return to most activities

Growing Rod Systems (MAGEC Rods)

For children under age 10 with early-onset scoliosis (curves appearing before skeletal maturity), traditional fusion would stop spinal growth and result in a short trunk. Growing rods address this:

  • MAGEC (MAGnetic Expansion Control) rods: Magnetically controlled rods that can be lengthened non-invasively using an external remote controller
  • Avoids repeated surgical lengthening procedures (traditional growing rods required surgery every 6–8 months)
  • Maintains curve control while allowing continued spinal growth
  • Eventually converted to definitive fusion when growth is complete
Research Update (R458): Magnetically Controlled Growing Rods — Updated Evidence

Systematic reviews of magnetically controlled growing rods (MCGRs) for early-onset scoliosis have refined the evidence base with data directly relevant to syndromic scoliosis:

  • 50% mean Cobb angle correction: MCGRs achieve approximately 50% correction of the major curve while preserving spinal growth — comparable to traditional growing rods but without repeated surgical lengthening under general anesthesia
  • No-anesthesia lengthening: The non-invasive magnetic lengthening procedure is performed in clinic using an external remote controller, eliminating the need for repeated general anesthesia. For Marshall syndrome patients — who face elevated anesthesia risk from difficult airway, cervical instability, and retinal detachment concerns — this is a significant safety advantage over traditional growing rods
  • Complication awareness: MCGR complications include rod breakage (~10%), implant-related metallosis (tissue staining from metal debris), and loss of distraction force over time requiring rod exchange. Families should understand that MCGR is a bridge strategy, not a one-time fix — definitive fusion is typically performed once growth is complete
  • COL11A1 consideration: Anchor point fixation may be less secure in bone with abnormal collagen composition. Discuss with the spine surgeon whether augmented fixation (larger screws, cement augmentation, or sublaminar band addition) is warranted given the underlying skeletal dysplasia

Vertebral Body Tethering (VBT) — Fusionless Surgery

VBT is a newer approach that preserves spinal motion and growth potential:

  • A strong, flexible polymer cord is attached to vertebral body screws on the convex side of the curve
  • The tether applies compression to the convex growth plates, slowing growth on that side
  • As the child grows, the concave side “catches up,” gradually correcting the curve — essentially harnessing the Hueter-Volkmann principle in reverse
  • FDA-approved since 2019; thoracoscopic (minimally invasive) approach available
  • Best candidates: ages 8–16, curves 30–65°, significant growth remaining (Sanders 3–5)
  • Recovery: 3–4 weeks to most activities (faster than fusion)
VBT complications to be aware of: Meta-analysis shows a 74% clinical success rate but a 52% complication rate and 16% unplanned reoperation rate. Most common complications are tether breakage, overcorrection (curve reversing past straight), and pulmonary issues. These rates should improve as surgical technique matures, but families should understand VBT is not risk-free.

Scoliosis in Connective Tissue Disorders: Different Rules (R536)

Scoliosis in connective tissue disorders like Marshall syndrome behaves fundamentally differently from adolescent idiopathic scoliosis (AIS), and families must understand three critical distinctions:

  • Faster progression: Curves in connective tissue disorders progress more rapidly than AIS curves of comparable initial magnitude. A 25° curve that might remain stable for years in AIS can progress to 40° within 12–18 months in a child with COL11A1 mutation. This means monitoring intervals should be shorter (every 3–4 months during growth spurts) rather than the standard 6-month AIS schedule
  • Poorer brace response: While bracing is still the first-line intervention, the success rate in connective tissue scoliosis is lower than the 72% success rate demonstrated in the BrAIST trial for AIS. Ligamentous laxity allows greater curve flexibility (which improves in-brace correction) but also allows more rapid loss of correction when the brace is removed. The net effect is that bracing may slow progression but is less likely to fully halt it. Families should understand that bracing buys time — potentially enough time to reach skeletal maturity without surgery — but may not be definitively curative
  • Higher surgical complication rates: When surgery becomes necessary, connective tissue patients face elevated risks including screw pullout from osteopenic bone, slower fusion consolidation, higher CSF leak rates from dural fragility, and accelerated adjacent segment degeneration. The decision to proceed to surgery should be made by a spine surgeon experienced in syndromic scoliosis, not a surgeon whose primary experience is AIS. Ask specifically: “How many patients with connective tissue disorders have you operated on?”

Kyphosis Management

Monitoring Kyphosis

Kyphosis is measured on a lateral (side-view) standing X-ray using the same Cobb angle method. Normal thoracic kyphosis ranges from 20–45°. Scheuermann-type kyphosis is typically diagnosed above 45° with associated vertebral wedging and endplate changes.

Bracing for Kyphosis

  • Milwaukee brace: The traditional brace for thoracic kyphosis — extends from the pelvis to a neck ring. Studies show average kyphosis reduction from 59° to 39° after 34 months of treatment. Most effective for skeletally immature patients with kyphosis under 70°.
  • Boston TLSO: An alternative for kyphosis under 70°. Less effective correction than Milwaukee but significantly better compliance (the neck ring of the Milwaukee brace is particularly unpopular with adolescents).
  • Full-time wear (>18 months) is typically needed to achieve improvement in vertebral wedging.
Research Update (R584): Thoracic Kyphosis — Scheuermann-like Presentation in COL11A1

The 43% rate of Scheuermann-like kyphosis in Marshall syndrome reflects a COL11A1-driven vertebral endplate and disc pathology that mimics classical Scheuermann’s disease but has a different underlying mechanism and potentially different treatment response:

  • Bracing is indicated for kyphosis between 50–80 degrees in skeletally immature patients. Below 50 degrees, observation with Schroth-based physiotherapy is typically appropriate. Above 80 degrees, surgical correction may be considered. The 50–80 degree range is the “bracing window” where external support has the best chance of halting progression and allowing partial vertebral remodeling during growth
  • Kyphosis + scoliosis (kyphoscoliosis): The combined three-dimensional deformity present in many Marshall syndrome patients requires careful brace design that addresses both the coronal and sagittal plane curves simultaneously. A brace designed for pure kyphosis or pure scoliosis alone will be inadequate
  • Sagittal balance matters as much as Cobb angle: In kyphosis management, restoring sagittal vertical axis alignment is critical for long-term spinal function. Progressive kyphosis shifts the body’s center of gravity forward, forcing compensatory lumbar lordosis that accelerates disc degeneration at lower levels

How Marshall Kyphosis Differs in Treatment

In true Scheuermann’s disease, the endplate changes are the primary pathology, and bracing can often halt progression and allow vertebral remodeling during growth. In Marshall syndrome:

  • The endplate changes are secondary to an ongoing collagen defect that bracing cannot correct
  • Vertebral remodeling may be less complete because the underlying collagen matrix remains abnormal
  • Combined scoliosis and kyphosis may require a different brace design than pure kyphosis
  • Longer monitoring after brace discontinuation is appropriate, as curves may resume progression when the underlying pathology persists

Surgical Indications for Kyphosis

Surgery for kyphosis is considered when:

  • Kyphosis exceeds 70–75° and is progressing
  • Neurological symptoms develop (see Lhermitte sign and cervical myelopathy)
  • Significant pain unresponsive to conservative measures
  • Bracing fails to control progression in a skeletally immature patient

Long-term Outcomes

Curves in Adulthood

In the general scoliosis population, curves under 30° at skeletal maturity tend to remain stable, while curves over 50° typically progress at about 1° per year throughout adulthood. For COL11A1 patients, the ongoing disc degeneration may shift this threshold lower — smaller curves may continue to progress as disc height loss creates progressive asymmetric loading.

Adjacent Segment Disease After Fusion

Adjacent segment disease (ASD) — degeneration of the disc levels immediately above and below a spinal fusion — occurs in 2–36% of fusion patients depending on follow-up duration. In COL11A1 patients, this concern is amplified:

  • Adjacent discs are already structurally compromised by the collagen defect
  • The increased mechanical stress from fusion is applied to discs with reduced capacity to handle load
  • ASD may develop earlier and progress faster than in patients with normal collagen
  • This is a strong argument for motion-preserving approaches (VBT, shorter fusion constructs) when surgically feasible

Pregnancy with Instrumented Spine

For women with Marshall syndrome who have undergone spinal fusion, pregnancy considerations include:

  • Back pain: Occurs in ~48% of women with scoliosis during pregnancy (vs. 34% in controls). Higher rates when fusion extends to L3 or below.
  • Cesarean section: Higher rates in women with scoliosis (~64% vs. ~33% in controls), particularly when the lowest instrumented vertebra is at L4 or below.
  • Epidural anesthesia: May be technically difficult or impossible through a fused spinal segment. Early consultation with an anesthesiologist experienced in instrumented spines is essential — ideally during pregnancy planning, not during labor.
  • Multidisciplinary planning: Obstetrician, anesthesiologist, and spine surgeon should collaborate to develop a delivery plan well in advance.

Quality of Life

Long-term studies of scoliosis fusion in adolescents show generally good quality of life outcomes. However, loss of spinal motion from fusion affects activities of daily living (bending, twisting), and this impact is lifelong. For COL11A1 patients, the additional burden of managing hearing loss, myopia, joint problems, and chronic pain makes quality-of-life optimization particularly important in all treatment decisions.

Questions for the Spine Specialist

  1. “Given the COL11A1 collagen defect, should we use more conservative Cobb angle thresholds for initiating treatment than standard AIS guidelines?” — Standard guidelines are based on idiopathic scoliosis with normal connective tissue. Your child’s underlying collagen disorder may warrant earlier intervention.
  2. “Can we use Sanders staging in addition to (or instead of) the Risser sign to assess growth remaining and predict curve progression risk?” — Sanders classification provides more precise growth assessment and better predicts scoliosis behavior than Risser alone.
  3. “Is EOS imaging available at your center, and can we use it for serial monitoring to reduce cumulative radiation exposure?” — Over a childhood of scoliosis monitoring, EOS reduces total radiation by approximately 80%.
  4. “Given the existing disc degeneration from COL11A1, is vertebral body tethering a better option than fusion to preserve motion and avoid accelerated adjacent segment disease?” — In patients with already-compromised discs, the long-term cost of fusion on adjacent segments may be higher than typical.
  5. “Should we assess bone density (DEXA scan) before making surgical decisions, given that collagen XI deficiency can affect bone quality?” — Bone quality directly affects implant fixation strength and fusion success.
  6. “Can you recommend a Schroth-certified or SEAS-trained physiotherapist for scoliosis-specific exercises alongside our monitoring/bracing plan?” — Scoliosis-specific exercises are most effective when supervised by a therapist certified in these methods, not generic physical therapy.
