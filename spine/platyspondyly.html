Platyspondyly & Vertebral Body Changes

Platyspondyly affects approximately 43% of individuals with Marshall syndrome and is one of the most diagnostically significant radiographic findings in COL11A1-related disorders. Its presence has direct consequences for growth assessment, bone density interpretation, and surgical planning — consequences that are frequently missed when standard reference ranges designed for typical spines are applied without adjustment.

What Is Platyspondyly

Platyspondyly (from the Greek platy, meaning flat, and spondylos, meaning vertebra) describes a condition in which vertebral bodies are abnormally flattened — their height is reduced relative to their width. In a typical spine, each vertebral body has a roughly square or slightly rectangular cross-section when viewed from the side. In platyspondyly, the vertebral body is compressed vertically, creating a wide, squat appearance that alters the entire biomechanical environment of the spine.

Platyspondyly is not a single disease but a radiographic sign — a finding that appears in several skeletal dysplasias including achondroplasia, spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, and mucopolysaccharidoses. In Marshall syndrome, it arises specifically from the effect of COL11A1 mutations on vertebral body growth plate cartilage, giving it a distinct mechanism and clinical context that differs from other conditions that share the same radiographic appearance.

How Platyspondyly Is Measured

The standard measurement is the vertebral body height-to-width ratio, assessed on lateral spine radiographs. In the lumbar spine, a normal ratio is approximately 0.8–1.0 (vertebral body height is close to its anteroposterior depth). Platyspondyly is generally defined when this ratio falls below 0.75, indicating that height is less than three-quarters of width. Severe platyspondyly can produce ratios below 0.5.

Additional features seen on imaging include:

  • Anterior wedging: The anterior height of the vertebral body is reduced more than the posterior, increasing thoracic kyphosis
  • Irregular endplates: The superior and inferior endplate surfaces appear uneven or scalloped rather than flat
  • Scheuermann-like changes: A pattern resembling Scheuermann’s disease, with endplate irregularities and anterior vertebral body wedging across multiple contiguous levels
  • Disc space changes: Relative expansion of disc space height compared to vertebral body height, inverting the normal ratio

Scheuermann-like vs. true Scheuermann’s disease: In classic Scheuermann’s kyphosis (a common adolescent condition), anterior wedging occurs in three or more contiguous vertebrae due to repetitive microtrauma during rapid growth. In Marshall syndrome, a visually similar appearance arises from a fundamentally different mechanism — defective endplate chondrocytes that fail to proliferate normally — and it is present from much earlier in development. The distinction matters for prognosis and treatment planning.

Why COL11A1 Mutations Cause Platyspondyly

To understand platyspondyly in Marshall syndrome, it helps to understand how vertebral bodies normally grow taller during childhood and adolescence. Vertebral height is gained through the vertebral body growth plates — also called primary ossification centers or endplate cartilage zones — located at the superior and inferior surfaces of each vertebral body. Like the long bone growth plates in the limbs, these zones contain columns of chondrocytes (cartilage cells) that proliferate, hypertrophy, and are gradually replaced by bone.

Normal Growth Plate Architecture

In a healthy vertebral growth plate, chondrocytes are organized into orderly longitudinal columns oriented vertically. Each column progresses through defined zones:

  1. Resting zone: Stem-like chondrocytes that replenish the column
  2. Proliferative zone: Rapidly dividing chondrocytes that elongate the column and drive height gain
  3. Hypertrophic zone: Enlarged chondrocytes that signal the transition to bone
  4. Calcification zone: Matrix mineralizes and bone replaces cartilage

This organized progression is what drives vertical growth. Type XI collagen, the product of COL11A1, is a critical structural component of the cartilage extracellular matrix surrounding these chondrocyte columns. It regulates fibril diameter in the collagen network that gives cartilage its mechanical properties and maintains the spatial organization of chondrocyte proliferation.

What Happens When COL11A1 Is Mutated

COL11A1 mutations disrupt the collagen fibril architecture of the growth plate matrix in two ways:

  • Fibril diameter dysregulation: Type XI collagen normally limits the diameter of type II collagen fibrils. Without functional COL11A1, fibrils become abnormally thick and disorganized, creating a mechanically altered matrix
  • Proteoglycan anchoring failure: The collagen network in cartilage traps and retains large proteoglycan aggregates (aggrecan) that hold water and provide compressive stiffness. Disorganized fibrils impair this retention

The consequence for chondrocyte columns is disorganization. Instead of proliferating in orderly vertical arrays, chondrocytes in COL11A1-deficient matrix lose their columnar orientation. The result is reduced vertical growth of each vertebral body — the cells are present and metabolically active, but their organized proliferative drive along the vertical axis is impaired. Width is less affected because lateral expansion of the vertebral body depends on periosteal bone formation rather than endplate chondrocyte columns.

A useful analogy: Think of chondrocyte columns as a stack of coins growing taller by adding one coin at a time from the bottom. Normal type XI collagen keeps the stack straight and aligned. In Marshall syndrome, the underlying surface is uneven, so coins land askew — the stack still grows, but it grows sideways more than upward, creating the characteristic flat, wide shape.

Why This Differs from Scheuermann’s Disease

In Scheuermann’s kyphosis, vertebral bodies start out normal. The anterior wedging develops because repetitive mechanical stress during adolescence causes the anterior endplate to fail, allowing disc material to herniate through it (Schmorl’s nodes) and disrupting anterior growth preferentially. The chondrocytes and collagen are initially normal; the problem is mechanical overload during a vulnerable growth period.

In Marshall syndrome platyspondyly, the chondrocytes and collagen matrix are intrinsically abnormal from the earliest stages of vertebral development. The flat shape is not a consequence of mechanical failure — it is the direct output of impaired chondrocyte column organization. This distinction explains why:

  • Platyspondyly in Marshall syndrome appears earlier and affects more levels than typical Scheuermann’s
  • Activity restriction — a cornerstone of Scheuermann’s management — does not address the underlying pathology in Marshall syndrome
  • The growth plate impairment also affects other cartilaginous structures simultaneously (joints, ribs, nasal cartilage), producing the multi-system phenotype of Marshall syndrome

Clinical Consequences

Short Trunk Stature

The most immediately visible consequence of platyspondyly is reduction in trunk height. Because each flattened vertebral body contributes less height than it would in a typical spine, the cumulative effect across the entire spinal column produces measurable short trunk stature. This is characteristically disproportionate: limb growth, which depends on long bone growth plates rather than vertebral endplates, is relatively less affected.

The clinical signature of disproportionate short trunk stature is:

  • Arm span greater than standing height — in a typical individual, arm span and height are nearly equal; a difference of >5 cm (arm span exceeding height) suggests reduced trunk height
  • Reduced sitting height relative to standing height
  • Elevated upper-to-lower segment ratio — though in platyspondyly affecting the entire spine, interpretation requires care
Sitting height vs. standing height measurement: The sitting height-to-standing height ratio (or the derived subischial leg length) is more informative than standing height alone in platyspondyly. A sitting height ratio below the 3rd percentile for age alongside normal or near-normal subischial leg length is a direct indicator of trunk-specific growth impairment. These measurements should be plotted longitudinally to track rate of change, not just absolute values.

Altered Spinal Biomechanics

Flattened vertebral bodies change how the spine distributes load across each motion segment. In a normal motion segment, the vertebral body bears approximately 80% of axial compressive load while the facet joints share the remaining 20%. Platyspondylic vertebrae alter this distribution in several ways:

  • Increased shear stress: A shorter vertebral body with a larger cross-sectional area relative to disc height changes the angle at which forces resolve across the endplate, increasing horizontal shear components
  • Altered facet loading: Changed load sharing shifts more force toward the facet joints, potentially accelerating facet arthrosis
  • Disc height-to-vertebral body height mismatch: When discs are relatively taller than vertebral bodies (which are compressed), the intervertebral joint moves closer to the facet plane, altering coupled motion patterns

Spinal Canal Narrowing

Platyspondyly can contribute to early-onset spinal stenosis through two mechanisms. First, the flattened vertebral body may narrow the spinal canal in the AP dimension if the posterior vertebral wall is displaced. Second, the altered biomechanics described above promote accelerated facet hypertrophy, ligamentum flavum thickening, and disc bulging — all of which encroach on canal space. Unlike the stenosis that develops in typical adults in their sixth or seventh decade, COL11A1-related stenosis can become symptomatic in the third or fourth decade due to the combination of structural narrowing and connective tissue degeneration.

Contribution to Kyphosis

When platyspondyly produces anterior wedging (the anterior height of the vertebral body is disproportionately reduced), each affected vertebra contributes a small kyphotic angle. Across multiple vertebral levels, these angles summate. In the thoracic spine, where normal kyphosis already exists, this summation can produce excessive kyphosis. In the lumbar spine, anterior wedging can reduce or eliminate the normal lordotic curve, flattening the lumbar spine and shifting the center of mass posteriorly. This interaction between platyspondyly and kyphosis is discussed in detail on the Scoliosis & Kyphosis page.

The DEXA Measurement Problem

This section addresses a clinically important but frequently overlooked consequence of platyspondyly: its effect on bone density measurement by standard dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry (DEXA).

How Standard Lumbar DEXA Works

The standard anteroposterior (AP) lumbar DEXA scan measures areal bone mineral density (aBMD), expressed in grams per centimeter squared (g/cm²). The scanner sums the bone mineral content (BMC, in grams) of each lumbar vertebral body and divides by the projected two-dimensional area of that vertebra as seen from above (in cm²). This is a two-dimensional measurement applied to a three-dimensional structure:

    aBMD (g/cm²) = BMC (g) ÷ Projected Area (cm²)

This formula works reasonably well when vertebral bodies have typical proportions. The issue arises when they do not.

Why Platyspondyly Distorts aBMD

A platyspondylic vertebral body is shorter (reduced height) but has roughly the same width and depth as a typical vertebra. From the scanner’s perspective looking down from above, the projected area is similar to or even larger than a typical vertebra. However, the volume of bone present in that vertebra is less — there is simply less vertebral body to scan because it is flattened.

The result is that the calculation divides a smaller-than-expected BMC by a normal-sized projected area, which should produce a low aBMD. But platyspondyly also compacts the bone mineral into a smaller vertical space, increasing the apparent density in that compressed volume. These effects interact in a counterintuitive direction: DEXA systematically overestimates bone mineral density when vertebral bodies are abnormally flat.

A “normal” DEXA result in a child with platyspondyly may be falsely reassuring. The Z-score comparison is made against a reference population with typical vertebral proportions. A patient whose aBMD appears at the 50th percentile for age may have genuinely low bone mineral content when the reduced vertebral volume is taken into account. Before concluding that bone density is adequate in a Marshall syndrome patient, the spine imaging findings must be reviewed and the measurement method evaluated for appropriateness.

Solutions: Volumetric BMD Measurement

Several approaches correct for the size artifact introduced by platyspondyly:

BMAD (Bone Mineral Apparent Density)

BMAD is a volumetric correction derived from standard DEXA data without requiring additional scans. The most commonly used formula estimates the volume of the vertebral body from its projected area and calculates a volumetric density:

    BMAD (g/cm³) = BMC ÷ (Projected Area)^(3/2)

The exponent of 3/2 is a geometric approximation that converts a 2D area measurement into an estimated 3D volume, under the assumption that vertebral body dimensions scale proportionally. In platyspondyly, this assumption is violated (height does not scale with area), so BMAD partially but incompletely corrects the bias. Published BMAD reference ranges exist for children and adolescents and should be used in preference to aBMD Z-scores when platyspondyly is present.

Lateral DXA

Lateral spine DEXA scans the vertebral body from the side rather than from above. The projected area in a lateral scan reflects vertebral body height (which is reduced in platyspondyly) rather than width, making the measurement less susceptible to the height-independent area artifact. However, lateral DXA has lower precision due to rib and soft tissue overlap at thoracic levels and is primarily applicable to the lumbar spine.

Quantitative CT (QCT)

QCT provides a true three-dimensional volumetric measurement of trabecular bone density within the vertebral body, expressed in mg/cm³ of calcium hydroxyapatite. Because QCT measures the actual volume of the region of interest rather than a projected area, it is not susceptible to the platyspondyly-related overestimation artifact. QCT is considered the reference standard for volumetric BMD in skeletal dysplasias. The main limitations are higher radiation dose than DEXA and less widespread availability of pediatric reference ranges.

Practical recommendation: When ordering bone density assessment in a Marshall syndrome patient known to have platyspondyly on imaging, request that the reporting clinician also calculate and report BMAD alongside aBMD. If QCT is available at the imaging center and radiation dose is acceptable (particularly relevant if serial measurements are planned), it provides the most accurate volumetric estimate. Document the presence of platyspondyly on the referral so the radiologist is aware of the confounding factor.

Growth Chart Implications

Standard height growth charts (such as CDC or WHO charts) compare a child’s standing height against a reference population with typical spinal anatomy. When platyspondyly reduces trunk height, standing height measurements underrepresent the child’s skeletal growth potential and make growth appear worse than it is relative to limb growth, or may obscure the disproportionate pattern if overall height happens to fall within a typical range.

Measurements That Provide More Information

Sitting Height

Sitting height (measured from seat surface to the top of the head with the child sitting upright) captures trunk and head height while excluding limb length. Platyspondyly reduces sitting height more than standing height. Serial sitting height measurements plotted against age-specific reference ranges can track the rate of trunk growth independently of limb growth.

Arm Span

Arm span (fingertip to fingertip with arms extended horizontally) is a proxy for expected standing height in the absence of spinal shortening, because upper limb length is determined by long bone growth plates rather than vertebral endplates. In a typical individual, arm span and standing height are within 2–3 cm of each other. In platyspondyly, arm span progressively exceeds standing height as the trunk fails to grow proportionately. A difference of >5 cm (arm span exceeding height) warrants evaluation; differences of 10 cm or more are seen in moderate platyspondyly.

Upper-to-Lower Segment Ratio

The upper segment (pubic symphysis to top of head, equivalent to sitting height) divided by the lower segment (pubic symphysis to floor, representing leg length) gives the upper-to-lower segment ratio. In a typical child beyond age 8, this ratio approaches 1.0. Platyspondyly increases the ratio if it disproportionately reduces the upper segment. However, if limb growth is also affected (as in some skeletal dysplasias), interpretation requires comparison with condition-specific charts rather than general population norms.

Condition-specific growth charts: General population growth charts were not designed for Marshall syndrome and may misclassify growth as either normal or severely impaired depending on the degree of platyspondyly and limb involvement. When available, height data from COL11A1 and Stickler syndrome cohorts provides more appropriate comparators. In their absence, the combination of sitting height, arm span, and upper-to-lower segment ratio gives a more complete picture of growth pattern than standing height alone.

Imaging Interpretation

What to Look For on Lateral Spine X-Rays

The lateral thoracolumbar spine X-ray is the primary imaging study for identifying and quantifying platyspondyly. Key features to assess include:

  • Vertebral body height-to-width ratio: Measured at each level as the anterior height divided by the AP depth. Values consistently below 0.75 across multiple levels define platyspondyly
  • Anterior vs. posterior height: If the anterior height is selectively reduced, anterior wedging is present and contributes to kyphosis
  • Endplate regularity: Normal endplates appear as smooth, slightly concave lines. In Marshall syndrome, endplates may be irregular, scalloped, or show step-off defects at the disc-vertebral junction
  • Schmorl’s nodes: Small indentations in the endplate where disc material has herniated into the vertebral body; commonly seen alongside platyspondyly and discussed further on the Schmorl’s Nodes page
  • Disc height: In platyspondyly, disc height may appear relatively preserved or increased compared to the flattened vertebral bodies, inverting the expected ratio

Progression During Growth

Platyspondyly in Marshall syndrome does not follow a simple linear course. The degree of flattening relative to expected vertebral body height may worsen during periods of rapid growth — particularly during the adolescent growth spurt — because the demand on growth plate chondrocytes is highest when growth velocity is greatest. During these periods, the gap between actual and expected vertebral body height can widen faster.

Radiographic monitoring during active growth should therefore be more frequent during the adolescent growth spurt (roughly ages 10–14 in girls, 12–16 in boys) than during slower growth phases. The specific monitoring interval depends on the severity of existing platyspondyly and the presence of associated kyphosis or scoliosis.

Stability in Adulthood

Once skeletal maturity is reached and the vertebral growth plates have fused, the degree of platyspondyly is generally stable. The shape of the vertebral bodies does not change further as a result of the COL11A1 mutation itself, though secondary degenerative changes (disc space narrowing, facet arthrosis, endplate sclerosis) can alter the radiographic appearance over time. The biomechanical consequences of platyspondyly — altered load distribution, stenosis risk — may become more clinically prominent in adulthood even as the primary shape change stabilizes.

Surgical Implications

When spinal surgery is required in a patient with platyspondyly — whether for kyphosis correction, scoliosis, or stenosis — the altered vertebral anatomy has direct implications for surgical planning and technique.

Pedicle Screw Placement

Pedicle screws are the primary fixation method in modern spinal instrumentation. In platyspondylic vertebrae, the altered body proportions affect pedicle anatomy:

  • Smaller pedicle heights: Because the vertebral body is flattened, the overall posterior element dimensions — including pedicle height — may be reduced, narrowing the safe corridor for screw placement
  • Altered entry point geometry: Standard entry point landmarks are defined relative to vertebral body landmarks that shift in platyspondyly
  • Different insertion trajectories: The reduced anterior-to-posterior depth of the vertebral body changes the safe screw length and trajectory angle

Preoperative CT scanning with reconstructions in all three planes is essential for surgical planning in platyspondylic spines, rather than relying on fluoroscopic landmarks alone. Intraoperative CT-based navigation or robotic guidance reduces the risk of pedicle breaches in atypical anatomy.

Kyphosis Correction

Correcting kyphosis that has developed on a foundation of platyspondyly requires accounting for vertebral body shape in the correction plan. Standard posterior-based correction techniques assume vertebral bodies of typical height; in platyspondyly, the vertebral bodies cannot be “restored” to a normal shape since the growth plate deficiency is intrinsic. The surgical goal shifts from normalization of vertebral body morphology to redistribution of regional alignment across the available disc and vertebral anatomy. Anterior column support (anterior interbody grafting or cage placement) may be particularly important when anterior height is severely deficient, to prevent correction loss as posterior instrumentation applies correction forces.

Spinal Fusion Considerations

The altered bone structure in platyspondylic vertebral bodies also affects fusion biology. The endplate cartilage abnormalities seen in Marshall syndrome (discussed in detail on the Endplate Calcification page) may affect how graft material integrates with the host endplate. Bone graft contact with irregular or calcified endplates may be less uniform than in typical anatomy, potentially affecting fusion rate. These considerations are not specific to platyspondyly alone but compound when platyspondyly, endplate irregularity, and connective tissue abnormalities are all present simultaneously.

For surgical consultation: When referring a Marshall syndrome patient for spinal surgery evaluation, include the most recent lateral spine X-rays and any available CT imaging, along with documentation of platyspondyly findings. Noting that the patient has a COL11A1-related connective tissue disorder — and therefore has abnormal collagen in ligaments, discs, and cartilage beyond just the vertebral bodies — gives the surgical team critical context for planning instrumentation and assessing fusion risk.
