TENS Practical Guide: Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation

Medical Disclaimer
This information is compiled from peer-reviewed research and clinical guidelines. It is intended for educational discussion with qualified medical professionals. These are NOT treatment recommendations. TENS protocols should be designed in consultation with a pain management specialist or physical therapist familiar with connective tissue disorders.

TENS is one of the most accessible, evidence-based, non-pharmacological pain management tools available. For Marshall syndrome, where pain is persistent, multi-origin, and often undertreated, TENS offers a drug-free method of modulating pain signals that can be used daily at home with minimal risk.

What Is TENS?

Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) delivers low-voltage electrical current through electrodes placed on the skin. The primary mechanism is explained by the gate control theory of pain, proposed by Melzack and Wall in 1965: stimulating large-diameter A-beta sensory fibers “closes the gate” in the dorsal horn of the spinal cord, inhibiting transmission of pain signals carried by smaller C-fibers and A-delta fibers to the brain.

In practice, TENS activates non-painful touch and vibration pathways that compete with and suppress pain signal transmission at the spinal cord level. The result is reduced pain perception without drugs, without sedation, and without systemic side effects.

Three Modes of TENS

  • Conventional (high-frequency) TENS: 50–120 Hz frequency, low intensity. Produces a comfortable tingling sensation. Pain relief begins within minutes but fades relatively quickly after the device is turned off (typically 30–60 minutes). Works primarily through segmental gate control.
  • Acupuncture-like (low-frequency) TENS: 2–10 Hz frequency, higher intensity (to the point of visible but tolerable muscle twitching). Stimulates release of endogenous opioids (endorphins, enkephalins) from the brainstem and spinal cord. Onset is slower (20–30 minutes) but post-stimulation relief lasts longer (2–4 hours).
  • Burst TENS: High-frequency bursts (typically 100 Hz) delivered in low-frequency packets (2–4 bursts per second). Combines gate control with endorphin release. Many patients find burst mode the most comfortable and effective option.
Key point: TENS is non-invasive, non-addictive, has no systemic side effects, and can be self-administered at home. It does not cure the underlying structural problems in Marshall syndrome, but it provides meaningful, repeatable pain relief that allows participation in physical therapy and daily activities.

Why TENS for Marshall Syndrome

Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutation) produces multiple categories of pain simultaneously, and TENS can address several of them:

  • Nociceptive pain from degenerating discs, joints, and connective tissue — TENS conventional mode activates gate control to suppress transmission of mechanical and inflammatory pain signals
  • Neuropathic pain from nerve root compression or irritation by disc material — TENS has moderate-quality evidence for neuropathic pain management, with best results from low-frequency or burst modes that trigger descending inhibitory pathways
  • Central sensitization — the nervous system “volume knob” gets turned up in chronic pain conditions (see Central Sensitization). Regular TENS use may help modulate central pain processing by repeatedly activating inhibitory circuits, though this effect requires consistent daily use rather than occasional application

The evidence base for TENS is strongest for chronic low back pain and osteoarthritic joint pain — both of which are core features of Marshall syndrome. A 2024 Cochrane review found moderate-certainty evidence that TENS provides clinically meaningful pain relief for chronic low back pain compared to sham stimulation.

Practical advantage: Unlike medications, TENS has no drug interactions, no hepatic or renal burden, and no ceiling dose. It can be used alongside every other intervention in the Marshall pain model without concern for pharmacological interactions.

Choosing a TENS Unit

Features to Look For

  • Adjustable frequency: Must allow both high-frequency (80–120 Hz) and low-frequency (2–10 Hz) modes. Fixed-frequency units are significantly less useful.
  • Adjustable pulse width: Range should include at least 50–250 μs. Wider pulse widths recruit more nerve fibers.
  • Multiple channels: At minimum 2 channels (4 electrodes). For treating multiple pain sites simultaneously (e.g., back + knee), 4 channels are ideal.
  • Burst mode: Not available on all units. Burst mode is particularly useful for Marshall syndrome’s chronic deep pain.
  • Rechargeable battery: Daily use makes disposable batteries impractical and expensive.
  • Timer function: Auto-shutoff after a set duration prevents falling asleep with the unit running.
  • Compact size: For use during school, car rides, or physical therapy sessions.

Consumer vs. Prescription Units

Over-the-counter consumer TENS units are widely available and range from $25–$80. These are adequate for many users. Prescription-grade units ($100–$400) offer wider parameter ranges, more channels, and are often covered by insurance with a physician’s order. For Marshall syndrome, where TENS is likely to become a long-term daily tool, a prescription unit with full parameter control is worth pursuing.

Kid-Friendly Considerations

  • Smaller electrode pads (2” × 2”) fit better on a child’s anatomy
  • Units with gentle ramp-up (gradual intensity increase) are less startling
  • Some units have child-lock features to prevent accidental intensity changes
  • Wireless electrode units eliminate lead wires that children may tug or catch on things

Electrode Placement Guide

Electrode placement determines which nerve pathways are stimulated. The general principle is to place electrodes so that the electrical current crosses the area of pain or the nerve pathway supplying that area.

Pain Location Electrode Positions Notes
Lower back Paravertebral, bilateral, L3–L5 (two electrodes on each side of the spine) Most common placement for Marshall syndrome. Covers the lumbar region where disc degeneration is most prevalent.
Upper back / thoracic Paravertebral, bilateral, T4–T8 For thoracic disc or rib-related pain. May help with the thoracic kyphosis pain common in Marshall syndrome.
Neck / cervical Paravertebral, bilateral, C5–C7 See cervical warning below.
Knee Two electrodes medial and two lateral to the patella, or one pair above and one pair below Effective for osteoarthritic knee pain. Use lower intensity than back placements.
Hip Over the greater trochanter (lateral hip) with a second pair posteriorly over the gluteal region Deeper joint — may require wider pulse width (200+ μs) and low-frequency mode for adequate penetration.
Shoulder Over the supraspinatus (top of shoulder) and deltoid (lateral shoulder) Useful for rotator cuff strain or shoulder instability pain.
Headache / suboccipital Base of skull, bilateral over suboccipital muscles (C1–C2 region) See cervical warning below. Effective for cervicogenic headaches.
Cervical Instability Warning
Marshall syndrome involves collagen XI deficiency affecting ligaments that stabilize the cervical spine, particularly the transverse ligament of the atlas (C1–C2). Before applying TENS to the neck or suboccipital region, ensure that cervical instability has been evaluated by your medical team. TENS itself does not cause instability, but the muscle relaxation it produces could theoretically reduce protective muscle guarding in an unstable cervical segment. Discuss cervical TENS placement with your child’s orthopedic specialist or physiatrist.
Absolute Placement Restrictions
NEVER place TENS electrodes:
  • Over the carotid sinus (front/side of neck near the jaw angle) — risk of blood pressure drop or cardiac arrhythmia
  • Across the chest (one electrode on front, one on back, or one on each side of the chest) — risk of cardiac interference
  • Over or near any implanted electrical device (pacemaker, defibrillator, spinal cord stimulator, intrathecal pump)
  • Over open wounds, infected skin, or areas of absent sensation
  • Directly over the eyes or through the head (temple-to-temple)

Settings and Protocols

Frequency Guide

Pain Type Frequency Mode Rationale
Acute flare / sharp pain 80–120 Hz Conventional Fast-acting gate control. Use at onset of flare for quickest relief.
Chronic dull / deep ache 2–10 Hz Acupuncture-like Endorphin release provides longer-lasting relief for persistent background pain.
Mixed / widespread pain 2–4 Hz burst at 100 Hz carrier Burst Combines both mechanisms. Often the best starting mode for Marshall syndrome.
Neuropathic / radiating 2–10 Hz Acupuncture-like Low-frequency stimulation better activates descending inhibitory pathways for nerve pain.

Pulse Width

Pulse width determines how many nerve fibers are recruited per electrical pulse:

  • 100–150 μs: Standard range for most conventional TENS applications. Comfortable, good for superficial pain.
  • 150–200 μs: Better for deeper structures (joints, deep muscles, paraspinal tissues). Recommended starting range for Marshall syndrome back pain.
  • 200–250 μs: Recruits the widest range of fibers. Use for deep hip or large-joint pain. May feel less comfortable; increase gradually.

Intensity

The correct intensity depends on the mode being used:

  • Conventional TENS: Increase until a clear, comfortable tingling is felt across the electrode area. There should be no muscle contraction and no pain. The sensation should be “strong but comfortable.”
  • Acupuncture-like TENS: Increase until gentle, rhythmic muscle twitching is visible. This is intentional — the higher intensity is needed to recruit A-delta fibers and trigger endorphin release. It should remain tolerable.
  • Burst TENS: Intensity similar to acupuncture-like mode. Mild rhythmic muscle contraction is expected.
Accommodation tip: During a session, the nervous system adapts and the sensation fades. This is normal. Increase intensity by 1–2 increments every 10–15 minutes to maintain the same perceived level. Do NOT start at maximum intensity.

Session Duration

  • Minimum effective session: 20 minutes (shorter sessions rarely produce meaningful analgesia)
  • Typical session: 30–45 minutes
  • Maximum recommended: 60 minutes per site (remove electrodes, rest skin for at least 1 hour before reapplying)
  • Daily frequency: 2–4 sessions per day is safe for daily long-term use. There is no evidence of harm from regular daily TENS use.

Pediatric Considerations

TENS is generally considered safe for children, though most clinical trials have studied adults. Practical considerations for pediatric use in Marshall syndrome:

Age Appropriateness

  • Under 4 years: Generally not recommended — children cannot reliably communicate whether the sensation is comfortable or painful
  • Ages 4–7: Can be used with direct adult supervision at all times. Start with very low intensity. Short sessions (15–20 minutes).
  • Ages 8–12: Can learn to self-apply with training. Adult should set initial parameters. Child can adjust intensity within a pre-set range.
  • Ages 13+: Can typically manage independently after initial training, with periodic check-ins on technique and electrode condition.

Skin Sensitivity

  • Children’s skin is thinner and more sensitive — always start at lower intensity than for adults
  • Check skin under electrodes after every session for redness or irritation
  • Use hypoallergenic electrode pads (latex-free, for sensitive skin)
  • Rotate electrode positions slightly between sessions to avoid localized skin irritation
  • If skin becomes red or irritated, skip TENS at that site for 24–48 hours

Making TENS Normal

  • Frame TENS as a “pain zapper” or “tingly helper” — something they control, not something done to them
  • Let the child control the intensity dial (within safe limits) to build a sense of agency
  • Pair TENS sessions with a preferred activity: reading, watching a show, drawing, or quiet play
  • Consistency matters more than duration — a daily 20-minute routine is better than sporadic 60-minute sessions
  • Keep a simple pain diary together so the child can see the pattern of what helps

Combining TENS with Other Treatments

TENS is most effective as part of a multimodal pain management strategy, not as a standalone treatment. Integration points for the Marshall syndrome protocol:

TENS + Physical Therapy

  • Before PT: A 20–30 minute TENS session before physical therapy can reduce baseline pain, allowing better exercise participation and range of motion. Use conventional (high-frequency) mode for rapid onset.
  • During PT: Some physical therapists apply TENS during exercises to reduce pain-related guarding and improve movement quality. Discuss with your PT.
  • After PT: If exercise triggers a flare, TENS can manage post-exercise soreness without reaching for rescue medications.

TENS + Heat or Cold

  • Heat packs applied before TENS increase skin conductivity and may improve electrode contact. Remove heat before applying electrodes.
  • Do NOT apply TENS and heat simultaneously in the same area — the combination can cause skin burns due to altered thermal sensation under TENS.
  • Cold packs can be used on adjacent areas during TENS without concern.

TENS + Exercise

  • Pre-exercise TENS can reduce pain enough to enable participation in therapeutic movement
  • For aquatic therapy, TENS must be removed before entering water (the device is not waterproof and water alters current distribution unpredictably)
  • Post-exercise TENS addresses the inflammatory pain response that often follows even gentle exercise in Marshall syndrome

Bedtime TENS and Sleep

Many children with Marshall syndrome experience alpha-delta sleep disruption — microarousals driven by pain intrusions during deep sleep. A TENS session in the 30–60 minutes before bed can reduce pain levels at sleep onset, potentially improving initial sleep quality. Use low-frequency (acupuncture-like) mode for longer-lasting post-session analgesia that extends into the early sleep period.

Protocol timing: See the Complete 24-Hour Protocol for how TENS fits into the overall daily schedule alongside supplements, exercise, photobiomodulation, and other interventions. Key windows: morning (before school), pre-PT, and pre-bedtime.

Limitations and When TENS Isn’t Enough

TENS is a pain modulation tool. It does not fix the underlying structural and molecular problems in Marshall syndrome. Understanding its boundaries prevents disappointment and ensures appropriate escalation.

What TENS Cannot Address

  • Structural instability: TENS cannot stabilize a hypermobile joint or a degenerated disc. If pain is primarily from mechanical instability, bracing, strengthening, or surgical consultation may be needed.
  • Cord or nerve root compression: If disc herniation is compressing the spinal cord or nerve roots, TENS may mask symptoms without addressing a potentially progressive neurological problem. New or worsening weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder changes require immediate medical evaluation regardless of TENS pain relief.
  • Severe central sensitization: In advanced central sensitization, the spinal cord and brainstem pain processing circuits are so disrupted that peripheral TENS input may be insufficient. TENS can be part of the solution but may need to be combined with pharmacological desensitization approaches (see Central Sensitization).
  • Inflammatory flares: TENS does not reduce inflammation. During active inflammatory episodes, anti-inflammatory interventions (pharmacological or otherwise) are needed alongside TENS.

When to Escalate

  • TENS no longer provides any perceptible pain relief despite parameter adjustments
  • Pain is progressively worsening despite consistent TENS use
  • New neurological symptoms appear (weakness, numbness, coordination changes, bowel/bladder changes)
  • Pain is preventing sleep despite bedtime TENS sessions
  • The child is requiring TENS for more than 4 hours per day to maintain function
TENS as one layer: In the Marshall syndrome pain model, TENS occupies the neuromodulation layer — it modulates how pain signals are processed. It works best when combined with the structural layer (exercise, PT, bracing), the molecular layer (supplements, medications), and the central processing layer (sleep optimization, stress reduction, central sensitization management). No single layer is sufficient alone.
