Flare Management & Pain Crisis Protocol

Before using any protocol on this page: Work with your pain specialist in advance to create a written, pre-authorized flare action plan. Medication decisions during a flare should never be improvised — they should execute a plan already agreed upon with your care team. This page provides a framework, not a prescription.

What Is a Flare?

A flare is a temporary, significant worsening of symptoms beyond a person’s established baseline. In Marshall syndrome, flares are not a sign of disease progression — they are a predictable feature of the condition that can be managed systematically once you understand their triggers and arc.

Key characteristics of a true flare:

  • Temporary: Flares have a beginning, a peak, and a resolution. If symptoms are escalating without any plateau, that requires evaluation — it may not be a flare
  • Beyond baseline: A flare means something has changed from the person’s usual daily symptom level. Baseline is individual — what matters is the delta, not the absolute severity
  • Typical duration: Most flares in Marshall syndrome last 2–7 days. Flares extending beyond 7 days warrant physician contact. Flares extending beyond 2 weeks suggest something else may be driving symptoms
  • Multisystem: Because Marshall syndrome affects connective tissue throughout the body, flares rarely involve only a single joint or region. Pain typically worsens diffusely, fatigue deepens, and autonomic symptoms (dizziness, temperature dysregulation) may amplify simultaneously

The First Hour Protocol

The first hour of a flare sets the trajectory. A systematic response during this window can meaningfully reduce peak severity and total duration. The following five steps should be executed in order.

Step 1: Assess — Flare or Red Flag?

Before doing anything else, determine whether this is a manageable flare or a situation requiring emergency care. This distinction is non-negotiable. See the Red Flags table immediately below.

Stop here and read the Red Flags section before proceeding with home management. Misidentifying a medical emergency as a flare is the most dangerous error in flare management.

Step 2: Position

Find a position that reduces mechanical load on the most affected area. Do not force a “correct” position — listen to the body. General principles:

  • Spinal pain: Semi-reclined with knees supported (reduces intradiscal pressure) is often preferable to both lying flat and sitting upright
  • Hip and knee pain: Elevation reduces swelling; avoid positions that lock joints in end-range
  • Cervical pain: Avoid unsupported head positions; a cervical pillow or roll under the neck may help; NEVER use traction without physician approval during a flare
  • Whole-body flare: Move slowly to find the position of least provocation; small positional adjustments every 20–30 minutes prevent stiffening

Step 3: Thermal Therapy

Thermal therapy is one of the most accessible and effective flare interventions when applied correctly. The decision between ice and heat is not arbitrary:

PresentationChoiceApplication
Acute inflammation: joint is swollen, warm to touch, recently injured or overusedIce / cold therapy15–20 minutes on, 20 minutes off; never directly on skin; do not use on areas with reduced sensation
Chronic stiffness: joint feels rigid, aching, better with movement once warmed upHeatMoist heat preferred (warm shower, heated pad with moisture layer); 15–20 minutes; avoid during acute swelling
Nerve pain: burning, shooting, electric sensationsEither, based on responseTry cool first; if it worsens burning, switch to gentle warmth; some individuals find contrast therapy (alternating) most effective for nerve symptoms
Diffuse whole-body flareWarm bath or showerBuoyancy reduces mechanical load simultaneously; water temperature should feel warm but not hot (hot water can worsen autonomic instability)

Step 4: Rescue Medications

Execute the pre-authorized plan established with your physician. Do not improvise. Key principles that should be part of any Marshall syndrome flare plan:

  • Timing matters more than dosing: Rescue medications work best when taken at the onset of a flare, not after pain has escalated. Waiting until pain is severe makes pharmacological intervention significantly less effective
  • NSAIDs vs acetaminophen: For acute musculoskeletal inflammation, NSAIDs generally outperform acetaminophen — but long-term or frequent NSAID use requires GI, kidney, and cardiovascular monitoring. Your plan should specify which to use and when
  • Topical options: Diclofenac gel applied to accessible joints provides local effect with minimal systemic exposure — valuable for reducing total systemic NSAID burden during multi-day flares
  • Opioids are rarely appropriate: Evidence from Stickler syndrome literature (the closest genetic relative to Marshall syndrome) explicitly notes that opioids are “often unhelpful for bone and muscle pain” in these populations. They also worsen autonomic instability. The pre-authorized flare plan should address this clearly to avoid ER opioid escalation
  • Adjunct options: Depending on the individual’s established regimen, adjuncts such as magnesium, low-dose muscle relaxants (pre-authorized), or gabapentinoids may be part of the flare plan — execute as prescribed, do not self-adjust
Keep a “flare kit” physically accessible: During a flare, the last thing a person in significant pain should need to do is search for medications, ice packs, or the written plan. A dedicated bag or box with all flare supplies — medications, ice pack, heat pad, written plan, emergency contacts — reduces the friction between flare onset and appropriate response.

Step 5: Document

Log the flare onset immediately, even briefly. Minimum information to capture:

  • Date and time of onset
  • Likely trigger(s) if identifiable
  • Pain severity (0–10) and primary location(s)
  • What was done in the first hour and how it helped or didn’t
  • Any red flag symptoms that were assessed and ruled out

Documentation is not bureaucracy — it is pattern recognition. After 10–20 logged flares, trigger patterns become visible that are invisible in the moment.

Days 1–3: Acute Phase Management

Once red flags are ruled out and the first-hour protocol is executed, the acute phase begins. The goal during Days 1–3 is to limit peak severity and prevent the accumulation of secondary consequences (deconditioning, sleep debt, central sensitization reinforcement) without causing harm through overintervention.

Activity Modification

Complete rest is not the goal. Bed rest during flares in connective tissue disorders worsens outcomes. The evidence consistently shows that gentle movement maintains tissue health, prevents muscle guarding that amplifies pain, and keeps proprioceptive circuits active. The goal is modified activity, not zero activity.
  • Eliminate high-impact, high-load, high-velocity activities: Running, jumping, heavy lifting, contact sports, and sustained static postures (including prolonged sitting or standing) should be stopped or drastically reduced
  • Maintain gentle movement: Short walks (even 5–10 minutes) if tolerable, gentle range-of-motion exercises, position changes every 20–30 minutes, supported stretching
  • Hydrotherapy: If accessible, warm water reduces gravitational load while allowing gentle movement — often the most tolerable form of movement during acute flares. See Aquatic Therapy for protocols
  • School and work: Modified attendance or remote work should be the norm during Days 1–3, not the exception. Forcing full-schedule participation through a peak flare increases both severity and duration. See the school communication section below

Medication Adjustment Protocols

Execute the pre-authorized flare plan. Do not improvise beyond it. Principles for the acute phase:

  • Scheduled dosing outperforms as-needed dosing during a flare: Once a flare is established, waiting for pain to spike before taking medication allows wind-up. Scheduled dosing (within safety limits) maintains a more stable blood level and more consistent effect
  • Duration limits: Even pre-authorized medications have duration limits — most NSAIDs should not be used at maximum dose continuously for more than 5–7 days without physician re-evaluation
  • Track medication use in the flare log: If more medication is needed than the plan anticipated, that is information the physician needs to revise the plan — not a signal to independently escalate

TENS for Acute Pain

Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation (TENS) is a valuable non-pharmacological tool during acute flares. It can be used in parallel with medication and does not interact with most drugs. See TENS Guide for full protocol. During a flare:

  • Higher-frequency TENS (80–150 Hz) is generally preferred for acute pain — activates gate control mechanisms more rapidly
  • Apply to the region of peak pain, not on inflamed or swollen skin
  • 20–30 minute sessions, 2–4 times per day during acute phase
  • Do not use over the cervical spine without physician guidance — especially relevant in Marshall syndrome given the known risk of cord involvement at that level

Sleep Optimization During Flares

Sleep is impaired during flares and poor sleep worsens flares — a vicious cycle. Practical measures:

  • Positioning: Side-lying with a pillow between knees reduces spinal load; a body pillow provides stability without forcing a fixed position throughout the night
  • Timing rescue medication: Taking a scheduled dose approximately 30 minutes before bedtime ensures therapeutic levels during the first deep sleep cycles, when pain most often disrupts sleep
  • Temperature: A slightly cool room (18–20°C / 64–68°F) with appropriate bedding is generally better than a warm room, which can worsen autonomic instability and inflammation
  • Weighted blankets: Some individuals find that gentle proprioceptive input from a weighted blanket reduces the heightened nervous system arousal that makes sleep difficult during flares
  • Screen curfew: Blue light suppresses melatonin, which is already disrupted in chronic pain populations; a 60-minute screen-free wind-down before bed is worth enforcing even during flares

Hydration and Anti-Inflammatory Nutrition

Flares are not the time to experiment with dietary changes. The goals are simple and achievable:

  • Hydration: Adequate hydration is essential for joint fluid, intervertebral disc health, and autonomic function. Target 8–10 glasses of water per day; more if fever is present or the individual exercises
  • Minimize pro-inflammatory foods: Ultra-processed foods, high-sugar foods, alcohol, and high-histamine foods (fermented products, aged cheeses, processed meats) may amplify the flare. This is not the time to go out for pizza
  • Anti-inflammatory foods: Omega-3 rich foods (fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed), colorful vegetables, and foods high in polyphenols (berries, green tea) support resolution without any risk
  • Magnesium: If magnesium is part of the individual’s supplement regimen, maintain it during flares — magnesium plays a direct role in NMDA receptor modulation and muscle relaxation

School and Work Communication

When to stay home vs. modify participation:

  • Stay home (Days 1–2, severe flare): When pain is severe enough that sitting for 30+ minutes is not tolerable, concentration is significantly impaired, or rescue medications cause sedation that makes learning or work unsafe
  • Modified participation (Days 2–3, moderate flare): Attend with accommodations — rest breaks, reduced writing requirements, ability to move, positioning support, reduced PE or physical activity
  • The written flare plan should include school communication language: Nurses and teachers cannot be expected to understand the complexity of Marshall syndrome flares. A brief, plain-language section of the flare plan explaining what a flare is, what accommodations help, and who to contact removes the burden from the child of explaining themselves while in pain

Days 3–7: Recovery Phase

The recovery phase begins when pain has peaked and is trending downward. The goal is controlled, gradual return to baseline — not rushing back to full activity.

The 50% Rule

The 50% Rule: On the first day of clearly improved symptoms, resume at approximately 50% of pre-flare activity level. If that goes well, increase by 10–20% per day. Do not return to full activity until you have maintained 80%+ activity for two consecutive days without symptom increase.

Rationale: Connective tissue that has just undergone a flare is not at full tensile strength. The inflammatory process that caused the flare leaves tissue temporarily more vulnerable. Returning to full load too quickly is the primary driver of rapid re-flare — a pattern that, over repeated cycles, contributes to the deconditioning spiral described in The Deconditioning Spiral.

Monitoring for Incomplete Recovery

Most flares resolve within 7 days. When they do not, or when recovery is incomplete, it matters:

  • Incomplete recovery: A person returns to approximately 80% of their pre-flare baseline but never quite gets back to where they were. Over multiple flares, baseline gradually erodes. This is a clinical signal — it may indicate structural progression, central sensitization entrenchment, or undertreated contributing factors
  • Rapid re-flare: Returning to full activity triggers another flare within days. This is a pacing failure, not a disease progression signal — but the 50% rule was not followed
  • Non-resolving flare: Still at acute severity at Day 7. Contact your physician — see the When to Call section below

When to Contact Your Physician During Recovery

  • Flare still at peak severity at 72 hours with no response to rescue medications
  • Still unable to tolerate any activity at Day 5
  • Flare duration exceeding 7 days
  • New symptoms that were not present at flare onset
  • Needing to use rescue medications at maximum dose for more than 5 consecutive days
  • Any red flag symptom at any point during the flare or recovery
  • Baseline after recovery is notably lower than before the flare

Preventing the Next Flare

Flare prevention is not about eliminating all risk — that is not achievable in Marshall syndrome. The goal is reducing flare frequency, shortening duration when they occur, and maintaining a baseline that does not erode over time.

The Flare Diary

Pattern recognition across multiple flares is not possible from memory alone. A structured flare diary captures the data needed to identify individual triggers. Essential fields:

  • Flare onset date, peak date, resolution date
  • Suspected trigger(s) with confidence level (certain / probable / possible)
  • Weather conditions at onset (if tracking barometric pressure, include readings)
  • Activity level in the 24–48 hours before onset
  • Sleep quality in the 48 hours before onset
  • Any illness, stress events, or hormonal events in the week before onset
  • What worked and what did not in management

After 10–20 logged flares, patterns typically emerge that are invisible in the moment. Many families discover that triggers cluster (e.g., weather change + poor sleep = almost certain flare, but either alone is tolerable) — this composite trigger understanding allows more targeted prevention.

Pacing: Activity Budgeting

The boom-bust cycle is the most common driver of flare frequency in chronic pain conditions. It works like this: good days lead to over-activity, which triggers a flare, which forces rest and deconditioning, which creates a new good day that the person then over-exploits again. The cycle is self-reinforcing.

  • Identify the daily activity ceiling: The maximum activity level that can be sustained across multiple days without triggering a flare. This is lower than what is possible on a good day
  • Bank energy on good days: Stop before reaching the ceiling, even when it feels possible to do more. Reserve capacity is what prevents flares, not maximizing daily output
  • Plan for recovery after high-demand events: Appointments, school events, travel, and emotionally demanding situations all cost physiological resources. Build recovery time into the schedule proactively, not reactively
Pacing is a skill, not a personality trait. It is learned, and it feels counterintuitive — especially for children and adolescents who want to participate fully. The most useful reframe: pacing is what makes more total participation possible over time, not less.

Weather Awareness

Barometric pressure drops are a well-documented trigger in musculoskeletal pain conditions. Practical implementation:

  • Apps such as WeatherX or standard weather apps that show barometric pressure trends can provide 24–48 hours of warning before a significant pressure drop
  • When a drop is forecast: increase rest, avoid scheduling high-demand activities, ensure rescue medications are accessible, and consider a proactive single NSAID dose if the physician-approved plan includes this
  • Correlate pressure readings with the flare diary to establish individual sensitivity thresholds — not everyone responds to the same magnitude of pressure change

Sleep Consistency as Prevention

Sleep is not passive recovery — it is the primary period during which pain-modulating systems restore. Consistent sleep timing (same bedtime and wake time, including weekends) maintains the circadian rhythm that governs pain threshold regulation. A single night of significantly disrupted sleep measurably lowers pain thresholds the following day.

Pre-emptive Management Before Known Triggers

When a trigger is predictable, pre-emptive management is appropriate. This requires physician involvement to pre-authorize:

  • Growth spurts: Typically identifiable from growth charts — periods of rapid height gain signal vulnerability. Reduce load, increase recovery, consider prophylactic activity modifications for the duration
  • Travel: Long-distance travel involves prolonged sitting, disrupted sleep, irregular meals, and often significant activity demands upon arrival. Pre-travel planning should include all of these variables
  • Stressful events: Exams, performances, family events with high emotional and physical demands. Reducing other stressors in the surrounding days and ensuring adequate sleep are the primary tools
  • Illness exposure: When a household member is ill, increased hand hygiene and rest prioritization may reduce both the likelihood and severity of illness-triggered flares

When to Call the Doctor vs. Go to the ER

Go to the Emergency Department immediately for:
  • Worst headache of the person’s life — sudden onset or rapidly escalating
  • Acute vision loss, new floaters, flashing lights, or visual field loss
  • New weakness, numbness, or loss of coordination in arms or legs
  • New inability to walk
  • Loss of or new difficulty with bladder or bowel control
  • Chest pain with palpitations or rapid heart rate not explained by anxiety
  • Fever with significant joint swelling and warmth
  • Any symptom that feels qualitatively different from any previous experience — trust that instinct
Call your physician (non-emergency) for:
  • Flare lasting more than 7 days without clear improvement
  • New symptom pattern not seen in previous flares
  • Rescue medications not controlling pain to a manageable level
  • Requiring maximum rescue medication doses for more than 5 consecutive days
  • Post-flare baseline noticeably lower than pre-flare baseline
  • More than 2 flares in a 4-week period
  • Suspicion that a new structural change (disc herniation, joint instability) may have occurred during the flare

Creating a Pre-Authorized Flare Plan

A pre-authorized flare plan is a written document created in advance, during a non-flare appointment, in collaboration with the pain specialist or managing physician. Its purpose is to remove decision-making from the moment of crisis — when pain impairs judgment, when emergency rooms may be unfamiliar with Marshall syndrome, and when the instinct to escalate or under-treat is highest.

What the Plan Must Include

  • Rescue medication list: Exact medications, doses, maximum frequency, and duration limits. Explicitly include what the physician authorizes and what is off-limits
  • Activity modification protocol: What to stop, what to maintain, and the return-to-activity framework (the 50% rule or equivalent)
  • Escalation criteria: The specific conditions under which the family should call the physician vs. go to the ER. These should match the tables above but be individualized to the patient’s specific history
  • Emergency red flag list: A condensed, clear version of the red flags table suitable for reading quickly during a crisis
  • Physician and specialist contact information: Including after-hours line
  • Diagnosis summary: For use at emergency departments that are unfamiliar with Marshall syndrome. Should include: diagnosis, relevant history (cervical cord involvement, retinal status, cardiac status), and a clear statement of what has and has not been evaluated structurally

Who Should Have a Copy

  • The patient (if old enough to self-manage any aspect) and both parents or guardians
  • School nurse — for flares that occur during the school day
  • Any regular caregivers, coaches, or adults who are frequently with the patient
  • The patient’s wallet or phone (a photographed summary card)
  • The emergency department — bring a physical copy to any ER visit
Review and update the plan annually, or after any significant change in condition, new structural finding (imaging), new medications, or a flare that the plan failed to manage adequately. A static plan becomes outdated as the patient grows, symptoms evolve, and the evidence base advances.
