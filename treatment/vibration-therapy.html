Whole-Body Vibration Therapy

Treatment research synthesis. This page describes therapeutic mechanisms based on published research. Evidence levels vary — see badges on each section. No treatment on this page is specifically approved for Marshall syndrome. Discuss with your child's medical team.

Whole-body vibration (WBV) is a non-invasive therapy where the patient stands on a vibrating platform. It has been shown to improve bone density, muscle strength, and balance in children with various musculoskeletal conditions. For Marshall syndrome, it represents a low-effort, high-compliance adjunct to physical therapy.

Evidence for Safety in Children Supported

The VIBE study (Vibration Intervention and Body composition in childhood) established the safety profile of WBV in pediatric populations. The study demonstrated improvements in bone mass and muscle function without adverse effects in growing children.

Recommended Protocol Supported

Frequency30–37 Hz

Platform typeSide-alternating (NOT synchronous vertical — side-alternating is gentler on the spine)
Duration10–20 minutes per session
Frequency of sessionsTwice daily
Minimum duration6+ months for measurable bone density effects

Marshall-Specific Considerations Hypothesis

Ophthalmologic clearance required. Given the retinal detachment risk in Marshall syndrome, obtain clearance from the vitreoretinal specialist before starting WBV. The vibration forces are low-magnitude, but any therapy involving repetitive mechanical loading should be reviewed in the context of vitreoretinal fragility.
  • Start at the lower end of frequency and duration (30 Hz, 10 min) and progress gradually
  • Monitor for any visual symptoms (flashes, floaters, curtain-like shadows) during and after sessions
  • Use a side-alternating platform — synchronous vertical platforms deliver higher peak forces to the spine
  • WBV can be done at home once a platform is obtained, making it a high-compliance option for children who may resist traditional exercise
