Complementary & Manual Therapies: An Evidence-Based Evaluation

Medical Disclaimer
This page is compiled from peer-reviewed research and clinical expert consensus for educational purposes only. It is NOT treatment advice. All decisions about complementary therapies must be made with a physician or specialist who knows your specific case. Safety ratings on this page reflect general risk in connective tissue disorders — individual circumstances may differ.

Marshall syndrome presents a distinctive challenge for complementary and manual therapies. Connective tissue fragility, cervical instability (CCI), hypermobile joints, thin or bruisable skin, and early disc disease create a risk profile that makes some widely-used therapies not merely unhelpful but actively dangerous. At the same time, several gentler modalities offer genuine benefit for the chronic musculoskeletal pain, muscle guarding, and autonomic dysfunction that characterize the condition.

This page evaluates each therapy on three axes:

  • Evidence quality — how well-supported the therapy is for relevant outcomes
  • Safety in connective tissue disorders — specific risks given Marshall syndrome pathology
  • Practical accessibility — how easy it is to find qualified practitioners and use the therapy regularly

Massage Therapy ● GREEN — with precautions

Evidence Summary

Massage therapy has moderate evidence for chronic musculoskeletal pain and good evidence for reducing muscle tension and guarding. Multiple systematic reviews support its use for non-specific low back pain and neck pain. Evidence is more limited for underlying connective tissue conditions specifically, but the indirect benefits — reduced muscle guarding, improved circulation, pain gate activation — are relevant and plausible in Marshall syndrome.

Pain Gate Mechanism
Massage activates large-diameter A-beta sensory fibers, which inhibit transmission of pain signals through small-diameter C fibers at the dorsal horn — the same “gate control” mechanism exploited by TENS. This peripheral inhibitory effect is immediate and does not require any systemic drug.

Benefits Specific to Marshall Syndrome

  • Muscle guarding: Chronic pain in Marshall syndrome drives paraspinal and peripheral muscle hypertonicity. Massage directly reduces this guarding, which can otherwise worsen postural mechanics and disc loading.
  • Circulation: Connective tissue fragility can impair local tissue perfusion. Massage mechanically improves lymphatic drainage and local blood flow.
  • Autonomic regulation: Regular massage has been shown to shift autonomic tone toward parasympathetic dominance, which is particularly relevant for the dysautonomia component of Marshall syndrome.
  • Sleep quality: Reduced muscle tension before bed improves sleep onset and quality — important given the bidirectional relationship between poor sleep and pain amplification in connective tissue disorders.

Precautions

Technique Restrictions
  • No deep tissue massage on hypermobile joints. Aggressive techniques on already-lax capsular and ligamentous structures can destabilize joints and cause micro-tears.
  • No aggressive cervical massage. Cervical instability in Marshall syndrome means even forceful soft-tissue work in the neck can be uncomfortable or provoke symptoms. Lighter, sustained pressure is appropriate; rapid or forceful techniques are not.
  • Bruising tendency: Inform your therapist explicitly. Lighter pressure is required. Deep effleurage and friction techniques that are routine in general massage may cause significant bruising in Marshall syndrome.
  • Skin fragility: Alert the therapist to avoid dragging on thin or fragile skin. Use of appropriate massage medium (oil or lotion) reduces friction.

What to Tell Your Massage Therapist

Use this script at the start of your first session and whenever you see a new therapist:

“I have a connective tissue disorder that affects collagen throughout my body. My joints are hypermobile — they move farther than normal joints — and my tissues are more fragile than they appear. I bruise easily. I need you to use lighter pressure than you would for a typical client, especially around my neck and any joints. Please avoid any stretching techniques that take my joints to their end range, and avoid deep tissue work. Swedish and relaxation techniques are fine. If something feels wrong at any point, I will tell you immediately, and I need you to stop.”

Frequency and Session Structure

  • Chronic pain management: Weekly or biweekly sessions are appropriate for ongoing symptom management. Monthly sessions may suffice during lower-symptom periods.
  • Session length: 45–60 minutes for adults. Longer is not necessarily better — cumulative pressure over a longer session may increase soreness.
  • Timing: Schedule sessions on days when you do not have other physically demanding activities. Post-massage tissue sensitivity typically resolves within 12–24 hours.

Pediatric Considerations

  • Massage is safe and beneficial in children when performed by a therapist trained in pediatric massage.
  • Use lighter pressure than for adults — children’s tissues are more responsive.
  • Shorter sessions: 20–30 minutes for younger children, increasing with age.
  • The child should have some control over the session — they should be able to say “stop” or “lighter” at any time and be taken seriously.
  • Parental education: parents should understand what the therapist is doing and why. They may wish to be present.
Finding a Qualified Therapist
Look for a licensed massage therapist (LMT) with experience in hypermobility, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, or connective tissue disorders. EDS-trained therapists understand tissue fragility and will not need extensive re-education about precautions. Therapists affiliated with physical therapy practices are often more medically oriented than spa-based therapists.

Acupuncture ● GREEN — with precautions

Evidence Summary

Acupuncture has moderate-to-good evidence for chronic pain conditions, with the most consistent findings in musculoskeletal pain (low back, neck, shoulder, knee), headache, and migraine prevention. There is also some evidence for neuropathic pain, which is directly relevant to the disc-related radiculopathy and small fiber neuropathy seen in Marshall syndrome. A large individual patient data meta-analysis (Acupuncture Trialists Collaboration, 2018) demonstrated that acupuncture is significantly more effective than sham acupuncture and usual care for chronic musculoskeletal pain.

Mechanisms Relevant to Marshall Syndrome

  • Endorphin release: Needling stimulates release of endogenous opioids (endorphins, enkephalins, dynorphins) at local and central levels. This contributes to post-session pain relief that can persist for hours to days.
  • Descending inhibitory pathway activation: Acupuncture activates the periaqueductal gray (PAG) and rostral ventromedial medulla (RVM) — the same descending pain inhibitory system implicated in central sensitization. This makes acupuncture particularly relevant for the central sensitization component of Marshall syndrome pain.
  • Local anti-inflammatory effects: Needling triggers a mild local inflammatory response that, paradoxically, resolves with net anti-inflammatory effects — similar to the hormetic mechanism of exercise. Locally, this can reduce cytokine-driven pain sensitization.
  • Autonomic modulation: Evidence from heart rate variability studies shows acupuncture increases parasympathetic tone, relevant for the dysautonomia component of Marshall syndrome.
Evidence Note
The evidence base for acupuncture has improved substantially in the past decade as blinding methodology (sham needles) has improved. Current high-quality trials support real acupuncture over sham for chronic musculoskeletal pain. Effect sizes are modest but clinically meaningful — comparable to NSAIDs for chronic conditions, without systemic side effects.

Precautions in Marshall Syndrome

  • Bruising risk: Thin-walled capillaries in connective tissue disorders increase bruising at needle insertion sites. Request that your acupuncturist use thinner gauge needles (0.16–0.18 mm vs. the standard 0.20–0.25 mm). Apply gentle pressure to each site after needle removal.
  • Skin fragility: Avoid needling over areas with particularly thin or fragile skin. Alert the practitioner before treatment begins.
  • Electroacupuncture near hearing aids: If electroacupuncture (needles connected to low-level electrical current) is used, inform the practitioner of any hearing aids, cochlear implants, or other electronic devices. Electrical current should not be applied near electronic hearing devices.
  • Cervical needling: If needling is planned for cervical points (common for headache treatment), inform the acupuncturist about cervical instability. Deep needling of cervical musculature near the spine should be approached cautiously.

Pediatric Considerations

  • Acupuncture is generally well-tolerated in children aged 7 and older. Studies in pediatric chronic pain have shown good outcomes with appropriate technique adaptation.
  • Needle-free alternatives for younger children or needle-averse patients:
    • Acupressure: Manual pressure at acupoints — no needles, safe, can be taught to parents for home use
    • Laser acupuncture (photobiomodulation at acupoints): Low-level laser applied to acupoints. Acceptable evidence for pediatric pain. Completely painless.
    • Shonishin: Japanese pediatric acupuncture using non-penetrating instruments for children under 7
  • Sessions for children should be shorter (20–30 minutes), fewer needles, and lighter stimulation.
Finding a Qualified Practitioner
In the United States, look for a Licensed Acupuncturist (L.Ac.) with a Master’s or Doctoral degree from an accredited program (NCCAOM certification). Physicians with acupuncture training (MD, DO) or physical therapists with dry needling certification are alternatives. Ask specifically about experience with hypermobility or connective tissue conditions.

Chiropractic Cervical Manipulation ● RED — CONTRAINDICATED

CRITICAL SAFETY WARNING: Do Not Allow Cervical Manipulation

High-velocity, low-amplitude (HVLA) cervical manipulation — the neck “cracking” technique performed by chiropractors — is contraindicated in Marshall syndrome. This is not a theoretical concern. The specific pathology of Marshall syndrome creates conditions under which cervical manipulation can cause catastrophic, irreversible neurological injury or death.

This includes techniques marketed as “gentle,” “low-force,” or “instrument-assisted” HVLA applied to the cervical spine.

Why Cervical Manipulation is Dangerous in Marshall Syndrome

Marshall syndrome creates three overlapping vulnerabilities in the cervical spine that make manipulation profoundly dangerous:

  1. Craniocervical Instability (CCI)
    Ligamentous laxity at the craniocervical junction (occiput-C1-C2) is common in Marshall syndrome. The alar and transverse ligaments, which normally constrain atlantoaxial rotation and limit odontoid excursion, are made of collagen and are directly affected by COL11A1/COL11A2 mutations. CCI means that the odontoid process of C2 can impinge on the brainstem or spinal cord during forced motion. A HVLA thrust applies exactly this kind of forced motion — rapidly and without the person’s ability to guard against it.
  2. Cervical Myelopathy and Disc Disease
    Early, severe cervical disc degeneration is a hallmark of Marshall syndrome. Multi-level disc herniation and osteophyte formation can produce cervical myelopathy (spinal cord compression). A forced HVLA technique on a myelopathic cervical spine can acutely worsen cord compression, producing tetraplegia, Brown-Séquard syndrome, or acute cord injury.
  3. Vertebral Artery Vulnerability
    The vertebral arteries travel through the transverse foramina of C1–C6 and are vulnerable to stretch or compression during rotation and extension. Connective tissue abnormalities affecting arterial wall integrity, combined with CCI, increase the risk of vertebral artery dissection during cervical manipulation. Vertebral artery dissection can cause posterior circulation ischemic stroke, affecting the brainstem and cerebellum — potentially fatal.
There Is No “Gentle” Exception
When a chiropractor says they use “gentle” techniques, “low force,” or “instrument-adjusting,” this does not eliminate the risks above. The Activator instrument, drop table techniques, and “upper cervical specific” techniques all apply force to an already-unstable cervical spine. The risks of vertebral artery dissection and cord injury are not proportional to the force applied — they are proportional to the instability of the structures being manipulated. In Marshall syndrome, that instability is structural and cannot be assessed by feeling alone.

Thoracic and Lumbar Chiropractic

● YELLOW — Chiropractic manipulation applied to the thoracic and lumbar spine is less immediately dangerous than cervical manipulation, but remains concerning in Marshall syndrome for two reasons:

  • Hypermobility: HVLA thrusts on hypermobile segments do not address the underlying instability and may transiently increase it. The short-term pain relief some patients experience is not evidence of therapeutic benefit — it may reflect capsular stretch or endorphin release, not structural correction.
  • Disc disease: Marshall syndrome causes early, severe lumbar disc degeneration. Forced spinal manipulation with existing disc herniation or significant degeneration carries risk of acute disc injury or nerve root impingement.
Better Alternative: Physical Therapist with Manual Therapy Training
Physical therapists trained in manual therapy (look for FAAOMPT certification or similar) can provide skilled hands-on treatment of the spine using techniques that are adapted to the patient’s pathology. Unlike chiropractors, PTs with manual therapy training operate within a broader rehabilitation framework, screen for contraindications, and are trained not to apply HVLA to unstable segments. This is the preferred route to spinal manual therapy in Marshall syndrome.

Craniosacral Therapy ● YELLOW

Evidence Summary

Craniosacral therapy (CST) is one of the most controversial manual therapies from an evidence standpoint. Its theoretical basis — that a distinct “craniosacral rhythm” can be palpated and therapeutically modulated — lacks reliable scientific support. Inter-rater reliability studies for palpation of the craniosacral rhythm are poor. There are no well-controlled randomized trials of CST in connective tissue disorders.

There are, however, some positive findings in small trials for headache management, chronic neck pain, and infant colic. These may reflect non-specific effects of gentle touch, therapeutic attention, and relaxation rather than any craniosacral mechanism specifically.

Evidence Review Update (R506): Craniosacral Therapy — What the Literature Actually Shows

A 2025 systematic review of craniosacral therapy evidence evaluated 18 RCTs and 12 observational studies across musculoskeletal pain, headache, and pediatric conditions. Key findings relevant to Marshall syndrome:

  • Inter-rater reliability remains poor: When multiple CST practitioners independently assessed the same patients, their findings of “craniosacral rhythm” rate, amplitude, and quality showed agreement no better than chance (kappa < 0.2). This means different practitioners palpating the same patient reach different conclusions about what they are feeling — undermining the theoretical basis of the therapy
  • Positive outcomes likely reflect non-specific effects: Studies showing benefit from CST consistently fail to demonstrate superiority over sham CST (light touch at non-craniosacral points). This suggests that the benefits patients experience — which are real — come from prolonged gentle touch, therapeutic attention, relaxation response activation, and expectations, rather than from any craniosacral mechanism specifically
  • Chronic pain: Low-quality evidence of modest benefit for chronic neck pain and tension headache, comparable to other relaxation-based interventions. No superiority over massage therapy or structured relaxation
  • Pediatric conditions: Very limited evidence. Small, unblinded studies in infant colic and pediatric headache show some positive results, but methodological quality is insufficient to draw conclusions

Bottom line for Marshall syndrome: CST is unlikely to harm (see safety profile below), but the evidence does not support specific therapeutic claims. If a patient or family finds CST subjectively helpful, the benefit likely comes from the relaxation and touch components — which are genuine therapeutic mechanisms, even if they are not the mechanisms CST claims to use.

Safety Profile

CST uses extremely gentle, non-forceful touch — pressures of 5 grams or less are typically cited. For most Marshall syndrome patients, this means CST is unlikely to cause physical harm. It does not involve joint manipulation, high-velocity thrusts, or tissue loading that could destabilize hypermobile joints.

CRITICAL: Cervical Manipulation During CST Is Contraindicated (R506)
Some CST practitioners incorporate cervical spine mobilization or “release” techniques as part of their craniosacral work — including sustained pressure, traction, or positional techniques applied to the upper cervical spine (occiput, C1, C2). In Marshall syndrome, this is contraindicated regardless of how gentle the practitioner claims to be. The craniocervical instability (CCI), vertebral artery vulnerability, and cervical myelopathy risks that make chiropractic cervical manipulation RED-rated apply equally to any manual technique that applies force to the upper cervical spine — including CST cervical “releases.” Before beginning CST, explicitly instruct the practitioner: “Do not apply any technique to my neck or upper cervical area.”

Additional caveats:

  • CST should not be used as a substitute for evidence-based medical management.
  • Verify that your CST practitioner has formal training (Upledger Institute or equivalent) — the technique is taught by many insufficiently trained practitioners.
  • Be wary of CST practitioners who claim to treat or cure serious medical conditions, diagnose pathology through palpation, or recommend stopping prescribed medications. These are red flags for an unsafe practitioner.

Practical Guidance

CST is worth considering in two situations: (1) when other physical therapies have not provided adequate headache or pain relief and a trial of something different is warranted, or (2) when the patient benefits from the parasympathetic-activating effects of calm, prolonged, low-stimulation bodywork. Given its low physical risk and potential for autonomic regulation effects, it is reasonable to try — with realistic expectations about the limited evidence base.

Osteopathic Manipulative Treatment (OMT) ● GREEN — with precautions

Why OMT Differs from Chiropractic

Osteopathic physicians (DOs) in the United States complete the same medical school curriculum as MDs, with additional training in osteopathic manipulative medicine. This is a fundamental difference from chiropractors: a DO has full medical training, prescribing authority, and a complete differential diagnostic framework. When a DO recommends OMT, they do so with knowledge of the underlying pathology — which means they understand when HVLA is contraindicated.

Not All DOs Perform OMT
Many DOs in primary care or specialty practice do not regularly perform OMT. To access OMT, look specifically for a DO who lists neuromusculoskeletal medicine (NMM) or osteopathic manipulative medicine (OMM) among their specialties, or ask your referring physician for a recommendation.

Appropriate Techniques in Marshall Syndrome

  • Muscle energy technique (MET): The patient generates a gentle isometric contraction against the practitioner’s counterforce, followed by relaxation into a new position. Highly appropriate in Marshall syndrome — no external force is applied; the patient’s own muscle activity drives the technique.
  • Myofascial release: Sustained gentle traction and pressure on fascia. Appropriate for reducing muscle guarding and improving regional mobility. Does not involve joint manipulation.
  • Counterstrain (positional release): The practitioner positions the body in a direction of comfort to release a tender point. Extremely gentle and passive — appropriate in Marshall syndrome.
  • Balanced ligamentous tension (BLT) / indirect techniques: The affected joint is guided to a position of ease to allow intrinsic correction. No external force beyond positioning. Low risk.

Technique to Avoid

Avoid HVLA on the Cervical Spine
High-velocity low-amplitude (HVLA) thrusting techniques carry the same risks on the cervical spine whether performed by a DO or a chiropractor. The underlying pathology — CCI, vertebral artery vulnerability, cervical myelopathy — does not respect the degree of the practitioner. When seeing a DO for OMT, explicitly discuss your cervical instability and request that HVLA not be applied to the cervical spine. A good osteopathic physician will agree without hesitation; there are effective alternatives.

Yoga & Tai Chi ● GREEN — modified

Evidence Summary

Both yoga and tai chi have good evidence for chronic musculoskeletal pain, balance improvement, and psychological wellbeing. Yoga has been studied extensively for low back pain, with multiple RCTs and meta-analyses showing benefit comparable to physical therapy for chronic non-specific low back pain. Tai chi has strong evidence for balance and fall prevention, and good evidence for pain and quality of life in chronic pain conditions including fibromyalgia and osteoarthritis.

The Hypermobility Problem with Yoga

Standard yoga is not appropriate for Marshall syndrome without modification. The emphasis on flexibility and “opening” joints is directly contraindicated. Many people with hypermobility are initially attracted to yoga precisely because they are naturally flexible — and this is a trap. End-range stretching in hypermobile individuals does not produce the same adaptive response as in normally-mobile individuals; instead, it further destabilizes already-lax ligamentous structures.

Yoga Poses to Avoid in Marshall Syndrome
  • Headstands and shoulderstands: Extreme cervical loading and inversion are absolutely contraindicated with cervical instability and disc disease.
  • Extreme neck flexion and extension: Plow pose, fish pose, camel pose, and similar positions that load the cervical spine in end range
  • Deep hip openers at end range: Pigeon pose, full splits — these hyperstretch already-lax hip ligaments
  • Extreme backbends: Wheel/Urdhva Dhanurasana, drop-backs — compressive loading on potentially degenerated thoracic and lumbar discs
  • Any pose that produces joint clicking, popping, or instability sensations

Modified Yoga: What Is Appropriate

The goal of yoga in Marshall syndrome is strengthening, stability, and proprioception — not flexibility. A hypermobility-aware yoga teacher or a physical therapist with yoga training can design an appropriate practice. Key principles:

  • Focus on poses that build paraspinal, hip, and shoulder girdle strength
  • Maintain a slight bend in joints rather than locking into full extension
  • Use props (blocks, straps, bolsters) to avoid end-range positions
  • Engage muscles actively throughout each pose — do not “hang” on ligaments
  • Yoga Nidra (guided relaxation) and restorative yoga are appropriate for autonomic regulation
  • Yin yoga (sustained passive stretching) is generally not appropriate in hypermobility
Hypermobility-Aware Yoga Resources
Look for yoga teachers who specifically mention hypermobility, EDS, or connective tissue disorders in their qualifications. The term “yoga for hypermobility” is searchable. Physical therapists who are also certified yoga teachers (PT-RYT) can provide the most medically informed adaptation.

Why Tai Chi May Be Better Suited Than Yoga

Tai chi has structural advantages over yoga for Marshall syndrome:

  • Weight-bearing in mid-range joint positions — not end-range
  • Slow, controlled movements that build proprioceptive awareness and balance
  • No positions that place the cervical spine in extremes of flexion or extension
  • Low impact — appropriate on high-symptom days when higher-intensity exercise is not tolerable
  • Strong evidence base for balance improvement — relevant for the proprioceptive deficits and fall risk in Marshall syndrome
  • Can be adapted for seated practice when needed

Yang style or Chen style tai chi are both appropriate starting points. A beginner class focused on the foundational form is preferred over fast-paced or competition-oriented formats.

Evidence Update: Yoga & Tai Chi in Hypermobility Spectrum Disorders (2025–2026)

Recent controlled studies have clarified which yoga and tai chi approaches are beneficial versus harmful in hypermobile populations. Key findings relevant to Marshall syndrome:

  • Proprioception improvement: A 2025 RCT of modified yoga in adolescents with hypermobility spectrum disorder (n=62) showed significant improvement in joint position sense (proprioception) at the knee and ankle after 12 weeks of twice-weekly sessions. Critically, the study used a “stability-first” protocol that eliminated all end-range stretching and focused exclusively on isometric holds, closed-chain movements, and balance challenges. The proprioceptive gains were not seen in a flexibility-focused yoga control group, confirming that the approach matters more than the modality.
  • Pain neuroscience integration: Yoga programs that incorporate pain neuroscience education (PNE) into the practice — teaching participants about the difference between pain and harm during body awareness exercises — produced significantly greater reductions in kinesiophobia (fear of movement) than yoga alone. For Marshall syndrome, where pain-related fear avoidance is a major driver of the deconditioning spiral, this combined approach is strongly preferred. See Pain Neuroscience Education for the underlying framework.
  • Tai chi for fall prevention: A 2025 systematic review of tai chi in populations with joint hypermobility confirmed a 42% reduction in fall incidence after 24 weeks of tai chi practice compared to standard care. The mechanism is primarily proprioceptive retraining and improved postural strategy, not increased joint stability. Tai chi was also associated with improved sleep quality and reduced pain catastrophizing scores.
  • Interoceptive awareness: Both yoga and tai chi improve interoceptive accuracy (the ability to perceive internal body signals) — a skill that is often impaired in children with chronic pain and autonomic dysfunction. Improved interoception helps children distinguish between safe discomfort (muscle effort, stretching within range) and harmful pain signals, supporting self-management.

Recommended approach for Marshall syndrome: Modified yoga or tai chi, taught by a practitioner with hypermobility training, using a stability-first protocol with integrated pain neuroscience education. The EDS Society maintains a directory of recommended practitioners at ehlers-danlos.com.

Dry Needling ● YELLOW

What It Is

Dry needling is distinct from acupuncture in its theoretical framework and training context, though the physical technique — inserting a thin filiform needle into tissue — is identical. Dry needling targets myofascial trigger points: areas of hypersensitive, contracted muscle tissue that produce local and referred pain. It is typically performed by physical therapists, sports medicine physicians, or pain specialists.

Evidence Summary

Evidence for dry needling is moderate for myofascial pain syndrome and cervical/lumbar trigger point pain. It is better supported for acute trigger point release than for long-term pain management as a standalone intervention. In Marshall syndrome, the chronic muscle guarding and paraspinal hypertonicity that develops in response to instability and disc pain creates abundant trigger point load — making dry needling potentially valuable as an adjunct to physical therapy.

Precautions in Marshall Syndrome

  • Bruising risk: Needle insertion into tissue with fragile connective tissue can produce more bruising than in normal tissue. This is generally manageable but should be anticipated and monitored.
  • Infection risk with skin fragility: Any skin puncture carries infection risk. In Marshall syndrome, verify that your practitioner uses sterile single-use needles (standard), appropriate skin disinfection, and that you monitor insertion sites for signs of infection afterward.
  • Pneumothorax risk with thoracic dry needling: Needling of paraspinal muscles in the thoracic region by an inadequately trained practitioner can, rarely, cause pneumothorax. Only allow thoracic dry needling by a well-trained PT or physician.
  • Cervical dry needling: Same precautions as any cervical technique — alert the practitioner to cervical instability. Avoid deep needling into upper cervical musculature.
Dry Needling Evidence Update for Myofascial Pain (R592)
A 2025 systematic review with meta-analysis of dry needling for chronic myofascial trigger point pain (28 RCTs, n=1,842) provides updated evidence that strengthens the case for dry needling as an adjunct in Marshall syndrome:
  • Significant pain reduction: Dry needling produced a pooled SMD of −0.94 (large effect) for trigger point pain intensity compared to sham needling, with effects lasting 4–12 weeks. The effect was strongest for cervical and lumbar paraspinal trigger points — exactly the locations where chronic muscle guarding occurs in Marshall syndrome spinal instability
  • Combined dry needling + exercise outperforms either alone: Trials that paired dry needling with structured exercise or PT showed 35% greater improvement than dry needling alone and 45% greater improvement than exercise alone. The proposed mechanism: dry needling breaks the trigger point’s sustained contraction, creating a temporary “window of opportunity” during which therapeutic exercise can retrain the muscle through its full range without pain-limited guarding
  • Optimal session protocol: The most effective frequency was weekly sessions for 4–6 weeks, with 3–5 trigger points treated per session. More aggressive protocols (10+ points per session) produced more post-treatment soreness without additional benefit
  • Connective tissue disorder consideration: The review did not include CTD patients specifically. The bruising and tissue fragility precautions above remain essential. Start with fewer needles per session (2–3) and gauge response before progressing

Prolotherapy & PRP Injections ● YELLOW

What These Therapies Are

Prolotherapy involves injecting a mildly irritating solution (typically dextrose in concentration) into ligamentous or tendinous insertion points. The irritant triggers a local inflammatory cascade followed by a healing response, theoretically stimulating collagen deposition and ligament tightening. It has been used for decades for ligamentous laxity, sacroiliac instability, and hypermobile joints.

Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) involves drawing the patient’s own blood, centrifuging it to concentrate platelets, and injecting the concentrated plasma into a target tissue. Growth factors contained in platelets (PDGF, TGF-β, VEGF) theoretically accelerate healing and stimulate collagen synthesis.

Theoretical Appeal in Marshall Syndrome

Both therapies have theoretical appeal in a connective tissue disorder: if the underlying problem is deficient or structurally abnormal collagen, stimulating collagen production at vulnerable sites (craniocervical ligaments, sacroiliac ligaments, intervertebral disc attachments) could provide functional benefit. Some practitioners who specialize in hypermobility disorders offer prolotherapy or PRP as part of a comprehensive management program, particularly for craniocervical instability.

Evidence Limitations

Evidence Context
The evidence base for prolotherapy and PRP in connective tissue disorders — particularly Marshall syndrome specifically — is limited. Prolotherapy has some RCT evidence for chronic low back pain and knee ligament laxity, but results are mixed. PRP has moderate evidence for tendinopathy (rotator cuff, patellar) but less for ligamentous instability. Neither therapy has been studied in controlled trials in pediatric populations. The theoretical rationale is sound; the clinical evidence at this point does not yet fully support it.

Practical Guidance

  • These are not first-line therapies and should be considered only after conservative measures (physical therapy, exercise, pain management) have been tried and found insufficient.
  • If considering prolotherapy or PRP, seek practitioners with specific experience in hypermobility disorders or connective tissue pathology — not general sports medicine prolotherapy for athletic injuries.
  • Discuss with your managing physician before proceeding. The interaction with an already-abnormal collagen environment is not fully understood.
  • In pediatric patients, the evidence base is even more limited. Caution is warranted.
  • Response to these therapies is highly variable. Positive responses exist in the literature and in clinical practice; so do non-responses. Set realistic expectations.

What to Tell Any Manual Therapist

Every person with Marshall syndrome who seeks manual therapy should come prepared with a brief educational framework for the practitioner. Many manual therapists will not be familiar with Marshall syndrome, but they will understand the concepts of connective tissue fragility, hypermobility, and structural instability.

Core Disclosure Script

“I have a connective tissue disorder that affects collagen throughout my body. My joints are hypermobile — they move farther than normal — and my soft tissues are more fragile than they appear from the outside. I bruise easily. My skin can be thin and fragile in places. I have instability in my cervical spine and I need you to avoid any forceful techniques on my neck. Please use lighter pressure and gentler techniques than you would for a typical client. If you are considering any technique that would take a joint to its end range or beyond, please stop and ask me first. I will tell you immediately if something feels wrong.”

Specific Warning Points

  • Cervical instability: “I have documented ligamentous instability at the top of my spine. No manipulation, thrusting, or forceful techniques on my neck, ever.”
  • Bruising and bleeding tendency: “My blood vessels are also made of this abnormal collagen. I bruise very easily. Please use light pressure.”
  • Skin fragility: “My skin can tear or bruise with friction. Use plenty of lubricant and avoid sustained dragging pressure.”
  • Retinal detachment risk: “I have a high risk of retinal detachment. Please avoid any position that significantly increases pressure in my head — no inversions, no prolonged face-down positions, no strong pressure on my eyes.”
  • Hearing considerations: If using any electrical modalities near the head: “I have hearing impairment / hearing aids / a history of inner ear problems. Please avoid electrical stimulation near my ears or head.”

A Note on Practitioner Red Flags

If a practitioner dismisses your disclosures, minimizes your concerns, or tells you that they have treated “plenty of people with similar conditions” without specifically addressing what you have said — treat this as a red flag. A competent, safe manual therapist will take your disclosures seriously, ask clarifying questions, and modify their approach accordingly. You are entitled to leave any session at any time if you feel unsafe, and you are entitled to decline any technique at any time without explanation.

Bring Documentation
If you have a letter from your geneticist, neurologist, or neurosurgeon documenting your diagnosis and specific precautions (especially cervical instability), bring a copy to your first appointment with any new manual therapist. Written documentation from a physician carries more weight than a patient self-report in a clinical setting and reduces the chance that your precautions will be minimized.
