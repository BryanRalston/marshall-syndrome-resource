Aquatic Therapy: The Optimal Exercise

Medical Disclaimer
This information is compiled from peer-reviewed research and is intended for educational discussion with qualified medical professionals. These are NOT treatment recommendations. All exercise protocols must be evaluated and approved by your child’s physician and physical therapist before implementation.

For a child with Marshall syndrome, aquatic therapy is not merely one exercise option among many — it is arguably the single most therapeutically dense physical intervention available. Water provides simultaneous axial unloading, proprioceptive input, cardiovascular conditioning, and pain modulation through mechanisms that cannot be replicated on land.

Axial Unloading by Depth

The degree of spinal unloading depends on immersion depth. This is the primary reason water is so valuable for Marshall syndrome spines:

Immersion DepthAxial Load ReductionClinical Implication
Waist (xiphoid process)~50%Sufficient for walking exercises with reduced disc compression
Chest (C7/shoulder)~65–80%Standing core exercises with minimal spinal load
Neck~85–90%Near-complete unloading; ideal for spinal mobility work
Full horizontal (swimming)~90–94%Maximal unloading; the spine approaches a zero-gravity state

At 90–94% axial unloading, a swimming child’s discs experience approximately 1/10th of the compressive force they bear during upright activities. This allows exercise-induced molecular benefits (irisin, BDNF, NRF2 activation, anti-inflammatory cytokines) to be delivered to the body without the mechanical cost of compression on vulnerable discs.

The Halliwick Concept

The Halliwick concept is a structured aquatic therapy framework originally developed for individuals with physical disabilities. It teaches water confidence and body control through a 10-point progression:

  1. Mental adjustment: Comfort and confidence in the water environment
  2. Disengagement: Independence from physical support
  3. Transversal rotation control: Rolling around the longitudinal axis
  4. Sagittal rotation control: Forward/backward rotation
  5. Longitudinal rotation control: Rotation around the vertical axis
  6. Combined rotation control: Multi-axis rotation management
  7. Upthrust (buoyancy): Understanding and using buoyancy
  8. Balance in stillness: Maintaining position without movement
  9. Turbulent gliding: Moving through water created by others
  10. Simple progression: Basic propulsion through water

For Marshall syndrome, the rotation control phases (3–6) are particularly valuable because they develop the deep spinal stabilizers (multifidus, transverse abdominis, pelvic floor) in a completely unloaded environment.

Marshall-Specific Safety Precautions

These precautions are specific to Marshall syndrome features and must not be overlooked.
  • Hearing protection: Marshall syndrome involves sensorineural hearing loss. Custom-molded swimming earplugs are mandatory to prevent water entry and potential infection that could worsen hearing function. If the child uses hearing aids, ensure proper waterproof storage during sessions
  • Eye protection: High myopia and vitreoretinal abnormalities are common in Marshall syndrome. Well-fitting swim goggles should be worn at all times to prevent chlorine irritation and to protect eyes from accidental impact
  • NO diving: Diving from blocks, platforms, or even the pool edge creates sudden increases in intraocular pressure and rapid deceleration forces on the cervical spine. Given the risk of retinal detachment (a known Marshall syndrome complication) and cervical disc vulnerability, diving is absolutely contraindicated
  • Water temperature: Pool water should be 33–34°C (91–93°F) for therapy sessions. This is warmer than standard lap pools (~27°C). Warm water reduces muscle guarding, decreases pain perception, and improves tissue extensibility. Cold water can trigger muscle spasm and increase pain. Hydrotherapy pools at rehabilitation centers typically maintain this range
  • Facial features: Marshall syndrome facial features (midface hypoplasia, flat nasal bridge) may affect goggle and mask fit. Test equipment before the first session to ensure a proper seal

Five Central Sensitization Mechanisms Addressed by Aquatic Therapy

Central sensitization (the nervous system amplifying pain signals) is a significant component of chronic Marshall syndrome pain. Aquatic therapy addresses it through five distinct mechanisms:

  1. Gate control activation: Hydrostatic pressure provides continuous, diffuse sensory input to large-diameter Aβ fibers across the entire immersed body surface. This “closes the gate” on pain transmission at the spinal cord level (Melzack-Wall gate control theory)
  2. Descending inhibition: Warm water immersion activates descending serotonergic and noradrenergic inhibitory pathways from the brainstem, reducing spinal cord excitability
  3. Cortisol and catecholamine modulation: Warm water immersion reduces cortisol and norepinephrine levels. Chronic cortisol elevation maintains central sensitization; reducing it helps reset pain thresholds
  4. Endocannabinoid release: Moderate-intensity aquatic exercise triggers endocannabinoid (anandamide) release, which directly modulates pain processing at both spinal and supraspinal levels
  5. Neuroplastic retraining: Successful, pain-free movement in water creates positive motor memories that gradually overwrite the learned pain associations from land-based movement. Over time, this recalibrates the brain’s threat assessment of spinal movement

Ai Chi: The Ideal Warm-Up

Ai Chi is a water-based exercise form combining elements of Tai Chi and Qigong, performed in shoulder-depth warm water. It consists of 19 choreographed movement patterns with synchronized breathing. For Marshall syndrome:

  • Slow, controlled movements provide gentle spinal mobilization without impact
  • Diaphragmatic breathing pattern activates the parasympathetic nervous system
  • Standing in shoulder-depth water provides ~70% axial unloading during warm-up
  • Progressive arm and leg patterns challenge balance without risk of falling
  • Meditative quality reduces cortisol and prepares the nervous system for more active exercise
Practical tip: Many children find Ai Chi boring in isolation. Pairing it with music or making it a “warm-up game” (mirroring the therapist, slow-motion challenges) dramatically improves adherence. The goal is consistent parasympathetic activation, not perfect form.
