Aquatic Therapy: The Optimal Exercise

Evidence confidence guide

Treatment research synthesis. This page describes therapeutic mechanisms based on published research. Evidence levels vary — see badges on each section. No treatment on this page is specifically approved for Marshall syndrome. Discuss with your child's medical team.

For a child with Marshall syndrome, aquatic therapy is not merely one exercise option among many — it is arguably the single best exercise modality available for this specific combination of pathologies. Water provides simultaneous axial unloading, proprioceptive input, cardiovascular conditioning, and pain modulation through mechanisms that cannot be replicated on land. No other single intervention addresses compromised discs, hypermobile joints, central sensitization, and deconditioning all at once.

2025 Guideline Support: The Cincinnati Children's Hospital 2025 clinical guidelines now formally include aquatic therapy as a recommended intervention for pediatric hypermobility conditions. This institutional endorsement reflects the growing evidence base for water-based rehabilitation in connective tissue disorders including Marshall syndrome.

Why Water Is Uniquely Suited to Marshall Syndrome Established

Five physical properties of water converge to create a therapeutic environment that is unmatched by any land-based modality:

  1. Buoyancy: 50–90% weight reduction depending on immersion depth. This unloads compromised discs while still allowing full-range movement — the core problem in Marshall syndrome is that the spine needs to move but cannot tolerate the compressive cost of movement on land
  2. Hydrostatic pressure: Water exerts equal pressure on all immersed surfaces. This reduces edema, improves proprioceptive awareness, and provides joint compression without impact — effectively a whole-body compression garment that adapts to depth
  3. Viscosity: Water provides natural resistance in all directions of movement. This builds strength without impact loading — resistance that increases with speed, allowing the child to self-regulate intensity simply by moving faster or slower
  4. Warmth (33–34°C): Therapeutic-temperature water reduces pain perception, decreases muscle spasm, and increases blood flow to periarticular tissues. This warmth is itself a treatment, not just a comfort measure
  5. Sensory input: Water provides constant, diffuse proprioceptive feedback across the entire body surface. For a child with hypermobility whose joint position sense is unreliable, this continuous input acts as an external proprioceptive system

Axial Unloading by Depth Established

The degree of spinal unloading depends on immersion depth. This is the primary reason water is so valuable for Marshall syndrome spines:

Waist (xiphoid process)~50%Sufficient for walking exercises with reduced disc compression
Chest (C7/shoulder)~65–80%Standing core exercises with minimal spinal load
Neck~85–90%Near-complete unloading; ideal for spinal mobility work
Full horizontal (swimming)~90–94%Maximal unloading; the spine approaches a zero-gravity state

At 90–94% axial unloading, a swimming child’s discs experience approximately 1/10th of the compressive force they bear during upright activities. This allows exercise-induced molecular benefits (irisin, BDNF, NRF2 activation, anti-inflammatory cytokines) to be delivered to the body without the mechanical cost of compression on vulnerable discs.

Swimming Stroke Modifications for Marshall Syndrome

  • Backstroke: Limit shoulder range to avoid hyperextension in hypermobile shoulders. Focus on controlled, symmetric arm recovery rather than maximal reach. Use a pull buoy between the knees if lower limb hypermobility causes excessive hip rotation
  • Modified breaststroke (supervised only): In rare cases where a therapist determines it appropriate, a modified breaststroke with reduced knee width and no glide-phase lumbar extension may be used therapeutically for short distances. This is an exception, not standard practice, and requires direct therapist supervision
  • Diving — absolutely contraindicated: All diving (from blocks, poolside, or standing) creates sudden cervical compression and rapid intraocular pressure changes. Given Marshall syndrome's cervical disc vulnerability and retinal detachment risk, diving is never acceptable regardless of depth or technique

Central Sensitization Modulation: Five Mechanisms Supported

Central sensitization (the nervous system amplifying pain signals) is a significant component of chronic Marshall syndrome pain. Aquatic therapy addresses it through five distinct, complementary mechanisms:

  1. Gate control activation: Hydrostatic pressure provides continuous, diffuse sensory input to large-diameter Aβ fibers across the entire immersed body surface. This activates Aβ fibers which “close the gate” on pain transmission at the spinal cord level (Melzack-Wall gate control theory), reducing the volume of nociceptive signals reaching the brain
  2. Descending inhibition: Warm water immersion activates descending serotonergic and noradrenergic inhibitory pathways from the brainstem, directly reducing spinal cord excitability and dampening pain signal amplification
  3. Cortisol reduction: Aquatic exercise reduces cortisol levels more effectively than equivalent land-based exercise. Chronic cortisol elevation maintains central sensitization; reducing it helps reset pain thresholds. The warm water environment also lowers norepinephrine and catecholamine levels
  4. Endocannabinoid activation: Moderate-intensity exercise in warm water boosts endocannabinoid (anandamide) release, which directly modulates pain processing at both spinal and supraspinal levels. The combination of warmth and movement appears to amplify this effect beyond what land-based exercise achieves
  5. Fear-avoidance reduction: Water feels inherently safe. The buoyancy removes the fear of falling, the warmth reduces guarding, and the sensory input provides reassurance. This directly reduces kinesiophobia (fear of movement) — a major driver of disability in chronic pain. Successful, pain-free movement in water creates positive motor memories that gradually overwrite learned pain associations from land-based movement, recalibrating the brain’s threat assessment of spinal movement

The Halliwick Concept Established

The Halliwick concept is a structured aquatic therapy framework originally developed for individuals with physical disabilities. It emphasizes independence and confidence in the water — making it an ideal framework for a 9-year-old with chronic pain who may have developed fear-avoidance behaviours around physical activity. The 10-point progression teaches water confidence and body control systematically:

  1. Mental adjustment: Comfort and confidence in the water environment
  2. Disengagement: Independence from physical support
  3. Transversal rotation control: Rolling around the longitudinal axis
  4. Sagittal rotation control: Forward/backward rotation
  5. Longitudinal rotation control: Rotation around the vertical axis
  6. Combined rotation control: Multi-axis rotation management
  7. Upthrust (buoyancy): Understanding and using buoyancy
  8. Balance in stillness: Maintaining position without movement
  9. Turbulent gliding: Moving through water created by others
  10. Simple progression: Basic propulsion through water

For Marshall syndrome, the rotation control phases (3–6) are particularly valuable because they develop the deep spinal stabilizers (multifidus, transverse abdominis, pelvic floor) in a completely unloaded environment. The emphasis on independence and progressive challenge also builds self-efficacy — the child learns that their body can move safely, which is itself therapeutic.

Marshall-Specific Safety Precautions

Nine non-negotiable safety rules. These are specific to Marshall syndrome features and must not be overlooked or relaxed, regardless of how competent the child becomes in the water.
  1. Custom earplugs EVERY session: Marshall syndrome involves sensorineural hearing loss (82.5% prevalence). Water entry into the ear canal compounds hearing risk and increases infection potential. Custom-molded swimming earplugs are mandatory — not optional, not “when available.” If the child uses hearing aids, ensure proper waterproof storage during sessions
  2. Swim goggles are mandatory (R520): High myopia and vitreoretinal abnormalities are common in Marshall syndrome. Well-fitting swim goggles with UV protection must be worn at all times — not optional, not “when available.” Goggles protect against chlorine irritation to already-vulnerable eyes, accidental impact, and provide visual orientation for children with high myopia who cannot see pool edges without correction. Note: Marshall syndrome facial features (midface hypoplasia, flat nasal bridge) may affect goggle fit — test equipment before the first session to ensure a proper seal. Prescription swim goggles are essential for children with significant myopia
  3. NO diving or jumping: Diving from blocks, platforms, or even the pool edge creates sudden increases in intraocular pressure and rapid deceleration forces on the cervical spine. Given the risk of retinal detachment (a known Marshall syndrome complication) and cervical disc vulnerability, all diving and jumping into water is absolutely contraindicated
  4. Water temperature 33–34°C (91–93°F) preferred; regular-temperature pools (27–30°C) acceptable with modifications (R520): Therapeutic-temperature water is ideal, but access to hydrotherapy pools is limited in many communities. Research (R520) supports that regular-temperature pools (27–30°C) are acceptable for cardiovascular conditioning, lap swimming, and strengthening phases — provided the child warms up gradually and wears a rash guard or wetsuit top for thermal insulation. Reserve warm therapeutic pools for pain-dominant sessions, Ai Chi, and initial acclimation. Avoid hot tubs and pools above 36°C during active exercise — the autonomic dysfunction common in Marshall syndrome increases the risk of heat-related vasodilation, hypotension, and syncope
  5. No butterfly or breaststroke EVER: Regardless of the child’s swimming ability, comfort level, or desire to try these strokes. The spinal loading patterns are incompatible with Marshall syndrome disc pathology. This rule does not relax with progression
  6. Exit if pain increases during session: Any increase in pain during aquatic exercise is a central sensitization flag. The session should stop. Do not push through. Aquatic therapy should always reduce pain, not increase it. If pain increases, the session intensity was too high or a new issue requires assessment
  7. Buddy system always: Hypermobility creates a risk of joint subluxation even in water. A buddy (therapist, parent, or trained lifeguard with awareness of the child’s condition) must be within arm’s reach at all times, particularly during the early weeks
  8. Ear drying protocol post-swim: After every session, ears must be thoroughly dried. Use custom drying drops if prescribed by ENT. Retained moisture in the ear canal increases otitis externa risk, which is particularly dangerous given existing hearing compromise
  9. Avoid chlorine-heavy pools: Chlorine is an irritant that can exacerbate respiratory and skin sensitivity. Where possible, use saltwater pools or UV-sanitized pools. If only chlorinated pools are available, rinse thoroughly immediately after each session

Aquatic Therapy Protocol Details: Evidence-Based Methods Supported

Pool Temperature: Why 33–36°C Is Critical

The therapeutic temperature range for Marshall syndrome aquatic therapy is 33–36°C (91–97°F) — significantly warmer than standard lap pools (~27°C) and even most recreational pools (~30°C). This is not a comfort preference; it is a therapeutic requirement:

  • 33–34°C: Baseline therapeutic range. Reduces muscle guarding, decreases pain perception, improves tissue extensibility. Suitable for active exercise phases (core stabilization, aerobic conditioning, lap swimming).
  • 34–36°C: Upper therapeutic range. Optimal for passive treatments (floating relaxation, Ai Chi, gentle mobilization), pain-dominant sessions, and initial acclimation for pain-avoidant children. Provides maximal muscle relaxation and parasympathetic activation.
  • Above 36°C: Risk of overheating during active exercise, particularly for children with autonomic dysfunction or POTS. Monitor for heat intolerance signs (dizziness, excessive fatigue, tachycardia). Active exercise at higher temperatures should be shorter in duration.
  • Below 33°C: May trigger muscle guarding, increase pain perception, and provoke mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS) responses in susceptible children. Cold-water exposure is contraindicated.

The Bad Ragaz Ring Method

The Bad Ragaz Ring Method (BRRM) is a systematic aquatic physiotherapy technique developed at the Ragaz thermal springs in Switzerland. It uses flotation rings (neck rings, pelvic rings, limb cuffs) to support the patient in a supine or side-lying position while the therapist provides resistance, stabilization, and guided movement.

  • Core principle: The patient is fully supported by flotation devices while the therapist holds a fixed point (usually the feet or pelvis). The patient then pushes or pulls against the therapist’s resistance through the water, creating isotonic and isokinetic exercise patterns.
  • Spinal benefit: Because the patient is horizontal and fully supported, axial loading is near zero. This allows spinal strengthening exercises that would be impossible on land for a child with compromised intervertebral discs.
  • Proprioceptive enhancement: The turbulence created by movement through water provides continuous proprioceptive feedback to joint mechanoreceptors. For hypermobile joints with unreliable proprioception, this external feedback system helps the nervous system recalibrate joint position sense.
  • Graded resistance: The therapist controls resistance by varying the speed of movement and the body position relative to the water surface. This allows precise dosing of exercise intensity without weights or machines.
  • Marshall-specific application: BRRM is particularly suitable for trunk rotation, hip abduction/adduction, and shoulder stabilization patterns — the same movement patterns needed to stabilize hypermobile joints. The therapist can protect the cervical spine by maintaining a neutral head position with a neck flotation ring while challenging the trunk and extremities.

The Halliwick Concept: Expanded Application

The Halliwick concept (introduced in the session structure above) deserves additional detail regarding its application to connective tissue disorders. Beyond the 10-point progression, Halliwick offers several principles that are uniquely valuable for Marshall syndrome:

  • One-to-one support ratio: Halliwick emphasizes a dedicated therapist or trained support person for each participant during the learning phases. For a child with joint hypermobility, this ensures immediate assistance if a subluxation occurs in the water and prevents the child from being left in an unsafe position.
  • Rotational control as deep stabilizer training: The rotation phases (transversal, sagittal, longitudinal, combined) are not just about water confidence — they systematically activate the deep spinal stabilizers (multifidus, transverse abdominis, internal obliques, pelvic floor) in a gravity-reduced environment. These are the same muscles targeted by land-based stabilization programs, but trained in a context where failure (loss of balance) results in a gentle drift in the water rather than a fall.
  • Buoyancy as the teacher: In Halliwick, buoyancy is not just weight relief — it is the primary teaching tool. The child learns to feel and control buoyancy forces, developing an internal sense of body position and movement that transfers to improved proprioception on land.

Marshall-Specific Aquatic Adaptations

Three non-negotiable Marshall-specific adaptations that go beyond the general safety precautions listed below:
  1. No diving — ever, from any height, in any context: All diving (from blocks, poolside, or standing) creates sudden cervical compression and rapid intraocular pressure changes. Given Marshall syndrome’s cervical disc vulnerability (see Disc Degeneration) and retinal detachment risk (see Eye Management), diving is absolutely contraindicated regardless of water depth, the child’s skill level, or recreational context. This rule extends to cannonballs, belly flops, and any entry that involves the head entering the water with velocity.
  2. Prescription swim goggles with UV protection: Standard swim goggles may not accommodate Marshall syndrome facial features (midface hypoplasia, flat nasal bridge). Prescription swim goggles are essential for children with high myopia to maintain visual orientation in the pool — a disoriented child in the water is a safety risk. Test goggle fit before the first session. If standard goggles leak due to facial anatomy, custom-molded swimming goggles are available from specialty optical suppliers. UV-protective lenses are non-negotiable given the cataract risk.
  3. Visual orientation cues in the pool: For children with high myopia or reduced visual acuity, the pool environment should include visual orientation aids: brightly colored lane markers, contrasting edge tiles, and clear depth markers. In open-water or unfamiliar pool settings, a buddy with a brightly colored swim cap improves visual tracking. The therapist should always position themselves where the child can see them, and verbal cues should supplement visual cues to account for combined hearing and vision deficits.

Combining Aquatic and Land-Based Therapy

Aquatic therapy is most effective when combined with land-based physical therapy for functional carryover. Skills developed in the reduced-gravity water environment — core stabilization, proprioceptive awareness, movement confidence — must be progressively transferred to weight-bearing activities on land.

  • Recommended frequency: 2–3 aquatic sessions per week, 30–45 minutes each, alongside 1–2 land-based PT sessions
  • Sequencing: Aquatic sessions are ideal for building strength and movement confidence; land-based sessions focus on translating those gains into real-world functional tasks (stair climbing, carrying a backpack, sitting tolerance)
  • Pool temperature: 33–34°C (91–93°F) remains the therapeutic target for Marshall syndrome. Standard recreational pools (typically 27–28°C) are too cold and may trigger muscle guarding or MCAS responses
  • Progression principle: As strength and confidence improve in water, gradually increase the proportion of land-based work. The long-term goal is an independent, sustainable exercise routine that includes both modalities

Ai Chi: The Ideal Warm-Up

Ai Chi is a water-based exercise form combining elements of Tai Chi and Qigong, performed in shoulder-depth warm water. It consists of 19 choreographed movement patterns with synchronized breathing. For Marshall syndrome:

  • Slow, controlled movements provide gentle spinal mobilization without impact
  • Diaphragmatic breathing pattern activates the parasympathetic nervous system
  • Standing in shoulder-depth water provides ~70% axial unloading during warm-up
  • Progressive arm and leg patterns challenge balance without risk of falling
  • Meditative quality reduces cortisol and prepares the nervous system for more active exercise
Practical tip: Many children find Ai Chi boring in isolation. Pairing it with music or making it a “warm-up game” (mirroring the therapist, slow-motion challenges) dramatically improves adherence. The goal is consistent parasympathetic activation, not perfect form.
