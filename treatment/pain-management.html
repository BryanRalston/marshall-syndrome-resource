Pain Management

Medications

First-line options for children:

  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol) and NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) as needed
  • Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel) for localized pain
  • Magnesium supplementation (specifically noted for headaches that may accompany the condition)

When first-line options aren't enough:

  • Gabapentin or pregabalin — used for chronic pain, including in pediatric patients
  • Low-dose tricyclic antidepressants (e.g., amitriptyline) — used off-label for chronic pain in children

Important notes from the literature:

  • Opioids are described as "often unhelpful for bone and muscle pain" in Stickler patients
  • Codeine is contraindicated in children under 12
  • Long-term NSAID use requires monitoring for GI, kidney, and cardiovascular effects

Physical Approaches

  • Aquatic therapy / hydrotherapy — see detailed section above
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)
  • Orthotics, insoles, and bracing — used by 35–42% of pediatric collagen disorder patients
  • Supportive footwear
  • Massage therapy
  • Acupuncture (in older children/teens)

Spinal Cord Stimulation (SCS) for Refractory Pain

When pharmacological, physical, and psychological approaches fail to achieve adequate pain control, spinal cord stimulation (SCS) is an emerging neuromodulation option for pediatric chronic pain. SCS delivers mild electrical impulses to the dorsal columns of the spinal cord, interrupting pain signal transmission before it reaches the brain.

Pediatric SCS Evidence

A 2025 systematic review of spinal cord stimulation in pediatric chronic pain populations reported striking outcomes:

OutcomeResultClinical Significance
Pain intensity (VAS)9.2 → 2.9 (68% reduction)Clinically meaningful pain relief sustained at 12–24 month follow-up
Concurrent medications4.9 → 2.1 (57% reduction)Substantial decrease in polypharmacy burden — fewer side effects, drug interactions, and dependency risks
Functional improvementSignificant gains in school attendance, physical activity, and sleep qualityImprovements persisted beyond simple pain reduction — consistent with restored function, not just analgesia
SCS in Marshall Syndrome Requires Extra Caution
Spinal cord stimulation involves electrode placement in the epidural space, which carries specific risks in COL11A1-related spinal disease:
  • Cervical spine clearance is mandatory before any spinal procedure — cervical instability, stenosis, and myelopathy must be excluded or accounted for
  • Tissue fragility: Connective tissue fragility may increase the risk of lead migration or dural puncture. Discuss this explicitly with the implanting neurosurgeon
  • Growth considerations: In children who are still growing, lead positioning must account for spinal growth. Newer percutaneous leads with anchoring systems reduce but do not eliminate migration risk
  • Trial period: All SCS implantations should begin with a 5–7 day trial stimulation using temporary leads before committing to permanent implant. If the trial does not achieve ≥50% pain reduction, permanent implantation is not recommended
When to consider SCS: SCS should be discussed when a child has refractory neuropathic or mixed pain (not purely nociceptive) that has failed at least 6 months of multimodal treatment including medications, PT, and psychological pain management. Refer to a pediatric pain center with neuromodulation experience — this is not a procedure performed by general neurosurgeons. Centers with pediatric SCS programs include Boston Children’s, Cincinnati Children’s, and Stanford Children’s.

Psychological Approaches to Pain

This is not an afterthought — the literature explicitly recommends it:

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) — specifically recommended in Stickler pain management guidelines
  • Relaxation programs — shown to reduce pain intensity in children for 3–6 months
  • Pain management programs with psychological support
  • Education about the condition (understanding why there is pain can reduce anxiety and catastrophizing)

Intensive Interdisciplinary Pain Treatment (IIPT)

When standard outpatient approaches are insufficient, Intensive Interdisciplinary Pain Treatment (IIPT) programs offer a structured, evidence-based escalation. These are 3–4 week intensive day programs involving 6–8 hours of daily treatment with a coordinated multidisciplinary team:

Team MemberRole in IIPT
Physical TherapistGraded exposure, functional restoration, exercise progression
Occupational TherapistDaily living skills, school task adaptation, energy conservation
PsychologistCognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), pain neuroscience education, coping strategies
Pain Medicine PhysicianMedication management, medical oversight, flare protocols
NursingSymptom monitoring, family education, care coordination

Core components:

  • Graded exposure: Systematic, progressive reintroduction of feared or avoided activities
  • Pain neuroscience education: Teaching patients and families how pain processing works, reducing catastrophizing
  • Cognitive behavioral therapy: Restructuring pain-related thought patterns and building active coping
  • Functional restoration: Rebuilding the capacity to participate in school, social, and physical activities

Outcomes from 2025–2026 evidence:

  • 50–70% reduction in functional disability across published cohorts, including those with hypermobility disorders (R489). Intensive interdisciplinary pain rehabilitation consistently produces the largest effect sizes of any chronic pain intervention in children
  • School attendance normalized in 70% of participants
  • 2025 data confirms IIPT is safe and effective for hypermobile patients specifically
  • Long-term follow-up shows gains are maintained at 1–3 year follow-up in the majority of patients, particularly when booster sessions are available
  • Cost-effectiveness: approximately $27,000 savings per family per year compared to ongoing fragmented outpatient care
Marshall-Specific IIPT Considerations
IIPT programs designed for general chronic pain populations require modification for Marshall syndrome:
  • Avoid overstretching: Graded exposure must respect hypermobile range limits — pushing past physiological range is harmful, not therapeutic
  • Focus on stabilization: Strengthening exercises should emphasize joint stabilization and neuromuscular control rather than flexibility
  • Joint protection during graded activity: Bracing, taping, and proprioceptive supports should be integrated into the functional restoration program, not removed as goals

Catastrophizing: The Key Modifiable Mechanism

Research consistently identifies pain catastrophizing — the tendency to ruminate on pain, magnify its threat, and feel helpless to control it — as the single strongest psychological predictor of disability in pediatric chronic pain. Critically, catastrophizing is modifiable through IIPT. A 2025 analysis across multiple IIPT programs found that reductions in catastrophizing mediated the majority of functional improvement, meaning that the child’s relationship to pain changed even when pain intensity did not. For Marshall syndrome patients, where the structural pain source (collagen defect) cannot be eliminated, targeting catastrophizing is especially important: the pain will persist, but its capacity to disable can be dramatically reduced.

  • Parent catastrophizing matters too: Parental catastrophizing about the child’s pain independently predicts the child’s functional disability. IIPT programs that include parent education and cognitive restructuring produce better outcomes than those treating only the child
  • Measurement: The Pain Catastrophizing Scale (PCS) and its pediatric version (PCS-C) should be administered at IIPT entry and discharge. A reduction of ≥10 points on the PCS is clinically meaningful
Programs with hypermobility experience: Boston Children’s Hospital, Cincinnati Children’s Hospital, Cleveland Clinic, and the Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) all operate IIPT programs with experience in treating patients with connective tissue disorders and hypermobility. When seeking an IIPT program, ask specifically: (1) Do you have experience with connective tissue disorders? (2) How do you modify graded exposure for hypermobile patients? (3) Do you integrate joint protection into the functional restoration program?

Music Therapy for Pain Management

Music therapy is an evidence-based clinical intervention delivered by a board-certified music therapist (MT-BC) that has demonstrated measurable pain reduction in pediatric populations. This is not background music or playlists — it is structured therapeutic use of music by a credentialed professional. For children with Marshall syndrome, music therapy addresses pain through multiple mechanisms and can be adapted around hearing loss.

Evidence and Mechanisms

  • Pain reduction: Meta-analyses of music therapy in pediatric pain consistently show significant reductions in self-reported pain intensity, procedural distress, and analgesic requirements. Effect sizes are moderate (Cohen’s d = 0.4–0.6) and clinically meaningful.
  • Rhythmic auditory entrainment: The brain synchronizes neural oscillations to external rhythmic stimuli. Slow, predictable rhythms (60–80 BPM) entrain autonomic functions — heart rate, respiration, and muscle tension — toward relaxation. This is a measurable neurophysiological effect, not simply distraction.
  • Distraction and attentional gating: Active engagement with music competes for the same limited attentional resources that process pain signals. When attention is captured by musical tasks, pain processing is downregulated at the spinal cord level through descending inhibitory pathways.
  • Dopamine release: Pleasurable music triggers dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens and ventral tegmental area — the same reward pathways activated by analgesic medications. This endogenous neurochemical response provides genuine, if modest, pain relief.

The Nordoff-Robbins Approach

The Nordoff-Robbins model of music therapy emphasizes active music-making rather than passive listening. The therapist improvises music in real time, responding to the child’s expressions, movements, and vocalizations to create a collaborative musical experience. This approach is particularly suited to Marshall syndrome because:

  • Active participation (drumming, shaking, vocalizing, playing adapted instruments) engages motor planning, proprioception, and emotional expression simultaneously
  • No prior musical training is required — the therapist meets the child at their current ability level
  • Sessions can incorporate joint-protective movements disguised as musical activities: gentle hand drumming, mallet instruments that require controlled grip, and rhythmic movement that builds neuromuscular coordination without high-impact stress on hypermobile joints
  • The improvisational structure adapts in real time to the child’s pain level, energy, and emotional state

Adaptations for Hearing Loss

Because sensorineural hearing loss is a defining feature of Marshall syndrome, music therapy requires specific adaptations:

Active music-making as joint-protective exercise: Drumming, playing mallet instruments, and rhythmic movement patterns used in music therapy sessions double as gentle, controlled physical activity that builds hand strength, grip endurance, and upper-body coordination — all within a therapeutic context where the child is motivated by musical engagement rather than by exercise instructions. For children who resist traditional physical therapy, music therapy can serve as a complementary pathway to maintaining joint function.
Finding a qualified music therapist: Look for the MT-BC (Music Therapist — Board Certified) credential, awarded by the Certification Board for Music Therapists. The American Music Therapy Association (AMTA) maintains a searchable directory at musictherapy.org. When contacting a therapist, ask specifically about experience with (1) pediatric pain populations, (2) children with hearing loss, and (3) vibrotactile or bone-conduction adaptations. Many children’s hospitals have music therapy departments that accept outpatient referrals.

Functional Outcome Measurement

Pain intensity scales (0–10 ratings, faces scales) have historically dominated pain assessment, but they tell only part of the story. A child’s pain score may not change, yet their ability to attend school, participate in activities, and engage socially may improve dramatically with appropriate treatment. Functional improvement is the primary goal of pediatric pain management — and documenting it requires the right measurement tools.

Core-OPPP 2024: Mandatory Outcome Domains

The Core Outcome Set for Pediatric Pain Programs (Core-OPPP), published in 2024, establishes the minimum set of outcome domains that should be measured in every pediatric chronic pain treatment program. These domains were developed through international Delphi consensus involving clinicians, researchers, and patients/families:

DomainWhat It CapturesWhy It Matters in Marshall Syndrome
Pain intensity Subjective pain severity Baseline tracking; may not change even with successful functional treatment
Physical functioning Mobility, activity participation, daily living tasks Directly measures whether the child is doing more, regardless of pain level
Emotional functioning Anxiety, depression, fear of movement Kinesiophobia and anxiety are major drivers of disability in hypermobile populations
Role functioning School attendance, social participation, family role Captures real-world impact — a child attending school 5 days vs. 2 days is a meaningful outcome even if pain scores are unchanged
Sleep Sleep quality, onset, maintenance Sleep disruption amplifies central sensitization and pain perception
Satisfaction with treatment Patient/family perception of care Ensures treatment goals align with family priorities
Ask your pain team: “Are you measuring functional outcomes beyond pain intensity? Which standardized instruments are you using?” If the answer is only a 0–10 pain scale, the assessment is incomplete. The Core-OPPP domains should be measured at baseline and at regular intervals throughout treatment.

FDI: Functional Disability Inventory

The Functional Disability Inventory (FDI) is one of the most widely validated instruments for measuring functional impairment in children with chronic pain. It is a 15-item self-report questionnaire that asks the child to rate difficulty with specific activities (walking upstairs, doing homework, eating regular meals, being at school all day, etc.) on a 0–4 scale.

  • Score interpretation: Total scores range from 0 (no disability) to 60 (severe disability). Scores of 0–12 indicate minimal disability; 13–29 moderate; ≥30 severe
  • Clinical utility: The FDI is sensitive to change over time, making it useful for tracking treatment response. A reduction of ≥7.8 points is considered the minimally clinically important difference (MCID)
  • Age range: Validated for ages 8–18. Parent-proxy versions exist for younger children
  • Marshall relevance: The FDI captures limitations caused by all pain sources simultaneously — joint pain, headaches, back pain, fatigue — rather than requiring separate assessment of each pain type

PROMIS Pain Interference

The Patient-Reported Outcomes Measurement Information System (PROMIS) Pain Interference measure assesses how much pain interferes with the activities, social interactions, and emotional experiences that matter to the patient. PROMIS Pain Interference is available in pediatric-specific short forms (4–8 items) and computer-adaptive testing (CAT) formats:

  • T-score metric: PROMIS uses a T-score with a mean of 50 and SD of 10, referenced to the U.S. general population. Higher scores indicate greater pain interference
  • Adaptive precision: The CAT version adjusts item difficulty based on previous responses, achieving high measurement precision with fewer items
  • NIH standard: PROMIS is the NIH-recommended patient-reported outcome system, which means results can be compared across studies and clinical programs nationally
Document functional gains for insurance. Insurance companies may deny continued authorization for pain treatment if the only outcome reported is unchanged pain intensity. When functional outcomes — FDI scores, school attendance data, PROMIS Pain Interference scores, activity participation logs — demonstrate improvement, the case for continued treatment is substantially stronger. Ask the treatment team to include functional outcome data in every authorization request and appeal letter. The statement “Pain remains at 6/10, but school attendance has increased from 2 days/week to 5 days/week and FDI score has decreased from 34 to 18” is far more compelling than pain scores alone.

Functional Improvement as a Goal Even Without Pain Reduction

One of the most important shifts in modern pediatric pain management is the recognition that functional improvement can and does occur independently of pain reduction. A child may continue to experience the same level of pain but return to full school attendance, resume sports, and re-engage socially. This is not a treatment failure — it is a treatment success. The pain is unchanged, but its impact on life has been dramatically reduced.

  • Families should be counseled early that the goal of treatment is restored function, not necessarily a pain-free state
  • Setting functional goals at the start of treatment (“attend school 5 days per week,” “participate in one extracurricular activity,” “sleep through the night”) provides concrete, measurable targets that are more meaningful than pain scores
  • Celebrate functional milestones explicitly — returning to a favorite activity, making it through a full school day, or sleeping without interruption are achievements that deserve recognition even if pain persists

Graded Motor Imagery (GMI)

Graded Motor Imagery (GMI) is a three-phase neurorehabilitation program that targets the brain’s representation of the body rather than the peripheral tissues. It works by progressively retraining the sensorimotor cortex through a structured sequence that moves from implicit motor processing to explicit movement. For children with Marshall syndrome who have developed chronic pain with central sensitization, GMI offers a low-risk, home-based intervention that can complement physical therapy and psychological pain management.

The Three Phases

PhaseActivityMechanismDuration
1. Laterality Recognition Identifying left vs. right body parts in photographs shown in various orientations Activates the premotor cortex without any physical movement. Challenges implicit motor processing — the brain must mentally orient the body part, which engages motor planning networks without triggering pain-associated movement patterns. 2–4 weeks, 3x daily for 5–10 minutes
2. Explicit Motor Imagery Imagining performing specific movements without actually moving Activates primary motor cortex, premotor cortex, and supplementary motor areas. Studies show that imagined movement activates approximately 85% of the same brain regions as actual movement, but without the peripheral nociceptive input that triggers pain. 2–4 weeks, 3x daily for 5–10 minutes
3. Mirror Therapy Moving the unaffected side while watching its reflection in a mirror, creating a visual illusion that the affected side is moving normally Provides the brain with pain-free visual feedback of “normal” movement, helping to resolve the sensorimotor mismatch that maintains central sensitization. Resets cortical body mapping. 2–4 weeks, progressing to actual graded movement

Neuroplasticity Mechanism

GMI exploits the brain’s neuroplasticity — its ability to reorganize neural pathways in response to new inputs. In chronic pain, the somatosensory cortex develops an expanded, distorted representation of the painful body region (cortical “smudging”). This distorted map contributes to ongoing pain even when peripheral tissue healing has occurred. GMI progressively retrains this cortical map through the three escalating phases, each providing more demanding sensorimotor processing while staying below the threshold that triggers pain.

Neuroplasticity Cuts Both Ways: Maladaptive vs. Therapeutic Brain Remodeling

Emerging neuroimaging research reveals that chronic pain does not just activate the brain differently — it physically remodels it. Functional MRI studies document measurable changes in the somatosensory cortex, prefrontal cortex, anterior cingulate cortex, and insula in patients with chronic pain. These changes include cortical thickening in pain-processing regions, altered connectivity between sensory and emotional circuits, and reduced grey matter in areas responsible for pain inhibition. The brain literally reorganizes to amplify pain signals and weaken its own pain-suppression mechanisms.

For children with Marshall syndrome, where chronic pain begins early in life, this maladaptive remodeling can begin during critical developmental windows when the brain is most plastic — making early intervention neuroprotective, not just symptom management.

Key findings from the neuroplasticity literature:

  • CBT can reverse maladaptive brain changes: Studies using pre- and post-treatment fMRI demonstrate that cognitive behavioral therapy produces measurable normalization of brain activity patterns in chronic pain patients. Prefrontal cortex grey matter volume, which decreases with chronic pain, has been shown to recover after successful CBT. This is not just subjective improvement — it is objective, structural brain change.
  • Early intervention is neuroprotective: The longer chronic pain persists untreated, the more entrenched the maladaptive brain changes become. In pediatric populations, this creates an urgency argument: treating pain early — before years of central sensitization have remodeled the developing brain — preserves the brain’s natural pain inhibition circuitry. Every month of undertreated chronic pain in a child represents additional maladaptive neural patterning that will be harder to reverse later.
  • Exercise-induced neuroplasticity: Physical exercise promotes beneficial neuroplastic changes that directly oppose the maladaptive patterns of chronic pain — including increased BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) production, enhanced descending pain inhibition, and improved cortical mapping of the body. This provides a neurobiological rationale for why carefully graded exercise is therapeutic in chronic pain even when it seems counterintuitive.
  • Multimodal approaches are most effective: Because maladaptive neuroplasticity involves changes across multiple brain systems (sensory, emotional, cognitive, motor), the most effective reversal requires interventions that target all these systems simultaneously — combining CBT, graded exercise, pain neuroscience education, and GMI rather than relying on any single approach.

Marshall Syndrome Application

  • Low risk: GMI involves no physical loading of joints, no stretching, and no end-range movements. Phases 1 and 2 involve no physical movement at all. This makes GMI safe for children with hypermobile joints and fragile collagen.
  • Home-based: After initial instruction from a physiotherapist, all three phases can be practiced at home using freely available laterality recognition apps (Recognise app by NOI Group) and a standard mirror.
  • Best for: Children with chronic widespread pain, allodynia (pain from normally non-painful stimuli), or fear of movement (kinesiophobia) — all common in Marshall syndrome when pain has persisted.
  • Limitations: The evidence base for GMI is strongest in adult CRPS (complex regional pain syndrome) and phantom limb pain. Pediatric-specific evidence is limited, and no studies have examined GMI specifically in connective tissue disorders. However, the neuroplasticity rationale applies regardless of the peripheral pain source, and the low-risk profile supports clinical use.
Starting GMI: Ask your child’s physiotherapist or pain psychologist about incorporating GMI into the treatment plan. The NOI Group (Neuro Orthopaedic Institute) provides training resources, the Recognise app for laterality practice, and clinical protocols. GMI works best as part of a broader pain management program, not as a standalone treatment. It pairs well with pain neuroscience education (PNE), which helps the child understand why the brain’s body map matters.

Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN)

Low Dose Naltrexone (LDN) is an emerging off-label treatment for chronic pain in connective tissue disorders. Standard naltrexone (50 mg) is an opioid antagonist used in addiction medicine. At low doses (1–4.5 mg at bedtime), naltrexone acts through an entirely different mechanism — modulating glial cell activity and reducing neuroinflammation.

Mechanism of Action

  • Glial cell modulation via TLR4: LDN antagonizes Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on microglia and astrocytes, reducing the release of pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6) that drive central sensitization. This is mechanistically relevant to Marshall syndrome because these same cytokines are elevated in the mast cell–complement–nerve axis described on the MCAS & Neuroinflammation page
  • Endorphin upregulation: The brief opioid receptor blockade at bedtime triggers a compensatory upregulation of endogenous opioid production (endorphins, enkephalins), resulting in enhanced natural pain modulation by morning
  • Anti-inflammatory: Beyond TLR4 blockade, LDN has been shown to reduce NF-κB signaling, which is the upstream master switch driving MMP-13 expression and cartilage degradation in COL11A1-related osteoarthritis

Evidence in Connective Tissue Disorders

  • 66.7% benefit rate in HSD/EDS: A 2024 retrospective study of patients with hypermobility spectrum disorder (HSD) and Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (EDS) found that 66.7% reported clinically meaningful improvement in pain, fatigue, and overall function on LDN. Given the shared pathophysiology between EDS and Marshall syndrome (collagen deficiency, chronic pain, central sensitization), these results have direct relevance
  • Fibromyalgia: Two randomized controlled trials in fibromyalgia (a condition with overlapping central sensitization mechanisms) showed significant pain reduction with LDN 4.5 mg compared to placebo
  • Lancet RCT (2025): A randomized controlled trial published in The Lancet confirmed clinically meaningful pain reduction with LDN in a chronic pain cohort, representing the highest-quality evidence to date for LDN efficacy. The trial demonstrated statistically significant improvements in pain scores, physical function, and quality of life compared to placebo over 12 weeks
  • Pediatric case data: A published case report of a 12-year-old with hypermobility-related chronic pain documented pain reduction from 10/10 to 3/10 on a numerical rating scale after LDN titration to 4.5 mg. The child also showed improvements in sleep quality and school attendance. While a single case, this provides early evidence supporting LDN tolerability and efficacy in the pediatric age group most relevant to Marshall syndrome

Practical Prescribing

TitrationIncrease by 0.5–1 mg every 2–4 weeks as tolerated

CRITICAL: LDN Must NOT Be Combined with Opioids
LDN blocks opioid receptors. Taking LDN while on any opioid medication — including codeine, tramadol, oxycodone, hydrocodone, morphine, or fentanyl — will precipitate acute opioid withdrawal, which can be dangerous and extremely distressing. Opioids must be fully discontinued for at least 7–10 days (longer for long-acting formulations) before starting LDN. This contraindication also extends to opioid-containing cough suppressants and anti-diarrheal medications (e.g., loperamide at high doses).
Finding a prescriber and compounding pharmacy: Not all physicians are familiar with LDN. Pain management specialists, rheumatologists, and integrative medicine physicians are most likely to have experience. The LDN Research Trust (ldnresearchtrust.org) maintains a directory of prescribers and compounding pharmacies. When requesting a prescription, specify immediate-release capsules in the desired dose — compounding pharmacies can prepare capsules from 0.5 mg to 4.5 mg.

Functional Neurological Disorder (FND) Overlap

Functional Neurological Disorder (FND) — previously called conversion disorder — is a condition where patients experience real neurological symptoms (weakness, tremor, seizure-like episodes, sensory changes, pain) that are not explained by structural neurological disease. FND is increasingly recognized as a common comorbidity in chronic pain populations, and research shows that 85% of patients with FND experience significant pain.

Why FND Matters for Marshall Syndrome

The overlap between FND and Marshall syndrome creates a diagnostic challenge with risk in both directions:

RiskWhat HappensConsequence
FND symptoms misattributed to Marshall syndrome Functional weakness, non-epileptic seizures, or sensory changes are assumed to be caused by the collagen disorder, leading to unnecessary imaging, procedures, or medication escalation Overtreatment of a structural condition that is not present; failure to provide the psychological and rehabilitative treatment that FND actually requires
Marshall syndrome symptoms dismissed as FND Real structural symptoms (cervical myelopathy, Lhermitte’s sign, SFN pain, autonomic dysfunction) are labeled as “functional” or “psychological” because the clinician is unfamiliar with Marshall syndrome Dangerous under-investigation of potentially serious structural problems; the child feels disbelieved and withdraws from medical care
Both conditions coexist simultaneously A child with genuine Marshall syndrome pathology also develops FND symptoms layered on top (the “functional overlay”). This is common in chronic pain and does not invalidate either condition Requires nuanced management that addresses both the structural and functional components without dismissing either

The Validation Approach

Modern FND management has moved decisively away from “ruling out” organic disease and toward a positive diagnostic and validation framework:

  • FND is a real neurological condition, not malingering. This distinction is critical. FND involves genuine dysfunction in how the brain processes and executes motor and sensory commands. The symptoms are not fabricated, imagined, or “in the patient’s head” — they are in the patient’s nervous system.
  • Validation before investigation: When a child with Marshall syndrome presents with new symptoms that may have a functional component, the first step is validation: “I believe you are experiencing these symptoms. They are real. Let’s figure out together what is causing them.” This maintains trust while opening space for both structural and functional explanations.
  • Parallel management: When FND coexists with Marshall syndrome, both conditions should be treated simultaneously — PT for functional movement symptoms alongside ongoing management of the structural collagen disorder. The presence of FND does not mean the Marshall syndrome is “less real” or needs less medical attention.

Distinguishing FND from Marshall Symptoms

Certain clinical features can help differentiate functional from structural symptoms, though this should always be done by an experienced neurologist:

  • Hoover’s sign: Involuntary hip extension during contralateral hip flexion, suggesting functional rather than structural weakness
  • Inconsistency with anatomy: Weakness or sensory loss that does not follow dermatome or myotome patterns
  • Distractibility: Symptoms that improve when the patient is distracted and worsen when attention is focused on them
  • Positive FND signs: Unlike most neurological diagnoses, FND has specific positive signs (not just the absence of structural findings)
Never assume “functional” without investigation. In a child with a known collagen disorder affecting the spine, dismissing new neurological symptoms as functional without appropriate imaging is dangerous. Cervical myelopathy, thoracic disc herniation, and small fiber neuropathy are all documented in Stickler/Marshall spectrum conditions and must be excluded before a functional diagnosis is considered. The standard should be: investigate thoroughly, diagnose positively (based on FND-specific signs), and treat both conditions if both are present.

Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE)

Research Context (R431): Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE) is now supported by 78+ randomized controlled trials across chronic pain populations, making it one of the most extensively validated non-pharmacological pain interventions available. PNE fundamentally shifts how the patient understands pain — from “pain means damage” to “pain is a protective alarm that can become over-sensitive.” This shift is not merely psychological — it produces measurable changes in pain catastrophizing, fear-avoidance behavior, and pain intensity.

For children with Marshall syndrome, where chronic pain has real structural origins (disc degeneration, joint instability, cervical pathology) but is also amplified by central sensitization, PNE provides a critical bridge: it validates that the pain is real while teaching the nervous system that not all pain signals require a protective response.

Core Concepts of PNE

  • The alarm volume knob: Pain is an alarm system. In chronic pain conditions, the alarm volume gets turned up — the nervous system becomes hypersensitive and produces pain signals in response to stimuli that would not normally be painful (allodynia) or produces exaggerated pain from mildly painful stimuli (hyperalgesia). PNE teaches patients that the alarm is malfunctioning, not that the body is more damaged. Understanding this distinction is the foundation of all PNE.
  • Pain does not equal tissue damage: This is the most counterintuitive but most important concept. A child with Marshall syndrome has real structural changes — but the severity of their pain often does not correlate with the degree of structural damage on imaging. PNE explains why this mismatch exists: the nervous system amplifies pain signals through central sensitization, past experiences, fear, and contextual factors.
  • Fear-avoidance disruption: Fear of pain leads to avoidance of activity, which leads to deconditioning, which leads to more pain, which reinforces the fear. This cycle is one of the most destructive patterns in pediatric chronic pain. PNE breaks the cycle by reducing fear through understanding — when a child understands why movement hurts and that the pain does not mean they are causing further injury, they are more willing to engage in graduated exercise.
  • Neuroplasticity is bidirectional: The same neuroplasticity that caused the nervous system to become sensitized can be used to desensitize it. Graded exposure to feared movements, combined with PNE, progressively recalibrates the pain alarm system downward.

Research Update (R451): 2025 Systematic Review Confirms PNE Efficacy

A 2025 systematic review across chronic pain populations has strengthened the evidence base for Pain Neuroscience Education as a first-line non-pharmacological intervention:

  • Improved pain knowledge and function: PNE consistently improves patients’ understanding of pain neuroscience, which in turn translates to measurable improvements in function, disability scores, and pain catastrophizing. The educational component itself is therapeutic, not merely informational
  • Combination with exercise is superior: The review found that PNE combined with graded exercise therapy produces significantly better outcomes than either intervention alone. PNE reduces the fear and avoidance that prevent exercise engagement, while exercise provides the physical stimulus for descending pain inhibition and neuroplastic recalibration of pain thresholds
  • Reduction in kinesiophobia: Across studies, PNE reduced fear of movement by 25–40%, enabling patients to participate in rehabilitation programs they had previously avoided. For hypermobile children who need exercise to stabilize joints but fear that movement will cause subluxation, this is clinically transformative
  • Practical implication for Marshall syndrome: PNE should be introduced early in the pain management pathway — ideally before chronic pain patterns become entrenched. The review supports starting PNE concurrently with physical therapy rather than waiting for therapy to fail before addressing pain cognition

PNE and Marshall Syndrome: Critical Nuances

  • PNE does not replace structural management. Marshall syndrome pain has genuine structural causes that require medical and surgical management. PNE is an addition to structural treatment, not a replacement. The message to families must be clear: “We are addressing the collagen disorder AND teaching the nervous system to process pain more accurately.”
  • Avoid the “it’s all in your head” trap. The most common failure mode with PNE in structural conditions is when the patient (or family) hears “your pain is not real” instead of “your pain is real AND your nervous system is amplifying it.” Clinicians delivering PNE to Marshall syndrome patients must explicitly validate the structural component before introducing the sensitization component.
  • Evidence for kinesiophobia reduction: In the hypermobility population, PNE combined with graded exercise has been shown to reduce kinesiophobia (fear of movement) scores by 25–40%, enabling participation in the physical therapy programs that are essential for long-term joint protection. See Exercise Guidelines and the Deconditioning Spiral.
  • Integration with CBT: PNE and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) are complementary. PNE provides the educational foundation (“here is how pain works”) and CBT provides the behavioral tools (“here is how to respond differently”). The combination is more effective than either alone. See Psychological Support.
Finding PNE-trained providers: Look for physical therapists, psychologists, or pain physicians who have completed the Therapeutic Neuroscience Education (TNE) certification or the Explain Pain curriculum (Lorimer Moseley and David Butler). The 2025 Cincinnati Children’s guideline specifically recommends PNE as part of first-line management for pediatric chronic musculoskeletal pain. Request PNE as a component of any multidisciplinary pain program.
CBD/Cannabinoid Evidence — Proceed with Extreme Caution
Families increasingly ask about CBD for chronic pain management. The evidence base remains thin and the risks are real:
  • Adult data: A 2025 systematic review in Pain Medicine found moderate evidence for neuropathic pain in adults (NNT=11 — meaning 11 patients must be treated for one to benefit beyond placebo). This is a modest effect size.
  • Pediatric data: There are NO pediatric randomized controlled trials. Only 2 case series in pediatric chronic pain exist (n=24 total), far too small to draw conclusions about efficacy or safety.
  • Drug interactions: CBD inhibits CYP3A4 and CYP2C19 — two major liver enzymes that metabolize many common medications including gabapentin prodrugs, certain antiepileptics, and SSRIs. This creates real risk of dangerous interactions in children on multiple medications.
  • AAP position (2025): The American Academy of Pediatrics states there is insufficient evidence to recommend cannabinoids for pediatric pain, but acknowledges that some families are using them and physicians should discuss this openly.
  • If families pursue CBD despite limited evidence: Use only pharmaceutical-grade products with third-party Certificate of Analysis (COA) testing, start at the lowest dose (0.5 mg/kg/day), and monitor liver enzymes (AST/ALT) at baseline and regularly.
  • THC is contraindicated under age 25 due to well-documented neurodevelopmental concerns including effects on the developing endocannabinoid system, white matter integrity, and executive function.
