Proprioception & Fall Prevention in Marshall Syndrome

Medical Disclaimer
This information is compiled from peer-reviewed research and clinical guidelines. It is intended for educational discussion with qualified medical professionals. These are NOT treatment recommendations. Balance training programs and home safety modifications should be designed in consultation with a physical therapist familiar with connective tissue disorders, hypermobility, and pediatric ophthalmology as relevant.

Falls in Marshall syndrome carry consequences that go well beyond a typical childhood tumble. The syndrome impairs balance through three overlapping sensory systems simultaneously, while simultaneously increasing the danger of every fall. Understanding this triple vulnerability is the foundation of an effective prevention strategy.

The Triple Sensory Deficit

Stable upright balance depends on the continuous integration of three sensory streams: vision, vestibular input from the inner ear, and proprioception from joints, muscles, and skin. When all three are healthy, the nervous system can compensate for degradation in any one of them. In Marshall syndrome, all three are compromised by the same root cause — defective type XI collagen (COL11A1).

1. Visual Deficit

Marshall syndrome is associated with high myopia, often severe, arising from abnormal collagen in the lens and sclera. Many individuals also have an elevated risk of vitreous degeneration, early-onset cataracts, and retinal detachment. The practical consequence for balance is significant:

  • Uncorrected or poorly corrected myopia blurs environmental reference points the visual system uses to judge distance, depth, and postural drift
  • Even with optimal correction, visual processing of peripheral flow — a key cue for detecting sway — may be impaired
  • In low light environments (dim rooms, nighttime), the visual channel is further attenuated precisely when the other two channels are most needed as backup
Visual and balance assessment: Ophthalmology and physical therapy evaluations should be coordinated. Balance assessments should always be performed both with and without corrective lenses, and the PT should know the current corrected and uncorrected visual acuity. A child who appears adequately balanced in a bright clinic with glasses may be significantly impaired at home in low light without them.

2. Vestibular Deficit

The vestibular apparatus — the semicircular canals and otolith organs of the inner ear — is structurally dependent on collagen for the integrity of the membranous labyrinth, the tectorial membrane of the cochlea, and the surrounding connective tissue. In Marshall syndrome, COL11A1 mutations directly affect the tectorial membrane, and vestibular structures share this collagen composition.

  • Vestibular hypofunction results in reduced or unreliable detection of head acceleration and tilt
  • Clinically, this presents as difficulty maintaining balance with eyes closed or on unstable surfaces, motion sensitivity, and tendency toward wider stance
  • Hearing loss in Marshall syndrome (conductive and sensorineural) reflects the same inner ear collagen pathology and often co-occurs with vestibular changes, though the correlation is not absolute
  • Vestibular deficits are frequently subclinical — not causing obvious vertigo — but detectable on formal testing and contributing meaningfully to balance instability

3. Proprioceptive Deficit

Proprioception is the continuous sensory reporting from mechanoreceptors in joint capsules, ligaments, tendons, and muscle spindles that informs the brain of limb position, joint angle, and movement without requiring visual confirmation. These mechanoreceptors are embedded in collagen-rich connective tissue. When that tissue is abnormal — as in Marshall syndrome — mechanoreceptor function is altered.

  • Hypermobile joints in Marshall syndrome have lax capsules with reduced tension at end range, which is precisely when joint-position sense signals are normally strongest
  • The result is a delayed or attenuated signal: the brain receives less accurate information about where the limb is and how fast it is moving
  • This is distinct from weakness — proprioceptive deficit affects timing and accuracy of postural corrections even when muscle strength is adequate
  • The ankle joint is particularly important: ankle proprioception is the first-line defense against trip-and-fall, and ankle hypermobility in connective tissue disorders consistently impairs this function
Why all three matter together: Healthy balance relies on redundancy — if one channel fails momentarily, the others compensate. In Marshall syndrome, all three are operating below baseline simultaneously. The margin for error that healthy individuals take for granted is substantially reduced or absent. A minor perturbation that a typical child absorbs automatically may exceed the recovery capacity of a child with Marshall syndrome.

Why Falls in Marshall Syndrome Are More Dangerous

The impaired sensory triad increases fall frequency, but the downstream consequences of those falls are also substantially more serious than in typical children or adults.

Fall Risk Red Flags — When to Treat Balance as Urgent
  • Recent retinal detachment, tear, or lattice degeneration: Any fall risk that could produce head impact must be treated as a medical emergency concern. Ophthalmology should explicitly advise on activity restrictions and contact sports before balance training begins.
  • Known or suspected craniocervical instability (CCI) or cervical myelopathy: Falls with head or neck impact can cause catastrophic spinal cord injury in the context of existing instability. A neurosurgery or spine specialist must clear the patient for activity before any balance training that risks falls.
  • History of fracture or known low bone density: Falls in the context of reduced bone density carry fracture risk disproportionate to the apparent force. Orthopedic evaluation and DEXA scan are appropriate before intensive balance programs.
  • Rapid balance deterioration: A sudden significant change in balance ability should prompt neurological evaluation rather than an assumption that it is “just hypermobility.” Chiari malformation, tethered cord, and cervical myelopathy can all worsen acutely.

Retinal Detachment Risk

High myopia in Marshall syndrome is associated with vitreous liquefaction and peripheral retinal changes that elevate the baseline risk of retinal detachment. Blunt head trauma — even relatively minor impact — can precipitate detachment in an already vulnerable retina. This is not a theoretical risk: it is the reason that many ophthalmologists advise restrictions on contact sports and activities with fall risk for highly myopic patients independent of any other diagnosis. In Marshall syndrome, that vulnerability is compounded.

Cervical Injury Risk

Craniocervical instability and cervical spine involvement occur in a subset of Marshall syndrome patients due to ligamentous laxity and potential structural anomalies. In this context, a fall that produces whiplash-type loading or direct head impact may cause neurological injury from cord compression or vascular compromise. Every patient with Marshall syndrome should have their cervical spine evaluated before high-risk physical activities, and any fall that produces new neurological symptoms (tingling, weakness, altered bladder or bowel function) requires urgent evaluation.

Fracture Risk

Reduced bone mineral density has been reported in connective tissue disorders with COL11A1 involvement. Normal-force falls that would produce only bruising in typical children may produce fractures in this population. This is an additional reason why fall prevention, rather than just fall recovery training, is the priority.

Proprioception Assessment: What Clinicians Measure

Assessment context: Balance and proprioception assessments in Marshall syndrome should be interpreted with awareness of the triple-deficit picture. A score that appears “mild” by population norms may represent a substantial impairment when considered against the elevated consequences of falls in this population.

Romberg Test

The Romberg test is the simplest screening tool for sensory ataxia. The patient stands with feet together, arms at sides, first with eyes open and then with eyes closed. A positive Romberg — significantly increased sway or loss of balance with eyes closed compared to eyes open — indicates that the patient is compensating for vestibular or proprioceptive deficits with vision. When vision is removed, the compensation fails. In Marshall syndrome, the Romberg test should be considered part of every routine physical assessment. A positive result identifies patients who will be disproportionately impaired in low-light environments or when visual attention is directed elsewhere (reading, looking at a phone, navigating unfamiliar terrain).

Single-Leg Stance Timing

Timed single-leg stance (eyes open and eyes closed) is a practical, equipment-free measure of functional balance. Age-referenced norms exist and allow comparison to typical development. For Marshall syndrome patients, the eyes-open and eyes-closed scores should be reported separately: a wide gap between the two scores indicates high visual dependence and predicts greater fall risk in low-light or distracted conditions. Progressive improvement in timed single-leg stance is also one of the most useful metrics for tracking response to a balance training program over time.

Joint Position Sense Testing

Joint position sense (JPS) testing quantifies proprioceptive accuracy directly. In the most common approach, the patient replicates a target joint angle with eyes closed, and the angular error is measured. The ankle and knee are the joints most relevant to fall prevention. Clinicians may also use threshold to detection of passive motion (TTDPM), measuring how large a movement must be before the patient detects it. High threshold values indicate reduced proprioceptive sensitivity. JPS testing is not always performed in routine physical therapy evaluations but should be requested when hypermobility is present, as it directly informs the design of proprioceptive retraining exercises.

Dynamic Balance Assessment

Static balance tasks measure only part of the picture. Dynamic assessments evaluate balance during movement and perturbation, which is closer to real-world fall scenarios.

  • Balance Error Scoring System (BESS): Six conditions across two surfaces (firm and foam) and three stance positions (double-leg, single-leg, tandem). Errors are counted (each sway, step, or loss of position). BESS is widely used in sports medicine and has published norms for children and adolescents. It provides a structured baseline and follow-up measure.
  • Y-Balance Test (YBT): The patient stands on one leg and reaches as far as possible in three directions (anterior, posteromedial, posterolateral) with the free limb. Composite reach distance normalized to limb length predicts lower-extremity injury risk and reflects integrated balance, strength, and flexibility. Significant asymmetry between limbs is a clinical flag for increased fall or injury risk.
  • Force platform posturography: If available at a referral center, computerized dynamic posturography provides quantitative decomposition of visual, vestibular, and somatosensory contributions to balance. This can confirm the triple-deficit pattern and guide targeted therapy. It is not required for treatment but provides the clearest mechanistic picture.

Progressive Balance Training Program

Balance training for Marshall syndrome should be progressive, age-appropriate, explicitly supervised until competency is established, and designed with awareness of the contraindications listed above. The goal is to build proprioceptive neuromuscular skill within safe limits, not to achieve maximum challenge as rapidly as possible.

General principles for all ages:
  • Always train near a wall, stable surface, or with a spotter present — the goal is skill building, not fall tolerance training
  • Progress surfaces before removing visual compensation: master an exercise on firm ground before attempting it on foam or an unstable surface
  • Eyes-closed variants dramatically increase proprioceptive demand — introduce them only when the eyes-open version is fully stable
  • Bare feet or thin-soled shoes improve sensory feedback from the floor; thick cushioned soles attenuate it
  • Brief, frequent sessions (10–15 minutes daily) produce better proprioceptive adaptation than infrequent longer sessions
  • Stop immediately and rest if joint pain, dizziness, or unusual fatigue occurs

Ages 3–6: Foundational Balance Play

At this age, balance training is most effective when embedded in play. Direct instruction is less effective than creating environments that naturally challenge and develop balance.

  • Balance beam walking: Start with a beam taped on the floor (no height), progress to a low wooden beam (5–8 cm height maximum). Walk forward, then sideways, then backward. The goal is controlled stepping, not speed.
  • Stepping stones: Scattered foam squares, tiles, or commercially available stepping stone sets. Hop or step from one to the next with control. Vary spacing to require single-leg landings and weight shifting.
  • Animal walks: Bear crawls, crab walks, and frog jumps activate proprioceptive feedback through the upper and lower extremities simultaneously. They also develop core stability that underpins all balance.
  • Inclined surfaces: Walking up and down gentle inclines (grassy slopes, ramps) challenges ankle proprioception in a low-risk setting.
  • Standing on one foot: Encourage any single-leg stance during play — the flamingo game, kicking a ball, stepping over obstacles. Count seconds and make it a game.

Ages 7–12: Structured Proprioceptive Training

School-age children can participate in more structured exercise with clear instruction. Proprioceptive training at this age produces durable neuromuscular adaptations if practiced consistently.

  • BOSU ball activities: Standing on the dome side (easier) or flat side (harder). Progress from double-leg to single-leg stance. Add arm movements to increase challenge. The unstable surface amplifies proprioceptive demand without requiring height or high-speed movement.
  • Single-leg activities: Single-leg squat to a chair (touch and return, not full sit), single-leg deadlift with bodyweight, clock reaches from single-leg stance (reach one foot to 12 positions around an imaginary clock).
  • Tandem walking: Heel-to-toe walking in a straight line, arms out for balance. Progress to tandem walking on a line with eyes closed (near wall for safety). Tandem walking challenges the mediolateral balance system that is most relevant to trip recovery.
  • Eyes-closed challenges: Any stable exercise performed with eyes closed dramatically increases proprioceptive load. Start with double-leg stance eyes closed on firm ground. Progress to single-leg eyes closed only when double-leg is fully stable.
  • Trampoline activities (supervised): Controlled bouncing and landing on a mini-trampoline builds proprioceptive feedback through repeated impact loading. Must be supervised, away from edges, and avoided if there are current retinal concerns or cervical instability — check with ophthalmology and neurology first.
  • Foam pad standing: Standing on a thick foam pad in various stances. Inexpensive, effective, and safe when done near a wall.

Ages 13 and Up: Advanced and Sport-Specific Training

Adolescents can participate in more complex and functionally relevant balance training. The emphasis shifts toward proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation and real-world movement patterns.

  • Sport-specific balance integration: Balance challenges incorporated into the specific movements of the individual’s chosen sport or activity. Single-leg landings from jumps, lateral shuffle with deceleration, pivoting under controlled conditions.
  • Perturbation training: A clinician or trained partner delivers small, unpredictable pushes or surface disturbances while the patient maintains balance. This trains reactive postural responses — the automatic corrections that prevent a stumble from becoming a fall. Must be conducted by a qualified physical therapist initially.
  • Proprioceptive neuromuscular facilitation (PNF): Diagonal movement patterns (D1 and D2 flexion/extension spirals) combined with resistance engage proprioceptive pathways through full range and challenge the nervous system to coordinate multi-joint position sense simultaneously. PNF is typically taught by a physical therapist and then practiced independently.
  • Agility ladder drills: Controlled footwork through an agility ladder on firm ground develops rapid, precise foot placement — a key component of trip recovery. Start slowly with full concentration; speed develops over weeks.
  • Dual-task training: Balance tasks performed while simultaneously completing a cognitive task (counting backward, catching a ball). Real-world falls frequently occur when attention is divided. Dual-task training directly targets this vulnerability.
Evidence-Based Proprioception Training Protocol
Proprioception is impaired in hypermobility because lax ligaments provide less sensory feedback to the brain about joint position. Structured proprioceptive training can partially compensate for this deficit. A 2025 systematic review found that proprioception training reduces injury rate by 45% in hypermobile populations.
  • Step 1 — Wobble board: Begin on a flat wobble board, then progress to a rounded (rocker) board. Stand with both feet, progressing to single-leg stance. The unstable surface forces continuous proprioceptive correction.
  • Step 2 — Single-leg stance: Start with eyes open near a wall for safety, then progress to eyes closed. Target: 30 seconds of stable single-leg stance with eyes closed. This is one of the most reliable measures of proprioceptive improvement.
  • Step 3 — Perturbation training: A therapist or trained partner delivers gentle, unexpected pushes while the patient maintains balance. This trains the reactive postural responses that prevent stumbles from becoming falls. Must be supervised initially.
  • Step 4 — Closed kinetic chain exercises: Wall sits and mini squats with tactile or visual feedback. These exercises load joints through functional ranges while the foot remains fixed, maximizing proprioceptive input through weight-bearing surfaces.
  • Step 5 — Aquatic proprioception: Water provides natural resistance and joint support simultaneously, allowing proprioceptive training with reduced fall risk and reduced joint stress. Particularly valuable for patients with pain-limited land-based exercise tolerance.

Frequency: 3 times per week, 15–20 minutes per session. Incorporate exercises into daily routine where possible (e.g., single-leg stance while brushing teeth).

For children: Gamify the protocol — balance beam walks as tightrope adventures, Simon Says with balance poses, yoga poses as animal shapes. Engagement drives consistency, and consistency drives adaptation.

Marshall-specific consideration: Visual impairment in Marshall syndrome complicates proprioception training because patients cannot rely on visual compensation the way most trainees do. Training must place heavier reliance on vestibular and somatosensory inputs from the outset, which paradoxically makes the training more effective at building true proprioceptive skill rather than visual-dependent balance.

Exercise Dose-Response Evidence

Knowing what exercises to do is only half the picture. The research on exercise dosing for proprioception and balance in hypermobile populations provides specific guidance on how much and how often training must occur to produce measurable improvement.

10-Week Balance Training: Pain, Function, and Fear

A controlled trial of 10-week structured balance training in patients with joint hypermobility demonstrated improvements across three domains simultaneously:

  • Pain reduction: Statistically significant decreases in self-reported pain intensity at rest and during activity, persisting through the follow-up period
  • Functional improvement: Measurable gains in dynamic balance (Y-Balance Test composite scores), single-leg stance duration, and self-reported physical function
  • Reduced kinesiophobia: Participants showed significant reductions in fear of movement (Tampa Scale of Kinesiophobia scores). This is particularly important because fear-avoidance is one of the strongest predictors of disability in chronic pain — breaking the cycle of fear and avoidance produces benefits that extend far beyond the balance improvements themselves
The kinesiophobia finding matters for Marshall syndrome: Many children with Marshall syndrome avoid physical activity because they or their parents fear that movement will cause injury. While caution is appropriate (see the fall risk considerations above), excessive avoidance leads to deconditioning, which paradoxically increases injury risk. Structured balance training provides a controlled environment where the child discovers through experience that guided movement is safe, directly reducing fear-avoidance beliefs.

Connective Tissue Manipulation Combined with Exercise

Research comparing exercise alone versus connective tissue manipulation (CTM) combined with exercise found that the combination produced significantly greater proprioceptive improvement than exercise alone. CTM is a manual therapy technique that applies specific tension to fascial planes, stimulating mechanoreceptors in the connective tissue and increasing proprioceptive input to the central nervous system. For children with Marshall syndrome, where the connective tissue itself is the source of proprioceptive impairment, this combined approach targets the deficit at its origin.

Practical implication: Ask the physical therapist whether they incorporate manual therapy techniques (connective tissue manipulation, myofascial release, or joint mobilization) alongside exercise prescription. The evidence suggests that hands-on treatment enhances proprioceptive outcomes beyond what exercise alone achieves.

Three-Phase Progressive Exercise Protocol

The most effective exercise programs for hypermobility-related proprioceptive deficits use a phased progression that systematically increases demand on the neuromuscular system:

PhaseWeeksFocusExercise Types
Phase 1: Isometric Weeks 1–4 Joint stabilization without movement. Building baseline muscle activation around joints without challenging range of motion Wall sits, isometric quad sets, static hip abduction holds, plank holds (modified as needed), single-leg stance on firm ground. All exercises held for 10–30 seconds with controlled breathing
Phase 2: Isotonic Weeks 5–8 Controlled movement through range. Building strength and proprioceptive feedback during dynamic loading Bodyweight squats (controlled depth), step-ups, lateral band walks, heel raises, single-leg deadlifts (bodyweight). All movements performed slowly (3-second concentric, 3-second eccentric) to maximize proprioceptive input
Phase 3: Functional Weeks 9–12 Real-world movement patterns. Integrating strength and proprioception into activities that replicate daily life and recreational demands Agility ladder drills (controlled pace), perturbation training on unstable surfaces, multi-directional stepping, dual-task balance challenges, sport-specific movements adapted to the child’s interests and safety clearance

Recommended Frequency and Structure

  • Supervised sessions: 2–3 times per week with a physical therapist, particularly during Phase 1 and the transition between phases. Supervision ensures correct form (critical in hypermobile joints where compensatory movement patterns develop easily) and allows progressive challenge adjustments
  • Daily home exercises: A brief (10–15 minute) home program performed every day between supervised sessions. Consistency matters more than duration — daily proprioceptive stimulus drives faster neural adaptation than infrequent longer sessions
  • Phase progression criteria: Advance from one phase to the next based on demonstrated competency, not on a fixed calendar. A child who has not mastered isometric holds with stable form should not progress to isotonic loading regardless of the week count
  • Maintenance after 12 weeks: After completing the 12-week protocol, reduce to 1–2 supervised sessions per month with continued daily home exercises. Proprioceptive gains are maintained with ongoing practice but will deteriorate within 4–6 weeks of complete cessation
Phase 1 is not optional. The isometric phase builds the baseline joint stabilization that protects hypermobile joints during the more demanding later phases. Skipping directly to isotonic or functional exercises in a hypermobile child risks joint irritation, subluxation, or pain that undermines the entire program. Four weeks of “boring” isometric work pays dividends in safer, more effective progression.

Home Safety Modifications

Environmental modification is not optional or supplementary — it is a core component of fall prevention in Marshall syndrome. The goal is to reduce fall triggers in the home to a minimum while balance training builds long-term capacity.

Bathroom

  • Non-slip mats: Inside the shower or tub, immediately outside the shower, and at the bathroom sink. Suction-cup bath mats inside the tub should be checked regularly for secure adhesion. Absorbent non-slip mats outside the tub prevent slipping on wet tile.
  • Grab bars: Install anchored grab bars (not suction-cup towel bar substitutes) in the shower, beside the toilet, and at the tub entry point. These must be wall-stud mounted to bear weight reliably.
  • Shower seat: A shower bench or fold-down shower seat eliminates the need to stand on a wet surface while reaching, bending, or washing hair — all activities that reduce visual attention and shift weight unexpectedly.
  • Lever-style faucets: Reduce the reaching and twisting that shift the center of mass.

Nighttime and Low-Light Conditions

Nightlights are among the highest-priority safety interventions in Marshall syndrome. Visual compensation for vestibular and proprioceptive deficits is eliminated in darkness. The combination of high myopia and dark environments creates extreme fall risk, particularly for nighttime bathroom trips.

  • Place motion-activated nightlights in every hallway, at stair tops and bottoms, and in the bathroom
  • Ensure that nightlights activate at a low enough light level to be on well before the person enters the dark area
  • Keep eyeglasses within reach of the bed and establish a habit of putting them on before standing, even for nighttime bathroom trips
  • Avoid sleeping medications that cause grogginess on nighttime awakening unless specifically discussed with a physician, as cognitive impairment compounds balance impairment

Stair Safety

  • Handrails on both sides of every staircase, not just one
  • Handrails should extend the full length of the stairs and be graspable (round profile, not flat board)
  • Stair treads should be non-slip — carpet or applied non-slip strips. Slick hardwood stairs are particularly hazardous.
  • Avoid carrying items that occupy both hands on stairs; use a backpack or tote bag that frees hands for the rail
  • Ensure adequate lighting at all times on stairs; motion-activated lights are appropriate

General Living Areas

  • Clear pathways: Remove items from the floor that create trip hazards — shoes, bags, charging cables, pet toys. Establish designated locations for these items off the floor.
  • Secure area rugs: Loose rugs are one of the most common fall causes in the home. Use non-slip rug pads or remove loose rugs entirely from high-traffic areas and from areas near stairs.
  • Furniture arrangement: Ensure that furniture is arranged to provide a continuous path of stable objects to hold while crossing a room. Avoid wide open spaces with no intermediate support if balance is significantly impaired.
  • Clutter management: Maintain consistent, clear floor paths particularly in areas traversed in low light or in a hurry (bedrooms, hallways, bathroom routes).

Kitchen

  • Reorganize frequently used items to accessible shelf heights that do not require stepping on stools or overhead reaching with shifted center of mass
  • Use a sturdy step stool with a handle (not a stepladder) for any reaching that cannot be eliminated; never use chairs or boxes
  • Anti-fatigue mats at the sink and stove reduce discomfort during prolonged standing and help maintain stable footing on potentially wet surfaces
  • Avoid rushing in the kitchen — time pressure during cooking is a consistent context for distraction-related falls

When to Request Vestibular Physical Therapy

Standard physical therapy addresses musculoskeletal strength, flexibility, and general balance. Vestibular physical therapy is a subspecialty that specifically addresses dysfunction of the vestibular system. In Marshall syndrome, where inner ear collagen involvement may produce vestibular hypofunction, vestibular PT may be indicated and is often underutilized.

Request a vestibular PT referral when any of the following are present:

  • Motion sensitivity: nausea, disorientation, or significant dizziness with head movement, riding in vehicles, or scrolling visual environments
  • Significantly positive Romberg test (much worse with eyes closed than open)
  • Documented vestibular hypofunction on formal testing (videonystagmography, rotary chair, VEMP testing)
  • Persistent complaints of dizziness or imbalance that are not explained by orthostatic hypotension or medication effects
  • Falls occurring specifically during or immediately after head movement
  • Balance that deteriorates substantially at the end of the day (suggesting compensatory fatigue)
What vestibular PT involves: Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) uses gaze stabilization exercises, habituation exercises, and balance retraining specifically designed to promote central compensation for peripheral vestibular hypofunction. It is an evidence-based specialty with strong outcomes data for vestibular disorders. A vestibular PT evaluation will also formally assess all three sensory channels and can guide the relative emphasis of the balance training program. Practitioners can be found through the Vestibular Disorders Association (VEDA) provider directory.

Footwear Considerations

Footwear for hypermobile feet involves a genuine trade-off that does not have a single correct answer for all individuals. Understanding the competing factors allows for informed, individualized decisions.

The Core Trade-Off

Stability-focused footwear (motion control shoes, high-top designs, rigid orthotics) provides external support that reduces the risk of ankle rolling and excessive pronation. However, it also attenuates sensory feedback from the ground and may reduce the proprioceptive training effect that comes from the foot adapting to terrain variations. Maximally flexible, thin-soled footwear maximizes sensory input but provides no external ankle support and may allow harmful ranges of motion in hypermobile joints.

Practical Guidance

  • Avoid completely unsupported footwear for high-risk activities: Flip-flops, backless sandals, and worn-out shoes with collapsed heel counters eliminate ankle support at exactly the moments when proprioceptive demands are highest. Reserve these for static, controlled environments only.
  • For daily walking and outdoor activities: A well-fitted shoe with a stable heel counter, moderate sole thickness, and good midfoot structure. This provides external support while allowing sufficient sensory input. Custom orthotic inserts from a podiatrist or orthotist experienced with hypermobility can address individual foot mechanics.
  • For balance training sessions: Discuss barefoot or minimal shoe training with the physical therapist. Controlled bare-foot proprioceptive exercises on firm surfaces are frequently used to maximize sensory input during training, while supportive footwear is then worn for real-world activities. This combines the training benefit of high sensory input with the safety benefit of support during uncontrolled environments.
  • High-top designs: High-top shoes and boots restrict ankle range of motion and may reduce the frequency of ankle rolling in significantly hypermobile individuals. The trade-off is some reduction in proprioceptive input through the restricted joint. For individuals with a history of ankle sprains or instability, the risk reduction often outweighs this cost.
  • Ankle bracing: Lace-up ankle braces or semi-rigid ankle orthoses provide external support during high-risk activities. They do not replace proprioceptive training but serve as a reasonable risk-reduction measure for sports, hiking, or other uneven terrain activities while training is still developing. Discuss with the PT whether continued bracing may become a crutch that limits proprioceptive development.

Technology Aids

A growing category of technology-assisted balance tools can supplement a physical therapy program. None replace supervised PT but some have reasonable evidence for home use.

Vibrotactile Biofeedback Devices

These devices detect postural sway and deliver vibrotactile cues (gentle vibration at the waist or ankle) when sway exceeds a threshold, providing a substitute sensory channel that does not depend on the compromised vestibular or proprioceptive systems. The FDA-cleared Quell and research-validated devices such as the BalanceBelt and SensBalance Vest have demonstrated measurable improvements in sway metrics in vestibular-impaired populations. While adoption remains limited by cost, these represent a mechanistically sound approach for individuals with documented vestibular or proprioceptive deficits.

Balance Training Applications

Several apps use phone accelerometers or pressure-sensing balance boards to provide real-time feedback during balance exercises and track progress over time:

  • Nintendo Switch Balance Board games (Wii Fit era): still available and still provide effective real-time visual feedback for weight shift training. Not a clinical tool, but motivating for children and adolescents and backed by positive evidence in older adult populations.
  • Humon and Sway Balance apps: Use phone accelerometers for postural sway measurement. Useful for tracking progress at home between PT sessions.
  • Pressure-sensing balance boards (e.g., Bobo board, Wii Balance Board clones): Provide weight-distribution visualization and sway metrics during standing exercises. Useful for keeping training engaging and for quantifying improvement.

Smart Home Lighting

Motion-activated smart lighting systems (Philips Hue, Lutron Caséta, or standard motion-sensor switches) can ensure that every nighttime bathroom or kitchen trip is automatically lit. This is a practical, low-cost, high-impact implementation of the nighttime safety principle described above. Whole-home motion-sensing lighting is achievable for a reasonable one-time cost and eliminates the reliance on habit or memory to turn on lights in drowsy or rushed conditions.

Starting a home exercise program: Begin with three exercises only. Master them before adding more. A 10-minute daily program that is actually done every day produces far better outcomes than an elaborate 45-minute program that is skipped most days. Suggested starting exercises for school-age children: (1) timed single-leg stance near a wall, eyes open — three sets of maximum duration each leg; (2) tandem walking along a hallway, hands free — five lengths; (3) clock reaches from single-leg stance — three rounds each leg. Reassess with the PT after four weeks and progress from there.

Sensory Processing and Proprioceptive Input Strategies

Proprioceptive impairment in Marshall syndrome does not exist in isolation. Many individuals experience broader sensory processing differences that affect how the nervous system organizes and responds to all sensory input — not just joint position sense. Occupational therapists with sensory integration (SI) certification are trained to evaluate and treat these overlapping deficits, and their involvement adds a dimension that physical therapy alone may not address.

Assess sensory processing at diagnosis: The Sensory Profile 2 (SP-2) is a standardized caregiver questionnaire that maps how a child processes sensory information across six domains (auditory, visual, touch, movement, body position, oral). It should be administered at the time of Marshall syndrome diagnosis and repeated annually. Results guide the design of a sensory diet and identify children who may benefit from targeted sensory integration therapy. Request administration by an occupational therapist with SI certification (look for the ASI or SIPT credential).

Weighted Blankets for Anxiety and Sleep

Weighted blankets provide sustained deep pressure input that activates proprioceptors in the skin, muscles, and joints. This deep pressure touch has a calming effect on the autonomic nervous system, reducing sympathetic arousal and promoting parasympathetic (rest-and-digest) activity. For children with Marshall syndrome who experience anxiety, difficulty settling for sleep, or sensory-seeking behavior, weighted blankets can be a low-risk, high-benefit intervention.

  • Weight guideline: Approximately 10% of body weight, plus or minus 1–2 pounds. A 60-pound child uses a 6-pound blanket. This is a guideline, not a rigid rule — some children prefer slightly more or less weight
  • Safety considerations: The child must be able to remove the blanket independently. Weighted blankets are not appropriate for infants or toddlers who cannot reposition themselves. Avoid in children with respiratory compromise
  • Timing: Most effective when introduced 20–30 minutes before the desired sleep onset. Can also be used during seated homework or reading periods to promote calm focus
  • Evidence: While the research base is strongest for anxiety and insomnia in adults, pediatric occupational therapy literature consistently supports deep pressure input as a regulatory strategy, and families frequently report meaningful subjective benefit

Compression Garments: Dual Purpose

Compression garments serve a dual purpose in Marshall syndrome that makes them particularly valuable. First, they provide continuous proprioceptive input by maintaining gentle sustained pressure against the skin and underlying mechanoreceptors — effectively amplifying the body-position signals that are attenuated by lax connective tissue. Second, they provide mechanical joint support that reduces the frequency of subluxations and microtrauma in hypermobile joints.

  • Compression shirts and shorts: Provide trunk and proximal joint proprioceptive input. Useful for children who seem unaware of their body position, frequently bump into objects, or use excessive force in fine motor tasks
  • Compression socks and sleeves: Target the ankle and wrist, where proprioceptive deficit has the most direct impact on balance and fine motor control
  • Graduated medical compression vs. athletic compression: Medical-grade graduated compression (prescribed by a physician) is appropriate when vascular or lymphatic concerns exist. For proprioceptive purposes alone, well-fitting athletic compression garments are usually sufficient and more accessible

Therapy Ball Seating

Replacing a standard chair with a therapy ball (also called a stability ball or Swiss ball) during seated activities creates a mildly unstable surface that requires continuous postural micro-adjustments. These adjustments activate the same proprioceptive and vestibular pathways that balance training targets, but in a low-risk seated context.

  • Sizing: The child’s hips and knees should both be at approximately 90 degrees when seated. Feet must be flat on the floor
  • Duration: Start with 10–15 minute periods and extend gradually. Some children fatigue quickly on a therapy ball; others prefer it indefinitely
  • School use: Therapy ball seating can be written into a 504 plan or IEP as a sensory accommodation. Some schools use ball chairs (therapy balls with a stabilizing base ring) to reduce rollaway risk
  • Alternative: A wobble cushion (inflatable disc placed on a standard chair) provides a milder version of the same proprioceptive challenge with less disruption in a classroom setting

The Sensory Diet

A sensory diet is a structured schedule of sensory activities designed by an occupational therapist with SI certification, tailored to the individual child’s sensory processing profile. It is not a food diet — it is a daily “menu” of sensory inputs provided at strategic intervals throughout the day to maintain optimal arousal and regulation.

Components of a Marshall-specific sensory diet may include:
  • Heavy work activities (pushing, pulling, carrying, climbing) scheduled before tasks requiring sustained attention
  • Compression or weighted input during transitions or high-anxiety periods
  • Proprioceptive breaks every 30–45 minutes during school — wall push-ups, chair push-ups, or carrying books to the library
  • Vestibular input (swinging, spinning, rocking) calibrated to the child’s tolerance — some children with vestibular hypofunction are sensory-seeking and benefit from increased vestibular input, while others are sensory-avoidant and require gradual, controlled exposure
  • Oral motor input (chewing gum, crunchy snacks, vibrating toothbrush) for children who seek oral proprioceptive input

The sensory diet should be reviewed and updated every 3–6 months as the child’s sensory processing and daily demands evolve. The OT designs the diet, but parents, teachers, and the child all implement it.
