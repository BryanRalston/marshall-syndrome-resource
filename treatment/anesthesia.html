Anesthesia Safety

This section contains safety-critical information. Every family member should be aware of it, and a printed copy should accompany the child to every medical procedure requiring sedation or general anesthesia.

The Data

A study of 502 patients with Stickler/Marshall syndrome undergoing anesthesia found specific risks that anesthesiologists must be prepared for.

Airway Concerns

  • Pierre Robin sequence (present in ~24% of Marshall patients) creates a difficult airway — the small, recessed jaw makes visualization of the vocal cords and passage of a breathing tube more challenging
  • Single-attempt intubation success rate: 93.2% — meaning nearly 7% of cases required multiple attempts, which is significantly higher than the general population rate
  • Risk factors for difficult intubation:
    • Receding mandible (micrognathia): odds ratio 3.0 — three times the normal difficulty risk
    • History of cleft palate repair: odds ratio 2.9

What to Do

  1. Print and carry the OrphanAnesthesia guidelines for Stickler/Marshall syndrome. OrphanAnesthesia is a project that creates anesthesia-specific guidelines for rare diseases, written for anesthesiologists. These guidelines can be downloaded at orphananesthesia.eu. Give a copy to the anesthesiologist before the day of any procedure.
  2. Inform every anesthesia provider about:
    • The COL11A1 diagnosis
    • Whether the child has Pierre Robin features (small jaw, history of cleft)
    • Whether cervical instability has been screened for — if not, or if there is known instability, cervical in-line stabilization must be maintained during intubation (no neck hyperextension)
    • Any cardiac history (see below)
  3. Request a pre-procedure anesthesia consultation for any elective procedure — don't let the first meeting with the anesthesiologist be on the morning of surgery
Comprehensive Perioperative Safety Protocol
Marshall syndrome affects multiple body systems, and each requires specific attention during any surgical procedure:
  • Airway management: Micrognathia combined with midface hypoplasia creates a potentially difficult intubation. A 502-patient case series documents significant airway complications in this population. Fiberoptic intubation equipment should be immediately available, and an experienced anesthesiologist should manage the airway. Avoid neck hyperextension due to cervical instability risk.
  • TMJ and mouth opening: Limited mouth opening is possible due to temporomandibular joint involvement — a smaller laryngoscope blade may be needed. Assess jaw mobility during the pre-operative consultation.
  • Eye protection: Protect eyes from pressure during prone positioning due to elevated retinal detachment risk. Tape eyelids carefully and use appropriate eye shields. Avoid any direct pressure on the globe.
  • Joint positioning: Hypermobile joints are at subluxation risk during positioning under anesthesia when protective muscle tone is absent. Pad bony prominences carefully and avoid positioning joints at end range.
  • Regional anesthesia preference: Regional anesthesia is preferred over general anesthesia where clinically appropriate, as it avoids airway manipulation entirely and reduces the risks associated with intubation in this population.
  • Post-operative considerations: Monitor for bleeding from fragile vessels and anticipate slower wound healing. Develop a pain management plan that accounts for the patient’s chronic pain background — standard post-operative protocols may be insufficient.
  • Pre-surgical timeline: Schedule an anesthesia consultation at least 2 weeks before any elective procedure. The anesthesiologist needs time to review the diagnosis, plan the airway approach, and arrange specialized equipment.

Local Anesthetic Resistance

Families and clinicians frequently report that local anesthesia “doesn’t work properly” in connective tissue disorders. This is not anecdotal coincidence — there are identified mechanisms that explain reduced local anesthetic (LA) effectiveness in collagen disorders, and understanding them allows clinicians to adapt their approach.

Mechanisms of Resistance

  • Rapid diffusion through loose connective tissue: Local anesthetics are deposited into tissue and must remain at a sufficient concentration around the target nerve to maintain blockade. In connective tissue disorders with lax, porous extracellular matrix, the anesthetic diffuses away from the injection site more rapidly than in normal tissue, resulting in faster clearance and shorter duration of effect. Evidence from EDS populations shows LA wearing off in approximately 60 minutes versus persisting for the expected duration in controls
  • Altered sodium channel sensitivity (SCN5A and related): Local anesthetics work by blocking voltage-gated sodium channels in nerve membranes. Pharmacogenomic variation in sodium channel genes (including SCN5A and SCN9A) can alter binding affinity, reducing the effectiveness of standard doses. Some patients may have functionally resistant sodium channel variants
  • CYP450 pharmacogenomic variation: The cytochrome P450 enzyme system metabolizes many local anesthetics. Genetic variation in CYP enzymes (particularly CYP1A2 and CYP3A4) can accelerate metabolism, shortening the effective duration of anesthesia. This variation is independent of the connective tissue disorder itself but may compound the tissue diffusion problem
  • Tissue pH changes: Inflammation and altered tissue composition in connective tissue disorders may shift local pH, affecting the ionization state of local anesthetics. LA drugs are weak bases that must be in their non-ionized form to cross nerve membranes. Acidic tissue environments (from inflammation or altered matrix chemistry) shift the equilibrium toward the ionized form, reducing the proportion of drug that can reach its target
EMLA cream is often insufficient. Topical anesthetic preparations (such as EMLA) rely on passive diffusion through intact skin, which is already a slow process. In connective tissue disorders with altered dermal structure, topical penetration may be further impaired. Do not assume that EMLA application has achieved adequate anesthesia — verify with the patient before proceeding. An additional local injection may be needed.

Clinical Strategies for Effective Local Anesthesia

StrategyRationale
Use articaine 4% as first-line agent Articaine has superior tissue diffusion compared to lidocaine due to its thiophene ring and ester linkage. Its ability to penetrate soft tissue and bone makes it particularly suitable when tissue architecture is altered. Preferred for dental procedures and minor surgical blocks
Increase volume of local anesthetic A larger depot compensates for faster diffusion away from the target nerve. Stay within safe maximum dose limits but use the upper end of the dosing range
Use supplementary nerve blocks Regional blocks closer to the nerve trunk (nerve blocks vs. local infiltration) place the anesthetic directly at the nerve sheath, reducing dependence on tissue diffusion. Consider adding a supplementary block if infiltration alone is inadequate
Allow 8–10 minutes for onset Onset time may be prolonged in altered connective tissue. Clinicians accustomed to 3–5 minute onset should wait longer before testing for adequate anesthesia and before beginning the procedure
Consider pharmacogenomic panel If local anesthetic resistance is a recurring problem, a pharmacogenomic panel testing CYP1A2, CYP3A4, and sodium channel gene variants can identify metabolic or receptor-level explanations and guide agent selection
Add epinephrine (vasoconstrictor) Epinephrine reduces local blood flow, slowing anesthetic clearance from the injection site. This directly counteracts the rapid diffusion problem. Use standard concentrations (1:100,000 or 1:200,000) unless contraindicated
Create an anesthesia protocol card. Families should carry a card (physical or digital) summarizing the child’s anesthesia history, including:
  • COL11A1 diagnosis and connective tissue disorder type
  • History of local anesthetic failures (agent, dose, procedure, and outcome)
  • Agents that have worked well and at what doses
  • Any known pharmacogenomic results
  • Airway management history (easy/difficult intubation, equipment required)
  • Other anesthesia-relevant features (cervical instability, cardiac findings, retinal status)
This card ensures that every new provider — dentist, surgeon, emergency physician — has immediate access to the patient’s specific anesthetic profile rather than starting from scratch each time.

Cardiac Considerations

Though less common, the following have been reported in COL11A1-related conditions and should be screened for:

  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (rare but serious) — affects anesthetic drug choices
  • Mitral valve prolapse — may require antibiotic prophylaxis for certain procedures

An echocardiogram at baseline is reasonable to rule out structural cardiac involvement.

Airway Management Protocol: Evidence-Based Approach

Marshall syndrome presents a predictable difficult airway due to the combination of micrognathia (small, recessed mandible) and midface hypoplasia (underdeveloped midface). These craniofacial features reduce mouth opening, limit mandibular space for tongue displacement, and alter the geometry of the oropharynx — all of which make direct visualization of the vocal cords and passage of an endotracheal tube more difficult.

PeDI-R Data: What the Evidence Shows

The Pediatric Difficult Intubation Registry (PeDI-R) is a multicenter registry that documents outcomes from difficult pediatric intubations across participating children’s hospitals. PeDI-R data provides the evidence base for the following recommendations:

  • Videolaryngoscopy as first-line approach: PeDI-R data demonstrates that videolaryngoscopy (VL) as the initial intubation technique — not as rescue after failed direct laryngoscopy — significantly improves first-pass success rates in children with craniofacial anomalies. In Marshall syndrome, where the difficult airway is predicted, VL should be the default first approach, not a backup plan.
  • Maintain spontaneous ventilation: For predicted difficult airways, the anesthesiologist should maintain spontaneous breathing during induction whenever possible. Neuromuscular blocking agents (paralytics) should not be administered until the airway is secured or a definitive rescue plan (including surgical airway access) is confirmed. If spontaneous ventilation is lost in a child with Marshall syndrome who cannot be intubated, the situation becomes immediately life-threatening.
  • Neutral neck positioning: Standard intubation technique involves extending the neck to align the oral, pharyngeal, and laryngeal axes for visualization. In Marshall syndrome, cervical instability risk requires neutral positioning throughout. Manual in-line stabilization (MILS) should be maintained during all airway manipulation. This constraint is an additional reason to prefer videolaryngoscopy, which provides better visualization with less neck extension than direct laryngoscopy.

Step-by-Step Airway Protocol for the Anesthesiologist

StepActionRationale
1. Pre-operative assessment Review craniofacial anatomy, prior intubation records, cervical spine imaging. Mallampati score, thyromental distance, jaw protrusion. Marshall syndrome = predicted difficult airway regardless of Mallampati score. Assessment confirms degree of difficulty and guides equipment selection.
2. Equipment preparation Videolaryngoscope (multiple blade sizes), fiber-optic bronchoscope, supraglottic airways (LMA), emergency surgical airway kit at bedside. All difficult airway equipment must be physically present and checked, not “available if needed.”
3. Induction with spontaneous ventilation Inhalational induction (sevoflurane) or carefully titrated IV induction maintaining spontaneous breathing. Avoid neuromuscular blockade until airway is secured. If intubation fails in a paralyzed child with micrognathia, mask ventilation may also be difficult — creating a “cannot intubate, cannot ventilate” emergency.
4. Intubation attempt Videolaryngoscopy as first-line. Neutral neck position with manual in-line stabilization. Smaller endotracheal tube available (0.5–1 size below predicted). Subglottic narrowing may be present in addition to supraglottic difficulty. Having a smaller tube immediately available prevents delay.
5. Rescue plan Defined before induction: supraglottic airway → fiber-optic intubation through SGA → surgical airway. Each step and its trigger clearly communicated to the team. The 502-patient series documents 7% requiring multiple attempts. A pre-defined rescue algorithm prevents ad-hoc decision-making during a crisis.
Micrognathia + midface hypoplasia = compound difficulty. Many anesthesiologists are experienced with one or the other of these features. In Marshall syndrome, both are often present simultaneously, creating a more challenging airway than either feature alone would produce. The micrognathia limits mandibular space and tongue displacement. The midface hypoplasia reduces the anterior-posterior depth of the nasopharynx and alters mask seal geometry. This combination warrants the most experienced airway manager available — not a trainee’s first attempt.
Research Update (R484): Anesthesia Considerations for COL11A1 Collagenopathies

Published anesthesia guidelines for collagen disorders have highlighted several Marshall syndrome-specific considerations beyond difficult airway management:

  • Total Intravenous Anesthesia (TIVA) preferred: TIVA using propofol and remifentanil infusions avoids the need for volatile anesthetic agents (sevoflurane, desflurane), which can increase intraocular pressure. For Marshall syndrome patients with high myopia and elevated retinal detachment risk, minimizing intraocular pressure fluctuations is an important safety consideration. TIVA also provides more precise control of anesthetic depth, reducing the hemodynamic instability that can worsen autonomic dysfunction
  • Cervical spine precautions are mandatory: Manual in-line stabilization (MILS) during all airway manipulation is required, not optional. Standard intubation technique that extends the neck is contraindicated until cervical instability has been ruled out by flexion-extension imaging. If cervical clearance has not been obtained, assume instability is present and proceed with neutral positioning
  • Succinylcholine avoidance: Consider avoiding succinylcholine (depolarizing neuromuscular blocker) due to the risk of masseter spasm in patients with TMJ involvement and the transient increase in intraocular pressure that accompanies fasciculations. Non-depolarizing agents (rocuronium with sugammadex reversal) provide a safer alternative
  • Regional anesthesia when feasible: For appropriate procedures (limb surgery, dental work, imaging under sedation), regional techniques avoid airway manipulation entirely and reduce systemic anesthetic exposure. Ultrasound-guided nerve blocks improve success rates and reduce the volume of local anesthetic needed — relevant given the local anesthetic diffusion issues described above
For families: At the pre-operative consultation (scheduled at least 2 weeks before any elective procedure), ask the anesthesiologist: (1) “Will you use a videolaryngoscope as your first approach?” (2) “What is your plan if intubation fails?” (3) “Will you maintain spontaneous breathing during induction?” If the anesthesiologist is unfamiliar with these concepts in the context of craniofacial anomalies, request a provider with pediatric difficult airway experience. Provide the OrphanAnesthesia guidelines in advance.
