Anesthesia Safety

This section contains safety-critical information. Every family member should be aware of it, and a printed copy should accompany the child to every medical procedure requiring sedation or general anesthesia.

The Data

A study of 502 patients with Stickler/Marshall syndrome undergoing anesthesia found specific risks that anesthesiologists must be prepared for.

Airway Concerns

  • Pierre Robin sequence (present in ~24% of Marshall patients) creates a difficult airway — the small, recessed jaw makes visualization of the vocal cords and passage of a breathing tube more challenging
  • Single-attempt intubation success rate: 93.2% — meaning nearly 7% of cases required multiple attempts, which is significantly higher than the general population rate
  • Risk factors for difficult intubation:
    • Receding mandible (micrognathia): odds ratio 3.0 — three times the normal difficulty risk
    • History of cleft palate repair: odds ratio 2.9

What to Do

  1. Print and carry the OrphanAnesthesia guidelines for Stickler/Marshall syndrome. OrphanAnesthesia is a project that creates anesthesia-specific guidelines for rare diseases, written for anesthesiologists. These guidelines can be downloaded at orphananesthesia.eu. Give a copy to the anesthesiologist before the day of any procedure.
  2. Inform every anesthesia provider about:
    • The COL11A1 diagnosis
    • Whether the child has Pierre Robin features (small jaw, history of cleft)
    • Whether cervical instability has been screened for — if not, or if there is known instability, cervical in-line stabilization must be maintained during intubation (no neck hyperextension)
    • Any cardiac history (see below)
  3. Request a pre-procedure anesthesia consultation for any elective procedure — don't let the first meeting with the anesthesiologist be on the morning of surgery

Cardiac Considerations

Though less common, the following have been reported in COL11A1-related conditions and should be screened for:

  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (rare but serious) — affects anesthetic drug choices
  • Mitral valve prolapse — may require antibiotic prophylaxis for certain procedures

An echocardiogram at baseline is reasonable to rule out structural cardiac involvement.
