Orthotics, Bracing & Supportive Devices in Marshall Syndrome

Medical Disclaimer
This information is compiled from peer-reviewed research and clinical guidelines. It is intended for educational discussion with qualified medical professionals. These are NOT treatment recommendations. Orthotic and bracing decisions must be made by credentialed orthotists, pedorthists, or orthopedic specialists who have examined the individual patient. Improperly fitted devices can cause skin breakdown, nerve compression, altered gait mechanics, and — in the context of connective tissue fragility — injury.

In typical musculoskeletal conditions, joints are stabilized primarily by ligaments. The muscles and tendons contribute, but the passive ligamentous system provides the baseline constraint that keeps joints within their intended range. In connective tissue disorders like Marshall syndrome, that passive system is compromised at the structural level: defective type XI collagen (COL11A1) produces ligaments, joint capsules, and tendons that are more extensible and less mechanically stiff than normal. The joints become hypermobile — capable of exceeding their functional range — and the sensory receptors embedded in those lax structures provide degraded proprioceptive signals to the brain.

This is the fundamental reason orthotics and bracing carry more weight in connective tissue disorders than in many other pediatric diagnoses. Where strengthening exercises can compensate for much of the instability in typical joint laxity, in collagen disorders the passive constraint deficit is structural and permanent. External support — orthotics, splints, braces — directly substitutes for the mechanical function that the connective tissue cannot reliably provide.

How common is orthotic use in this population?
Across pediatric collagen and connective tissue disorder cohorts, studies consistently find that 35–42% of patients use at least one orthotic device. In hypermobility-spectrum disorders specifically, foot orthotic use often exceeds 50% by early adolescence. In Marshall syndrome, where systemic collagen involvement is confirmed at the genetic level, orthotic need should be assessed proactively rather than reactively — waiting for pain or deformity to develop before evaluation delays intervention.

Why External Support Works Differently in Connective Tissue Disorders

Three mechanisms explain the particular effectiveness of orthotics in this population:

1. Passive Restraint Substitution

A rigid or semi-rigid orthosis physically limits joint excursion beyond the prescribed range. It does externally what the ligament was supposed to do internally. This reduces the frequency of microinstability events — the small, partial subluxations that are often not dramatic enough to count as a formal dislocation but accumulate into chronic pain, inflammation, and joint damage over months and years.

2. Proprioceptive Augmentation

Even semi-rigid and soft orthoses improve proprioceptive function through cutaneous mechanoreceptor stimulation. The compression and contact of a brace against the skin around a joint activates sensory receptors that feed position-sense information to the nervous system, partially compensating for the degraded signals from the lax joint capsule. This effect is clinically measurable and helps explain why patients with connective tissue disorders often report improved stability perception even from relatively soft compression devices.

3. Load Distribution

Custom orthoses redirect mechanical forces across larger surface areas, reducing peak stress on specific anatomical points. In the foot, for example, a custom foot orthotic (CFO) can redistribute load away from a chronically subluxing midfoot joint. This does not correct the underlying laxity but substantially reduces its symptom burden and long-term degenerative consequences.

Foot and Ankle Orthotics

The foot and ankle represent the most common site of orthotic intervention in pediatric connective tissue disorders. Hyperpronation — excessive inward rolling of the foot — is nearly universal in ligamentous laxity and produces downstream consequences at the knee, hip, and lumbar spine through kinematic chain effects. Addressing foot biomechanics is often the first priority in orthotic planning.

Custom Foot Orthotics (CFOs)

CFOs are rigid or semi-rigid insoles fabricated from a cast or digital scan of the individual foot, designed to provide biomechanically specific support. They differ fundamentally from over-the-counter arch supports, which are designed to fit a statistical average foot and provide generic cushioning rather than individualized biomechanical correction.

When to pursue custom over OTC: If a child with Marshall syndrome has tried two or more OTC insoles without sustained improvement in pain or function, or if a pedorthist or orthotist identifies significant biomechanical deviation during gait analysis, custom fabrication is warranted. OTC insoles are a reasonable first step for mild, uncomplicated overpronation, but should not be the ceiling when there is meaningful connective tissue involvement.

UCBL Orthoses

The University of California Biomechanics Laboratory (UCBL) orthosis is a deep-cup, rigid device that encloses the heel and midfoot with high medial and lateral walls. It provides more aggressive control of hindfoot valgus and hyperpronation than a standard CFO and is the appropriate escalation when a flatter orthosis fails to control excessive motion. UCBL orthoses require specific footwear with sufficient volume to accommodate them and are typically not compatible with everyday athletic shoes without a size adjustment.

Supramalleolar Orthoses (SMOs)

SMOs extend above the ankle malleoli, providing control not only of the foot but of the ankle in the coronal plane. They are appropriate when ankle instability compounds the foot pathology — which is common in Marshall syndrome — and when UCBL-level foot control alone is insufficient. SMOs can be fabricated with trimline modifications to allow more or less sagittal-plane motion depending on the clinical goal.

Ankle-Foot Orthoses (AFOs)

AFOs extend up the lower leg and are appropriate for more severe presentations: significant ankle instability with recurrent subluxation, foot drop from peripheral neuropathy, or valgus/varus deformity beyond what an SMO can address. AFOs exist on a spectrum from flexible (tone-reducing AFOs for neuromuscular presentations) to rigid (solid ankle AFOs that prevent all sagittal motion). In Marshall syndrome without significant neurological involvement, articulated AFOs that permit controlled sagittal motion while blocking frontal-plane instability are often preferable to solid designs, as they allow more normal gait mechanics.

Evidence-Based Foot & Ankle Management

Recent research has strengthened the evidence base for foot orthotic intervention in hypermobile populations, with implications directly relevant to Marshall syndrome.

2025 OSTRICH RCT
The OSTRICH (Orthotic Management of Flat Feet in Children) randomized controlled trial compared custom foot orthoses plus exercise therapy versus exercise therapy alone for symptomatic flexible flat feet in children. Results demonstrated that the combination of orthoses with exercise produced significantly greater improvement in pain and function than exercise alone, providing Level 1 evidence supporting orthotic intervention as an adjunct to — not a replacement for — therapeutic exercise in this population.

Orthotic selection for hypermobile flat feet:

  • Custom semi-rigid with medial arch support is recommended over soft or accommodative orthoses for hypermobility-associated flat feet. Semi-rigid devices provide meaningful biomechanical correction while allowing some adaptive motion — soft orthoses compress under body weight and fail to control the excessive pronation that characterizes hypermobile flat feet
  • UCBL orthotic for moderate-to-severe flat feet: When standard CFOs with medial posting are insufficient to control hindfoot valgus, the UCBL orthosis provides deeper heel cup containment and higher medial-lateral walls that mechanically limit calcaneal eversion. This is the appropriate escalation before considering an AFO
  • AFOs for severe flatfoot with ankle instability: When flat foot deformity is accompanied by significant ankle instability — recurrent ankle sprains, lateral giving way, or visible talar displacement — ankle-foot orthoses are indicated to address both the foot and ankle components simultaneously. Articulated AFOs are preferred over solid designs to preserve sagittal-plane motion

Supportive footwear characteristics:

  • Firm heel counter: The rigid structure at the back of the shoe must resist medial collapse when squeezed. A heel counter that deforms easily under finger pressure will not support a hypermobile hindfoot. Test by squeezing the heel of the shoe — it should resist compression
  • Rigid shank: The shank (midsole stiffener between the heel and ball of the foot) should resist torsion. Hold the shoe at both ends and twist — excessive flexibility indicates inadequate midfoot support. A rigid shank prevents the shoe from collapsing under the midfoot arch during stance phase
  • Removable insole: Required to accommodate custom orthoses. Wearing a custom orthotic on top of a non-removable factory insole raises the heel, alters biomechanics, and reduces the depth available for the orthotic to function

Intrinsic foot muscle strengthening:

Orthoses provide passive support, but active strengthening of the intrinsic foot muscles builds an internal dynamic arch support system. These exercises complement orthotic use and should be incorporated into the home exercise program:

  • Towel curls: Place a towel flat on the floor; use the toes to scrunch the towel toward the heel. 3 sets of 10 repetitions daily. Targets the flexor digitorum brevis and lumbricals
  • Marble pickups: Pick up marbles one at a time with the toes and transfer them to a cup. 10–15 repetitions per foot. Develops individual toe dexterity and intrinsic muscle activation
  • Short foot exercise: While seated, actively raise the arch by drawing the ball of the foot toward the heel without curling the toes. Hold for 5 seconds, repeat 10 times. This targets the abductor hallucis and is the most specific exercise for dynamic medial arch support

Knee Orthotics

Knee involvement in Marshall syndrome typically presents as patellar maltracking, generalized knee hypermobility with recurrent subluxation, or early degenerative changes secondary to chronic instability. The bracing approach differs substantially depending on which of these is primary.

Patellar Tracking Braces

Patellar maltracking — the patella deviating laterally during knee flexion and extension — is common in hypermobility due to quadriceps vector asymmetry and lateral retinacular tightness against a lax medial restraint. Patellar tracking braces use a buttress, strap, or sleeve design to apply a medial force on the patella throughout the range of motion, reducing lateral subluxation risk and improving tracking mechanics. These are typically lightweight and compatible with athletic activity. Fitting should confirm the buttress is positioned to the lateral aspect and that the device does not migrate during movement — a common problem with sleeve-style designs in pediatric use.

Hinged Knee Braces for Instability

When generalized knee laxity causes recurrent partial or full subluxation — the knee “giving way” — a hinged knee brace with medial-lateral support straps provides more robust constraint than a tracking sleeve. Custom hinged braces are fabricated to the exact anatomical geometry and are significantly more effective than off-the-shelf hinged braces for managing multi-directional laxity. Off-the-shelf hinged braces are a reasonable trial for mild instability or while awaiting custom fabrication.

Unloader Braces for Compartment Offloading

In individuals with Marshall syndrome who develop early osteoarthritis secondary to years of hypermobility-related joint stress — a trajectory that begins in childhood without intervention — unloader braces redirect axial load away from the damaged compartment. These are typically used for medial compartment OA and require precise fitting to achieve the intended valgus angulation. Unloader braces are a conservative alternative to surgical realignment osteotomy in patients with unicompartmental disease who are not yet candidates for joint replacement.

Wrist and Hand Splints

Hand and wrist hypermobility in Marshall syndrome affects functional tasks across the lifespan, from pencil grip in school-aged children to keyboard use and instrumental music in adolescents and adults. The small joints of the hand are particularly prone to microinstability that accumulates into chronic pain without obvious acute events.

Ring Splints for Finger Hyperextension

Silver ring splints and Oval-8 splints are the primary intervention for finger joint hyperextension. They are sized to fit individual finger joints — typically the proximal interphalangeal (PIP) or distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint — and physically block extension beyond a set limit while allowing full flexion. Silver ring splints are custom-fabricated from sterling silver or stainless steel to an exact measurement and are cosmetically preferable for long-term daily use. Oval-8 splints are off-the-shelf plastic alternatives appropriate for shorter-term use or fitting trials before committing to custom silver.

Fitting ring splints: Ring splint sizing requires measurement of the joint circumference at the specific joint level (not the finger generally) and the joint's range of motion. A ring splint that is too loose provides no benefit; one that is too tight restricts circulation. A certified hand therapist or orthotist with ring splint experience should perform the fitting. Many families successfully self-fit Oval-8s using manufacturer sizing guides as a starting point, with subsequent adjustment.

Wrist Splints

Wrist splints serve two distinct purposes in Marshall syndrome. First, for carpal tunnel syndrome — which occurs at elevated rates in connective tissue disorders, possibly due to laxity-related dynamic compression of the carpal tunnel during wrist motion — nocturnal neutral-position wrist splints reduce overnight compression of the median nerve and are first-line conservative management. Second, for wrist instability from ligamentous laxity, daytime wrist splints provide the passive constraint the radiocarpal and midcarpal ligaments cannot. The degree of rigidity should be matched to the severity of instability: rigid thermoplastic for severe instability or post-surgical support, soft neoprene with palmar stay for mild to moderate instability during functional activities.

Thumb Spica Splints

Carpometacarpal (CMC) instability of the thumb — the joint at the base of the thumb where it meets the wrist — is particularly disabling because it compromises the pinch and grip mechanics that most fine motor tasks depend on. Thumb spica splints immobilize the CMC joint while leaving the fingers free. Custom thermoplastic thumb spicas provide the most precise fit and the most mechanical control; prefabricated neoprene spica sleeves are a reasonable starting point. CMC instability in children with connective tissue disorders tends to progress without support, making early intervention preferable to waiting for frank subluxation or degenerative change.

Spinal Bracing

Scoliosis occurs at substantially higher rates in Marshall syndrome than in the general population, reflecting both the structural effects of defective spinal collagen and the altered load distribution that arises from axial hyperlaxity. Spinal bracing decisions require close coordination between orthopedics and orthotics, with frequent monitoring in growing children.

Boston Brace

The Boston brace is a thoracolumbosacral orthosis (TLSO) designed using a symmetrical prefabricated module that is then custom-modified. It applies three-point pressure to reduce curves primarily in the thoracolumbar and lumbar spine. The Boston brace is the most widely used scoliosis brace in the United States and has the largest evidence base for curve progression prevention. It is typically prescribed for curves of 25–40 degrees in skeletally immature patients, worn 16–23 hours per day, and continued until skeletal maturity.

Rigo-Chêneau Brace

The Rigo-Chêneau (RC) brace is a custom-fabricated device based on a classification system that accounts for the three-dimensional pattern of the scoliotic curve, not just the frontal-plane Cobb angle. Unlike the Boston brace's symmetrical shell design, the RC brace is asymmetric and specifically contoured to derotate the vertebral column while applying curve-specific corrective forces. Evidence suggests the RC approach achieves greater in-brace correction, though comparative long-term outcome data are still accumulating. Fabrication requires an orthotist with specific RC training and experience, which limits availability.

Thoracolumbosacral Orthoses (TLSOs)

TLSO is the generic category encompassing both Boston and RC designs, as well as other custom-fabricated spinal orthoses that extend from the thorax to the sacrum. TLSOs are the appropriate level of support for thoracolumbar curves. For primarily lumbar curves, an LSO (lumbosacral orthosis that does not extend to the thorax) may suffice.

Soft Lumbar Corsets

For lumbar pain management rather than curve control, soft elastic lumbar corsets and support belts provide proprioceptive feedback, warmth, and mild mechanical support. They do not significantly alter spinal alignment or prevent deformity progression and should not be substituted for rigid bracing when structural intervention is indicated. Their appropriate role is symptomatic relief during high-demand activities in adults and adolescents with lumbar hyperlaxity and associated pain, not primary scoliosis management.

Rigid Lumbar Orthoses (LSOs)

Rigid LSOs are custom-fabricated thermoplastic devices that significantly restrict lumbar motion. They are appropriate for acute disc herniation with radiculopathy, post-surgical lumbar stabilization, or severe symptomatic lumbar instability that has not responded to soft supports and physical therapy. In Marshall syndrome, rigid LSOs require careful attention to abdominal and truncal skin tolerance given connective tissue fragility.

Cervical Collars

Cervical orthoses range from soft foam collars that provide warmth and minimal motion restriction to rigid two-piece Philadelphia or Miami J collars that substantially limit flexion-extension and rotation, to halo-vest systems for maximal immobilization. In Marshall syndrome, the indication for cervical support is craniocervical instability (CCI) or cervical hypermobility with associated symptoms — occipital headache, neck pain with position changes, upper extremity paresthesias, or signs of cord involvement.

Cervical collar use in CCI requires specialist oversight.
Soft cervical collars provide minimal mechanical restraint and are not appropriate primary management for significant CCI. Rigid cervical collars provide meaningful restriction but can cause neck muscle deconditioning and skin breakdown with prolonged use. The decision regarding cervical orthotic type, wear schedule, and duration must be made by a neurosurgeon or spine specialist who has reviewed upright flexion-extension imaging. Self-prescribing or using a collar based on symptom perception alone — without specialist evaluation — is not appropriate in this population.

Custom vs. Off-the-Shelf: Decision Framework

The choice between custom and prefabricated orthotic devices involves clinical severity, cost, insurance coverage, and patient factors. The following framework reflects general clinical practice, not absolute rules.

Consider Off-the-Shelf When… Consider Custom When…
Symptoms are mild and intermittent Symptoms are moderate to severe or daily
Prior OTC trial was partially effective One or more OTC trials failed to improve function
Foot anatomy is near-typical Significant structural deformity or unusual anatomy
Short-term or transitional use expected Long-term or permanent need anticipated
Tight budget and coverage not available Insurance will cover custom; cost is not a barrier
Diagnosis is recent; assessing response to support Diagnosis is established; biomechanics well-characterized
Cost reality: Custom orthotics represent a significant upfront cost, but when amortized over their lifespan and weighed against the cost of downstream joint damage, pain management, and corrective procedures, they are frequently cost-effective. The calculus is different in growing children, where replacement every 12–18 months is necessary, and insurance coverage becomes proportionally more important.

Orthotic care involves several distinct credentialed specialties. Knowing which specialist to see for a given need prevents delays and ensures appropriate expertise.

Finding specialists with connective tissue experience: Not all orthotists have experience with hypermobility disorders. These are the closest analog conditions in most practices and indicate familiarity with the relevant clinical challenges. A specialist who has never encountered a hypermobile patient may over-stiffen devices or fail to account for skin fragility and pressure sensitivity.

Growth Considerations in Children

Children's feet, legs, and spines grow continuously until skeletal maturity (typically mid-to-late adolescence). Orthotic devices fabricated to a given size become both mechanically inadequate and potentially harmful as the child grows beyond them. General replacement schedules for growing children:

  • Foot orthotics: Every 12–18 months in children under 10; every 18–24 months in older adolescents whose growth is slowing
  • SMOs and AFOs: Every 12–18 months; sooner if the child complains of pressure, the device is visibly straining at edges, or shoe size has changed significantly
  • Spinal braces: Reassessed every 4–6 months during peak growth; the Cobb angle may change rapidly during growth spurts and brace modifications or replacement may be needed on a shorter cycle
  • Ring splints and wrist splints: Remeasurement at each annual visit; fingers can grow substantially between fittings in younger children

Breaking In New Devices: Graduated Wear Schedules

Orthotic devices — especially rigid ones — require a breaking-in period before full-time wear. Introducing a device too aggressively causes skin breakdown, discomfort, and resistance to continued use. This is especially true in Marshall syndrome, where skin is more sensitive to pressure and shear.

A typical graduated schedule for a new rigid device:

  • Days 1–2: 1–2 hours per day; remove and inspect skin for redness
  • Days 3–5: 3–4 hours per day; continue skin checks after each wear session
  • Days 6–10: Increase by 1–2 hours per day as tolerated
  • Week 2+: Work toward prescribed wear time (often 16+ hours/day for spinal braces)
Fitting red flags — return to the orthotist if:
  • Redness that does not resolve within 20–30 minutes of device removal
  • Skin breakdown, blistering, or open areas at any point
  • Numbness, tingling, or color change (blanching or cyanosis) of skin or digits while wearing the device
  • Pain that is new or worsening after the first week of wear
  • The device has visibly warped, cracked, or the strapping hardware has failed
  • Gait has worsened or become more effortful after several weeks of wear
  • A child who was tolerating the device suddenly refuses it — this frequently indicates a fit problem, not purely behavioral resistance

Documentation for Medical Necessity

Insurance approvals for orthotics depend on a clear chain of documentation linking diagnosis to functional impairment to the specific device requested. The following elements strengthen prior authorizations:

  • Confirmed diagnosis of Marshall syndrome with the prescribing provider's documentation of COL11A1 involvement
  • Documented functional limitations: specific activities the patient cannot perform or performs with pain or risk due to the affected joint
  • Objective findings: gait analysis results, clinical hypermobility score (Beighton scale), imaging findings (subluxation, deformity, degenerative change)
  • Conservative treatment history: prior OTC orthotic trials that were insufficient, physical therapy that was unable to resolve the instability
  • Specification of the device's role: “to prevent recurrent subluxation of [joint] during weight-bearing activity, which [conservative treatment] has not sufficiently controlled”
When a claim is denied: Request the denial in writing with the specific coverage exclusion cited. Many orthotic denials cite “not medically necessary” when the submission lacked functional documentation rather than when the device is genuinely excluded. A peer-to-peer review call between the prescribing specialist and the insurance medical director frequently reverses these denials. For plan-level exclusions, a formal appeal citing clinical guidelines and the established treatment standard for connective tissue disorders is the next step. See the Insurance Navigation page for detailed appeal strategy.

Compliance in Children: Practical Strategies

Orthotic compliance in pediatric patients is a recognized clinical challenge. Children may find devices uncomfortable, embarrassing, or interfering with play and social activities. In connective tissue disorders, where the need for orthotics may persist across the entire childhood and adolescence, building sustainable compliance habits early is essential.

Why Children Resist

  • Discomfort: New devices cause pressure and sensory novelty. In children with heightened sensory sensitivity — which co-occurs at elevated rates with connective tissue disorders — this is more intense than typical.
  • Social visibility: Braces and splints mark the child as different from peers. This becomes particularly salient in middle school and adolescence.
  • Perceived interference: Children associate the device with not being able to play, run, or participate normally. Whether or not this is accurate, the perception drives avoidance.
  • Poor fit: A child who complains consistently is often reporting genuine discomfort from a fitting problem, not purely behaving. This should be the first consideration before behavioral strategies.

Strategies That Work

  • Involve children in device selection: Where clinically appropriate, offer choices of color, pattern, or design. Many pediatric orthotic manufacturers offer patterned shells and color options. The device feeling personally chosen rather than imposed significantly improves adoption.
  • Graduated introduction: Do not attempt full prescribed wear time on day one. Use the graduated schedule and celebrate incremental milestones. The goal is successful building of tolerance, not forced compliance that breeds aversion.
  • Name it normally: Frame the orthotic as a tool the child uses, not a medical burden. “Your ankle helper” or “your knee support” normalizes the device without medicalization.
  • Connect with peers: Connecting with other children who use orthotics through support groups or online communities (with parental oversight appropriate to age) powerfully normalizes the experience. Seeing peers who are active and engaged despite wearing braces reframes the child's perception.
  • Align with motivation: If a child wants to play soccer or dance or ride a bike, frame the orthotic as the thing that makes that possible, not the thing preventing it. Link compliance to the child's own goals.

When to Push Through vs. When to Pause

Not all resistance is behavioral. The following situations warrant a return to the orthotist rather than increased behavioral strategies:

  • Resistance that began after a period of successful wear (suggests the device no longer fits correctly)
  • Specific complaints about a defined location of pain or pressure (suggests a focal fit problem)
  • Worsening of the original symptoms while wearing the device (suggests the device is not providing the intended mechanical effect)
  • Skin changes visible after wear (suggests pressure distribution problem)

Special Considerations in Marshall Syndrome

Skin Fragility and Pressure Management

Connective tissue abnormality in Marshall syndrome affects not only joints and ligaments but also skin integrity. The skin may be more prone to shear injury, slower to heal from pressure marks, and more sensitive to moisture accumulation under devices. Practical mitigation strategies:

  • All rigid orthoses should be lined or the skin interface padded with moisture-wicking materials (not standard foam, which retains moisture)
  • Inspect the skin under devices daily — especially during breaking-in periods — and after any period of unusually high activity
  • During hot weather or physical exertion, device wear time may need to be reduced temporarily and moisture management (liner changes, breathable socks) increased
  • Report any skin breakdown immediately; do not attempt to modify the device at home by adding foam padding, which may alter the mechanical function and create new pressure points

Growth Spurts and Accelerated Replacement

Growth spurts can occur rapidly and unpredictably. A foot orthotic that fit correctly three months ago may be significantly undersized after a growth spurt. Signs that a device has been outgrown include:

  • The child's toes extend to or beyond the edge of the insole
  • The brace trimlines are visibly pressing into the skin at the distal edge
  • The child reports that the device “feels different” or is newly uncomfortable without other explanation
  • The corresponding shoe size has increased by one or more sizes since the last orthotic fitting

Families should budget for more frequent replacement than standard schedules suggest during peak growth periods (typically ages 9–12 in girls and 11–14 in boys, though this varies). Coordinating orthotic replacement timing with annual school physicals or specialist visits creates a natural review opportunity.

Scoliosis Bracing with Concurrent Hypermobility

Scoliosis bracing in a child with connective tissue hypermobility presents a specific clinical tension. Standard scoliosis bracing achieves curve correction partly by loading the trunk asymmetrically, but a child with systemic laxity may not tolerate the pressure distribution of a standard TLSO shell without skin breakdown, and the hypermobile trunk may deform around the brace rather than being corrected by it. Clinical adaptations that experienced orthotists may apply:

  • More frequent fit checks (every 3–4 months rather than every 6) to catch early pressure problems and verify the device is achieving in-brace correction
  • Softer interface materials at bony prominences and areas of skin sensitivity
  • Collaboration with the prescribing orthopedic surgeon to verify that the in-brace radiographic correction is occurring, not just that the brace is being worn
  • Awareness that brace-related pain in this population more frequently indicates a fitting issue than in typical scoliosis patients; pain-driven removal cycles disrupt the hours-per-day targets that determine brace effectiveness

Night Splinting for Ankles and Wrists

Night splinting maintains a joint in a corrected or neutral position during sleep, preventing the soft tissue shortening and contracture that can develop when hypermobile joints rest in repeatedly malaligned positions. In Marshall syndrome, night splinting is most commonly used for:

  • Ankles: A dorsiflexion night splint or boot maintains the ankle at 90 degrees, counteracting the tendency of hypermobile plantarflexors to adaptively shorten when the foot rests in plantarflexion. This is particularly useful in children who complain of morning heel pain or tightness.
  • Wrists: A neutral-position wrist splint during sleep reduces overnight median nerve compression in carpal tunnel presentations and maintains the wrist in a mechanically favorable position when the lax ligaments are not actively controlled by muscle activity.
Night splint tolerance: Night splints are notoriously difficult to keep on sleeping children. Strategies that improve retention include: applying the splint after the child is drowsy but before full sleep; using softer, neoprene-based designs rather than hard thermoplastic for night use; securing straps snugly but not tightly; and placing the splinted limb in a comfortable, supported position before the child falls asleep. A child who is repeatedly removing the splint during the night should be reassessed for fit and comfort rather than assumed to be non-compliant.

Coordinating Orthotics with the Rest of the Treatment Plan

Orthotics are most effective when integrated into a broader management plan rather than prescribed in isolation. Key coordination points:

  • Physical therapy: The orthotic provides passive support, but the muscles around the joint still need to be strengthened to provide active stability. PT and orthotic use should be coordinated so the PT can adjust the exercise program based on which joints are supported and which are not. An orthosis should not become a substitute for the active stability that PT can build.
  • Footwear selection: Most orthotics require specific footwear characteristics to function correctly — sufficient volume, appropriate heel counter rigidity, removable insole. The orthotist should specify footwear requirements at fitting. Incompatible footwear frequently causes both device failure and pressure problems.
  • School and activity accommodations: Children wearing orthoses may need accommodations: extra time between classes if mobility is temporarily reduced, seating near the door, a second set of orthoses for school if devices are frequently forgotten. A 504 plan or IEP addendum can formalize these accommodations with documented medical need.
  • Pain management: Orthotics reduce pain by improving joint mechanics, but they do not eliminate all pain, particularly during the initial period of adjustment. The pain management plan (see Pain Management) should account for the transition period when introducing new devices.

3D-Printed Custom Splints and Smart Orthotic Technology

Advances in 3D scanning and additive manufacturing are transforming custom orthotic fabrication, with particular relevance to connective tissue disorders where precise fit and lightweight design are critical.

3D-Printed Custom Splints

  • Patient preference is overwhelming: A 2024 study (Martinez et al.) found that 89% of patients preferred 3D-printed splints over traditionally fabricated devices, citing superior comfort, lighter weight, and better aesthetics. For children and adolescents, where compliance is often the primary barrier to orthotic effectiveness, this preference translates directly into better outcomes.
  • Precision from 3D scanning: A handheld 3D scanner captures the exact geometry of the patient’s limb in seconds, producing a digital model from which the splint is designed and printed to ±0.1 mm tolerances — far exceeding the precision achievable with traditional plaster casting. This sub-millimeter accuracy is particularly important in connective tissue disorders where even slight misalignment can create pressure points on fragile skin.
  • Lighter and more comfortable: 3D-printed splints use lattice structures and optimized material distribution to provide equivalent mechanical support at 30–50% less weight than traditional thermoplastic fabrication. For children wearing devices for extended periods, the weight reduction directly improves compliance.
  • Water-resistant designs: Unlike traditional plaster or fabric-lined splints, many 3D-printed designs are waterproof or highly water-resistant, allowing the child to bathe, swim, and participate in aquatic therapy without removing the device. This is a significant practical advantage for families.
  • Rapid pediatric replacement: When a child outgrows a device or the fit needs adjustment, the digital model can be modified and a new splint printed within hours rather than requiring a new casting appointment and multi-day fabrication cycle. For rapidly growing children with Marshall syndrome, this means devices can be replaced in days rather than weeks — eliminating the gap period where the child has no support.

Commercially Available 3D-Printed Hand Orthoses

  • Durathumb: A 3D-printed thumb stabilizer designed specifically for CMC (carpometacarpal) and MCP joint instability. Provides rigid support in a low-profile, lightweight design that allows continued use of the hand during daily activities. Particularly relevant for Marshall syndrome patients with thumb hypermobility affecting grip strength.
  • Duradigit: A 3D-printed finger orthosis for PIP and DIP joint hyperextension. Functionally equivalent to traditional silver ring splints but produced through 3D printing for faster turnaround and lower cost — especially advantageous for growing children who outgrow ring splints every 6–12 months.

3D-Printed TMJ Splints

Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) dysfunction is common in Marshall syndrome due to midface hypoplasia, connective tissue laxity, and altered jaw mechanics. 3D-printed TMJ splints offer advantages over traditionally fabricated occlusal splints:

  • Digital occlusal scanning captures the bite relationship with higher precision than dental impressions, particularly in children with small mouths or gag reflex sensitivity
  • Iterative refinement: The digital file can be adjusted and reprinted if the initial fit requires modification, without requiring a complete new impression and laboratory fabrication cycle
  • Material options: 3D printing allows selection of splint rigidity from soft (nightguard) to hard (repositioning splint) based on the specific TMJ dysfunction pattern, all from the same digital model

3D-Printed Ring Splints for Finger Hyperextension

  • Traditional silver ring splints (e.g., Silver Ring Splint Company) remain the gold standard for cosmetic appearance and durability in daily wear.
  • 3D-printed alternatives are emerging as a cost-effective option, particularly for growing children who will outgrow the splint within 6–12 months. 3D-printed ring splints can be produced from biocompatible nylon or resin, customized to exact joint measurements, and replaced rapidly as the child grows.
  • An active 2025 clinical trial is evaluating 3D-printed hyperextended finger splints specifically in patients with joint hypermobility, assessing both biomechanical effectiveness and patient-reported outcomes compared to traditional ring splints. Results from this trial will clarify whether 3D-printed ring splints achieve equivalent functional outcomes at lower cost.

Smart Splint Technology

  • Emerging “smart splint” designs integrate pressure sensors directly into the orthotic device, providing real-time data on how forces are distributed across the joint during daily activities.
  • IoT-connected monitoring: Sensor data can be transmitted wirelessly to the orthotist or clinical team, enabling remote monitoring of device fit, wear patterns, and mechanical function without requiring an in-person visit.
  • For Marshall syndrome patients specifically, smart splints could detect early signs of device migration, pressure concentration (skin breakdown risk), and changes in joint mechanics that indicate the need for refitting — identifying problems before they cause symptoms.
  • This technology is still in early clinical adoption, but it represents a significant advancement for connective tissue disorder populations where precise fit is critical and remote monitoring reduces the treatment burden on families who already attend numerous specialist appointments.
Availability note: 3D-printed orthotics are increasingly available through specialized orthotic clinics and some children’s hospitals. Not all insurance plans cover 3D-printed devices yet, though coverage is expanding. Ask the orthotist about 3D scanning and printing options, particularly for children in rapid growth phases where traditional custom devices become cost-prohibitive due to frequent replacement needs.

Kinesiology Tape: Proprioceptive Augmentation Without Rigidity

Kinesiology tape (elastic therapeutic tape applied to the skin over muscles and joints) has emerged as a useful adjunct in the management of joint hypermobility, particularly for situations where rigid bracing is impractical, socially unacceptable to the child, or unnecessary for the level of instability present.

Evidence Base

  • A 2024 meta-analysis confirms that kinesiology taping produces measurable improvement in proprioception — the sense of joint position and movement — through stimulation of cutaneous mechanoreceptors. This is the same proprioceptive augmentation mechanism as bracing, achieved through a lower-profile, less restrictive medium.
  • A randomized controlled trial in patients with hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS) demonstrated that shoulder kinesiology taping improved range of motion and proprioception compared to sham taping and no intervention. The hEDS population shares the same category of ligamentous laxity as Marshall syndrome, making these findings directly relevant.
  • The proprioceptive effect appears to be genuine (not placebo): sham taping applied without therapeutic tension does not produce the same proprioceptive improvements as correctly applied kinesiology tape.

Best Applications in Marshall Syndrome

  • Shoulders: Rotator cuff and scapular taping for glenohumeral instability. The shoulder is difficult to brace effectively, making tape a practical first-line option.
  • Knees: Patellar taping for maltracking (McConnell technique or kinesiology tape variant). Can be used alongside or as a transition from patellar tracking braces.
  • Ankles: Stirrup and figure-eight taping patterns for lateral ankle instability during sport or high-demand activities.
  • Thoracic spine: Postural taping provides proprioceptive cues that remind the child to maintain upright posture without the restriction of a rigid brace.

Important Limitations

  • Kinesiology tape is an adjunct, not a replacement for physical therapy and structured strengthening programs. Tape provides sensory feedback; it does not build the active muscle stability that PT develops.
  • Tape does not provide significant mechanical restraint — it cannot prevent subluxation or dislocation in joints with severe instability. For those situations, rigid or semi-rigid bracing remains necessary.
  • Skin sensitivity is common in connective tissue disorders. Test a small patch for 24 hours before full application. Hypoallergenic tape variants are available for sensitive skin.
  • Tape requires reapplication every 3–5 days and correct application technique (which can be taught to families by a physiotherapist for ongoing home use).
Practical approach: Kinesiology tape works best as part of a layered support strategy: tape for mild instability or proprioceptive supplementation during activities, soft bracing for moderate instability during daily function, and rigid bracing for severe instability or high-demand situations. The child does not need to choose one approach exclusively — different joints and different activity contexts may call for different levels of support.

3D-Printed Orthotics & Custom Devices

3D printing is transforming orthotics from a slow, analog craft into a rapid, digitally precise manufacturing process — with particular advantages for the pediatric connective tissue disorder population.

Why 3D Printing Matters for Marshall Syndrome

  • Patient preference is overwhelming. A 2024 study (Martinez et al.) found that 89% of patients preferred 3D-printed orthoses over traditionally manufactured equivalents, citing improved comfort, lighter weight, better aesthetics, and more precise fit. For children who already resist wearing braces, improved comfort and appearance directly translate to better compliance.
  • Rapid replacement for pediatric growth. Children outgrow orthotics. In Marshall syndrome, where orthotics may be needed for multiple joints simultaneously, the frequency of replacement creates a compounding cost and time burden. 3D-printed orthotics can be remanufactured from a digital scan in days rather than the weeks required for traditional fabrication. The digital file is stored and can be scaled to accommodate growth, reducing rescan requirements.
  • Precision fit for hypermobile joints. 3D scanning captures joint anatomy with sub-millimeter accuracy, and parametric design allows the orthotist to fine-tune stiffness zones, pressure distribution, and range-of-motion limits digitally before manufacturing. For hypermobile joints where the margin between therapeutic support and excessive restriction is narrow, this precision is clinically meaningful.
  • Production speed: 2–4 hours from scan to device. Advanced 3D printing workflows now produce finished custom orthotics in as little as 2–4 hours from digital scan to wearable device. Traditional custom fabrication typically requires 1–3 weeks including mould preparation, lab work, and fitting adjustments. For pediatric patients experiencing an acute flare, joint injury, or rapid growth spurt, same-day turnaround eliminates the gap period where the child has no functional support.
  • Variable stiffness zones within a single device. One of the most significant advances in 3D-printed orthotics is the ability to engineer variable stiffness within a single device — rigid at the joint fulcrum where support is needed, flexible at the edges for comfort, and graduated in between. Traditional thermoplastic fabrication produces devices with uniform stiffness, requiring the orthotist to choose between adequate support (too rigid at the edges, causing pressure points) and adequate comfort (too flexible at the joint, providing insufficient support). 3D printing eliminates this compromise through lattice density modulation and multi-material printing, where different regions of the same orthotic use different infill patterns or materials to produce precisely tailored mechanical properties.
  • Pediatric-specific advantages. Children present unique challenges for orthotics: rapid growth, lower compliance, sensitivity to weight and appearance, and the need for frequent replacement. 3D-printed devices address all of these: lighter weight improves compliance, digital files allow rapid size scaling as the child grows, customizable colours and patterns increase willingness to wear the device, and the dramatically lower per-device production time and cost make frequent replacement economically feasible. For a child with Marshall syndrome who may need orthotics for ankles, wrists, fingers, and spine simultaneously, the cumulative cost and time savings are substantial.

Insurance Advocacy for 3D-Printed Orthotics

Insurance coverage for 3D-printed orthotics is inconsistent but improving. Key advocacy strategies:

  • HCPCS codes are the same. 3D-printed orthotics use the same billing codes (L-codes) as traditionally manufactured equivalents. The manufacturing method does not change the code. Frame the claim as the orthotic device, not the manufacturing technology.
  • Document medical necessity for custom fabrication. Generic off-the-shelf orthotics are often covered at a lower tier. To justify custom fabrication (whether traditional or 3D-printed), document the specific anatomical features that require custom fitting: hypermobility severity, joint angle measurements, failed OTC attempts, and the clinical consequences of inadequate bracing (subluxation frequency, fall risk, pain).
  • Growth-based replacement justification. For pediatric patients, document growth rate and the frequency at which the current orthotic becomes inadequate. Insurance companies are more likely to approve timely replacement when growth-related refitting is documented prospectively rather than requested after the device has already failed.
  • Appeal template. If denied, the appeal should emphasize: (1) the specific diagnosis (Marshall syndrome / COL11A1 collagen disorder with documented joint hypermobility), (2) failed conservative measures, (3) medical necessity for custom fabrication due to anatomical specificity requirements, and (4) the HCPCS code equivalency with traditionally manufactured devices.
Finding a 3D-printing orthotist. Not all orthotists offer 3D-printed devices. Ask your orthopedic team for referrals to a certified orthotist (CO) or certified prosthetist-orthotist (CPO) who uses digital scanning and 3D printing. Academic medical centers and large orthotic practices are more likely to offer this technology. The American Board for Certification in Orthotics, Prosthetics & Pedorthics (abcop.org) maintains a provider directory.
