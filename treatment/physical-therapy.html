Physical Therapy Protocol

Physical therapy is the single most important ongoing intervention for spinal health in Marshall syndrome. It should be viewed not as a treatment for a specific symptom but as a lifelong practice for maintaining function.

Finding the Right Therapist

Look for a physical therapist experienced with connective tissue disorders or hypermobility syndromes (e.g., Ehlers-Danlos syndrome). Standard PT protocols designed for typical children may be harmful — they often push range of motion that hypermobile patients already have in excess, when the actual need is stability and control.

Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–6)

The initial phase must be gentle. 81% of pediatric patients with collagen disorders experience pain exacerbation after exercise — starting too aggressively will cause a flare, undermine trust, and potentially set back progress.

  • Proprioception training — body awareness exercises, balance boards, single-leg stance with eyes closed
  • Isometric spinal stabilizers — engaging core muscles without movement (planks with modifications, dead bugs, bird-dogs)
  • Closed kinetic chain exercises — movements where hands/feet stay fixed (wall sits, modified push-ups against a wall)
  • Low-load controlled movement — slow, deliberate movements through partial range of motion

Phase 2: Progressive Loading (Weeks 6–12+)

Once the foundation is stable and the patient tolerates Phase 1 without significant flares:

  • Progressive resistance — gradually increasing load on stabilizing muscles
  • Eccentric strengthening — controlled lowering movements (the "negative" phase), which builds strength through a range of motion and is particularly effective for tendon and ligament health
  • Postural alignment training — awareness and correction of habitual postures that stress the spine

Recommended Activities

ActivityWhy It Works
SwimmingNear-perfect for Marshall syndrome — buoyancy eliminates axial loading, warm water reduces pain, full range of motion work without gravity stress. Specifically and repeatedly recommended in the literature.
CyclingLow-impact, builds lower extremity and core strength, no axial loading
Modified yogaFlexibility is NOT the goal (they likely have too much). Focus on strength-building poses, balance, body awareness. Avoid extreme ranges of motion.
PilatesCore stabilization focus aligns well with therapeutic goals. Mat-based preferred over reformer initially.
WalkingLow-impact baseline activity. Supportive footwear important.

Activities to Avoid

ActivityWhy
High-impact sports (running, basketball, volleyball)Axial loading stresses compromised vertebrae and discs
Contact sports (football, hockey, wrestling)Risk of spinal injury with already-compromised structures
TrampoliningRetinal detachment case reports in Stickler/Marshall patients — also high axial impact
GymnasticsCombines extreme range of motion (worsens instability), high impact, and spinal loading
Heavy overhead liftingAxial compression of the spine under load

Aquatic Therapy

Aquatic therapy deserves special mention. It is specifically recommended in the Stickler/Marshall literature for several reasons:

  • Buoyancy reduces effective body weight by up to 90%, allowing strengthening without spinal compression
  • Warm water (typically 88–92°F in therapeutic pools) reduces muscle tension and pain
  • Hydrostatic pressure provides gentle compression that supports joints
  • Full range of motion work is possible in water that would be painful on land
  • If only one therapeutic activity is pursued, swimming/aquatic therapy should be it
