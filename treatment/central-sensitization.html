Critical Concept
Central sensitization means the nervous system itself has changed — amplifying pain signals beyond what the structural damage would predict. In children, this rewiring can happen in as little as 3–6 months of chronic pain. Early intervention is critical.

What Is Central Sensitization? Established

Central sensitization is the process by which the central nervous system “turns up the volume” on pain processing. It is not about the tissues being more damaged — it is about the spinal cord and brain interpreting normal signals as painful and painful signals as catastrophic.

  • Wind-up pathway: Persistent C-fiber input → NMDA receptor activation → dorsal horn plasticity → pain amplified beyond what the structural damage explains
  • Nociplastic pain: The IASP’s third pain mechanism (alongside nociceptive and neuropathic). This category was created specifically because central sensitization does not fit either traditional category — the pain is real but the mechanism is neural amplification, not tissue damage or nerve injury
  • 2016 landmark study (Di Stefano et al.): Proved central sensitization exists in joint hypermobility/EDS patients — demonstrating lowered cold and heat pain thresholds and increased wind-up ratios compared to healthy controls
  • 2025 study: Confirmed signs of central sensitization in adolescents with hypermobility — including diminished exercise-induced hypoalgesia, meaning that exercise, which normally reduces pain sensitivity, fails to do so once central sensitization is established
  • Self-sustaining: Once established, central sensitization is self-sustaining even if the original peripheral input is removed. This is why surgery alone may not resolve pain — the spinal cord has already rewired

Why Marshall Syndrome Creates Extreme Risk Hypothesis

Marshall syndrome presents a convergence of risk factors for central sensitization that is, frankly, alarming. Each factor alone increases risk. Together, they create near-certainty that central sensitization will develop without intervention.

Glial Cell Activation — The Self-Amplifying Fire Established

Glial cells (microglia and astrocytes) are the immune cells of the central nervous system. When they activate in response to persistent pain, they create a self-sustaining inflammatory loop that maintains pain independently of the original injury.

  • Microglia activation: Persistent pain input activates spinal microglia via TLR-4 and p38 MAPK signaling pathways. These are CNS-resident immune cells that shift from a surveillance state to an activated, pro-inflammatory state
  • Inflammatory mediator release: Activated microglia release TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, BDNF, and prostaglandin E2 — all of which directly sensitize dorsal horn neurons
  • GABA reversal (critical): BDNF released by microglia downregulates KCC2 transporters on dorsal horn neurons. This causes a paradoxical reversal where GABA — normally the brain’s primary inhibitory neurotransmitter — becomes excitatory. The braking system literally starts accelerating
  • Astrocyte cascade: Astrocytes activate next, establishing bidirectional microglia-astrocyte signaling loops that are extremely difficult to interrupt once established
  • Key insight: Once glia are activated, they maintain pain independently of the original peripheral input. Removing the peripheral source (e.g., via surgery) does not turn off activated glia
  • Cervical cord compression massively amplifies this: A 2019 Nature study demonstrated that compression severity correlated directly with the proportion of activated microglia. More compression = more glial activation = more self-sustaining pain
  • Lhermitte’s sign = evidence of actively inflamed, hyperexcitable spinal cord segments. This is not just a symptom — it is a clinical marker of ongoing neuroinflammation at the exact site where pain amplification occurs

The Pain-Autonomic Vicious Cycle Supported

Central sensitization does not stay confined to pain pathways. Once it reaches the upper cervical cord and brainstem, it engages autonomic circuits that create a self-reinforcing loop:

  • C1–C3 sensitization: Central sensitization at the upper cervical levels sensitizes the trigeminal nucleus caudalis, which receives convergent input from cervical and trigeminal pathways
  • Trigeminovascular reflex: The sensitized nucleus drives autonomic output via the trigeminovascular reflex — producing red ears, facial flushing, and temperature dysregulation
  • Sympathetic over-activity: Pain causes sympathetic nervous system activation → vasoconstriction → tissue ischemia → more nociceptor activation → more central sensitization
  • His autonomic symptoms are NOT separate conditions. They are direct evidence that central sensitization has engaged brainstem circuits. Red ears and flushing are markers of central sensitization severity

Alpha-Delta Sleep — The Nightly Reset That Isn’t Happening Established

Sleep is when the nervous system is supposed to reset — consolidating memories, clearing metabolic waste, and dampening pain sensitization. When central sensitization disrupts sleep architecture, the reset never happens.

  • Alpha-delta intrusion: Alpha waves (8–13 Hz) intrude into delta-wave deep sleep, producing a hybrid state that looks like sleep on the surface but is not restorative. The patient sleeps for 10 hours and wakes exhausted
  • Prevalence: 66% of fibromyalgia patients show alpha-delta intrusion on polysomnography. Central sensitization and alpha-delta sleep are so tightly linked that some researchers consider them aspects of the same process
  • Bidirectional cycle: Pain disrupts sleep → poor sleep lowers pain thresholds → more pain → worse sleep. This cycle can accelerate rapidly in children
  • Detection: Polysomnography with full EEG montage can detect alpha-delta intrusion, but you must specifically request alpha intrusion reporting. Standard PSG focuses on AHI (apnea-hypopnea index) for sleep apnea and may not analyze or report EEG waveform intrusion patterns

Measuring Central Sensitization — Quantitative Sensory Testing Established

Central sensitization is not a vague concept — it can be objectively measured using Quantitative Sensory Testing (QST). This is critical because it provides a measurable baseline to track whether treatment is working.

  • Pressure Pain Thresholds (PPT) at remote sites: Lowered PPT at sites far from the injury (e.g., testing the forearm for a spine patient) indicates central sensitization. If the patient is tender everywhere, the problem is central, not peripheral
  • Temporal Summation: Repeated identical stimuli produce increasing pain ratings. Enhanced temporal summation = dorsal horn hyperexcitability (the wind-up phenomenon)
  • Conditioned Pain Modulation (CPM): Apply a painful stimulus at one site while testing pain thresholds at another. Normally, pain inhibits pain (diffuse noxious inhibitory controls). Deficient CPM = impaired descending inhibition — the braking system is not working
  • Validated in children: QST has been validated in children aged 6 and older with published pediatric protocols. It is not an experimental or adult-only test
  • Tracking: QST provides quantitative data that can be repeated over time to assess whether central sensitization is improving, stable, or worsening in response to treatment
Question for the doctor: “Can we order Quantitative Sensory Testing to objectively assess whether central sensitization has developed? This would give us a measurable baseline to track treatment response.”

Treatment — Breaking the Cycle Supported

Structural intervention (such as decompression surgery if indicated) removes the nociceptive input that is driving sensitization. But it does not reverse existing central sensitization. The activated glia, the rewired dorsal horn, the impaired descending inhibition — these persist after the peripheral source is removed. Treatment must combine structural and central approaches.

1. Low-Dose Naltrexone (LDN)

LDN blocks TLR-4 receptors on microglia, reducing the release of TNF-α and IL-1β that maintain central sensitization. A 14-year retrospective study showed a 65% benefit rate. LDN is off-label for this use and should be discussed with the medical team. See Pain Convergence Stack for dosing details.

2. Low-Dose Amitriptyline

At low doses (10–25 mg at bedtime), amitriptyline enhances descending inhibitory pathways (serotonin and norepinephrine) that dampen dorsal horn excitability. It simultaneously improves sleep quality by suppressing alpha-delta intrusion. This is a two-for-one intervention targeting both central sensitization and the sleep disruption that perpetuates it.

3. Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE)

Age-appropriate education about how pain works is itself an evidence-based intervention. Understanding that “your body’s alarm system is working too hard — it’s like a smoke detector that goes off when you’re making toast” reduces catastrophizing, fear-avoidance, and hypervigilance. PNE has been shown to reduce pain intensity and improve function in pediatric chronic pain.

4. Graded Motor Imagery (GMI)

A three-stage program that retrains the cortical body map without activating peripheral nociceptors:

  1. Laterality recognition: Identifying left/right body parts in images (activates premotor cortex without movement)
  2. Imagined movements: Mentally rehearsing movements without performing them (activates motor cortex without peripheral input)
  3. Mirror therapy: Using mirror visual feedback to create painless movement illusions (provides cortical “proof” that movement is safe)

5. Aquatic Therapy

Water provides an exercise stimulus while dramatically reducing nociceptive load via buoyancy. The hydrostatic pressure reduces edema, and the warm water activates descending inhibitory pathways via thermoreception. See Aquatic Therapy for the full protocol.

6. Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation (TENS)

TENS activates A-beta fibers that inhibit pain transmission at the dorsal horn via gate control mechanisms. It is non-invasive, has no side effects, and can be used at home. While not a standalone treatment for central sensitization, it can reduce the nociceptive barrage that maintains it.

7. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT has the strongest pediatric evidence base of any psychological intervention for chronic pain. It specifically addresses catastrophizing (the strongest predictor of pain chronification in children), fear-avoidance behavior, and hypervigilance — all of which are both consequences and drivers of central sensitization.

8. Sleep Optimization

Breaking the alpha-delta cycle may be the single highest-yield intervention. Every night of non-restorative sleep is a night where descending inhibition fails to recover and central sensitization consolidates further. See the evening protocol in the Complete 24-Hour Protocol for specific sleep optimization strategies including timed-release melatonin, magnesium glycinate, and sleep hygiene measures.

Juvenile Fibromyalgia Overlap Supported

Juvenile fibromyalgia (JFM) shares striking mechanistic overlap with central sensitization in connective tissue disorders. Recognizing this overlap is critical because treatments that work for JFM directly target the central sensitization that drives pain amplification in Marshall syndrome.

The Hypermobility-Fibromyalgia Connection

  • 40% of JFM patients have joint hypermobility — this is not a coincidence. Hypermobile joints generate more nociceptive input (subluxations, microtrauma, abnormal joint loading), which drives the dorsal horn plasticity that underlies both conditions
  • Shared mechanism: Both JFM and central sensitization in hypermobility disorders demonstrate lowered pain thresholds on QST and increased wind-up ratios, confirming that the same spinal cord-level amplification process is at work
  • Diagnostic overlap: A child with Marshall syndrome who develops widespread pain, fatigue, sleep disruption, and cognitive fog may meet JFM diagnostic criteria — but the underlying driver is central sensitization from their connective tissue disorder, not a separate condition

Screening: Widespread Pain Index + Symptom Severity Scale

The Widespread Pain Index (WPI) and Symptom Severity Scale (SSS) are the validated screening tools for fibromyalgia-spectrum central sensitization. They can be applied in pediatric patients to quantify the extent of pain amplification:

  • WPI: Counts the number of body areas (out of 19) where the patient has experienced pain in the past week. A score ≥7 indicates widespread pain
  • SSS: Rates severity (0–3 each) of fatigue, waking unrefreshed, and cognitive symptoms, plus somatic symptom count. Score range 0–12
  • Fibromyalgia criteria: WPI ≥7 and SSS ≥5, OR WPI 4–6 and SSS ≥9. These thresholds identify patients with clinically significant central sensitization regardless of the underlying structural diagnosis
Critical treatment sequencing: Central sensitization must be addressed before exercise rehabilitation programs can succeed. A centrally sensitized nervous system responds to exercise with increased pain (exercise-induced hyperalgesia) rather than the normal analgesic response. Starting an aggressive physical therapy program in a child with active central sensitization will likely increase pain, reinforce fear-avoidance, and undermine adherence. The correct sequence is: (1) reduce central sensitization with pharmacological and educational interventions, (2) restore sleep architecture, (3) begin graded exercise once the nervous system can respond normally to physical activity.

Questions for the Medical Team

Bring these specific questions to your next appointment:

  1. “Can we order Quantitative Sensory Testing to assess whether central sensitization has developed?”
  2. “Can we get polysomnography with full EEG montage, specifically reporting alpha intrusion into NREM sleep?”
  3. “If cervical decompression is indicated, what is the plan for concurrent central desensitization? Decompression alone may not resolve pain if central sensitization is already established.”
  4. “Would low-dose amitriptyline at bedtime address both central sensitization and sleep disruption simultaneously?”
  5. “Would you consider low-dose naltrexone as a glial modulator to reduce microglial-driven neuroinflammation?”
  6. “Can we get a referral for CBT with a therapist who has pediatric pain neuroscience education experience?”
