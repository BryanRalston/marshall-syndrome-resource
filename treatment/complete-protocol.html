Complete 24-Hour Protocol

Medical Disclaimer
This information is compiled from peer-reviewed research and is intended for educational discussion with qualified medical professionals. These are NOT treatment recommendations. This protocol combines multiple interventions that must each be individually evaluated and approved by your child’s physician. Never implement a complex protocol without medical oversight.

This page integrates the morning protocol, daytime nutrition, evening wind-down, and overnight protection into a single 24-hour cycle. Every element is timed to align with circadian biology and the specific inflammatory rhythms of Marshall syndrome disc pathology. The protocol is designed for a school-age child (~9 years old, ~30 kg) and should be adapted to your child’s specific needs with physician guidance.

Afternoon (2:00–5:00 PM)

This is the optimal exercise window for Marshall syndrome. Disc pressure has normalized from overnight rehydration (1–2 hour danger window has passed), and cortisol levels support physical activity:

  • Aquatic therapy / swimming: See Aquatic Therapy for full protocol. 45–60 minute sessions, 2–3x per week
  • Land-based PT: On non-swimming days. Core stabilization, proprioceptive training, gentle cardiovascular activity. Avoid high-impact (running, jumping)
  • PBM session: If scheduled (3x/week), apply photobiomodulation before swimming or as a standalone session on rest days
  • Post-exercise snack: Protein + carbohydrate within 30 minutes (e.g., Greek yogurt with berries). The post-exercise window is when irisin-mediated NRF2 activation peaks — providing NRF2 substrates (protein for glutathione synthesis) at this time supports the response

Evening Protocol (7:00–9:00 PM)

The evening protocol is the most critical block of the day. It prepares the body for the overnight protection window and deploys the 5-agent pain convergence stack.

7:00 PM — Screen Curfew

Non-negotiable: All screens off or switched to strong blue-light filtering (Night Shift, f.lux, or amber glasses). Blue light (460–480 nm) suppresses pineal melatonin production for up to 90 minutes after exposure. Late-evening screen use directly delays melatonin onset, shifting the protection window and leaving the 2 AM destruction period uncovered.

7:00–7:30 PM — Gentle Evening Stretching

  • Supine spinal twist: 30 seconds each side. Gentle rotational mobilization of thoracolumbar junction
  • Knees-to-chest: 60 seconds. Opens posterior disc space and stretches paraspinal muscles that have been guarding all day
  • Prone press-up (modified cobra): 5 repetitions, held 5 seconds each. McKenzie-style extension for anterior disc space opening. Only if tolerated — skip if this position increases pain
  • Hamstring stretch: 30 seconds each side, supine with strap. Tight hamstrings increase pelvic posterior tilt and alter lumbar loading

7:30–8:00 PM — Warm Bath

9:00 PM — Bedtime

  • LDN (1.5 mg): Taken at lights-out. The brief opioid receptor blockade (4 hours) is followed by a rebound upregulation of endogenous endorphin production that peaks during the 2–4 AM window
  • Sleep position: Side-lying with pillow between knees (reduces spinal torsion and maintains neutral lumbar alignment)
  • Room conditions: Cool (18–20°C / 64–68°F), dark (blackout curtains or sleep mask), quiet. Cool temperature supports deep sleep and growth hormone secretion
  • Sleep duration target: 10–11 hours for a 9-year-old. This is not optional — growth hormone release occurs primarily during slow-wave sleep, and GH is the primary driver of childhood cartilage and collagen repair

The 2 AM Destruction Window

Critical concept: Between approximately 1:00 and 4:00 AM, inflammatory cytokine levels (IL-1β, TNF-α, IL-6) peak as part of the normal circadian immune rhythm. In Marshall syndrome, these cytokines activate MMPs that degrade already-compromised disc tissue. This is not theoretical — multiple studies confirm that disc degradation is not constant but peaks during this nocturnal inflammatory window.

The entire evening protocol is designed to have maximum protective effect during this window:

  • Timed-release melatonin: Maintains therapeutic melatonin levels through 2–4 AM (immediate-release peaks and crashes by midnight)
  • LDN endorphin rebound: The 4-hour blockade (9 PM–1 AM) releases into endorphin upregulation just as the inflammatory window begins
  • PEA: Evening dose maintains PPAR-α activation through the night
  • Magnesium: NMDA antagonism reduces pain signaling amplification during the window

Why Timed-Release Melatonin (Not Immediate-Release)

This distinction is critical and frequently misunderstood:

FormulationPlasma Profile2 AM CoverageAssessment
Immediate-releaseRapid spike at ~30 min, crashes to near-zero by midnightNONE — melatonin is gone before the destruction windowHelps with sleep onset only; misses the inflammatory protection window entirely
Timed-releaseGradual rise, sustained plateau through 2–5 AM, gentle declineFULL COVERAGE — therapeutic levels maintained through the entire windowMimics natural pineal secretion profile; provides both sleep support AND anti-inflammatory protection
Dose rationale: 0.3–1 mg is the established physiological range for sleep support. Higher doses (1–3 mg extended-release) may be appropriate for specific therapeutic goals such as anti-inflammatory and anti-ferroptotic protection — emerging research supports this, but it should be discussed with your physician. Very high doses (5–10 mg, commonly sold over the counter) create supraphysiological peaks that can cause morning grogginess and paradoxically disrupt sleep architecture. The goal is to support the natural melatonin curve, with higher therapeutic doses only under medical guidance.

Tracking and Adjustment

This protocol is a starting framework, not a rigid prescription. Track these variables to guide adjustments:

  • Morning stiffness duration: Record daily. Trend over weeks matters more than day-to-day variation. Decreasing duration indicates improving overnight inflammatory control
  • Night waking: Number and timing of pain-related wake events. If consistently waking at 2–3 AM, the timed-release melatonin dose or timing may need adjustment
  • Energy level: Rate 1–5 at morning, midday, and evening. Persistent morning fatigue may indicate sleep quality issues despite adequate duration
  • Pain quality: Distinguish aching (mechanical) from burning/tingling (neuropathic) from stiffness (inflammatory). Each responds to different elements of the protocol
  • Exercise tolerance: Track swimming distance and post-exercise pain. Both should trend positively over 6–12 weeks
Remember: This is a child. Perfection is the enemy of consistency. A protocol that is followed 80% of the time for years will vastly outperform a perfect protocol that is abandoned after two weeks. Build habits gradually, prioritize the highest-impact elements (sulforaphane, swimming, timed-release melatonin, sleep hygiene), and be flexible with the rest.
