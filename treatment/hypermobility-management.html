Hypermobility: From Protection to Management

The double-edged feature. Joint hypermobility in Marshall syndrome is a double-edged feature — protective flexibility in childhood that transitions to instability and early arthritis. Proprioceptive training and joint protection strategies started early can slow this transition.

Nearly all children with Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutations) have some degree of joint hypermobility. Unlike the benign flexibility seen in many young children, Marshall hypermobility stems from a structural defect in the connective tissue itself — defective type XI collagen that alters the mechanical properties of ligaments, tendons, and joint capsules throughout the body. Understanding this mechanism is the key to managing it effectively.

Understanding Hypermobility in COL11A1

How Defective Type XI Collagen Causes Laxity

Type XI collagen is a regulatory collagen that controls the diameter of type II collagen fibrils — the primary structural collagen in cartilage, ligaments, and the vitreous humor of the eye. The COL11A1 gene encodes the alpha-1 chain of the type XI collagen heterotrimer. When COL11A1 is mutated:

  • Fibril diameter regulation fails. Type II collagen fibrils form with irregular, often enlarged diameters, reducing the organized tensile strength of connective tissue.
  • Cross-linking is impaired. Type XI collagen normally nucleates and organizes type II fibrils. Without proper type XI, the fibrillar lattice is disorganized and mechanically weaker.
  • Ligaments become lax. Ligaments rely on organized collagen fibrils for tensile strength. Disorganized fibrils stretch more easily under load, resulting in increased range of motion at every joint.
  • Joint capsules are loose. The capsule surrounding each joint is similarly affected, providing less passive restraint to movement.

The Beighton Score

The Beighton score is the standard clinical tool for quantifying generalized joint hypermobility. It uses five maneuvers scored on a 9-point scale:

How Marshall Hypermobility Differs

It is important to distinguish Marshall syndrome hypermobility from other conditions:

  • Benign joint hypermobility: Common in children (10–15% of the population), typically asymptomatic, and decreases naturally with age. No underlying collagen defect — just natural variation in ligament stiffness.
  • Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS): A systemic connective tissue disorder with widespread hypermobility, skin involvement, and often autonomic dysfunction. The genetic basis is unknown (no identified gene). hEDS patients often have soft, velvety, or stretchy skin — features not typically prominent in Marshall syndrome.
  • Marshall syndrome (COL11A1): Hypermobility is part of a known structural collagen defect that also affects cartilage, the vitreous, the inner ear, and facial development. The hypermobility is predictable (it follows from the collagen defect) and progressive (it leads to early osteoarthritis because the same collagen defect affects articular cartilage). The distinguishing feature is that Marshall hypermobility coexists with vitreoretinal disease and sensorineural hearing loss.

The Protective Phase

In young children, hypermobility can actually be protective. This is counterintuitive but important to understand:

  • Flexible joints absorb impact better. A toddler who falls (which happens constantly) benefits from joints that give rather than resist. Rigid joints transmit impact forces directly to bone; flexible joints dissipate energy through soft tissue deformation.
  • Fewer fractures in early childhood. Hypermobile children often have fewer fractures from falls compared to their peers, precisely because their joints bend rather than break.
  • Natural movement exploration. Young children naturally move through full range of motion during play. Hypermobility at this age rarely causes pain because the tissues are still healthy and resilient.
When to appreciate flexibility vs. when to worry. In a child under 5 with Marshall syndrome, hypermobility alone is not a problem. It becomes a concern when it is accompanied by: joint pain during or after activity, visible instability (knees buckling, ankles rolling), reluctance to bear weight or participate in physical activity, or recurrent sprains or subluxations. The critical distinction is that hypermobility only becomes a clinical problem when it causes instability, pain, or injury.

The Transition to Instability

The protective phase does not last. Over time, hypermobility becomes pathological through a well-understood cascade:

  1. Repeated microtrauma to ligaments. Every time a joint moves to the end of its (excessive) range, the ligaments sustain microscopic damage. In normal tissue, this heals completely. In COL11A1-defective tissue, repair is imperfect — the replacement collagen is also defective.
  2. Progressive ligament laxity. Each cycle of microtrauma and imperfect repair makes the ligament slightly more lax. The joint becomes incrementally less stable over months and years.
  3. Joint instability. Once ligaments can no longer hold the joint in proper alignment, the joint begins to sublux (partially dislocate) during normal activities.
  4. Abnormal joint loading. An unstable joint does not distribute forces evenly across the articular surface. Certain areas of cartilage bear disproportionate load.
  5. Early osteoarthritis. Abnormal loading accelerates cartilage breakdown, compounded by the fact that the cartilage itself is structurally deficient (type XI collagen is essential for cartilage integrity). See Osteoarthritis in Marshall Syndrome for the full picture.
The puberty acceleration. This transition typically accelerates during puberty. Hormonal changes (particularly estrogen fluctuations) increase ligament laxity further, growth spurts change joint biomechanics rapidly, and increased body mass adds load to already-vulnerable joints. The combination of hormonal laxity, rapid growth, and increased athletic participation makes the pubertal years a high-risk window for the transition from protective hypermobility to pathological instability.

Joint-by-Joint Protection Strategies

Each commonly affected joint area requires specific strategies. The general principle is the same everywhere: strengthen the muscles that stabilize the joint, avoid positions that stress lax ligaments, and use external supports when muscles alone are insufficient.

Elbows

Elbow hyperextension is one of the five Beighton criteria and is common in COL11A1 hypermobility. While elbows are less frequently the source of major disability than knees or shoulders, chronic hyperextension causes cumulative ligament damage and contributes to pain during weight-bearing activities (push-ups, crawling, carrying).

  • Teach “soft elbows.” Never lock elbows straight during weight-bearing activities such as push-ups, planks, or carrying objects with extended arms. A micro-bend should become habitual.
  • Proprioceptive training. Wall push-ups with focus on controlled range (stop before full extension), gradually progressing to floor push-ups with elbows slightly bent throughout.
  • Strengthen biceps and triceps. Co-contraction of the elbow flexors and extensors provides active joint stability. Use controlled, mid-range bicep curls and tricep presses — never locking out at full extension.
  • Avoid carrying heavy bags with extended elbows. Use a forearm carry position (elbow bent to 90 degrees) for grocery bags, backpacks carried by hand, and similar loads.
  • Compression sleeves. Neoprene elbow sleeves during sports provide proprioceptive feedback and gentle warmth without restricting functional range.

Hips

Hip hypermobility in COL11A1 disorders contributes to labral tears, anterior impingement, snapping hip syndrome, and accelerated hip OA. The hips are among the most commonly replaced joints in Stickler/Marshall syndrome.

  • Core strengthening is hip protection. A stable pelvis reduces compensatory hip movement. Planks, bird-dogs, dead bugs, and Pallof presses all engage the deep core muscles that anchor the pelvis during gait.
  • Gluteal strengthening. The gluteus medius and minimus are the primary lateral hip stabilizers. Clamshells, side-lying hip abduction, monster walks with resistance bands, and single-leg bridges target these muscles specifically.
  • Avoid W-sitting in children. W-sitting (knees bent, feet splayed out to either side) places extreme internal rotational and valgus stress on the hip capsule and reinforces ligament laxity. Redirect to cross-legged sitting, long-sitting, or side-sitting positions consistently.
  • Monitor for hip clicking, catching, or groin pain. These symptoms may indicate labral involvement or anterior impingement and warrant imaging evaluation (MR arthrogram is the gold standard for labral tears).
  • Avoid extreme hip positions. Full lotus, deep butterflies, and splits stress the hip capsule in directions of maximal laxity. Yoga modifications should eliminate these positions.

Knees

The knees are often the first joints to become symptomatic in Marshall syndrome because they bear full body weight and are frequently hyperextended.

  • Avoid hyperextension when standing. Teach children to stand with a "micro-bend" in the knees rather than locking them backward. This is a habit that must be consciously practiced until it becomes automatic.
  • Proprioceptive training. Single-leg balance, wobble board exercises, and eyes-closed standing all train the neuromuscular system to stabilize the knee without conscious effort.
  • Quadriceps and hamstring co-contraction. Isometric wall sits, mini squats (never past 90 degrees), and step-ups build the muscles that actively stabilize the knee.
  • Hinged knee braces. When proprioceptive training and strengthening are insufficient, a hinged knee brace with hyperextension stops provides mechanical limits. Use during sports or prolonged standing, not 24/7 (to avoid muscle atrophy).

Ankles

  • High-top shoes. Provide passive ankle support during daily activities. Not a substitute for strengthening, but an easy baseline intervention.
  • Ankle taping for sports. Figure-of-eight taping or lace-up ankle braces during athletic activities provide inversion/eversion restraint without fully immobilizing the joint.
  • Calf raises and single-leg balance. Strengthen the peroneal muscles that resist ankle inversion (the most common direction of ankle sprains).
  • Ankle-foot orthoses (AFOs). Reserved for significant instability that does not respond to bracing and strengthening. AFOs control both the ankle and the subtalar joint. A pediatric orthotist should evaluate the need.

Wrists and Hands

  • Writing fatigue. Hypermobile fingers and wrists fatigue quickly during handwriting. Use ergonomic pencil grips (triangular or weighted), allow keyboard use for longer assignments (see School 504 Accommodations), and take frequent breaks.
  • Wrist splints. Lightweight wrist splints (with or without thumb support) can help during writing-intensive tasks. Daytime splints should be flexible enough to allow functional grip; nighttime splints can be more rigid for rest positioning.
  • Ring splints. Oval-8 plastic splints or silver ring splints block hyperextension at the PIP and DIP joints of the fingers while allowing full flexion. They increase proprioceptive awareness and prevent the painful "snapping back" of hyperextended fingers. See the Orthotics and Bracing section below for details.
  • Ergonomic tools. Thick-handled utensils, scissors with spring-return, and jar openers reduce the grip force required for daily tasks.

Shoulders

  • Avoid overhead throwing. The shoulder is a highly mobile joint even in typical anatomy. In hypermobile individuals, overhead throwing (baseball, volleyball serving) places extreme stress on the anterior capsule and labrum. Modify sports participation accordingly.
  • Rotator cuff strengthening. External rotation with resistance bands, prone Y-T-W exercises, and scapular stabilization exercises protect the glenohumeral joint by improving dynamic stability.
  • Sleeping position. Avoid sleeping with the arm overhead or under the pillow, which stretches the anterior capsule. Side-lying with a pillow hugged to the chest keeps the shoulder in a neutral position.

Spine

Spinal hypermobility in Marshall syndrome is covered extensively in other sections due to its connection with disc disease. See Physical Therapy for core stabilization programs and Osteoarthritis for disc degeneration specifics. The key principle: a strong core (transversus abdominis, multifidus, pelvic floor) acts as a muscular corset that compensates for ligamentous laxity in the spine.

Feet

  • Flat arches (pes planus). Extremely common in COL11A1 hypermobility. The ligaments supporting the medial longitudinal arch are lax, causing the arch to collapse under body weight.
  • Arch support. Start with off-the-shelf supportive insoles (Superfeet Green or PowerStep Original are commonly recommended starting points). If symptoms persist, custom-molded orthotics from a podiatrist provide more precise correction.
  • Footwear recommendations. Firm heel counter (squeeze the back of the shoe — it should not collapse), structured midsole with arch support, wide toe box to avoid crowding hypermobile toes, and avoid flat shoes (ballet flats, flip-flops, Converse) that provide no support.
  • When to see a podiatrist. Persistent foot or ankle pain, visible arch collapse when standing, asymmetric wear patterns on shoes, or toe deformities (bunions, hammertoes) developing in childhood.

Proprioceptive Training: The Core Intervention

What Is Proprioception?

Proprioception is the body's sense of joint position and movement — the ability to know where your limbs are in space without looking at them. It relies on mechanoreceptors embedded in ligaments, joint capsules, tendons, and muscles that send continuous position data to the brain.

Why Proprioception Is Impaired in COL11A1

In Marshall syndrome, proprioception is impaired at the hardware level:

  • Defective mechanoreceptors. The mechanoreceptors in ligaments and joint capsules are embedded in collagen-rich tissue. When the collagen matrix is structurally abnormal (as in COL11A1 mutations), the mechanoreceptors do not transduce mechanical signals as effectively.
  • Excess joint play. Because the ligaments are lax, there is more "noise" in the proprioceptive signal — the joint moves through a larger range before the receptors register meaningful position data.
  • Delayed protective reflexes. The neuromuscular response to unexpected joint movement (the ankle about to roll, the knee about to hyperextend) is slower, increasing the risk of injury.

Proprioceptive Training Exercises

The goal is to train the neuromuscular system to compensate for impaired ligament-based proprioception by developing muscle-based joint awareness:

Orthotics, Bracing & Support

External supports serve two purposes in hypermobility: mechanical restraint (physically limiting excessive range of motion) and proprioceptive enhancement (the compression and skin contact from a brace provides additional sensory input that improves joint position sense). The key principle is to use the minimum effective support — over-bracing leads to muscle atrophy and increased dependence.

Exercise Principles for Hypermobile Joints

Here is the central paradox of hypermobility management: exercise is the single most important intervention, but the wrong exercise causes injury. The goal is to build strength and neuromuscular control without stressing already-lax ligaments.

Principles

  • Closed-chain over open-chain. Closed-chain exercises (where the hand or foot is fixed — squats, push-ups, step-ups) produce co-contraction of multiple muscle groups, compressing and stabilizing the joint. Open-chain exercises (leg extensions, arm curls) can allow the joint to move through excessive range under load.
  • Controlled range of motion. Never move to the end of the available range. Stop short of full extension at every joint. "50–75% of full range" is a useful cue.
  • Isometric before dynamic. Isometric holds (wall sits, planks, static lunges) build strength and proprioceptive control without joint movement. Master isometric stability before progressing to dynamic exercises.
  • Eccentric strengthening. Controlled eccentric loading (slowly lowering weight, slow descent in a squat) builds strength through the lengthening phase, which is particularly important for deceleration control in hypermobile joints.
  • Low-impact over high-impact. Swimming, cycling, and elliptical training provide cardiovascular benefits without the joint stress of running or jumping.

What to Avoid

  • Yoga stretching and passive stretching. Hypermobile joints do not need more flexibility. Yoga poses that emphasize end-range positions (splits, deep backbends, full lotus) are particularly risky. If yoga is desired, focus exclusively on strength-building poses (warrior, plank) with cues to avoid hyperextension.
  • Plyometrics and bouncing. Box jumps, jump squats, and any explosive landing activity places enormous stress on lax ligaments and increases subluxation risk.
  • Hyperextension at any joint. This applies to exercise and daily life. No locking knees during squats, no hyperextending elbows during push-ups, no pressing the spine into deep extension during back exercises.
  • Heavy overhead lifting. Shoulder instability makes overhead press and snatch-type movements high-risk without extensive preparation and scaled weight.

Pool Exercise: The Ideal Environment

Aquatic exercise is arguably the single best exercise modality for hypermobile individuals. Buoyancy reduces joint loading by 50–90% (depending on immersion depth), warm water relaxes muscles and reduces pain, water resistance provides gentle strengthening in all directions, and hydrostatic pressure provides proprioceptive input similar to compression garments. See Aquatic Therapy for a comprehensive program guide and Exercise Recommendations for land-based protocols.

Physical Therapy: Finding the Right Approach

Finding a PT Who Understands Hypermobility

Not all physical therapists understand hypermobility disorders. A therapist trained in general orthopedic rehabilitation may inadvertently prescribe stretching, flexibility work, or end-range mobilization exercises that worsen instability and cause injury. The difference between an informed PT and an uninformed one can be the difference between improvement and harm.

  • Look for experience with hypermobility spectrum disorders (EDS, Marfan, Stickler/Marshall). Even experience with one of these conditions translates well to the others.
  • Confirm they understand that flexibility is not the goal. The first question to ask is: “What is your approach to stretching in hypermobile patients?” The correct answer is some version of “we don’t stretch hypermobile joints.”
  • Look for knowledge of proprioceptive training. Ask whether their program includes balance training, unstable surfaces, and eyes-closed progressions. If the answer focuses only on strengthening without mentioning proprioception, look elsewhere.
  • Willingness to learn: If no hypermobility-experienced PT is available locally, a skilled general PT who is willing to study the condition and collaborate with the treating physician can be effective. Bring a printed summary of the diagnosis and this resource.
Red Flags in Physical Therapy
If a physical therapist prescribes aggressive stretching, yoga-style flexibility work, passive mobilization of already-hypermobile joints, or tells a child with Marshall syndrome they need to “loosen up” or “increase range of motion,” seek a different provider immediately. These individuals already have excessive range of motion. Additional stretching destabilizes joints further, increases subluxation risk, and accelerates the path to early osteoarthritis.

Dry Needling: A Specific Caution in Hypermobility

Dry needling should generally be AVOIDED in hypermobile patients unless performed by a specialist with specific expertise in connective tissue disorders.

Dry needling — the insertion of thin needles into myofascial trigger points or taut bands of muscle — is an increasingly popular physical therapy modality for pain management. However, in hypermobile connective tissue disorders like Marshall syndrome, it carries risks that are not present in the general population.

Why Taut Bands May Be Protective

The central concern is this: in hypermobile patients, taut bands in muscles may serve a protective stabilization function. When ligaments are lax and joint capsules are loose, the surrounding muscles must provide active stabilization. Muscles that are chronically engaged in stabilization develop palpable tautness that a general PT may identify as “pathological” trigger points requiring release. But these taut bands may be:

  • Compensatory stabilizers: Muscles that have tightened to protect unstable joints. Releasing them removes a layer of active joint protection and can immediately worsen instability.
  • Proprioceptive anchors: Taut muscles contribute to the body’s sense of joint position. Releasing them can reduce proprioceptive input, which is already impaired in hypermobility.
  • Protective splinting: Particularly around the cervical spine, shoulder girdle, and hip, increased muscle tone serves as a functional splint. In a patient with collagen-deficient ligaments, this muscular splinting is doing the work that the ligaments cannot.

Specific Risks in Marshall Syndrome

  • Worsened joint instability: Releasing taut bands that are providing compensatory stabilization can unmask underlying instability, leading to increased subluxation frequency, joint pain, and functional decline
  • Collagen-deficient tissue response: Needle insertion creates micro-trauma in connective tissue. In COL11A1 mutations, the healing response may be impaired or delayed, potentially causing more tissue disruption than intended
  • Cervical spine risk: Dry needling of cervical paraspinal muscles in a patient with Marshall syndrome cervical disc degeneration and potential instability carries risk of neural irritation or worsening of cervical symptoms
  • Paradoxical pain increase: Some hypermobile patients report that dry needling provides brief relief followed by increased pain and instability, consistent with the removal of protective muscle tone

When Dry Needling Might Be Considered

Dry needling is not absolutely contraindicated in all hypermobile patients, but it should only be considered when:

  • The treating practitioner has specific expertise in connective tissue disorders and understands the protective role of muscle tone in hypermobility
  • The target muscle has been specifically assessed to determine whether its tautness is pathological (genuinely contributing to pain) rather than compensatory (providing needed stabilization)
  • The patient has adequate baseline muscular stability in the surrounding region — meaning other muscles can compensate if the targeted band is released
  • Alternative approaches (gentle isometric strengthening, proprioceptive training, thermal modalities) have been tried first and proven insufficient
What to say to your PT: If dry needling is proposed, ask: “Have you considered whether these taut bands might be providing compensatory stabilization for my child’s hypermobile joints? Can we try strengthening the surrounding muscles first before attempting to release them?” A PT who understands hypermobility will recognize this as a valid concern. A PT who dismisses it may not be the right provider.

Strengthening Over Stretching

The fundamental principle of PT in hypermobility is the reversal of the typical rehabilitation approach:

  • Conventional PT: Stretch tight structures, restore range of motion, then strengthen
  • Hypermobility PT: Strengthen weak muscles to compensate for lax ligaments. Stretching is generally unnecessary and often harmful. The goal is to reduce effective range of motion by building muscular control throughout the available range.

The muscles surrounding hypermobile joints must work harder than normal to maintain alignment during every movement. They function as active stabilizers where passive stabilizers (ligaments, joint capsules) are structurally insufficient. Building endurance in these muscles is equally important as building raw strength — a muscle that is strong but fatigues quickly leaves the joint unprotected.

Exercise Prescription Principles for the PT

  • High repetition, low resistance: Build muscular endurance before peak strength. Fatigued muscles in hypermobile joints lead to compensatory movements and subluxation risk.
  • Controlled mid-range: Exercise within the middle 50–75% of available range. Avoid end-range positions where ligament laxity is maximal and protective muscle activation is minimal.
  • Slow, controlled movements: No ballistic or plyometric exercises in early phases. Speed challenges proprioception, and hypermobile joints need time to develop neuromuscular control before adding velocity.
  • Symmetry correction: Address side-to-side strength and stability imbalances aggressively. Asymmetry in hypermobility compounds injury risk on the weaker side.
  • Consistency over intensity: 20 minutes daily is more effective than 60 minutes three times per week. Stabilizing muscles need constant reinforcement; long gaps between sessions allow deconditioning to creep back.

Activity Guidance

Physical activity is essential — not optional — in COL11A1 hypermobility. Inactivity leads to muscle deconditioning, which worsens joint instability in a vicious cycle. The goal is choosing the right activities, not avoiding activity altogether. Beyond joint-level benefits, regular exercise activates the NRF2 pathway, suppressing inflammatory enzymes (including MMP-13) that drive cartilage and disc degeneration. See NRF2 Convergence for the molecular mechanism.

Caution Activities

These activities can be appropriate with proper training, supervision, and protective equipment, but carry elevated risk:

  • Contact sports (soccer, basketball, hockey): Risk of joint injuries from collisions and rapid direction changes. May be acceptable at recreational level with adequate muscle conditioning and appropriate bracing. Competitive level carries significantly higher risk.
  • Gymnastics: Hypermobile children often excel early because their flexibility appears advantageous. However, repetitive extreme ranges of motion and high-impact landings cause cumulative ligament and cartilage damage that manifests later. Recreational classes with modifications may be acceptable; competitive gymnastics is strongly discouraged.
  • Dance: Similar to gymnastics. Hypermobile individuals may appear gifted, but pursuing extreme positions (turnout, splits, arabesques) damages lax joints. Low-impact styles (modern, ballroom) are safer than ballet or hip-hop.
  • Strength training: Highly beneficial when done correctly (closed-chain, controlled range, proper form), but incorrect form or excessive weight risks subluxation. Professional instruction is essential for the first 6–12 months.

Activities to Avoid

  • Extreme ranges of motion: Competitive stretching, contortion, splits training, party tricks that “show off” hypermobility
  • Heavy impact: Trampolining, box jumps, plyometrics, running on concrete or hard surfaces
  • Collision sports: Rugby, American football, martial arts with sparring or throwing
  • Overhead throwing: Baseball pitching, javelin, cricket bowling, volleyball serving (high shoulder subluxation risk)
  • Powerlifting and Olympic lifting: Heavy deadlifts, squats to maximal depth, snatches, and clean-and-jerk all load joints at or near end-range under extreme force
The “Party Trick” Conversation: Many hypermobile children discover that bending their fingers backward, hyperextending their elbows, or demonstrating unusual flexibility gets a reaction from peers. While this is a natural response, each demonstration stresses already-lax ligaments and contributes to cumulative damage. Frame the conversation positively: “Your body is special and you need to protect it, the way an athlete protects their body for the long run.”

Daily Life Modifications

Joint protection is not just about exercise — it extends into every activity of daily life:

Carrying and Lifting

  • Backpack with waist strap. A hip belt transfers load from the shoulders (unstable) to the pelvis (stable). Avoid single-shoulder bags entirely.
  • Distribute weight evenly. When carrying grocery bags or equipment, split the load between both hands rather than overloading one side.
  • Lift with legs, never with a rounded back. This applies to everyone, but is critical in hypermobile spines where disc injury risk is already elevated.

Writing and School Work

  • Ergonomic pencil grips. Triangular, weighted, or built-up grips reduce the force needed to maintain pencil control with hypermobile fingers.
  • Laptop or tablet for longer assignments. Typing is less fatiguing than handwriting for most hypermobile children. This is an appropriate 504 accommodation.
  • Slant board. An angled writing surface reduces wrist extension and shoulder elevation.
  • Frequent breaks. 5 minutes of hand rest for every 15–20 minutes of writing.

Sleeping

  • Side-lying with pillow between knees. Maintains hip and spinal alignment and prevents the top knee from dropping into hyperextension.
  • Avoid stomach sleeping. Forces the neck into extreme rotation and the lumbar spine into hyperextension.
  • Supportive pillow under wrists. If wrist pain is an issue, a small rolled towel under the wrist maintains neutral position during sleep.

Sitting

  • Avoid W-sitting in young children. W-sitting (sitting on the floor with knees bent and feet splayed out to either side) places extreme rotational stress on the hips and knees and reinforces ligament laxity. Redirect to cross-legged, long-sit, or side-sit positions.
  • Supportive chair with feet flat on the floor. Feet should not dangle (use a footrest if needed). Avoid chairs that encourage slouching.
  • Move frequently. Static positions are poorly tolerated by hypermobile joints. Change position every 20–30 minutes.

Standing

  • Never lock the knees. Maintain a micro-bend at all times. This is the single most important standing habit to develop.
  • Shift weight regularly. Standing on one leg for extended periods overloads that side. Shift between feet or use a footrest to alternate.
  • Anti-fatigue mat. For prolonged standing (kitchen, lab), a cushioned mat reduces joint stress.

When Hypermobility Needs Medical Attention

Most hypermobility in Marshall syndrome can be managed with the strategies above. Seek medical evaluation when:

  • Repeated subluxations or dislocations. If a joint is subluxing (partially coming out of place) more than once or twice, the passive restraints have failed and active management alone is insufficient.
  • Joint effusion (swelling). Persistent or recurrent swelling after activity indicates ongoing joint damage and possible cartilage injury.
  • Progressive instability despite physical therapy. If 3–6 months of consistent PT has not improved stability, further evaluation is needed.
  • Pain that limits function. Pain that prevents participation in school, play, or daily activities warrants specialist evaluation.
  • Neurological symptoms. Numbness, tingling, or weakness in a limb associated with joint instability may indicate nerve compression from subluxation.

Who to See

  • Pediatric rheumatologist. For systemic evaluation of hypermobility, ruling out other connective tissue disorders, and coordinating multi-system care. Rheumatology is typically the first referral for generalized hypermobility with pain.
  • Pediatric orthopedic surgeon. For evaluation of specific joint instability, particularly if surgery is being considered. Orthopedics focuses on individual joints rather than the systemic picture.
  • Pediatric physiatrist (PM&R). For comprehensive rehabilitation planning, bracing prescriptions, and coordination of PT/OT services.

Surgical Considerations

Surgery is occasionally necessary for severe, recurrent joint instability that does not respond to conservative management. Common procedures include:

  • Capsular plication. Tightening the joint capsule to reduce excessive laxity. Used most often at the shoulder.
  • Ligament reconstruction. Replacing a damaged ligament with a tendon graft (e.g., ACL reconstruction at the knee).
  • Soft tissue stabilization. Various procedures to reinforce the passive restraints around an unstable joint.
Surgery in connective tissue disorders has higher failure rates. This is a critical consideration. The same collagen defect that caused the instability also affects the tissue used for surgical repair. Studies of capsular plication in hypermobile patients show continuing instability events in up to 53% of cases. Ligament reconstructions using the patient's own tissue (autograft) may stretch out over time because the graft tissue is also collagen-deficient. Surgery should be considered a last resort after exhaustive conservative management, performed by a surgeon experienced with connective tissue disorders, and with realistic expectations about outcomes and the potential need for revision.

Shoulder Instability

The shoulder is the most commonly affected joint in hypermobility-related instability, and multidirectional instability (MDI) — instability in two or more directions (anterior, posterior, inferior) — is the pattern most frequently seen in connective tissue disorders like Marshall syndrome. Unlike traumatic shoulder instability (which typically occurs in a single direction after a specific injury), MDI reflects the global ligament laxity of the condition and presents particular challenges for management.

Understanding Multidirectional Instability (MDI)

In a healthy shoulder, the joint capsule and glenohumeral ligaments provide passive restraint, while the rotator cuff and periscapular muscles provide dynamic stabilization. In COL11A1-related disorders, the capsule and ligaments are inherently lax due to the underlying collagen defect, placing a disproportionate burden on the muscular stabilizers. When these muscles fatigue, subluxation episodes occur — often with minimal provocation (reaching overhead, rolling over in sleep, carrying a bag on one shoulder).

  • Presentation: The child may report a “dead arm” sensation, feelings of the shoulder “slipping,” pain with overhead activities, or frank subluxation events where the arm partially comes out of the socket and spontaneously relocates
  • Sulcus sign: Downward traction on the arm produces a visible dimple below the acromion — this is the hallmark physical exam finding for inferior laxity in MDI
  • Bilateral involvement: In connective tissue disorders, MDI is almost always bilateral, even if symptoms are more prominent on one side. Both shoulders should be evaluated even when the child presents with unilateral complaints.

Conservative Management: The First-Line Approach

Conservative management is the cornerstone of MDI treatment in connective tissue disorders and must be given a thorough trial before surgery is considered:

  • Periscapular stabilizer strengthening: The muscles that anchor the scapula to the thorax (serratus anterior, lower and middle trapezius, rhomboids) are the foundation of shoulder stability. When these muscles are weak, the scapula moves excessively during arm elevation, creating a moving platform that the rotator cuff cannot effectively stabilize. Exercises include wall push-ups with plus (scapular protraction), prone Y’s and T’s, and resisted scapular retraction.
  • Rotator cuff strengthening: High-repetition, low-resistance external rotation and internal rotation exercises with resistance bands. The key is endurance training — the rotator cuff must function continuously throughout the day, not just produce brief bursts of force.
  • Neuromuscular retraining: Proprioceptive deficits in hypermobile shoulders mean the brain does not receive accurate information about joint position. Retraining involves rhythmic stabilization exercises, perturbation training on an unstable surface, and eyes-closed drills that force reliance on proprioceptive rather than visual feedback.
  • Closed-chain exercises: Weight-bearing through the hands (planks, wall slides, bear crawls) activates the shoulder stabilizers co-contractively, which is more protective than open-chain movements.

Surgical Options When Conservative Treatment Fails

When a minimum of 6 months of consistent, supervised physical therapy has failed to reduce instability events to an acceptable level, surgical intervention may be considered. The two primary procedures are:

  • Arthroscopic capsular plication: The surgeon tightens the stretched joint capsule by folding and suturing redundant tissue, reducing capsular volume. This is a less invasive procedure with faster initial recovery, and it is the most commonly used surgical approach for MDI.
  • Open capsular shift: Through a larger incision, the surgeon detaches a section of the capsule, shifts it to a tighter position, and reattaches it. This provides a more aggressive tightening and is typically reserved for severe cases or revision surgery after failed arthroscopic plication.
Poor surgical outcomes in connective tissue disorders. The evidence for surgical management of shoulder MDI in patients with underlying connective tissue disorders is sobering. Studies show that 53% of patients with connective tissue disorders continue to experience instability events after surgical stabilization, compared to significantly better outcomes in patients with traumatic instability and normal collagen. The repaired tissue is composed of the same defective collagen that caused the instability in the first place, and it tends to stretch out over time. This does not mean surgery is never appropriate, but it does mean expectations must be carefully calibrated.

Marshall-Specific Shoulder Management Principles

Given the COL11A1 collagen defect, shoulder instability management in Marshall syndrome follows stricter guidelines than general orthopedic practice:

  • Minimum 6 months of physical therapy before surgery is considered. Many orthopedic surgeons recommend 3 months of PT before discussing surgery. In Marshall syndrome, this is insufficient. The collagen defect means muscular compensation is even more critical than in the general population, and developing adequate periscapular and rotator cuff endurance takes longer. Six months of consistent, supervised PT is the minimum threshold.
  • Extended postoperative rehabilitation (2x standard). Standard post-surgical rehabilitation for shoulder stabilization typically runs 4–6 months. In Marshall syndrome, plan for 8–12 months. The collagen-deficient repair tissue heals more slowly and requires longer protection before resuming full loading. Early return to activity risks stretching out the repair.
  • Lifelong activity modification. Even after successful surgery, permanent modifications to activity are necessary. Overhead sports (volleyball, swimming butterfly, baseball pitching), heavy lifting above shoulder height, and contact sports that risk direct shoulder impact should be permanently avoided or significantly modified. This is not a temporary restriction — it reflects the permanent nature of the underlying collagen defect.
  • Ongoing maintenance PT: Unlike the general population, where discharged PT patients can maintain gains with home exercise alone, patients with Marshall syndrome benefit from periodic PT check-ins (every 3–6 months) to address gradual deconditioning and adapt the program as the child grows.
The Role of the Surgeon. If surgery is being considered for shoulder instability in a child with Marshall syndrome, seek a pediatric orthopedic surgeon or sports medicine surgeon who has specific experience with connective tissue disorders. Ask explicitly: “What is your experience with capsular plication or capsular shift in patients with underlying collagen disorders?” A surgeon unfamiliar with the higher failure rates may set unrealistic expectations for the outcome.

Questions for the PT or Specialist

  1. "Do you have experience treating hypermobility in connective tissue disorders like Stickler or Marshall syndrome?" — Hypermobility management in collagen disorders differs significantly from general hypermobility or sports-injury rehabilitation. A PT who understands the underlying collagen defect will design a fundamentally different program.
  2. "What is your approach to proprioceptive training, and how will you progress my child's program over time?" — The answer should include balance training, unstable surfaces, eyes-closed progressions, and a clear timeline. If the answer focuses only on strengthening without mentioning proprioception, consider a different provider.
  3. "Which joints should we prioritize for bracing versus strengthening, and when should bracing be reduced?" — A good clinician will have a plan for both starting and weaning external supports. Indefinite bracing without a reduction plan suggests a passive approach.
  4. "What activities or sports should we modify or avoid, and what are safe alternatives?" — The answer should be specific (not just "be careful") and should include positive recommendations, not just restrictions.
  5. "At what point would you recommend orthopedic referral for surgical evaluation?" — Understanding the threshold for escalation helps set expectations and ensures that conservative management is given a fair trial before surgical options are considered.

Pain Management in Hypermobility

Central Sensitization

Chronic pain in hypermobility is not simply a matter of joints being “loose.” Over time, repeated pain signals from unstable, subluxing joints can fundamentally rewire the central nervous system, creating a state called central sensitization — a measurable amplification of pain processing in the spinal cord and brain.

  • The pain volume is turned up: Dorsal horn neurons in the spinal cord become hyperexcitable, amplifying incoming pain signals so that stimuli that should cause mild discomfort are perceived as severe pain. This is called wind-up.
  • Widespread pain develops: Pain spreads beyond the originally injured joints to areas that are structurally intact. A child with knee instability may develop pain in the hips, back, and shoulders — not because those joints are damaged, but because the central nervous system is amplifying all sensory input.
  • Allodynia emerges: Light touch, clothing pressure, or gentle movement triggers pain responses. This is a hallmark of central sensitization and is distressing because it is invisible to outside observers.
  • Fatigue and cognitive fog: Central sensitization consumes neural processing resources, contributing to the fatigue, concentration difficulties, and sleep disruption commonly reported in hypermobility syndromes.
  • Autonomic dysregulation: Central sensitization can amplify autonomic nervous system responses, contributing to temperature sensitivity, flushing, sweating changes, and heart rate variability.

See Central Sensitization for a detailed exploration of this mechanism and its management.

Why This Matters for Treatment: When central sensitization is present, treating only the peripheral joints (with bracing, strengthening, or even surgery) will not fully resolve the pain. The central nervous system itself has become part of the problem and requires its own treatment approach — graded activity, pain neuroscience education, sleep optimization, and in some cases pharmacological management of central pain processing.

Pain Does Not Always Equal Damage

This is one of the most important — and most counterintuitive — concepts for individuals with hypermobility to internalize. In a sensitized nervous system, the relationship between tissue damage and pain perception is profoundly disrupted. Pain intensity does not reliably indicate the severity of structural injury.

Graded Activity

Graded activity (also called graded exercise therapy) is the evidence-based approach to managing chronic pain in hypermobility. Rather than responding to pain by resting (which leads to deconditioning and worse pain in a vicious cycle), or pushing through pain until injury occurs (the boom-bust pattern), graded activity uses a structured, time-based approach that decouples activity from pain level:

  1. Establish a baseline: Determine the amount of activity that can be performed consistently on both good and bad days without triggering a multi-day pain flare. Even if this is very small — 5 minutes of walking, 3 minutes of pool exercise — it is the starting point.
  2. Set a time-based quota: Perform the baseline amount regardless of how pain feels that day. Not more on good days, not less on bad days. This consistency is the therapeutic mechanism — it teaches the nervous system that activity is safe.
  3. Increase gradually: Add 10–20% more activity every 1–2 weeks, as long as the current level is being sustained consistently without multi-day flares.
  4. Expect fluctuations: Pain levels will vary day to day. The activity quota does not change in response to daily pain variation — only in response to the scheduled progression timeline.
  5. Track progress over weeks, not days: Week-over-week trends are the meaningful signal. Daily variation is noise. A pain diary that tracks both pain levels and activity completion helps identify real trends versus random fluctuation.
When to Stop Activity Immediately
Stop and seek evaluation if you experience: sharp, sudden joint pain during movement (not gradual onset); a feeling of the joint “giving way,” popping, or subluxing; new numbness, tingling, or weakness in a limb; progressive worsening during activity (pain should improve or plateau with warm-up, not steadily worsen); or new joint swelling, warmth, or redness. These signs suggest acute injury, not chronic sensitization, and warrant medical assessment.

The Deconditioning Trap: In hypermobility, the consequences of rest-based deconditioning are more severe and more rapid than in the general population. Muscles that are already working harder than normal to compensate for lax ligaments lose their stabilizing capacity quickly with disuse. Even 1–2 weeks of inactivity can produce measurable loss of joint stability. When in doubt — and in the absence of signs of acute injury — gentle movement is almost always better than complete rest.

Ring Splints & Hand Therapy for Hypermobile Fingers

The hands are among the most functionally affected areas in hypermobile connective tissue disorders. In Marshall syndrome, where COL11A1 mutations weaken the collagen that stabilizes finger joints, the small joints of the hand are particularly vulnerable to hyperextension, swan-neck deformity, and boutonniere deformity — patterns that cause pain, fatigue, and functional limitation long before frank joint damage is visible on imaging.

Ring Splints: External Stability for Hypermobile Fingers

Research Context (R425): Ring splints are small, low-profile orthotic devices worn on the fingers to prevent hyperextension at the proximal interphalangeal (PIP) and distal interphalangeal (DIP) joints. Originally developed for rheumatoid arthritis, they have been widely adopted in the hypermobility and EDS communities as first-line joint protection for the hands. Clinical evidence from the EDS and hypermobility literature shows that ring splints reduce pain during grip and pinch tasks by 30–50%, improve grip strength, and decrease the frequency of painful hyperextension events.

Which Fingers to Splint

  • PIP hyperextension block: The most common application. Prevents the proximal interphalangeal joint from hyperextending beyond neutral, which is the position that causes swan-neck deformity and pain during grip. A figure-8 style splint sits across the PIP joint and allows full flexion while blocking hyperextension.
  • DIP hyperextension block: Prevents mallet finger and DIP hyperextension, particularly important for writing and fine motor tasks.
  • MCP ulnar drift prevention: A lateral support splint at the metacarpophalangeal joint can reduce the ulnar drift that hypermobile individuals develop over time.
  • Thumb CMC stabilization: The thumb carpometacarpal joint is highly vulnerable in hypermobility. A thumb ring splint or short opponens splint stabilizes the joint during pinch and grip without eliminating thumb mobility.

Hand Therapy: Structured Rehabilitation

Ring splints are most effective when combined with a structured hand therapy program delivered by a certified hand therapist (CHT) — an occupational therapist or physical therapist with advanced specialization in hand and upper extremity rehabilitation. Hand therapy for hypermobile fingers focuses on:

  • Intrinsic muscle strengthening: The intrinsic hand muscles (lumbricals, interossei) provide dynamic stabilization of finger joints. Targeted strengthening of these muscles partially compensates for ligamentous laxity. Exercises include putty gripping, finger abduction/adduction against resistance, and lumbrical-plus exercises.
  • Proprioceptive retraining: Joint position sense in hypermobile fingers is impaired. Proprioceptive exercises — including joint repositioning tasks, texture discrimination with eyes closed, and finger tracking exercises — retrain the brain’s awareness of finger position, reducing the frequency of accidental hyperextension.
  • Grip ergonomics: A hand therapist assesses and modifies how the child grips writing instruments, utensils, and tools. Built-up grips, ergonomic pens, and adaptive utensils reduce the force needed and minimize hyperextension during daily tasks.
  • Joint protection education: Learning to use joints in their mid-range rather than at end-range, avoiding sustained grip tasks, and using the largest joint available for a task (push with the palm rather than the fingertips).
Practical recommendation: Start with a trial of Oval-8 splints on the 2–3 most symptomatic fingers to confirm benefit before investing in custom silver ring splints. Many hand therapists stock Oval-8 sizing sets and can fit them during a single visit. If the trial confirms functional improvement (less pain during writing, fewer hyperextension events), proceed to silver splints for long-term use. Request a hand therapy referral through your occupational therapist, rheumatologist, or orthopedic hand specialist.
