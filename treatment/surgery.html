Surgical Considerations

Surgery should be approached with specific caution in collagen disorder patients.

General Principles

  • Prophylactic surgery is NOT recommended — surgical intervention should only be pursued for documented instability or myelopathy (spinal cord compression with neurological signs)
  • Published guidelines explicitly warn of higher complication rates in patients with collagen disorders
  • Tissue fragility affects wound healing — collagen-deficient tissues hold sutures less reliably and heal more slowly
  • All surgical decisions should involve a spine surgeon experienced with skeletal dysplasias or connective tissue disorders

Specific Surgical Considerations

ScenarioGuideline
Decompression in skeletally immature patientsMust include instrumented fusion — decompression alone in a growing spine with compromised structural integrity risks progressive instability
Disc replacementNo data exists on artificial disc replacement in COL11A1 patients — the compromised endplates and surrounding tissues make outcomes unpredictable
Early-onset scoliosisGrowth-preserving techniques (growing rods, VEPTR/vertical expandable prosthetic titanium rib) should be considered rather than definitive fusion, to allow continued spinal growth
Spinal fusionMay be necessary for severe progressive scoliosis or instability, but should be deferred as long as safely possible in growing children

Pre-Surgical Requirements

Before any surgery — spinal or otherwise — ensure the surgical team is aware of:

  • The COL11A1 diagnosis and its implications for tissue quality
  • Potential airway difficulties (see Anesthesia Safety)
  • The need for cervical precautions if instability has not been ruled out
  • Altered wound healing expectations
