This section explains a specific molecular problem in COL11A1-deficient discs that has direct implications for what treatments to use — and what to avoid.

The Problem Supported

The Wnt/beta-catenin signaling pathway is a fundamental cellular communication system that controls cell growth, differentiation, and tissue maintenance. In healthy disc cells, Wnt signaling is tightly regulated.

Research in ATDC5 cells (a cartilage cell model) has shown that COL11A1 deficiency directly increases Wnt/beta-catenin signaling via the AKT/GSK3-beta axis. In plain language: when type XI collagen is defective, the brakes on Wnt signaling are released, and the pathway becomes overactive.

Excessive Wnt signaling in disc cells drives:

  • Chondrocyte hypertrophy (cells enlarge and lose function)
  • Matrix metalloproteinase production (tissue-destroying enzymes)
  • Accelerated disc degeneration

What This Means for Treatment Hypothesis

LITHIUM IS CONTRAINDICATED. Lithium is a potent Wnt activator (it inhibits GSK3-beta, which releases more Wnt/beta-catenin signaling). In a child whose discs already have excessive Wnt activity from COL11A1 deficiency, lithium would amplify an already-destructive pathway. If lithium is ever considered for psychiatric indications, the medical team must be informed of this specific contraindication.

What Opposes Excessive Wnt Supported

  • Klotho — a natural Wnt antagonist. Klotho is released during exercise (one more reason exercise is molecular medicine for this condition). See Exercise as Molecular Medicine.
  • Lorecivivint — a pharmaceutical Wnt inhibitor currently in Phase 1 clinical trials for disc disease. This is the most mechanistically aligned drug for COL11A1-driven disc degeneration, but it is not yet available. Worth monitoring.
Ask your doctor about: Whether any Wnt pathway-activating medications are in the child's current or planned treatment regimen. This includes lithium, certain GSK3-beta inhibitors, and some bone-building agents. These should be reviewed in light of the Wnt hyperactivation in COL11A1 deficiency.
