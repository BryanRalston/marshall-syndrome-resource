The Gut-Disc Axis

The intervertebral disc — long considered sterile — harbors its own microbiome directly connected to the gut.

58 Shared Bacterial Species

Metagenomic studies identified 58 bacterial species shared between gut and disc microbiomes, reaching the disc via hematogenous spread through endplate capillaries, bacterial translocation when gut barrier is compromised, and bacterial outer membrane vesicles.

Butyrate: Anti-Ferroptosis via NRF2/GPX4

Butyrate (from fiber fermentation) has direct anti-ferroptosis effects:

  • Activates NRF2 in disc cells
  • NRF2 upregulates GPX4
  • Enhances glutathione synthesis
  • Suppresses NF-κB inflammatory signaling
  • Strengthens gut barrier

TMAO: Upregulates Piezo1 Iron Entry

Harmful: TMAO (from gut bacteria processing red meat) directly upregulates Piezo1 in disc cells, increasing non-canonical iron entry and promoting ferroptosis.

Reduce TMAO by limiting red meat, increasing fiber, and consuming resveratrol-containing foods.

The SASP–Microbiome–Ferroptosis Triangle

  1. SASP disrupts gut barrier integrity
  2. Gut dysbiosis increases TMAO, reduces butyrate, allows bacterial translocation
  3. Ferroptosis (via Piezo1 + reduced GPX4) kills disc cells, creating more senescent cells
  4. More senescent cells → more SASP → cycle repeats

Dietary Interventions

Increase

  • Fiber: 25–30 g/day (vegetables, fruits, legumes, whole grains)
  • Fermented foods: yogurt, kefir, sauerkraut, kimchi, miso
  • Polyphenols: berries, dark chocolate, green tea (prebiotics for Akkermansia)

Minimize

  • Red meat (TMAO precursor; limit to 1–2 servings/week)
  • Processed foods, artificial sweeteners, excess sugar
