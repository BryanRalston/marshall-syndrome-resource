Insurance Navigation & Financial Advocacy

This page provides general guidance for US families only and is not legal or financial advice. Insurance laws, coverage mandates, and benefit structures vary significantly by state, plan type, and employer. Consult a patient advocate, health insurance counselor, or healthcare attorney for guidance specific to your situation. Nothing here constitutes legal advice.

Families navigating Marshall syndrome face insurance battles that most families never encounter: repeated denials for hearing aids, rejections of genetic testing as “not medically necessary,” out-of-network specialist costs, and lifetime benefit limits. This page equips you with the vocabulary, processes, and resources to advocate effectively for coverage your child needs.

Prior Authorization: Step-by-Step Workflow

Prior authorization (PA) is a requirement that your insurer approve a service, test, or medication before you receive it. Failure to obtain PA when required almost always results in a full denial of payment. Follow this workflow every time PA is required.

  1. Confirm PA is required before the appointment. Call the member services number on the back of your insurance card and ask: “Does CPT code [code] require prior authorization for a [age]-year-old dependent on my plan?” Get the representative’s name and the call reference number.
  2. Obtain the PA request form. Download it from the insurer’s website or ask your specialist’s office to initiate it electronically through their portal. Most specialist offices have a dedicated PA coordinator.
  3. Gather supporting clinical documentation. Work with your specialist to prepare: the specific diagnosis code (ICD-10), CPT code for the service, a letter of medical necessity (LMN), relevant test results, prior treatment history showing what has already been tried, and clinical guidelines supporting the requested service (e.g., GeneReviews recommendations, AAP guidelines).
  4. Submit and get a tracking number. Confirm receipt with the insurer. Note the submission date and tracking number. Federal law generally requires insurers to decide within 15 business days for standard requests and 72 hours for urgent requests.
  5. Follow up proactively. If you have not received a decision within the required timeframe, call and escalate. Document every call.
  6. If approved, keep the approval number and confirm it covers the specific date of service and the specific provider.
  7. If denied, request the full denial letter with the specific reason and the policy or clinical criteria used to make the decision. You have the right to this documentation. Proceed to the appeals process below.
Tip: Ask the specialist’s office to use the most specific ICD-10 code available for Marshall syndrome (Q87.5 for Marfan/connective tissue syndromes is sometimes used; confirm current coding with your geneticist’s billing department). Vague coding is a common reason for denial.

Insurance Denial Appeal Process

A denial is not the end. Most families who appeal denials for medically necessary care win at some level of the appeals process. There are three formal levels of appeal under the Affordable Care Act.

Level Who Reviews Typical Timeframe Key Actions
Level 1: Internal Appeal The insurance company itself reviews the denial using a different reviewer than the one who denied 30 days (standard); 72 hours (urgent/expedited) Submit a formal written appeal with a strong letter of medical necessity from the treating specialist; include peer-reviewed literature; request expedited review if clinically urgent
Level 2: External Review Independent review organization (IRO) not affiliated with your insurer; reviewers must be licensed clinicians 45 days (standard); 72 hours (expedited) Request external review within 4 months of the internal appeal denial; the insurer must accept the IRO’s binding decision; self-funded employer plans may have different rules
Level 3: State-Level Review / Complaint State Insurance Commissioner or Department of Insurance Varies by state (weeks to months) File a complaint with your state insurance department; applicable if the insurer violated state law or their own policy; state-regulated plans only (not self-funded ERISA plans)
Key fact: Self-funded employer health plans are governed by federal ERISA law, not state insurance law. This means many state insurance mandates—including hearing aid mandates—do not apply to these plans. Ask your HR department whether your plan is “fully insured” or “self-funded” (also called “self-insured”). This distinction determines which rules apply to you.

Sample Appeal Letter Language

The following template language is provided as a starting point. Each appeal must be customized with your child’s specific diagnosis, clinical history, and the insurer’s exact denial reason. Have the treating specialist review and sign all medical necessity sections.

For Hearing Aid Coverage

Substitute the bracketed fields with specific details. The specialist or audiologist should provide the supporting clinical language.

Re: Appeal of Denial for Hearing Aid Coverage — Member ID [ID] — Claim/Reference [#]

Dear Appeals Review Board:

I am writing to appeal the denial of coverage for [bilateral/unilateral] hearing aid(s) for my child, [Member ID / date of birth], issued on [denial date].

My child has a confirmed diagnosis of Marshall Syndrome (ICD-10: [code]), a rare autosomal dominant collagen disorder caused by pathogenic variants in the COL11A1 gene. Sensorineural hearing loss is a cardinal feature of this condition, affecting the majority of individuals and progressing throughout childhood if not addressed with amplification. This is not elective hearing loss treatment—it is medically necessary intervention for a genetically confirmed condition with documented audiological impact.

Enclosed please find:

  • Letter of medical necessity from the treating audiologist dated [date]
  • Audiology evaluation results confirming [type and degree] hearing loss
  • Genetic evaluation report confirming Marshall Syndrome diagnosis
  • Published clinical guidelines confirming amplification as standard of care (GeneReviews, ASHA guidelines)
  • Documentation that denial of amplification will cause measurable harm to speech, language, and educational development

We request expedited review given the active impact on the child’s developmental and educational progress. We are prepared to provide additional documentation or arrange a peer-to-peer consultation at the insurer’s request.

Sincerely, [Parent/Guardian Name, date, contact information]

For Genetic Testing

Re: Appeal of Denial for Genetic Testing — CPT [81479 or specific code] — Member ID [ID]

Dear Appeals Review Board:

I am appealing the denial of [genetic test name] for my child on the basis that it is not medically necessary. This determination is incorrect given the following clinical facts.

My child presents with [list key clinical findings: midface hypoplasia, hearing loss, high myopia, etc.], a constellation of features consistent with a collagen connective tissue disorder. Your child’s geneticist has recommended molecular genetic testing to confirm or establish a diagnosis of Marshall Syndrome or related Stickler syndrome spectrum disorder. This testing is not performed for curiosity—confirmation of a COL11A1 pathogenic variant will:

  • Establish the correct surveillance protocol for retinal detachment (65% lifetime risk if confirmed), preventing blindness
  • Guide anesthesia planning for any future surgical procedures, preventing potentially fatal airway complications
  • Determine inheritance pattern and risk to other family members
  • Enable appropriate educational and developmental support through accurate diagnosis

Denial of this test is not a cost-saving measure—it increases downstream costs by delaying diagnosis and surveillance that prevents far more expensive interventions (retinal detachment surgery: $10,000–$50,000+; complications of failed airway management in undiagnosed patients).

Enclosed: letter of medical necessity from the treating geneticist, clinical note documenting presenting features, published guidelines supporting genetic confirmation in suspected collagen disorders.

For Specialist Visits (Out-of-Network)

Re: Request for Out-of-Network Exception / Appeal of Out-of-Network Denial — [Specialist Name/Specialty]

Dear Appeals Review Board:

I am requesting an out-of-network exception (or appealing the denial of out-of-network coverage) for care provided by [specialty] for my child with Marshall Syndrome.

Marshall Syndrome is a rare disease affecting approximately 1 in 7,500–9,000 individuals. There are no in-network providers within [reasonable distance] who have documented experience treating this condition. My child requires a specialist with expertise in [collagen connective tissue disorders / vitreoretinal disease in collagen disorders / etc.].

Under the ACA, insurers must cover out-of-network emergency care at in-network rates and must not impose higher cost-sharing for out-of-network providers when no in-network provider with adequate expertise is available in the plan’s geographic area. I request that the plan apply in-network cost-sharing to the services provided, given the documented absence of qualified in-network alternatives.

Enclosed: documentation that [X] in-network providers were contacted and declined due to [lack of experience with this rare condition / no available appointments within a clinically appropriate timeframe], letter from your child’s geneticist explaining why subspecialist expertise is required.

State Hearing Aid Mandates

Coverage for pediatric hearing aids varies dramatically by state. Many states have enacted mandates requiring insurers to cover hearing aids for children—often up to age 18 or 21—with benefit limits typically ranging from $1,000 to $3,000 per ear every 2–3 years.

Key fact: As of 2024, over 25 states have enacted some form of pediatric hearing aid coverage mandate for state-regulated (fully insured) plans. Coverage and benefit amounts vary widely. States with more comprehensive mandates include Arkansas, Connecticut, Delaware, Georgia, Illinois, Massachusetts, Missouri, and others. Check your state insurance department website for the current mandate in your state.
Tip: Contact your state’s Department of Insurance directly to confirm whether your plan is subject to the state mandate. If your plan is self-funded (ERISA), state mandates do not apply—but you can still appeal on medical necessity grounds and request a voluntary benefit exception from your employer.

When invoking a state mandate in an appeal letter, cite the specific state statute by name and number. Your state audiology association (find it through the American Academy of Audiology at audiology.org) can provide the exact statutory language and may offer help with appeals.

GINA Protections: What They Mean for Your Family

The Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA), enacted in 2008, provides two key protections for families who have undergone genetic testing:

Title What It Covers What It Does NOT Cover
Title I — Health Insurance Health insurers cannot use genetic information to make eligibility decisions, determine premiums, or require pre-existing condition waiting periods. Applies to group health plans and individual market plans (ACA plans). Does not cover life insurance, disability insurance, or long-term care insurance. Does not apply to the Indian Health Service. Does not prevent insurers from charging more based on a current medical condition (as opposed to genetic predisposition).
Title II — Employment Employers (15+ employees) cannot use genetic information in hiring, firing, pay, job assignments, promotions, or any other term of employment. Does not cover employers with fewer than 15 employees. Does not prevent employers from taking action based on an actual disability (as opposed to genetic predisposition).
Practical implication: Having a confirmed COL11A1 genetic variant does not give a health insurer any legal basis to deny coverage or raise premiums under a group or ACA plan. If you believe a health insurer has violated GINA, file a complaint with your state insurance department (state-regulated plans) or the Department of Labor (employer group plans) or HHS Office for Civil Rights (ACA marketplace plans).

Important limitation: If a family member is considering purchasing individual life insurance, disability insurance, or long-term care insurance, genetic testing results may legally be used by those insurers in underwriting decisions. Consult a financial advisor or insurance attorney about the timing of genetic testing relative to these applications if relevant to your situation.

SSI and SSDI for Children with Rare Disease

Social Security offers two programs that may benefit children with significant disability related to Marshall syndrome. The distinction between them is important:

Program Eligibility Medical Benefit Financial Benefit
SSI (Supplemental Security Income) Children under 18 with a qualifying disability AND family income/assets below SSA thresholds. Most relevant for families with limited income. Automatic Medicaid eligibility in most states (provides a second insurance payer, covering what private insurance does not) Monthly cash benefit (amount varies; ~$943/month maximum in 2024, reduced by family income)
SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance) Primarily for adults who have worked and paid into Social Security. Children may qualify based on a parent’s earnings record if the parent is disabled, retired, or deceased (CDB — Childhood Disability Benefits). Medicare eligibility after 24-month waiting period Monthly benefit based on parent’s earnings record
Key fact: SSI’s medical eligibility standard for children requires that the child have a “marked and severe functional limitation” expected to last 12 months or longer. For children with Marshall syndrome, relevant qualifying limitations may include significant hearing loss, low vision, developmental delays, or a combination of conditions. The SSA evaluates all impairments together—even if no single condition meets a listing individually, the combined impact may qualify.
Tip: SSI applications have high initial denial rates. If denied, appeal. The hearing level (before an Administrative Law Judge) has a significantly higher approval rate than initial decisions. Consider working with a Social Security disability attorney or advocate; they typically charge a fee only if you win (capped by law at 25% of back benefits, maximum $7,200).

Marshall syndrome, as a genetic connective tissue disorder with documented multi-system involvement, is not on SSA’s Compassionate Allowances list (which expedites cases with the most severe diagnoses), but a well-documented application with medical records from all treating specialists, audiological records, vision records, and a detailed functional report from your child’s treatment team strengthens the case significantly.

Financial Assistance Programs

NORD (National Organization for Rare Disorders)

NORD administers disease-specific assistance programs funded by pharmaceutical companies and foundations. Programs change as funding becomes available. Visit rarediseases.org and search their Patient Assistance Programs directory. NORD also maintains a Patient Services team that can help families navigate available resources.

Manufacturer Copay Cards and Patient Assistance Programs

If your child takes a brand-name medication related to their care (certain eye drops, pain management medications, etc.), the manufacturer likely offers a copay card or patient assistance program. These are typically found on the manufacturer’s website under “Patient Support” or “Copay Assistance.”

Important limitation: Most manufacturer copay assistance programs cannot be used with Medicaid, Medicare, or other government-funded insurance. They are designed for commercially insured patients who still face high out-of-pocket costs. Some states have laws restricting how manufacturer assistance applies to plan cost-sharing—ask the manufacturer’s program coordinator about restrictions before relying on this assistance.

Hospital Charity Care

Every hospital that receives federal funding (essentially all nonprofit hospitals) is legally required to have a charity care policy. Families who cannot afford their out-of-pocket costs may qualify for partial or full forgiveness of the bill—regardless of insurance status.

  • Ask for the hospital’s financial counselor or patient financial services department before, during, or after care.
  • Request the hospital’s charity care application in writing. There is no penalty for applying.
  • Income thresholds vary by hospital. Many programs cover families up to 400% of the federal poverty level; some go higher.
  • Even families with insurance can apply if high deductibles or out-of-pocket maximums create genuine financial hardship.
  • If the hospital initially declines, ask them to reconsider with additional documentation (bank statements, tax returns, documentation of other medical expenses).

Additional Resources

  • NeedyMeds (needymeds.org): Comprehensive database of patient assistance programs by drug and condition.
  • RxAssist (rxassist.org): Patient assistance program center for prescription drugs.
  • State Children’s Health Insurance Program (CHIP): Low-cost or free coverage for children in families who earn too much for Medicaid but cannot afford private insurance. Income thresholds vary by state; apply at healthcare.gov or your state Medicaid office.
  • Hearing Loss Association of America (hlaa.org): Resources specific to hearing device funding and insurance advocacy.
  • Alexander Graham Bell Association for the Deaf and Hard of Hearing (agbell.org): Financial aid and advocacy resources for hearing device coverage.
  • EHDI (Early Hearing Detection and Intervention) programs: State-funded programs that may provide hearing aid assistance for infants and young children—contact your state health department.

Documenting Medical Necessity

Insurance denials most often cite “not medically necessary” as the reason. A well-constructed medical necessity argument preempts this denial and strengthens any appeal. Build your documentation file proactively.

  • Every service request should have a letter of medical necessity (LMN) written by the treating specialist on official letterhead. Generic letters lose appeals. The LMN must be specific: describe the condition, the functional impact, what has already been tried, why this specific treatment is required, the clinical consequences of non-treatment, and the supporting evidence base.
  • Use peer-reviewed citations. Insurers cannot ignore published clinical guidelines. GeneReviews entries for Stickler/Marshall syndrome, AAP hearing guidelines, and AAO ophthalmology guidelines are appropriate sources. Ask the specialist to cite these explicitly.
  • Document functional impact in your child’s own terms. A letter from a school speech therapist documenting that hearing loss is causing measurable speech and language delay is powerful corroborating evidence alongside the audiologist’s clinical note.
  • Keep a paper trail of everything. Date-stamp every document. Log every phone call with the representative’s name, employee ID, and call reference number. Send important correspondence by certified mail or email with read receipt.
  • Request the insurer’s clinical criteria document that was used to make the denial decision. You have the right to this under the ACA. Read it carefully—sometimes the denial is based on a clerical error or outdated criteria that can be directly refuted.
Tip: Build a single organized binder (physical or digital) with tabs for: insurance cards and policy documents, EOBs (Explanation of Benefits) for all major services, all LMNs and specialist letters, all PA approvals and denials, all appeal letters sent, and all correspondence received. This binder becomes your central command for every future claim.

How to Request a Peer-to-Peer Review

A peer-to-peer (P2P) review is a phone call between the treating specialist and the insurance company’s medical director who denied the claim. It is one of the most effective tools for overturning prior authorization denials.

  1. Ask the specialist’s office to request a P2P immediately after a PA denial. Most insurers allow P2P requests within a short window (often 3–5 business days of the denial). This window matters—act quickly.
  2. The specialist calls the insurer’s medical director directly and argues the clinical case physician-to-physician. This is a very different conversation than a written appeal—two clinicians discussing patient care.
  3. Prepare the specialist with:
    • The exact denial reason and criteria cited
    • Published clinical guidelines supporting the request
    • The specific clinical details of the patient that distinguish this case from the denial criteria
    • What the consequences of non-coverage will be for the patient
  4. P2P reviews have a high success rate—particularly for rare diseases where the insurer’s medical director may simply be unfamiliar with the condition. The treating specialist explaining Marshall syndrome directly to the reviewer often changes the outcome.
  5. If the P2P review is unsuccessful, proceed to the formal internal appeal with the P2P outcome noted in your letter.
Key fact: The right to request a P2P review is not always explicitly stated in the denial letter, but virtually all major insurers permit it. Call the provider services line (not the member services line) and ask specifically: “Can the treating physician request a peer-to-peer review of this denial?”

Working with a Patient Advocate

A patient advocate is a professional who works on behalf of patients to navigate healthcare systems, insurance disputes, billing errors, and care coordination. For families managing a rare disease across multiple specialists, a patient advocate can be transformative.

Types of Patient Advocates

  • Independent patient advocates: Professionals you hire directly. Fees typically range from $100–$400/hour or a flat project rate. For complex insurance disputes involving large sums, this is often cost-effective. Find advocates through the Patient Advocate Foundation (patientadvocate.org) or the Alliance of Professional Health Advocates (aphadvocates.org).
  • Hospital-based patient advocates: Most hospitals have a patient relations or patient advocate office. They are hospital employees, so they advocate within the hospital system rather than against the insurer, but they can help resolve billing errors, coordinate communication between departments, and navigate the charity care process.
  • Non-profit patient advocacy organizations: The Patient Advocate Foundation (patientadvocate.org) offers free case management services for chronic and rare disease patients. Their case managers can help with insurance denials, workplace accommodation, and financial assistance navigation. This is a particularly valuable free resource.
  • Disease-specific advocacy organizations: Stickler Involved People (sip.org) and the Stickler Syndrome Support Group may be able to connect you with families who have navigated similar insurance issues and can share strategies that worked.

What to Bring to a Patient Advocate

  • The complete denial letter with the denial reason and criteria cited
  • A summary of all treatments already tried and their outcomes
  • Letters of medical necessity from the treating specialists
  • Your insurance policy documents (Summary of Benefits and Coverage, or the full SPD for employer plans)
  • Timeline of the issue: dates of service, PA submission, denial, all calls made
Tip: Before hiring an independent advocate, start with the Patient Advocate Foundation (patientadvocate.org). They provide free case management and have experience handling rare disease insurance appeals. Their case managers know the system and the language to use.
