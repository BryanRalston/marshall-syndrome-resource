Insurance Navigation — The Rare Disease Coverage Playbook

The Reality of Rare Disease Coverage

  • 100% of orphan drugs require prior authorization
  • 85% are placed on the highest formulary tier
  • Only 17% of insurers have developed strategies for rare disease drugs
  • Appeal window: 30–180 days from denial — miss it and you lose your right to contest

This is NOT about whether your child deserves coverage. It’s about knowing the system.

The Six-Step Coverage Playbook

  1. GET EVERYTHING IN WRITING: Every denial, every approval, every phone call reference number. For every call, record the date, time, name of the representative, and the reference or case number. Screenshot or save online portal denials — portal content can change without notice. Create a binder — physical or digital — and file everything chronologically. If a representative tells you something on the phone, ask for the reference number and follow up with a written confirmation request.
  2. APPEAL IMMEDIATELY: File your first-level appeal within 30 days of any denial. Include a medical necessity letter from your specialist, peer-reviewed evidence supporting the treatment or test, and genetic test results confirming the COL11A1 mutation. Use the exact CPT and ICD codes in your appeal — vague language gets denied, precise billing codes get reviewed. Reference OMIM #154780 (Marshall syndrome) to establish the rarity and medical legitimacy of the condition. Make the case specific to your child, not generic.
  3. SECOND-LEVEL APPEAL: If the first appeal fails, most plans allow a second internal appeal. This is your opportunity to add additional evidence, obtain letters from additional specialists, or request a peer-to-peer review where your doctor speaks directly with the insurance company’s medical director.
  4. EXTERNAL REVIEW: If internal appeals fail, request an independent external review — this is a federal right under the Affordable Care Act (ACA). An independent physician who was not involved in the original denial reviews your case. This is often the most effective step, because the reviewer has no financial interest in the outcome.
  5. STATE INSURANCE COMMISSIONER: File a complaint with your state’s insurance commissioner if the coverage decision violates state mandates or appears to be applied unfairly. Many states have specific rare disease or genetic testing mandates that insurers must follow.
  6. LEGAL OPTIONS: Patient advocacy organizations can provide pro-bono legal assistance for coverage disputes. You do not have to fight alone, and the threat of legal action often accelerates resolution.

What Gets Denied — And How to Fight It

Item Why Denied How to Appeal
Genetic testing panels “Not medically necessary” Letter from geneticist explaining diagnostic necessity. Cite ACMG (American College of Medical Genetics) guidelines for genetic testing in connective tissue disorders.
Specialized imaging (T1rho MRI, flexion-extension MRI) “Experimental” or “not standard” Peer-reviewed publications supporting use in skeletal dysplasia. Letter from orthopedist explaining why standard imaging is insufficient for this specific condition.
Off-label supplements (CoQ10, D-ribose) “Not FDA-approved treatment” Published evidence of mitochondrial support. Note: these are supplements, not drugs — they may not need insurance coverage and can often be purchased over-the-counter at lower cost than a copay.
Physical/aquatic therapy beyond session limits “Exceeds benefit maximum” Medical necessity letter emphasizing that this is a lifelong condition requiring ongoing therapy, not an acute injury with a defined recovery timeline. Request a medical exception to the session cap.
Specialized ophthalmology (prophylactic retinal treatment) “Preventive, not treatment” Document retinal detachment risk (>50% lifetime in Stickler/Marshall spectrum). Prevention is treatment when the alternative is emergency surgery and potential blindness.
Future gene therapies “Experimental” Expanded access/compassionate use programs. Clinical trial enrollment. See the Expanded Access section below.

The “Network Adequacy” Argument

If your insurer has no in-network specialist experienced with Marshall syndrome or Stickler syndrome, you have a powerful argument for out-of-network coverage at in-network rates. This is called network adequacy — insurers are legally required to provide access to appropriate specialists within their network. When they cannot, they must cover out-of-network care without penalizing you for going outside the network.

How to build a network adequacy case:
  1. Document your search. Contact every potentially relevant in-network specialist (geneticist, craniofacial specialist, connective tissue specialist) and ask directly: “Do you have experience treating Marshall syndrome or Stickler syndrome?” Record names, dates, and their responses.
  2. Request a gap exception. Call your insurer and request a “network gap exception” or “network adequacy exception.” Explain that you contacted [number] in-network providers and none had experience with your child’s rare condition.
  3. Provide documentation. Submit a letter listing every provider you contacted, the dates of contact, and confirmation that none had relevant experience — along with the name and credentials of the out-of-network specialist you need to see.
  4. Cite regulatory requirements. Under the ACA and most state insurance regulations, insurers must maintain networks adequate to serve their enrollees’ needs. For rare diseases, this often means covering out-of-network specialists when no qualified in-network alternative exists.

Genetic Testing Coverage Strategy

Whole exome sequencing or targeted COL11A1 panel testing is frequently denied by insurers who classify it as “not medically necessary” or “experimental.” This denial can be overturned with a targeted appeal strategy.

Three arguments that win genetic testing appeals:
  1. Testing will confirm the diagnosis and change clinical management. A confirmed COL11A1 mutation directs surveillance schedules (retinal exams, hearing evaluations, spinal imaging), guides anesthesia precautions, and informs surgical planning. Without confirmation, clinicians must order broader and more expensive workups to rule out overlapping conditions.
  2. Testing prevents unnecessary procedures. A confirmed genetic diagnosis eliminates the need for invasive diagnostic procedures, repeat imaging studies, and specialist consultations aimed at ruling out other conditions. The one-time cost of genetic testing is far less than the cumulative cost of a diagnostic odyssey.
  3. Clinical diagnostic criteria are already met. When the clinical presentation — midface hypoplasia, sensorineural hearing loss, high myopia, skeletal findings — is consistent with Marshall syndrome, genetic testing is the definitive next step recommended by the American College of Medical Genetics (ACMG) guidelines for connective tissue disorders.

Laboratory financial assistance: Many genetic testing laboratories (including GeneDx, Invitae, and others) offer financial assistance programs or reduced self-pay rates if insurance denies coverage. Ask the lab about their patient assistance options before paying the full billed amount.

Patient Assistance Programs

When insurance coverage falls short, patient assistance programs can fill the gap. These programs are designed specifically for families facing the financial burden of rare disease care.

  • Pharmaceutical company programs: Many manufacturers offer copay assistance or free medication programs for rare disease treatments. Ask your prescribing physician or geneticist’s office which programs apply to your child’s medications — they often know from experience with other patients.
  • NORD Patient Assistance Programs: The National Organization for Rare Disorders maintains a patient assistance program database searchable by diagnosis and medication. This is one of the most comprehensive directories available.
  • Copay accumulator workaround: Some insurers use “copay accumulator” programs that prevent manufacturer copay assistance from counting toward your deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. Check whether your plan uses an accumulator and, if so, whether your state has passed legislation prohibiting this practice.

Key Organizations That Can Help

  • NORD (National Organization for Rare Disorders): Financial assistance programs, insurance navigation guides, and advocacy resources. One of the most comprehensive rare disease support organizations in the U.S. — rarediseases.org
  • Global Genes: Insurance navigation toolkit specifically designed for rare disease families. Practical, step-by-step guidance.
  • Stickler Involved People (SIP): Disease-specific community that may have insurance experience to share. Connecting with families who have already navigated coverage battles for Stickler/Marshall conditions can save you months of learning.
  • The Assistance Fund: Copay assistance for rare disease medications. If your medication is covered but the copay is prohibitive, this organization can help bridge the gap.
  • Patient Advocate Foundation: Free case management for insurance disputes. They will assign a case manager who works on your behalf — this is one of the most underused resources available — patientadvocate.org
  • State-specific rare disease advisory councils: Many states have them, and they can connect you with local resources, legislative efforts, and other families in your area navigating similar challenges.

SSI Disability Benefits for Children (R502)

Supplemental Security Income (SSI) is a federal program administered by the Social Security Administration (SSA) that provides monthly cash benefits to children with disabilities in low-income families. For Marshall syndrome families facing significant medical expenses, SSI can be a critical financial lifeline — but the application process is complex, and initial denial rates exceed 60%.

Qualifying for SSI as a Child

A child under 18 may qualify for SSI if they meet all three of the following criteria:

  1. Medical eligibility: The child has a medically determinable physical or mental impairment that results in “marked and severe functional limitations” and has lasted or is expected to last at least 12 months
  2. Income eligibility: The family’s income and resources fall below SSA thresholds (in 2026, generally under ~$2,000 in countable resources for an individual; parental income is “deemed” to the child using a complex formula)
  3. Citizenship and residency: The child must be a U.S. citizen or qualifying non-citizen residing in the United States
Marshall Syndrome and SSA’s “Blue Book” Listings

The SSA uses a Listing of Impairments (“Blue Book”) to evaluate disability claims. Marshall syndrome is not listed by name, but children may qualify under multiple relevant listings:

  • Section 2.00 — Special Senses (Vision): If best-corrected visual acuity is 20/200 or worse, or visual field is 20 degrees or less in the better eye
  • Section 2.10 — Hearing Loss: If hearing loss meets specified audiometric thresholds (pure-tone average, word recognition scores)
  • Section 1.00 — Musculoskeletal Disorders: Spinal disorders, joint dysfunction, or skeletal abnormalities that limit ambulation or fine motor function
  • Functional equivalence: Even if no single listing is met, the child may qualify if the combination of impairments produces “marked” limitations in two or more domains (acquiring/using information, attending/completing tasks, interacting with others, moving about, caring for self, health/physical well-being)

Marshall syndrome’s multi-system nature — vision loss, hearing loss, chronic pain, joint instability, fatigue — often qualifies through the functional equivalence pathway even when no single impairment meets a listing threshold.

Application Tips for Marshall Syndrome Families

Strengthen your application:
  • Apply early and document everything. The SSI application process takes 3–6 months for an initial decision, and appeals can extend to 12–24 months. Apply as soon as medical documentation supports the claim — benefits are retroactive to the application date, not the onset of disability
  • Include ALL impairments, not just the “worst” one. List every affected system: vision, hearing, spine, joints, pain, fatigue, autonomic dysfunction, sleep disruption, school absences. SSA evaluates the cumulative functional impact
  • Get a detailed RFC (Residual Functional Capacity) assessment from the treating physician describing exactly what the child cannot do: “cannot sit for more than 30 minutes,” “cannot hear speech in noise,” “misses 3+ school days per month due to pain flares”
  • Include school records: 504 Plans, IEPs, attendance records, teacher statements about functional limitations, and any psychological evaluations
  • Expect initial denial. Over 60% of initial SSI applications are denied. This does not mean you are ineligible. File a Request for Reconsideration within 60 days of denial. If denied again, request an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) hearing — ALJ approval rates are significantly higher than initial decisions
  • Consider a disability attorney or advocate. SSI attorneys work on contingency (paid from back benefits if approved, typically 25% of past-due benefits up to a federal cap). Organizations like NORD and the Patient Advocate Foundation can provide referrals
SSI and Medicaid
In most states, SSI approval automatically qualifies the child for Medicaid, which provides comprehensive health coverage with no premiums and minimal copays. For families who are currently uninsured or underinsured, this Medicaid linkage may be even more valuable than the monthly cash benefit. Some states also offer “Katie Beckett” or TEFRA waivers that allow children with disabilities to qualify for Medicaid based on the child’s disability alone, without considering parental income. Check your state’s Medicaid waiver programs.

Expanded Access & Compassionate Use

The FDA allows manufacturers to provide investigational drugs to seriously ill patients outside of clinical trials through expanded access (also called compassionate use) programs. This pathway is particularly relevant for future gene therapies targeting COL11A1.

  • How it works: The treating physician submits an application to the FDA on behalf of the patient. The manufacturer must also agree to provide the drug. Both parties must consent — the FDA cannot compel a manufacturer to participate.
  • When it applies: The patient must have a serious or life-threatening condition, no comparable or satisfactory alternative treatment must be available, and the potential benefit must justify the potential risks.
  • Relevance to Marshall syndrome: As gene therapy research targeting collagen disorders advances, expanded access may become a pathway to treatment before full FDA approval. Families should be aware this option exists and discuss it with their geneticist when new therapies enter clinical development.
  • Right to Try Act (2018): This federal law allows terminally ill patients to access investigational drugs that have completed Phase I trials without requiring FDA approval for individual use. Its applicability to Marshall syndrome is limited — it applies specifically to terminal conditions — but it is worth knowing about as part of the broader landscape of access to experimental treatments.

Reducing Avoidable Emergency Department Visits (R577)

Families of children with Marshall syndrome frequently use the emergency department (ED) for pain flares, respiratory concerns, and symptom escalation that could, with preparation, be managed outside the ED. Avoidable ED visits are costly (averaging $2,000–$5,000 per visit), stressful, and often result in care by providers unfamiliar with the condition who may order unnecessary tests or provide suboptimal treatment. Proactive care coordination can dramatically reduce ED utilization.

The AAP Emergency Information Form (EIF)
The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) Emergency Information Form (EIF) is a standardized one-page document that summarizes a child’s diagnosis, medications, surgical history, emergency contacts, and condition-specific instructions for emergency providers. For Marshall syndrome, the EIF should include:
  • COL11A1 diagnosis with anesthesia and airway precautions
  • Cervical spine precautions (no manipulation, avoid excessive extension)
  • Retinal detachment warning signs and ophthalmology contact
  • Current pain management regimen and escalation plan
  • Primary specialist contact numbers

Download the AAP EIF template from aap.org. Keep copies in the child’s school file, medical binder, car, and phone (photo). See also the Emergency Medical Card.

Four strategies to reduce avoidable ED visits:
  1. Written action plans for common crises: Work with the pain medicine team and primary care physician to create written flare management protocols that specify: (1) home-based interventions for the first 1–2 hours, (2) when to call the specialist’s after-hours line, and (3) specific criteria that warrant an ED visit. See Flare Management
  2. Care coordination through a medical home: Designate one provider (typically the pediatrician or a rare disease coordinator) as the central point of contact who can triage symptoms by phone before an ED visit. Many concerns can be resolved with a phone call to the specialist rather than an ED evaluation
  3. Telehealth for acute consultations: Several pediatric pain programs and rare disease centers now offer telehealth urgent consultations that can assess symptoms, adjust medications, and determine whether an ED visit is truly necessary — all within 1–2 hours of contact
  4. Educate ED staff proactively: If your child does require ED care, handing the AAP EIF and Emergency Medical Card to the triage nurse immediately changes the quality of care. Providers who understand the diagnosis from the first minute make fewer errors than those who discover it mid-evaluation
Telehealth for Rural and Underserved Families (R586)
A 2025 multi-center study on specialty access for pediatric rare disease families confirmed that telehealth eliminates geographic barriers that otherwise delay diagnosis and treatment by an average of 14 months:
  • Genetics and genetic counseling: Telehealth genetic counseling is now standard of care, with the NSGC (National Society of Genetic Counselors) endorsing video-based sessions as equivalent to in-person for pre- and post-test counseling. Families in rural areas can access board-certified genetic counselors regardless of distance from a genetics center
  • Pain psychology and ACT/CBT: Virtual delivery of Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (vACT) and Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) has been validated for pediatric chronic pain populations, with outcomes comparable to in-person delivery. This is transformative for Marshall syndrome families who may live hours from the nearest pediatric pain psychology program (see Psychological Support)
  • Rheumatology and pain management follow-up: After an initial in-person evaluation, many rheumatology and pain medicine programs now offer telehealth follow-up visits for medication management, exercise program adjustments, and treatment monitoring. Ask your specialist team about telehealth options at the first visit
  • Insurance coverage mandate: As of 2025, the majority of US states have telehealth parity laws requiring insurers to cover telehealth visits at the same rate as in-person visits. If a telehealth claim is denied, cite your state’s telehealth parity statute in the appeal. The Center for Connected Health Policy (cchpca.org) maintains a current 50-state tracker of telehealth coverage laws
  • Cross-state licensing: The IMLC (Interstate Medical Licensure Compact) now includes 42 states, allowing physicians licensed in one compact state to practice telehealth into other compact states. This dramatically expands the pool of accessible specialists for rare disease families

Building Your Case Proactively

Don’t wait for a denial to prepare. The strongest insurance cases are built before they are needed.

  • Maintain a MEDICAL BINDER with: genetic test results, specialist letters, hospitalization records, imaging reports, therapy progress notes, and a current medication list. Organize by specialist and date. Keep both a physical and a digital copy.
  • Get LETTERS OF MEDICAL NECESSITY for every specialist visit — ask the doctor to write one even before you need it. A letter written in the moment, when the doctor is seeing the patient and can describe the clinical rationale in detail, is far more powerful than one reconstructed from memory months later.
  • Document FUNCTIONAL IMPACT: How does the condition affect daily life, school, activities, sleep, social participation? Insurance companies respond to functional language — “unable to attend school 3 days per month due to pain” is more compelling than “has joint pain.”
  • Know your plan: Read the Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC) annually. Know what is covered, what the exclusions are, and what the appeal process looks like before you need it. Understanding your plan’s formulary, tier structure, and exception process saves critical time when a denial arrives.

Key Documents to Keep in Your File

Whether you are fighting an active denial or building your case proactively, keep these documents organized and accessible at all times:

  • Genetic test results (COL11A1 variant report)
  • All specialist letters confirming diagnosis
  • Emergency protocol / doctor letter (see Emergency Medical Card)
  • 504 Plan or IEP documentation
  • All denial letters and appeal submissions
  • Explanation of Benefits (EOBs) for every claim
  • Phone call log: date, time, representative name, reference number, and summary of what was discussed
  • Hospitalization and surgical records
  • Current medication list with dosages
  • Letters of medical necessity from each treating specialist
