Advocacy & Policy Organizations

Rare disease advocacy is not optional — it is how treatments get developed, funded, and approved. Families affected by Marshall syndrome can multiply their impact by engaging with organizations that bridge the gap between patient experience and research/policy decisions.

Rare Disease Umbrella Organizations

OrganizationWhat They DoHow to Engage
NORD (National Organization for Rare Disorders) Patient assistance programs, research grants, advocacy training, and policy engagement. NORD operates the largest rare disease patient assistance network in the US and provides individual and family assistance programs for medication, travel, and insurance navigation. Join as a member. Access the NORD Patient Assistance Programs. Attend Rare Disease Week on Capitol Hill (annual event in Washington, DC). Apply for NORD research grants if you are connected with a researcher studying collagen XI.
EveryLife Foundation for Rare Diseases Policy advocacy focused on FDA reform, insurance coverage, and research funding for rare diseases. EveryLife leads the Rare Disease Congressional Caucus engagement and coordinates Rare Disease Week at the NIH. Sign up for advocacy alerts. Participate in Rare Disease Legislative Advocates (RDLA) training. Contact your members of Congress through their advocacy portal during legislative action campaigns.
Global Genes Rare disease patient advocacy organization providing toolkits, community building, and the RARE Advocacy Summit. Focuses on empowering patients and families to become effective advocates. Access their Advocacy Toolkit (free). Attend the annual RARE Patient Advocacy Summit. Use their “Toolkits for Rare” to build skills in storytelling, legislative engagement, and nonprofit development.

Condition-Specific Organizations

  • Stickler Involved People (SIP): The primary patient organization serving the Stickler/Marshall community. SIP provides family networking, educational resources, and organizes biennial conferences with clinical experts. Because Marshall syndrome and Stickler syndrome type 2 share the COL11A1 gene, SIP is the most directly relevant patient organization. Website: sticklers.org
  • EDS Society: While focused on Ehlers-Danlos syndromes, the EDS Society has extensive resources on hypermobility management, autonomic dysfunction, and pain management that are directly applicable to Marshall syndrome. Their clinical resources and provider directory are valuable for finding knowledgeable clinicians.
  • Cleft Palate Foundation: Relevant for children with Marshall syndrome who have cleft palate history. Provides support, resources, and connections to cleft/craniofacial teams.

Legislative and Research Policy

  • Give Kids a Chance Act (2025): This legislation, currently in Congress, would create incentives for developing pediatric rare disease treatments, including extended exclusivity periods for drugs developed specifically for rare pediatric conditions. For conditions like Marshall syndrome where the pediatric population is small and commercially unattractive to drug developers, this type of legislation is essential for creating a viable path to treatment development. Track its progress and contact your representatives in support.
  • FDA Community Resource Dialogue Council (CRDC) Patient-Family Advisory Committee (PFAC): The FDA actively seeks patient and family input on drug development priorities, clinical trial design, and outcome measures through its PFAC program. Rare disease families can apply to serve on these advisory committees, directly influencing how the FDA evaluates treatments for their condition.
  • Rare Diseases Clinical Research Network (RDCRN): An NIH-funded network of research consortia studying rare diseases. RDCRN actively involves patients in research design, recruitment, and outcome measurement. While there is no current RDCRN consortium specifically for Marshall syndrome, involvement with the broader network creates visibility for the condition and may contribute to future consortium development.
Start here: If you do one thing, join Stickler Involved People (SIP) and NORD. SIP connects you with families who understand your specific diagnosis, and NORD provides the advocacy infrastructure to push for research and policy changes. Both organizations are free or low-cost to join, and both amplify the visibility of conditions that are otherwise invisible in the healthcare system.
