Newly Diagnosed: Your First 30 Days

If you are reading this page, you have probably just received a diagnosis of Marshall syndrome — for yourself or for someone you love. That moment can feel like the ground shifting beneath you. The word "syndrome" is heavy, the internet is vast and frightening, and the list of potential complications can feel impossible to absorb all at once.

Take a breath. You found this site, which means you are already doing the right thing. Knowledge is power, and the prognosis for people with Marshall syndrome has improved dramatically over the past two decades thanks to proactive monitoring and management. Most people with Marshall syndrome live full, active lives. You are not facing a crisis — you are starting a process.

You don't need to do everything at once. This guide breaks the first month into manageable steps. Read one section at a time. Come back when you are ready for the next piece. There is no race.

Week 1: Breathe & Understand

What Marshall Syndrome Actually Is

Marshall syndrome is a heritable connective tissue disorder caused by variants in the COL11A1 gene. It affects collagen — the structural protein found throughout the body — which is why it can influence the eyes, ears, joints, spine, and facial features. A detailed explanation is available in The Basics: What Is Marshall Syndrome.

It Is a Spectrum

This is one of the most important things to understand from the start: Marshall syndrome varies enormously from person to person, even within the same family. Some individuals have significant visual involvement; others have primarily hearing or joint symptoms. A diagnosis does not tell you exactly what someone's experience will look like. Severity, the combination of features, and how the condition progresses are all highly individual.

You Are Not Alone

Marshall syndrome is rare, but there is a community. Connecting with others who understand the day-to-day reality of this condition — as a patient or as a parent — can be one of the most grounding things you do. See the Support & Community page for organizations, online groups, and family networks.

A Word About Internet Research

It is natural to search. But unfiltered internet searches on rare syndromes tend to surface the most dramatic cases, the worst outcomes, and the most alarming forums. This is a statistical artifact of who posts online, not a representative picture. Worst-case scenarios exist, but they are not your story by default. Use curated, medically reviewed resources — like this one — rather than general searches when you need information.

Week 2: Build Your Team

Marshall syndrome is a multi-system condition, which means no single doctor manages it alone. Building the right team of specialists is the most important practical step of the first month. Here is the order to prioritize referrals, based on clinical urgency:

1. Ophthalmologist — Most Time-Sensitive

Retinal detachment is the most serious acute risk in Marshall syndrome, and it can occur without warning. A comprehensive retinal screening should happen as soon as possible — ideally within the first few weeks of diagnosis. Look for a retinal specialist or a low-vision ophthalmologist familiar with connective tissue disorders. This is the one referral that should not wait.

2. Audiologist

Hearing loss in Marshall syndrome is often sensorineural, progressive, and begins in childhood. An audiologist baseline assessment establishes where hearing is today, which is essential for tracking change over time. Even if hearing seems fine right now, baseline documentation matters.

3. Orthopedist or Physiatrist

Joint hypermobility, early arthritis, and cervical spine abnormalities are common. An orthopedic or physiatric evaluation establishes a spine and joint baseline, identifies any structural concerns, and guides activity recommendations. A physiatrist (rehabilitation medicine specialist) can be particularly valuable for functional management.

4. Geneticist

If the diagnosis has not already been confirmed genetically, a clinical geneticist can order or review genetic testing, confirm the diagnosis, discuss inheritance patterns, and address questions about implications for other family members. Genetic counseling is also an option for adults making reproductive decisions.

Finding specialists who know connective tissue disorders: Many specialists will not have seen Marshall syndrome specifically. That is normal — it is rare. What matters is finding someone familiar with connective tissue disorders broadly (Marfan syndrome, Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, Stickler syndrome). Marfan and Stickler specialists often have directly applicable expertise. Patient advocacy organizations can sometimes help locate knowledgeable providers.

What to Bring to First Appointments

  • A written summary of all known symptoms and when they were first noticed
  • Any prior imaging, lab results, or genetic test reports
  • A list of current medications and supplements
  • A list of questions prepared in advance
  • A second person if possible — appointments are easier to process with two sets of ears

See the Questions for Your Doctor page for specialty-specific question lists.

Week 3: Baseline Assessments

Baseline tests do two things: they tell you where things stand right now, and they give future providers a reference point for tracking change over time. Requesting these in the first month is ideal.

Tests to Request and Why

Comprehensive eye exam with retinal evaluation
Assesses refractive error (typically high myopia), vitreous changes, retinal status, and any signs of early detachment risk. This is the foundation of ongoing eye monitoring.
Audiogram
A full hearing test across frequencies establishes a hearing baseline. Sensorineural hearing loss can progress slowly; without a baseline, it is impossible to know whether any change is new or longstanding.
Spine X-rays
Cervical spine abnormalities and scoliosis are both associated with Marshall syndrome. A baseline spinal imaging series allows future comparison and guides activity recommendations.
Echocardiogram
Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) occurs at elevated rates in connective tissue disorders. An echocardiogram screens for MVP and other structural cardiac findings. Most people have normal results, but having a baseline on file is important.
DEXA scan (if age-appropriate)
Bone density concerns may apply, particularly for adults or those with relevant risk factors. Ask the geneticist or primary care provider whether this applies to your situation at this time.

Start a Symptom Log

A simple ongoing log of symptoms — what, when, severity, duration, any triggers — becomes surprisingly valuable over time. Patterns that are invisible day to day become clear across months. The Symptom Tracker tool on this site can help structure that process.

Create Your Emergency Card

An emergency medical card ensures that any provider who encounters the patient in an emergency context has the key information they need. See the Emergency Card page to create one now. It takes about ten minutes and provides real peace of mind.

Week 4: Set Up for Success

School Accommodations

For children and adolescents, school accommodations can make a significant practical difference. Two formal accommodation frameworks exist in U.S. schools:

  • 504 Plan — For students whose disability affects learning in a general education setting. Easier to obtain than an IEP; appropriate for most Marshall syndrome students who do not require specialized instruction.
  • IEP (Individualized Education Program) — For students who need specialized instructional supports. More comprehensive but also more involved to establish.

Accommodations worth requesting depending on the child's specific needs:

  • Preferential seating (front of classroom for hearing and vision access)
  • Hearing support (FM system, captioning, or interpreter if warranted)
  • Extended time on tests and assignments
  • Enlarged print or digital access to materials
  • Activity modifications for physical education
  • Permission to leave class for medical needs without penalty
  • Flexibility around absences for medical appointments

Schools are legally required to evaluate requests. Bring documentation from a physician when requesting accommodations.

Activity Guidelines

Physical activity is generally encouraged and healthy for people with Marshall syndrome, but some modifications may be appropriate. High-impact or contact sports, activities with fall risk or collision risk, and extreme exertion may require clearance or modification depending on spine status, joint stability, and eye findings. The Sports & Activity Clearance guide covers this in detail.

Start the Monitoring Schedule

Marshall syndrome is managed through consistent monitoring over time — not intensive intervention, but regular check-ins with the right specialists on the right schedule. The Monitoring Schedule section outlines what to track and how often. Setting up a recurring calendar now means it becomes routine rather than reactive.

Join Support Communities

This is worth repeating at the end of the month, after you have had time to process the diagnosis. Online communities and patient organizations provide practical knowledge, emotional support, and a sense of normalcy that is hard to find anywhere else. See Support & Community for options.

Self-Care for Parents and Caregivers

If you are a parent or caregiver who has just received this diagnosis for a child, your own wellbeing matters too. Caregiver burnout is real, and the fear that comes with a new rare disease diagnosis is real. Acknowledge that this is hard. Be intentional about support for yourself, not just for the person diagnosed. The Family Impact page addresses the caregiver experience directly.

Things That Can Wait

One of the most useful things to know is what does not need to happen this month. The following are all real and worth addressing eventually — but not urgently, and trying to do everything at once leads to overwhelm, not better outcomes.

  • Genetic testing of siblings — Important to discuss with a geneticist, but not a clinical emergency. It can be planned thoughtfully.
  • Trying every supplement — Collagen supplements, vitamin D, magnesium — there are many things people try. None are harmful in moderation, but none are urgent either. Let the specialist team weigh in first.
  • Reading every page on this site — This site exists to be a comprehensive reference. That does not mean you need to read it all in week one. The Basics and this guide are the right starting point.
  • Making major lifestyle changes — Small, sustainable adjustments are more effective than dramatic overhauls. The monitoring and living sections will help identify what changes actually matter for your specific situation.
  • Resolving every uncertainty — There will be questions that take months or years to answer. That is uncomfortable but normal with rare connective tissue disorders. Sitting with uncertainty while taking measured, proactive steps is the actual skill.
