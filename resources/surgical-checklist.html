Pre-Surgical Checklist for Marshall Syndrome

CRITICAL: Print this page and give it to the surgical and anesthesia team before ANY procedure — dental, ophthalmologic, orthopedic, or any surgery requiring anesthesia. Children with Marshall syndrome face unique surgical risks from their collagen disorder. The surgical team MUST be informed of these risks before the procedure begins. An uninformed team can make dangerous assumptions about the airway, cervical spine, tissue handling, and medication response.

Airway Management

Difficult airway risk due to micrognathia and midface hypoplasia. The receding mandible and midface underdevelopment characteristic of Marshall syndrome create a structurally difficult airway. This is the single most dangerous surgical risk in this population.
  • Experienced anesthesiologist required: A 502-patient Stickler series published in Anesthesia & Analgesia (2021) found that 92.7% of type 1 and 96.9% of type 2 patients were managed with a single intubation attempt — but only when performed by an experienced anesthesiologist who was aware of the diagnosis in advance.
  • Receding mandible + cleft palate history = higher risk of multiple airway attempts. Do not assume a straightforward intubation.
  • TMJ dysfunction may limit mouth opening, further complicating laryngoscopy.
  • Equipment: Have a videolaryngoscope and fiber-optic bronchoscope available and ready before induction — not as a backup plan, but as the primary approach.
  • Post-operative airway obstruction risk: Tongue base prolapse from micrognathia can cause airway compromise after extubation. The team may need prone positioning, nasopharyngeal prongs, or CPAP in the post-anesthesia care unit.
  • Pierre Robin sequence is present in approximately 24% of Marshall syndrome patients. Its presence should trigger the highest level of airway alerting for the anesthesia team.

Cervical Spine Precautions

Cervical spine instability may be present. COL11A1 mutations compromise the ligaments that stabilize the cervical spine. Neck hyperextension during intubation or positioning can cause catastrophic spinal cord injury.
  • In-line stabilization during intubation if cervical instability is suspected or has not been excluded by imaging.
  • Pre-operative flexion-extension cervical X-rays are recommended before any elective procedure involving intubation. This should be completed weeks before surgery, not on the day of the procedure.
  • Avoid hyperextension of the neck during all positioning — intubation, prone positioning, lateral decubitus, and any repositioning during the case.

Anesthetic Resistance

Issue Detail Action
Local anesthetic resistance 88% of Stickler/EDS patients show resistance to lidocaine. Standard doses may be completely ineffective. Use articaine (4% with 1:100,000 epinephrine) as first-line. May need 2–3x standard dose. Monitor total dose limits carefully.
Fluoroquinolone AVOID Tendon rupture risk is significantly elevated in collagen disorders. NEVER use ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, or moxifloxacin. Choose alternative antibiotics for surgical prophylaxis.
Medication sensitivity Altered drug metabolism is possible due to connective tissue differences affecting drug distribution and binding. Review all medications with the anesthesiologist during the pre-operative consultation. Document any previous adverse reactions.

Tissue Fragility & Wound Healing

Collagen disorders cause three distinct surgical problems: wound dehiscence, diffuse bleeding, and delayed healing. The surgical team must plan for all three.

  • Sutures: Use horizontal mattress technique. Leave sutures in place 2x longer than standard removal timelines. A wound that normally has sutures removed at 7–10 days should retain them for 14–20 days.
  • Friable tissues: Tissues may not hold suture material well. The surgeon should anticipate the need for wider bites, layered closure, and reinforcement with retention sutures for high-tension wounds.
  • Bleeding: Expect diffuse bleeding that may be difficult to control. Blood vessel fragility in collagen disorders means bleeding can come from broad tissue surfaces rather than identifiable vessels. Have additional hemostatic agents available.
  • Delayed wound healing: Plan for wound healing timelines that are 50–100% longer than standard. Avoid tension on closure.
  • Steri-strips and tissue adhesive should be used as adjuncts to sutures, not as replacements. External wound support should continue for 4–8 weeks post-operatively.

Cardiac Considerations

  • Mitral valve prolapse is present in approximately 45.6% of Stickler/Marshall spectrum patients. This is not a rare comorbidity — it should be assumed until excluded.
  • Pre-operative echocardiogram is recommended for any patient who has not been screened recently (within the past 12 months).
  • Endocarditis prophylaxis if mitral valve prolapse with regurgitation is present, per AHA guidelines. Confirm with the patient's cardiologist before surgery.
  • Monitor for arrhythmias perioperatively. The collagen abnormality can affect cardiac valve tissue and conduction pathways.

Eye Protection

  • Retinal detachment risk: Marshall syndrome carries a 65% lifetime risk of retinal detachment. Protect the eyes during all positioning and draping. Any external pressure on the globe can trigger a detachment.
  • Avoid pressure on the eyes: If prone positioning is required, use orbital protection frames that completely eliminate pressure on the globes. Standard foam face pads are not adequate.
  • Post-operative monitoring: Any new floaters, flashes of light, curtain or shadow over vision, or sudden vision changes after surgery require an immediate ophthalmology consult. Do not wait for a scheduled follow-up.

The Pre-Surgical Letter Template

Copy this letter, fill in the blanks, and give it to the surgical and anesthesia team before every procedure. Having this information in writing ensures that critical safety details are not lost during verbal handoffs between team members. Print multiple copies — one for the surgeon, one for the anesthesiologist, one for the pre-op nursing team, and one for the chart.

To the Surgical/Anesthesia Team:

[Child's name] has Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutation), a genetic collagen disorder affecting connective tissue throughout the body. The following considerations are critical for safe surgical care:

  1. Difficult airway risk — micrognathia and midface hypoplasia are present. Possible cervical instability. Request experienced anesthesiologist with videolaryngoscope and fiber-optic bronchoscope available.
  2. 88% local anesthetic resistance — use articaine (4% with 1:100,000 epinephrine) as first-line. Lidocaine is likely ineffective.
  3. Tissue fragility — leave sutures in 2x longer than standard. Expect delayed healing and diffuse bleeding.
  4. Mitral valve prolapse — [confirmed / not yet screened]. If confirmed with regurgitation, endocarditis prophylaxis is indicated.
  5. Retinal detachment risk (65% lifetime) — protect eyes from any pressure during positioning. Post-op: any new floaters or flashes = immediate ophthalmology consult.
  6. AVOID fluoroquinolone antibiotics (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) — elevated tendon rupture risk in collagen disorders.

Contact [specialist name] at [phone number] for questions about this patient's specific surgical risks.
