Emergency Medical Card & Travel Information

Every family should carry a printed emergency card and ensure the child wears a medical ID bracelet. In an emergency, the treating physician needs to know about the difficult airway, cervical spine precautions, and retinal detachment risk within the first minutes. ER doctors will not know Marshall syndrome — your card speaks for you when you cannot.

Why You Need a Medical Emergency Card

Marshall syndrome is a rare genetic condition affecting approximately 1 in 7,500–9,000 people. Most emergency physicians will never have encountered it. In time-critical situations — a car accident, a sudden retinal detachment, an allergic reaction requiring intubation — there is no time to research a rare diagnosis. A printed emergency card provides the critical information a treating physician needs within seconds.

Two situations make this especially urgent:

  • Airway emergencies: The midface hypoplasia and micrognathia common in Marshall syndrome create a difficult airway. Standard intubation may fail. The anesthesiologist needs to know this before the first attempt, not after multiple failed attempts in a crisis.
  • Retinal detachment: With a 65% lifetime risk, a sudden vision emergency can happen anywhere. The ER team needs to know this is not a routine eye complaint — it requires immediate vitreoretinal surgical consultation.

Medical ID Bracelets

A medical ID bracelet ensures first responders are immediately alerted to critical information even if the patient is unconscious or unable to communicate. For Marshall syndrome, the airway alert alone justifies wearing one.

What to Engrave

MARSHALL SYNDROME
DIFFICULT AIRWAY - COL11A1
SEE WALLET CARD

If space allows, add: NO NECK HYPEREXTENSION and any critical drug allergies.

When to Start Wearing One

  • Immediately after diagnosis — there is no age too young. Infant and toddler medical ID anklets and shoe tags are available.
  • Update the bracelet after significant medical changes (new diagnosis, new medication, surgery).
  • Consider a second ID for the diaper bag, car seat, or backpack for very young children.
  • Involve the child in choosing their bracelet — they are more likely to wear one they helped pick out.

Digital Emergency Information

Supplement your physical card and bracelet with digital emergency information stored on the patient's phone. First responders are trained to check for these.

Apple Health Medical ID (iPhone)

  1. Open the Health app → tap your profile picture → Medical ID.
  2. Tap Edit and fill in:
    • Medical Conditions: Marshall Syndrome (Stickler Type 2) - COL11A1 - Difficult Airway - Cervical Spine Precautions - High Retinal Detachment Risk
    • Medical Notes: See wallet card. Do NOT hyperextend neck. Request fiber-optic intubation. OrphanAnesthesia.eu for anesthesia guidelines.
    • Allergies & Reactions: List all known allergies.
    • Medications: List all current medications.
    • Emergency Contacts: Add parents/guardians and key specialists.
  3. Enable "Show When Locked" so it is accessible without unlocking the phone.

Google Emergency Information (Android)

  1. Open Settings → Safety & emergency → Medical information.
  2. Fill in the same information as above.
  3. Also add emergency contacts under Settings → Safety & emergency → Emergency contacts.

ICE Contacts

Add ICE (In Case of Emergency) as a prefix to key contacts in the phone’s address book: "ICE - Mom," "ICE - Dad," "ICE - Dr. [Geneticist]." Many first responders still check the contact list for ICE entries.

School Emergency Card

School nurses and administrators need a version of the emergency card adapted to the school setting. Provide a copy at the start of every school year and update it whenever medical information changes. Request a meeting with the school nurse to walk through it.

Tip: Combine this with a 504 Plan or IEP (Individualized Education Program) that documents medical accommodations, PE modifications, and emergency protocols.

The school card should include everything on the standard emergency card above, plus:

  • Fainting / vasovagal protocol: If the child has a history of syncope or vasovagal episodes, include positioning instructions (lay flat, elevate legs) and when to call 911 vs. the school nurse.
  • Seizure protocol: If applicable, include seizure first aid steps and when to administer rescue medication.
  • Medication administration: List any medications that may need to be given during school hours, with dosing, timing, and who is authorized to administer them.
  • PE and physical activity modifications: Specify restrictions based on the child’s specific profile — contact sports restrictions (cervical instability, retinal detachment risk), impact limitations, activity-specific guidance from the orthopedic specialist and ophthalmologist. See building your specialist team.
  • Hearing accommodations: Preferential seating, FM system use, captioning needs, quiet testing environment if applicable.
  • Vision accommodations: Large print materials, seating position, screen magnification, lighting needs.
  • Parent/guardian contacts: Multiple phone numbers with the order to try them.
  • Authorized pickup: Who is authorized to pick up the child in a medical emergency.

Travel Considerations

Traveling with a rare genetic condition requires advance preparation. Most of this is straightforward once set up, but the first trip takes some planning.

Carry Medical Records

  • Keep a printed copy of the emergency card in your carry-on, wallet, and checked luggage.
  • Carry a letter from the geneticist on hospital letterhead summarizing the diagnosis, key precautions, and current medications. This is invaluable if you need emergency care in an unfamiliar hospital.
  • Store a digital copy of all medical records, recent test results, and imaging reports in a secure cloud service (Google Drive, iCloud, etc.) accessible from any device.
  • If the child has hearing aids or cochlear implants, carry documentation for airport security explaining that these devices cannot go through certain scanners.

Airport Security & TSA

  • TSA allows you to request a TSA Notification Card (available at tsa.gov) to discreetly inform officers about medical conditions.
  • Hearing aids can stay in during screening. Cochlear implant processors may need to be removed for certain scanners — check with your audiologist.
  • Carry medications in original labeled containers with a doctor’s letter if traveling internationally.
  • Consider TSA PreCheck or TSA Cares (a helpline for travelers with disabilities: 855-787-2227) for a smoother experience.

International Travel

  • Translate key medical terms into the language of your destination. Critical phrases:
    • "Difficult airway — do not force intubation"
    • "Do not hyperextend the neck"
    • "High risk of retinal detachment — emergency ophthalmology consult"
    • "Collagen disorder — COL11A1 gene mutation"
  • Research hospitals near your destination before you travel. Identify the nearest facility with a vitreoretinal surgeon and pediatric anesthesiology.
  • Carry your geneticist’s letter translated into the destination language if possible.
  • MedicAlert’s 24/7 hotline offers multilingual translation services — another reason to consider their service.

Travel Insurance

  • Standard travel insurance often excludes pre-existing conditions. Look for policies that explicitly cover them, or purchase a "pre-existing condition waiver" add-on.
  • Ensure the policy covers emergency medical evacuation — critical if traveling to areas with limited specialist availability.
  • Document your medical history honestly on the application. An undisclosed pre-existing condition can void coverage entirely.
  • Keep the insurance company’s emergency number in your phone and on the printed emergency card.

Emergency Department Preparedness

Emergency Department Preparedness Checklist

Emergency Information Form (EIF)

The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) publishes a standardized Emergency Information Form (EIF) template designed specifically for children with special health care needs. This is a structured, one-page document that emergency physicians are trained to recognize and use. Complete the AAP EIF template and keep copies in three places: on the child’s phone (as a saved PDF or photo), in the child’s wallet or backpack, and posted on the refrigerator at home for babysitters and emergency responders.

Medical Alert Bracelet Engraving

The medical ID bracelet should communicate the most critical information in the fewest words. For Marshall syndrome, the recommended engraving includes:

  • Collagen XI disorder (more recognizable to ER physicians than “Marshall syndrome”)
  • Difficult airway (triggers anesthesiology consultation before any intubation attempt)
  • Cervical instability (prevents routine neck hyperextension during airway management or trauma assessment)
  • Retinal detachment risk (ensures any eye complaint is treated as urgent)

Critical ED Scenarios

  1. Retinal detachment = ophthalmology STAT, not next-day: If a child with Marshall syndrome presents with sudden flashes, floaters, a curtain or shadow across vision, or sudden vision loss, this is a surgical emergency requiring vitreoretinal consultation within hours. Do not accept “we’ll have ophthalmology see them in the morning.” Delays beyond 24 hours for a macula-on detachment significantly worsen visual outcomes.
  2. Cervical injury = lower threshold for CT/MRI: Standard cervical spine clearance protocols (NEXUS, Canadian C-Spine Rule) assume normal connective tissue. In Marshall syndrome, the ligamentous laxity and potential craniocervical instability warrant a lower threshold for advanced imaging (CT or MRI) rather than relying on clinical clearance alone. Communicate this to the treating physician explicitly.
  3. Joint subluxation = splint in comfort position: Joint hypermobility means subluxations (partial dislocations) may occur with minimal trauma. The appropriate ED management is to splint the joint in the position of comfort, provide pain management, and arrange orthopedic follow-up. Forced reduction attempts in a hypermobile joint can cause additional soft tissue damage.
  4. Anaphylaxis-like presentation = consider MCAS: Some individuals with connective tissue disorders have comorbid mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), which can produce anaphylaxis-like episodes (flushing, hypotension, tachycardia, urticaria) without a clear allergen trigger. If anaphylaxis treatment (epinephrine) is needed and no obvious allergen is identified, mention the possibility of MCAS to the treating team for appropriate follow-up.

Self-Advocacy Training

By age 10, the child should be able to state their diagnosis and their top 3 emergency concerns. Practice this with them regularly. A child who can say “I have Marshall syndrome, a collagen disorder. I have a difficult airway, my neck should not be bent back, and any vision change is an emergency” provides critical information even when a parent is not present or is unable to communicate (e.g., if both parent and child are involved in an accident).

Annual ED Bag Check

Once a year (a good time is the child’s birthday or the start of a new school year), review the emergency preparedness kit:

  • EIF current: Does the Emergency Information Form reflect the current diagnosis, medications, specialists, and emergency contacts? Update and reprint if anything has changed.
  • Medications listed: Are all current medications (names, doses, frequencies) accurately listed on the EIF and the emergency card? Medication changes are the most common reason an emergency document becomes outdated.
  • Emergency contacts verified: Are all phone numbers still active? Are the listed specialists still the correct providers? Has the primary care physician changed? Call each number to confirm it still reaches the right person.
