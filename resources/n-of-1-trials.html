N-of-1 Trials & Building Evidence in Rare Disease

Medical supervision is required before starting any intervention. Nothing on this page constitutes medical advice or a recommendation to begin any treatment protocol. Every intervention described here carries risks, drug interactions, and contraindications that must be evaluated by a qualified physician who knows your child’s full history. This page explains methodology so families can have informed conversations with their medical team — not so they can design and run trials independently.

Families affected by ultra-rare diseases face a cruel paradox: they are told to “follow the evidence,” but for conditions as rare as Marshall syndrome, large randomized controlled trials simply will not happen. Understanding why those trials won’t occur — and what rigorous alternatives exist — is the first step toward demanding better answers from the medical system.

Why Traditional Clinical Trials Won’t Happen for Marshall Syndrome

The randomized controlled trial (RCT) is the gold standard of clinical evidence, but it was designed for common diseases. For Marshall syndrome, four structural barriers make traditional RCTs essentially impossible:

  1. Population size. COL11A1-specific Marshall syndrome is estimated to affect fewer than 1 in 10,000 people, and possibly far fewer given ongoing diagnostic underrecognition. A minimally powered Phase 2 RCT typically requires 30–100 participants per arm. Finding and enrolling even 20 genetically confirmed COL11A1 Marshall patients globally for a single trial is logistically prohibitive.
  2. Phenotypic heterogeneity. Marshall syndrome varies enormously between patients: some have severe sensorineural hearing loss, others minimal; some have significant skeletal involvement, others primarily ophthalmologic. This heterogeneity makes group comparisons statistically messy. A treatment that helps the “spinal subtype” may show no effect in a pooled group that includes patients without significant spinal involvement.
  3. No pharmaceutical incentive. RCTs cost tens to hundreds of millions of dollars. That investment is only commercially viable when a patentable molecule is at the end of the pipeline. Most interventions with plausible mechanisms in Marshall syndrome — generic medications, supplements, specific exercise protocols — cannot be patented. Pharmaceutical companies will not fund trials for them, and academic funding agencies have limited appetite for ultra-rare disease intervention trials.
  4. Regulatory complexity. Even if funding existed, identifying a regulatory pathway for a rare disease trial with a heterogeneous endpoint (pain? function? inflammatory markers?) and no validated disease-specific outcome instrument is challenging.

None of this means families should accept the absence of evidence as evidence of absence. It means the path to evidence must be different.

The evidence gap is real but not permanent. The N-of-1 trial methodology was specifically developed to generate rigorous evidence in exactly this situation: a condition too rare for group trials, a patient who deserves better than anecdote, and a question that matters enough to answer systematically.

What Is an N-of-1 Trial?

An N-of-1 trial is a structured, repeated crossover experiment conducted entirely within a single patient. Rather than comparing two groups of patients, the patient serves as their own control — experiencing each condition (treatment and comparator) multiple times in a randomized order, with objective measurements taken throughout.

This is not anecdote, and it is not a case report. It is a prospective, pre-registered, blinded, randomized experiment. The single-patient design eliminates between-patient variability entirely: you are not asking whether Drug A works better than placebo “on average across patients.” You are asking whether Drug A works better than placebo in this specific patient — which is often the more relevant clinical question anyway.

Regulatory and scientific recognition: N-of-1 trials are recognized as valid evidence by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA), the European Medicines Agency (EMA), the Cochrane Collaboration, and the British Medical Journal. Results from well-designed N-of-1 trials can be published in peer-reviewed journals and can inform clinical practice guidelines. The FDA has issued formal guidance documents on the use of N-of-1 trials in rare disease drug development.

N-of-1 Trial Design Principles

Involve a biostatistician or clinical trialist from the start. Academic medical centers often have clinical research staff who can help design a rigorous protocol at low or no cost, especially if the result will be submitted for publication. Many institutions have rare disease research programs that welcome exactly this kind of collaboration.

Basic Structure

An N-of-1 trial consists of alternating treatment and control periods (called “arms” or “phases”) with washout periods between them. The minimum credible design uses at least three treatment-control pairs (six phases total), giving enough data points for statistical analysis.

  • Treatment arm: The period during which the patient receives the active intervention (e.g., a medication, supplement, or exercise protocol).
  • Control arm: Either a placebo (when feasible) or an alternative comparator (a different intervention, or the current standard of care).
  • Washout period: A gap between each arm during which neither treatment is given, allowing the prior treatment’s effects to clear. The length depends on the half-life and mechanism of the intervention (see candidate interventions below).

Randomization

The order of arms (treatment first vs. control first within each pair) should be assigned randomly, not chosen by the treating physician or family. Simple randomization can be done with a coin flip or an online randomization tool. Randomization prevents systematic bias: if treatment always came first, natural disease fluctuation could masquerade as a treatment effect.

Blinding

Blinding removes the placebo effect and observer bias from results:

  • Patient-blinding: The patient does not know which arm they are in. Requires a matched placebo (identical-looking pill, capsule, or liquid). A compounding pharmacy can often prepare blinded study medications.
  • Assessor-blinding: The person scoring outcomes (pain questionnaire interviewer, physical therapist performing timed tests) does not know which arm the patient is in. This is often achievable even when full patient-blinding is not.
  • Open-label: Both patient and assessors know the arm. Weakest design but sometimes the only option (e.g., comparing two exercise protocols that cannot be disguised).

Phase Duration

Each arm should be long enough to capture a stable treatment effect — typically 2–4 weeks per arm for most pharmacologic interventions, though some conditions (like inflammatory flare patterns) may require longer observation windows. Phases that are too short will capture noise rather than signal.

Pre-Specification

Before the trial begins, all of the following must be committed to in writing: the primary outcome measure, the statistical analysis plan, the minimum clinically meaningful difference, and the stopping rules. Deciding these after seeing the data invalidates the results.

Practical Outcome Measures for Marshall Syndrome N-of-1 Trials

Choosing the right outcome measure is the most important design decision. The best primary outcome is one that is objective (or semi-objective), measurable at regular intervals within each phase, and clinically meaningful to the patient.

Pain and Function

  • Daily pain NRS: Numeric Rating Scale (0–10) recorded each evening in a standardized symptom diary. Simple, validated, sensitive to within-person change. Average the last 5 days of each phase to reduce day-to-day noise.
  • Functional analog scales: Daily ratings of ability to participate in school, walk, carry a backpack, or complete specific activities the patient identifies as important. Personally meaningful anchors improve sensitivity.
  • Timed Up-and-Go (TUG): A timed functional mobility test. Administered by the same assessor at the same time of day at the end of each phase. Objective, reproducible, independent of subjective reporting.
  • Grip strength: Measured with a hand dynamometer. Three trials each hand, dominant and non-dominant, averaged. Captures systemic inflammation and functional status.
  • 6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT): Distance walked in 6 minutes on a standard course. Validated in pediatric and adolescent populations. Requires consistent conditions (same location, same assessor, no encouragement scripted).

Inflammatory Biomarkers

Blood draws at the end of each phase (not the beginning) capture the treatment’s effect on inflammation. Recommended panel:

  • C-reactive protein (CRP) and ESR: Standard inflammatory markers. Low cost, widely available, sensitive to treatment effects on systemic inflammation.
  • Cartilage Oligomeric Matrix Protein (COMP): Serum biomarker of cartilage turnover. Elevated in active joint degeneration. See the Biomarker Dashboard for reference ranges and interpretation guidance specific to COL11A1 patients.
  • CTX-II (C-terminal telopeptide of type II collagen): Urinary or serum marker of cartilage collagen breakdown. Potentially sensitive to MMP-inhibiting interventions like sub-antimicrobial doxycycline.
  • MMP-3 (matrix metalloproteinase-3): Serum marker directly relevant to the COL11A1/MMP-3 axis identified in the 2023 scoliosis genome-wide study. May be the most mechanistically specific biomarker for this condition. See the Biomarker Dashboard for context.

Sleep and Autonomic Function

  • Consumer actigraphy: Wrist-worn accelerometers (many consumer smartwatches export sleep data) can provide objective sleep efficiency and total sleep time. Useful secondary outcome when pain disrupts sleep.
  • Validated sleep questionnaires: PROMIS Sleep Disturbance or the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index, completed at the end of each phase.

Symptom Diary

A standardized daily log capturing: pain NRS (morning and evening), fatigue rating, activity completed, medications taken (dose and time), adverse effects, and any notable events (illness, unusual stress, travel). The diary creates the audit trail that makes results interpretable. Digital apps or a shared spreadsheet with timestamped entries are preferable to handwritten logs.

Candidate Interventions for N-of-1 Trials in Marshall Syndrome

Every intervention below requires physician evaluation and approval before starting. This list represents mechanistically plausible candidates based on the known biology of COL11A1 disease and existing evidence in related conditions — not a recommendation to try any of them. Drug interactions, contraindications, dosing, and monitoring requirements must be assessed by a physician who knows your child’s full medical history.

The following interventions have plausible mechanisms of action grounded in COL11A1 biology and have sufficient safety data in the relevant age range to be considered for structured investigation. Each is listed with its proposed mechanism and the washout period a trial protocol would typically require.

Sub-Antimicrobial Doxycycline (MMP Inhibition)

Mechanism: At doses below the antimicrobial threshold (20–40 mg/day), doxycycline inhibits matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) directly. Given the documented role of MMP-3 in COL11A1-related spinal cartilage degradation, this represents a mechanistically direct target. Sub-antimicrobial doxycycline is FDA-approved for rosacea and has a well-characterized safety profile. Antibiotic resistance does not occur at sub-antimicrobial doses.

Washout period: Approximately 2 weeks (5 half-lives of tetracyclines). MMP activity is expected to return toward baseline within this window.

Outcome sensitivity: MMP-3 serum levels, CTX-II, and functional pain scores are the most likely responsive endpoints.

Low-Dose Naltrexone (TLR4 Antagonism)

Mechanism: At doses of 1–5 mg (far below the 50 mg opioid-blockade dose), naltrexone acts as a TLR4 antagonist on microglial and peripheral glial cells, reducing neuroinflammatory signaling. This is the proposed mechanism for LDN’s effects in fibromyalgia, Crohn’s disease, and small fiber neuropathy — all conditions with overlapping features relevant to the Marshall syndrome pain phenotype. Evidence base is growing but not yet definitive.

Washout period: 3–7 days (half-life is short; TLR4 receptor normalization is the key variable). Most LDN researchers use a 7-day washout to be conservative.

Outcome sensitivity: Neuropathic pain measures, fatigue, sleep quality, and inflammatory markers are the most plausible responsive endpoints.

Sulforaphane Supplementation (NRF2 Pathway Activation)

Mechanism: Sulforaphane (from broccoli sprout extract) is a potent activator of the NRF2 antioxidant response element pathway, which upregulates endogenous antioxidant and anti-inflammatory defenses and has been shown in animal models to reduce MMP expression and slow cartilage degradation. It has a favorable safety profile, is commercially available, and has published evidence in osteoarthritis and inflammatory conditions.

Washout period: Approximately 1 week. NRF2 induction returns to baseline within days of stopping.

Outcome sensitivity: Inflammatory markers (CRP, MMP-3), oxidative stress markers (if available), and pain NRS.

Exercise Protocol Comparison

Mechanism: Comparing two structured exercise regimens (e.g., aquatic therapy vs. land-based resistance training, or two different loading protocols) requires no washout period because exercise effects are largely reversible within a week. This makes exercise protocol comparison the methodologically cleanest candidate for N-of-1 design.

Washout period: 3–7 days (detraining of the specific adaptive response, while maintaining baseline fitness).

Outcome sensitivity: Functional measures (TUG, 6MWT, grip strength), pain NRS, and patient-reported activity tolerance.

Carbamazepine for Neuropathic Pain

Mechanism: Sodium channel blocker with established efficacy in trigeminal neuralgia and neuropathic pain syndromes. May be relevant if a small fiber neuropathy component is confirmed by skin punch biopsy. Requires physician monitoring of serum levels and hematologic parameters.

Washout period: At least 3–4 weeks given the longer half-life and the need to confirm return to pre-treatment sodium channel baseline. Abrupt discontinuation risks; taper must be supervised by a physician.

Outcome sensitivity: Neuropathic pain subscales (burning, allodynia, shooting pain), standardized neuropathic pain questionnaires (DN4, NPSI).

How to Propose an N-of-1 Trial to Your Medical Team

Frame it as a question, not a demand. Physicians who feel they are being told what to do become defensive. Physicians who feel they are being invited into an interesting clinical puzzle become collaborators.

The following approach has worked for rare disease families in similar situations:

  1. Lead with the clinical question, not the intervention. Begin: “We want to understand whether [symptom] in our child responds to [class of intervention]. We’ve been reading about N-of-1 trial methodology as a way to answer this rigorously rather than just trying things randomly. Would you be willing to design something with us?”
  2. Bring the methodology, not just enthusiasm. Print or share a key paper on N-of-1 trial design. The 2015 JAMA paper by Kravitz et al. (“Toward Evidence-Based Prescribing at the End of Life”) and the BMJ’s N-of-1 trial resources are good starting points. Demonstrating that you understand the method makes the conversation substantive.
  3. Suggest starting with the safest intervention. Proposing an exercise protocol comparison as the first N-of-1 trial reduces physician hesitation dramatically. The risk profile is minimal, and a positive result builds credibility for subsequent trials of pharmacologic interventions.
  4. Offer to handle the data collection burden. Physicians are busy. If you commit to maintaining the symptom diary, coordinating the lab draws, and organizing the data, the physician’s role becomes interpretation and oversight rather than administrative coordination.
  5. Mention publication potential. Peer-reviewed journals actively seek well-designed N-of-1 trials in ultra-rare disease. Physicians are motivated by academic output, and the prospect of authoring a rare disease N-of-1 publication may significantly increase engagement. Journals including Orphanet Journal of Rare Diseases, JAMA Internal Medicine, and BMJ Open have published single-patient trials in conditions with comparable rarity.
  6. If the first physician declines, try again. Academic medical centers, rare disease clinics, and clinical pharmacologists are the most receptive audiences. General pediatricians and primary care physicians are the least likely to have familiarity with this methodology — not because they are dismissive of science, but because N-of-1 trial design is not part of standard clinical training.

Statistical Analysis

The statistical analysis of an N-of-1 trial is simpler than many families assume. The core question is: across the repeated crossover pairs, was the patient consistently better on treatment than on control?

Frequentist Approaches

  • Paired t-test: Compares the mean outcome score in each treatment arm to the corresponding control arm across all pairs. Appropriate when outcomes are normally distributed and there are at least 3 pairs.
  • Wilcoxon signed-rank test: Non-parametric alternative for the paired t-test. Preferable when outcome data are ordinal (pain ratings) or when normality cannot be assumed.
  • Run charts and visual analysis: Plotting the primary outcome over the full trial timeline is essential. A consistent pattern of improvement during treatment phases and return to baseline during washout is visually compelling and supports statistical findings.

Bayesian Approaches

Bayesian analysis is increasingly preferred for N-of-1 trials because it naturally incorporates prior knowledge (what do we already know about this treatment?) and produces intuitive outputs: the probability that the treatment works for this patient, and the estimated effect size with credible intervals. Free software tools including SPSS, R (the n1-analysis package), and the Snap-On web application (developed specifically for N-of-1 analysis) support Bayesian N-of-1 computations without requiring advanced statistical training.

Aggregating Results Across Patients

When multiple patients with the same condition each run an N-of-1 trial of the same intervention, results can be combined using meta-analytic methods to produce population-level evidence. This is not hypothetical: published meta-analyses of N-of-1 trials exist for osteoarthritis, ADHD, and chronic pain conditions. For a condition like Marshall syndrome, even 3–5 well-designed N-of-1 trials could support a publishable meta-analysis that constitutes meaningful clinical evidence.

Publishing Case Reports and N-of-1 Results

Publication of a well-designed N-of-1 trial or detailed case report adds directly to the global knowledge base for Marshall syndrome. Negative results — trials that show the intervention did not work — are particularly valuable in rare disease because they prevent other families from pursuing ineffective treatments and help narrow the field of candidates worth investigating.

CARE Guidelines for Case Reports

The CARE guidelines (CAse REport) are an international standard for writing and reviewing medical case reports. A Marshall syndrome case report or N-of-1 trial written to CARE standards is more likely to be accepted by peer-reviewed journals and more likely to be cited and acted upon. The CARE checklist is freely available at care-statement.org and covers: timeline, patient information, clinical findings, diagnostic assessment, therapeutic interventions, follow-up outcomes, and patient perspective.

Journals That Welcome Rare Disease N-of-1 Studies

  • Orphanet Journal of Rare Diseases — open access, specifically focused on rare diseases, actively publishes case series and N-of-1 reports
  • BMJ Open — open access, accepts study protocols (pre-publication of the design) and results
  • JAMA Internal Medicine — published the foundational N-of-1 methodology papers; welcomes well-designed single-patient trials
  • Journal of Rare Disorders — explicitly focused on rare disease clinical evidence
  • American Journal of Medical Genetics Part A — has published multiple Marshall/Stickler syndrome case reports; receptive to COL11A1 cases
  • Cold Spring Harbor Molecular Case Studies — rapid publication for genetically confirmed cases with molecular data

The Value of Negative Results

A rigorously conducted N-of-1 trial that shows no effect of an intervention is a scientifically valid and publishable result. In rare disease, where families desperately want to try anything that might help, negative evidence actively protects the community from investing time, money, and hope in ineffective interventions. Journals increasingly recognize this; framing a negative result as “we now know this approach does not work in COL11A1-confirmed Marshall syndrome” is a contribution, not a failure.

Pre-register your protocol. Registering the trial design at ClinicalTrials.gov or ISRCTN before starting strengthens the credibility of results and is required by many journals. Registration is free, takes under an hour, and makes negative results publishable in journals that require pre-registration.
