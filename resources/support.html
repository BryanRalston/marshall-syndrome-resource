Telehealth for Rare Disease Specialist Access (R405)

Telehealth access expanded significantly in 2025–2026. Federal and state policies enacted during the pandemic have been made permanent or extended, dramatically improving specialist access for rare disease families who previously had to travel hundreds of miles for expert consultations. Updated evidence (R485) quantifies the impact: telehealth for rare disease populations has demonstrated 84% reduction in specialist wait times and 92% reduction in travel burden, with patient satisfaction scores equivalent to in-person visits for non-procedural consultations. For Marshall syndrome families — who typically coordinate 5–8 subspecialties spread across multiple institutions — telehealth is not a convenience but a critical access strategy.

For Marshall syndrome families, telehealth is particularly valuable because the syndrome requires coordination across multiple subspecialties (genetics, ophthalmology, audiology, orthopedics, pain medicine, PT) — specialists who rarely exist in the same geographic location.

Practical advice for telehealth visits:
  • Request a pre-visit summary upload — send recent imaging, audiograms, and lab results to the specialist’s portal before the appointment so the video time focuses on interpretation and planning, not data review
  • Keep a Marshall syndrome summary letter (diagnosis, mutation, current specialists, current medications, key concerns) that can be sent to any new telehealth provider in advance. This eliminates the need to re-explain the diagnosis at every new visit
  • Ask whether the specialist does virtual multidisciplinary team conferences — some academic centers offer coordinated virtual visits where multiple subspecialists participate in a single session, reducing the appointment burden on the family

Service Dogs for Chronic Illness

Service dogs trained for chronic illness can provide meaningful daily support for individuals with Marshall syndrome. Unlike emotional support animals, service dogs are task-trained working animals with full ADA access rights to public spaces, housing, and transportation regardless of pet policies.

Service Dog TypeTasks PerformedMarshall Syndrome Application
Mobility assistance dog Bracing for balance, retrieving dropped items, opening doors, carrying bags, providing stability on stairs Joint hypermobility, balance impairment from vestibular dysfunction, fatigue-related fall risk, and chronic pain limiting bending and reaching
Medical alert dog Detecting physiological changes (heart rate shifts, blood pressure drops) and alerting the handler before symptoms become dangerous POTS-related presyncope and syncope episodes — dogs can detect autonomic changes and alert the handler to sit or lie down before fainting
Hearing dog Alerting to sounds: doorbells, alarms, name being called, approaching vehicles, timers Progressive sensorineural hearing loss, particularly in environments where hearing aids are removed (swimming, sleeping) or insufficient
ADA Access Rights
Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), service dogs are permitted in all public accommodations including schools, hospitals, restaurants, airplanes, and workplaces. Businesses may only ask two questions: (1) “Is this a service animal required because of a disability?” and (2) “What task has the dog been trained to perform?” They may not ask about the nature of the disability, request documentation, or require the dog to demonstrate the task. For children with Marshall syndrome, this means the service dog accompanies the child to school, medical appointments, and all community activities.
Practical considerations:
  • Wait times: Accredited service dog organizations typically have 12–24 month wait lists. Begin the application process early
  • Cost: Many organizations provide service dogs at reduced or no cost, funded by donations. Others charge $15,000–$30,000. Financial assistance programs exist through organizations like Assistance Dogs International (ADI)
  • Accreditation: Look for organizations accredited by Assistance Dogs International (ADI) at assistancedogsinternational.org — ADI certification ensures standardized training and ethical practices
  • Skin sensitivity: For children with fragile or sensitive skin (common in collagen disorders), request a dog trained with silk or padded harness configurations to prevent skin irritation from standard nylon equipment
  • Multi-task training: A single service dog can be trained for multiple tasks (mobility + hearing alert + medical alert), making them particularly valuable for multi-system conditions like Marshall syndrome

Summer Camps for Children with Chronic Illness (R513)

Summer camp is one of the most powerful resilience-building experiences available to children — and camps specifically designed for children with chronic illness or medical complexity remove the barriers that make traditional camps inaccessible. For a child with Marshall syndrome, camp provides peer connection with other children who understand medical challenges, independence in a medically safe environment, and mastery experiences that build confidence.

Preparing your child for camp:
  • Medical information packet: Prepare a comprehensive medical summary including diagnosis, medications, activity restrictions (see Sports Clearance), emergency protocols, and specialist contact information. Be specific: “no contact sports, no trampolines, polycarbonate eye protection required for all physical activities”
  • Pre-camp medical team communication: Contact the camp’s medical director directly to discuss Marshall syndrome — most camp medical staff will not be familiar with the condition. Provide the Emergency Medical Card and key safety information (retinal detachment signs, cervical precautions, hearing aid management)
  • Activity modifications: Work with the camp to identify which activities can be done as-is, which need modification, and which should be avoided. Most medical camps are experienced at adapting activities and will welcome specific guidance
  • Hearing and vision accommodations: Ensure the camp knows about hearing aids, FM system needs, and any vision accommodations. A buddy system with a hearing camper can help in noisy outdoor environments where hearing aids are less effective
  • Emotional preparation: For children who have not been away from parents for extended periods, practice with short separations. Discuss what to do if they feel unwell, how to communicate with camp medical staff, and reassure them that it is okay to sit out an activity if their body says stop

Genetic Counseling

If not already done, the family should pursue formal genetic counseling to:

  • Understand the inheritance pattern (autosomal dominant — 50% chance of passing to each child)
  • Interpret their specific mutation and its known associations
  • Discuss family planning implications
  • Connect to appropriate registries and research
