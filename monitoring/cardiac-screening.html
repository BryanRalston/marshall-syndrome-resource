Cardiac Screening: Valves, Rhythm & the Collagen Connection

Key Recommendation: Type XI collagen contributes to cardiac valve structure. Echocardiographic screening is recommended at baseline for all Marshall syndrome patients, with periodic follow-up based on findings.

Why Cardiac Screening?

Type XI Collagen in the Heart

The heart’s four valves are not simple flaps of tissue. They are highly organized structures composed of three distinct layers — the ventricularis, spongiosa, and fibrosa — each with a specialized extracellular matrix of collagen, elastic fibers, and proteoglycans. While types I and III collagen provide the primary load-bearing framework, type XI collagen regulates collagen fibrillogenesis within the valve leaflets, controlling fiber diameter and spatial organization. This is the same regulatory role that type V collagen plays in other connective tissues.

In Marshall syndrome, the COL11A1 mutation produces defective type XI collagen. When this collagen cannot properly regulate fibril assembly in valve tissue, the downstream result is disorganized collagen architecture — thinner fibers, irregular spacing, reduced tensile strength. This creates the structural conditions for valve leaflets that are more compliant, more prone to billowing, and more susceptible to progressive degeneration.

Three Structures, One Vulnerability

The COL11A1 mutation does not affect just one part of the mitral valve apparatus. It compromises three interdependent collagen-rich structures:

  1. Valve leaflets: Collagen is the primary structural component of the mitral valve leaflets. Abnormal type XI collagen alters fibril diameter regulation, leading to myxomatous degeneration — the thickening, redundancy, and floppy quality that defines prolapse.
  2. Chordae tendineae: These collagen-rich “heart strings” tether the valve leaflets to the papillary muscles, preventing them from inverting during ventricular contraction. Weakened chordae stretch or rupture, allowing the leaflet to prolapse further into the atrium and worsening regurgitation.
  3. Mitral annulus: The fibrous ring that anchors the valve sits within connective tissue. When this supporting framework is weakened by defective collagen, the annulus can dilate progressively, pulling the leaflets apart and preventing proper coaptation — even if the leaflets themselves are only mildly affected.

This triple vulnerability explains why MVP in connective tissue disorders can be more progressive than idiopathic MVP in the general population: the disease process affects the entire valve apparatus, not just the leaflets.

Prevalence in Stickler/Marshall Spectrum

The prevalence of mitral valve prolapse (MVP) in the Stickler/Marshall spectrum has been debated in the literature, with conflicting study results:

  • An early study of 57 Stickler syndrome patients found MVP in 45.6% (26/57), with similar rates in males (42.9%) and females (50.0%), and no age-related increase in prevalence
  • A later UK study of 78 genetically confirmed Stickler patients from 25 pedigrees found no significant MVP above general population rates
  • The general population prevalence of MVP is approximately 2–3%
Why the discrepancy? The conflicting results likely reflect differences in diagnostic criteria (older echocardiographic criteria were less specific), genetic subtype composition (COL2A1 vs COL11A1 vs COL11A2 mutations may carry different cardiac risk), and study size. Until subtype-specific cardiac data are available for Marshall syndrome (COL11A1), the conservative approach is to screen and establish a baseline.
NEW: COL11A1 Identified as Novel Mitral Valve Prolapse Gene (2025)

A 2025 whole exome sequencing study of 80 sporadic MVP cases, published in Genes & Genomics, has identified COL11A1 as a novel MVP-related gene, predominantly associated with fibroelastic deficiency — the most common pathological substrate of degenerative MVP.

Key findings:

  • Patients with COL11A1 variants had younger onset of MVP compared to those without COL11A1 variants
  • COL11A1-variant carriers showed higher early diastolic peak velocities on echocardiography, suggesting altered valve dynamics
  • COL11A1 is required for myocardial morphogenesis and heart valve development — mouse mutants with Col11a1 deficiency show measurable cardiac function reduction

This study provides the first direct genetic evidence linking COL11A1 to MVP outside the Stickler/Marshall clinical context, strengthening the biological rationale for cardiac screening in all COL11A1 mutation carriers.

Recommendations based on this evidence:

  • Echocardiogram at diagnosis and every 2–3 years thereafter (more frequently if abnormalities found)
  • Auscultation for MVP murmur (mid-systolic click, late systolic murmur) at every well-child visit and annual physical
  • Endocarditis prophylaxis should be discussed with cardiology if significant MVP with regurgitation is identified

Marshall syndrome shares the fundamental mechanism: defective structural collagen altering valve leaflet integrity. The question is not whether COL11A1 mutations can affect valve tissue, but how often and how severely they do. Notably, the Marshall/Stickler prevalence figure falls between Marfan (where cardiac screening is universal) and EDS (where it is less emphasized), reinforcing the case for routine baseline screening.

Understanding Mitral Valve Prolapse

What Happens in MVP

The mitral valve sits between the left atrium and left ventricle, normally opening to allow blood flow in one direction. In MVP, one or both valve leaflets bulge (prolapse) backward into the left atrium during ventricular contraction instead of closing cleanly. This can range from a mild anatomical variant causing no symptoms to a progressive condition with significant regurgitation.

The Connective Tissue Mechanism: Myxomatous Degeneration

In connective tissue disorders, MVP results from myxomatous degeneration of the valve leaflets:

  • The spongiosa layer expands with excessive proteoglycan and mucopolysaccharide deposits, increasing water content
  • The fibrosa’s collagen layer thins and becomes disorganized
  • Type III collagen content increases (replacing the normal type I predominance)
  • Elastin fibers fragment
  • The net result: thickened, redundant, floppy leaflets that cannot maintain their normal saddle-shaped closure geometry

In Marshall syndrome, defective type XI collagen likely contributes to this process by failing to properly regulate fibril formation in the valve leaflets, predisposing to the same cascade of matrix disorganization seen in other collagen disorders.

Symptoms of MVP

Many people with MVP have no symptoms at all. When symptoms occur, they may include:

  • Palpitations — awareness of heartbeat, skipped beats, or rapid heart rate
  • Chest pain — atypical, often sharp or stabbing, not related to exertion
  • Fatigue — sometimes profound, out of proportion to activity level
  • Exercise intolerance — reduced stamina, breathlessness with exertion
  • Dizziness and lightheadedness — especially on standing
  • Dyspnea — shortness of breath, particularly when lying flat
  • Anxiety and panic attacks — may reflect autonomic dysfunction rather than primary psychiatric illness

Most children and adults with MVP have the benign form and lead entirely normal lives. The key is establishing a baseline and tracking changes over time, because progression from benign to significant can occur gradually, especially in the context of a connective tissue disorder where the underlying matrix abnormality is ongoing.

The progression pathway: While most MVP remains benign, the potential trajectory in untreated cases is: prolapse → progressive regurgitation → left atrial and ventricular dilation (volume overload) → heart failure. This progression is rare in childhood but can develop over decades, which is precisely why serial echocardiographic monitoring matters. In connective tissue disorders where all three valve apparatus structures (leaflets, chordae, annulus) are affected, the risk of progression may be higher than in idiopathic MVP.

Echocardiogram Protocol

What the Test Involves

A transthoracic echocardiogram (echo) is completely non-invasive. It uses ultrasound waves to produce real-time images of the heart. There is no radiation, no injection, and no pain. A technician places a transducer on the chest wall with gel, and the examination takes approximately 30–45 minutes. Children do not need sedation for this test.

What to Measure

For a Marshall syndrome patient, the echocardiogram should specifically assess:

  • Mitral valve morphology: Leaflet thickness, redundancy, prolapse degree (in millimeters beyond the annular plane)
  • Mitral regurgitation: Graded as none/trace/mild/moderate/severe using color Doppler
  • Aortic root dimensions: Measured at the annulus, sinuses of Valsalva, sinotubular junction, and ascending aorta, then indexed to body surface area (Z-scores in children)
  • Left atrial size: Enlargement suggests hemodynamically significant regurgitation
  • Left ventricular dimensions and function: Ejection fraction and end-diastolic/systolic dimensions
  • Other valve assessment: Tricuspid and aortic valve morphology and function
  • Ascending aorta: Full measurement to screen for any dilation

Who Orders It

A baseline echocardiogram can be ordered by the child’s geneticist, pediatrician, or ENT (if they are the primary Marshall syndrome coordinator). Abnormal findings should prompt referral to a pediatric cardiologist, ideally one familiar with connective tissue disorders. Ongoing monitoring should be directed by cardiology.

Other Cardiac Considerations

Aortic Root Dilation

Aortic root dilation is the hallmark cardiac finding in Marfan syndrome, but it can occur in other connective tissue disorders as well. In Marshall syndrome:

  • Aortic root dilation is less common than in Marfan or Loeys-Dietz, but should still be measured at every echocardiogram
  • In children, aortic root dimensions must be indexed to body surface area using Z-scores (a Z-score >2 is considered dilated)
  • Even mild dilation (Z-score 1.5–2.0) warrants closer follow-up in the context of a collagen disorder
  • If dilation is detected, repeat imaging in 6–12 months to assess the rate of growth; stable mild dilation is very different from progressive dilation
  • A 2024 AHA scientific statement on aortopathy management in children recommends individualized surveillance based on the underlying genetic condition and rate of change

Arrhythmias Associated with MVP

MVP is associated with several rhythm disturbances:

  • Premature ventricular contractions (PVCs): The most common arrhythmia in MVP, usually benign
  • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT): Episodic rapid heart rate originating above the ventricles
  • Ventricular tachycardia (VT): Rare but important — recent research has identified “arrhythmic MVP” as a subset with increased sudden cardiac death risk, particularly with bileaflet prolapse, mitral annular disjunction, and fibrosis of the papillary muscles
  • Atrial fibrillation: Can develop if mitral regurgitation causes significant left atrial enlargement
When to add rhythm monitoring: If a Marshall syndrome patient reports frequent palpitations, near-syncope, or exercise-triggered symptoms, a 24–48 hour Holter monitor or 2-week event monitor can capture intermittent arrhythmias that a single ECG may miss.

Endocarditis Prophylaxis Guidelines

The endocarditis prophylaxis landscape has changed significantly. Current AHA/ACC guidelines (2022):

  • MVP without regurgitation: Antibiotic prophylaxis is NOT recommended before dental procedures. These patients are at no higher risk than the general population.
  • MVP with documented regurgitation: Previously classified as moderate risk, but current guidelines have narrowed the indications for prophylaxis to high-risk categories only (prosthetic valves, prior endocarditis, certain congenital defects, cardiac transplant with valve regurgitation).
  • Practical advice: Discuss with your cardiologist. If there is significant mitral regurgitation, some cardiologists still recommend prophylaxis before dental work even though the formal guidelines have narrowed, particularly in the context of a connective tissue disorder where valve tissue is structurally abnormal.

MVP, Arrhythmia & POTS: Integrated Management

MVP Prevalence in Hypermobility Spectrum Disorders

While the Stickler/Marshall-specific data remain conflicting, broader hypermobility research provides additional context. A systematic review of hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos syndrome (hEDS) found MVP in approximately 3.5% of patients — elevated above the general population rate of 2–3% but substantially lower than the 45.6% reported in the early Stickler study. This suggests that while collagen disorders do elevate MVP risk, the degree of elevation may be more modest than early studies indicated, reinforcing the importance of baseline screening rather than assuming MVP is present.

Baseline Echocardiogram: Non-Negotiable

Regardless of which prevalence estimate is correct, a baseline echocardiogram at diagnosis settles the question for each individual patient. The test is non-invasive, painless, takes 30–45 minutes, and requires no sedation. It provides:

  • Definitive assessment of mitral valve morphology and function
  • Aortic root dimensions indexed to body surface area (Z-scores)
  • Left atrial and ventricular size (detects volume overload from regurgitation)
  • A reference point for all future comparisons — progression can only be measured against a known baseline

Beta-Blocker Therapy: Dual Indication

Beta-blockers occupy a unique position in Marshall syndrome cardiac management because they address two common comorbidities simultaneously:

IndicationMechanismAgentDosing Notes
Arrhythmia from MVP Reduces ventricular ectopy by blocking sympathetic stimulation of the myocardium; slows conduction through the AV node; reduces myocardial oxygen demand Propranolol, atenolol, or metoprolol Standard pediatric dosing; titrate to heart rate response. Monitor for exercise intolerance, fatigue, and bronchospasm
POTS / autonomic dysfunction Low-dose propranolol reduces inappropriate tachycardia on standing without excessive reduction in cardiac output; improves symptom burden in hyperadrenergic POTS Propranolol (preferred for POTS due to non-selective action and anxiolytic properties) Start low (10 mg BID in adolescents, weight-adjusted in younger children); increase gradually. Not all POTS subtypes respond — neuropathic POTS may respond better to midodrine or fludrocortisone
Beta-blocker caution in hypermobility: Beta-blockers reduce exercise capacity by limiting heart rate response. In a child with Marshall syndrome who is already prone to deconditioning and where exercise is a critical protective intervention (NRF2 activation, disc protection, bone density), over-aggressive beta-blockade can paradoxically worsen functional status. Use the lowest effective dose and monitor exercise tolerance carefully. If exercise capacity drops significantly, reassess the risk-benefit calculation.

Recumbent Aerobic Reconditioning

For children with MVP-related arrhythmias and/or concurrent POTS, recumbent (horizontal) exercise provides the cardiovascular benefits of aerobic conditioning while minimizing orthostatic stress and arrhythmia provocation:

  • Recumbent cycling: Legs at or above heart level eliminates the orthostatic component. Start with 10–15 minutes at low resistance, progressing to 30–45 minutes over 4–6 weeks.
  • Swimming / aquatic therapy: Hydrostatic pressure supports venous return, effectively eliminating POTS symptoms during exercise. Water temperature should be 84–88°F (29–31°C) — warm enough for comfort, not so warm that it promotes vasodilation and worsens tachycardia.
  • Rowing (recumbent): Engages both upper and lower body while maintaining a semi-recumbent position. Excellent for building the muscle pump that supports venous return.

The CHOP (Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia) Modified Dallas Protocol for POTS reconditioning provides a structured, evidence-based exercise progression that begins entirely recumbent and gradually introduces upright exercise over 3 months as the cardiovascular system reconditions. This protocol has demonstrated significant improvement in POTS symptoms, exercise capacity, and quality of life in adolescent populations.

Exercise is treatment, not just fitness. In the context of MVP with arrhythmias and/or POTS, a structured recumbent exercise program is a medical intervention with evidence-based efficacy comparable to pharmacotherapy. It should be prescribed with the same specificity as a medication: type, duration, frequency, intensity, and progression criteria. See Autonomic Dysfunction for the full POTS management pathway.

The Autonomic-Cardiac Connection

MVP + Dysautonomia: A Common Overlap

Approximately 40% of people with MVP also have what has been termed “mitral valve prolapse syndrome” or dysautonomia. This is not coincidental. The overlap is well-documented and has been studied since the 1980s:

  • Patients with symptomatic MVP frequently show elevated resting heart rate and plasma norepinephrine levels
  • Exaggerated heart rate responses during Valsalva maneuver and diminished bradycardic recovery
  • Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (POTS) was initially described with clinical features resembling MVP syndrome
  • Shared symptoms: palpitations, dizziness, exercise intolerance, fatigue, chest pain, cold extremities, headaches, and gastrointestinal disturbances

The Differential Diagnosis Challenge

In Marshall syndrome, where both structural collagen defects and autonomic features may coexist, it can be difficult to determine which symptoms are cardiac (from the valve) and which are autonomic (from nervous system dysregulation):

Clinical approach: When a Marshall syndrome patient presents with palpitations, dizziness, or exercise intolerance, both cardiac and autonomic evaluations should be performed. An echocardiogram assesses the valve; a tilt table test or active standing test assesses autonomic function. Treating one without evaluating the other risks missing a treatable cause. See Autonomic Dysfunction for the full autonomic evaluation pathway.

Why This Matters in Marshall Syndrome

The connective tissue itself may be the common root. Defective collagen in valve leaflets causes MVP. Defective collagen in blood vessel walls may alter baroreceptor sensitivity and vascular compliance, contributing to autonomic dysfunction. In connective tissue disorders like Ehlers-Danlos syndrome, this dual mechanism is well-documented. Marshall syndrome likely follows the same pattern, though specific studies are lacking.

Exercise with MVP

The Marshall Paradox

Exercise is critical for Marshall syndrome patients — it activates NRF2-mediated disc protection, maintains joint mobility, builds muscle support around hypermobile joints, and supports bone density. Yet exercise also increases hemodynamic stress on the mitral valve. This creates a practical dilemma that requires clear guidance.

When Exercise Is Safe

  • MVP without regurgitation: No restrictions. Full participation in all sports and activities, including competitive athletics.
  • MVP with mild regurgitation, normal LV function, no arrhythmias: Most sports are safe. May need periodic monitoring during growth spurts.
  • MVP with symptoms but no significant regurgitation: Symptoms may be autonomic rather than cardiac. Evaluate with cardiology and consider autonomic testing before restricting activity.

When to Modify

  • MVP with moderate regurgitation: May need to avoid high-intensity isometric exercises (heavy weightlifting) and collision sports. Endurance and moderate-intensity activities usually remain safe.
  • MVP with severe regurgitation or LV dilation: Exercise prescription should be individualized by cardiology. Low-to-moderate intensity is usually permitted.
  • MVP with ventricular arrhythmias (especially exercise-triggered): Restrict competitive sports until fully evaluated. This is the subset where “arrhythmic MVP” carries the highest risk.

Practical advice for parents: Do not let cardiac screening become a reason to avoid exercise. The vast majority of children with MVP can be fully active. Get the baseline echo, get cardiology clearance, and then let them play. The benefits of exercise in Marshall syndrome — from disc protection to mental health — almost always outweigh the cardiac risk in mild MVP.

Questions for the Cardiologist

Bring these to your first cardiology appointment. They communicate that you understand the connective tissue context:

  1. “Given the COL11A1 mutation and its effect on type XI collagen, should we measure aortic root dimensions in addition to evaluating the mitral valve?” — This ensures a comprehensive connective tissue-aware echocardiogram rather than a focused MVP-only study.
  2. “Is the degree of mitral regurgitation (if any) hemodynamically significant, and what is the threshold for increasing our monitoring frequency?” — Establishes clear criteria for when ‘watchful waiting’ should shift to active management.
  3. “Are there any exercise restrictions based on today’s findings, and can we get written clearance for sports participation?” — Exercise is essential for Marshall syndrome; getting explicit clearance prevents well-meaning but unnecessary activity avoidance.
  4. “Some of the symptoms — palpitations, dizziness, fatigue — overlap between MVP and autonomic dysfunction. Would a tilt table test or Holter monitor help distinguish the source?” — Demonstrates awareness of the MVP-dysautonomia overlap and helps direct the workup toward the right cause.
  5. “How should we approach dental and surgical prophylaxis? Even though current guidelines have narrowed endocarditis prophylaxis indications, does the underlying connective tissue disorder change that calculus?” — Opens the conversation about individualized rather than guideline-default care in a rare condition.

Key Takeaways

  • Type XI collagen plays a regulatory role in cardiac valve structure; COL11A1 mutations compromise three interdependent structures — valve leaflets, chordae tendineae, and the mitral annulus — providing a strong biological rationale for cardiac screening even if prevalence data are conflicting
  • A baseline echocardiogram at diagnosis is the minimum standard — it is non-invasive, painless, and establishes a reference for all future comparisons
  • Most MVP in connective tissue disorders is mild and benign; do not let the diagnosis become a barrier to exercise
  • Aortic root measurement should be included in every echocardiogram, even though dilation is less common than in Marfan syndrome
  • Symptoms like palpitations and dizziness may be cardiac, autonomic, or both — evaluate both pathways before attributing symptoms to only one cause
  • The monitoring schedule should intensify during puberty and rapid growth, when connective tissue stress increases
  • Exercise clearance from cardiology allows confident, proactive participation in physical activity — which is one of the most protective interventions available in Marshall syndrome
