Sleep and Circadian Health

Sleep is when discs rehydrate and repair. Optimizing sleep is optimizing disc recovery.

Sleep Position

Mattress

Medium-firm mattresses are recommended. Research shows that soft mattresses increase disc loading by 49% compared to medium-firm. This is a significant and easily correctable factor.

Duration

For a child this age, 9–11 hours of sleep per night is the target. This is not just about general health — longer sleep means a longer rehydration window for the discs. Discs lose water during the day under gravitational loading and rehydrate during recumbent rest at night. More sleep = more rehydration time.

The Circadian Connection

A clock gene called BMAL1 regulates approximately 6% of the disc transcriptome (the genes that are active in disc tissue). When the circadian rhythm is disrupted — irregular sleep schedules, late nights, blue light exposure before bed — BMAL1-regulated disc maintenance genes don't function optimally.

  • Maintain consistent sleep and wake times, even on weekends
  • Limit screen time in the hour before bed (or use blue light filters)
  • Morning light exposure helps set the circadian clock

A Key Insight: Movement During Sleep Helps

Gentle position changes during sleep are actually beneficial, not harmful. Research shows that convection (movement of fluid caused by body position changes) increases nutrient transport to the disc by 4.97x compared to diffusion alone. So a child who shifts position during the night is helping their discs get nutrients, not disrupting recovery.

Practical tips: A medium-firm mattress with a knee bolster (for back sleeping) or body pillow (for side sleeping). Consistent bedtime. No screens for 30–60 minutes before bed. Don't worry about position changes during the night — they help. If the child wakes with significant morning stiffness (>30 minutes), discuss with the doctor — this may indicate an inflammatory component.
