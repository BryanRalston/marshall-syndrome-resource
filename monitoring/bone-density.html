Key Insight
In Marshall syndrome, bone quality may be compromised independently of bone quantity. Standard DEXA scans measure mineral density (quantity) but cannot assess collagen matrix integrity (quality). A normal DEXA result does not guarantee normal bone strength when the underlying collagen scaffold is abnormal. Conversely, a low DEXA result may be an artifact of skeletal geometry rather than true osteoporosis.

COL11A1 and Bone Biology

Type XI collagen, encoded by COL11A1, is present in trabecular (spongy) bone and plays a regulatory role in the cartilage-to-bone transition known as endochondral ossification. This is the process by which nearly all bones in the body form — cartilage templates are gradually replaced by mineralized bone tissue. COL11A1 mutations can affect bone biology through several mechanisms:

  • Osteoblast function: Osteoblasts (bone-building cells) deposit new bone matrix on a collagen scaffold. When the scaffold is structurally abnormal due to mutant type XI collagen, the resulting bone matrix may have altered mechanical properties even if mineral content appears adequate
  • Bone matrix quality: Bone strength depends on both mineral density and the organic matrix (predominantly collagen). A collagen disorder can produce bone that is normally mineralized but structurally weaker — analogous to rebar that is properly encased in concrete but made of a weaker alloy
  • Cartilage template abnormalities: Because bone forms from a cartilage precursor, abnormalities in the cartilage template (disorganized collagen fibrils, altered proteoglycan interactions) can propagate into the final bone structure
  • Growth plate mineralization: Type XI collagen regulates fibril diameter and spacing in growth plate cartilage. Disruption of this regulation can alter the timing and pattern of mineralization at the growth plate, affecting both bone formation rate and bone microarchitecture

The net effect is that bone density measurements alone may not capture the full picture of skeletal fragility in Marshall syndrome. Clinical fracture history and bone quality assessments provide essential complementary information.

Osteopenia vs. Osteoporosis

These terms describe a spectrum of reduced bone density, but their definitions and clinical implications differ — particularly in the context of a collagen disorder.

Category Definition (Adults) Clinical Meaning
Normal T-score ≥ −1.0 Bone density within one standard deviation of a healthy young adult
Osteopenia T-score between −1.0 and −2.5 Reduced bone density; increased fracture risk but not yet osteoporosis
Osteoporosis T-score ≤ −2.5 Significantly reduced bone density; substantially elevated fracture risk
T-scores vs. Z-scores: Getting This Right Is Critical
T-scores compare bone density to a young adult reference population and must not be used in anyone under age 20. In pediatric patients, Z-scores must be used instead — these compare bone density to age-, sex-, and (when available) ethnicity-matched peers. A Z-score ≤ −2.0 is classified as “low bone mineral density for chronological age” — not “osteoporosis.” This terminology distinction is not academic: the diagnosis of osteoporosis in children and adolescents requires both low bone density AND a clinically significant fracture history. Neither criterion alone is sufficient.
  • Fracture-based diagnosis: The International Society for Clinical Densitometry (ISCD) 2023 pediatric position defines clinically significant fractures as: ≥2 long bone fractures by age 10, ≥3 long bone fractures by age 19, or any vertebral compression fracture. Only when these fracture criteria are met and Z-score is ≤ −2.0 should the term “osteoporosis” be applied. A low Z-score without qualifying fractures is “low bone mineral density for age” — a description, not a diagnosis requiring treatment
  • Height-adjusted Z-scores: In Marshall syndrome, where short stature is common, standard Z-scores systematically overestimate bone fragility. DEXA measures areal density (g/cm²), which is inherently size-dependent — smaller bones produce lower readings even when volumetric density is normal. The ISCD mandates height Z-score adjustment or BMAD calculation in all children with short stature. Without this adjustment, a healthy short child may be misclassified as having low bone density and unnecessarily started on bisphosphonates
  • Ask your radiologist explicitly: “Were height-adjusted Z-scores or BMAD used? Was the Z-score calculated against ethnicity-matched norms?” If the DEXA report shows only T-scores or unadjusted Z-scores, request reinterpretation before any treatment decisions are made

In collagen disorders like Marshall syndrome, these numbers tell only part of the story. Bone density (measured as mineral content per area) reflects the quantity of mineral in bone. But bone quality — the integrity of the collagen matrix, the microarchitecture of trabecular networks, the distribution of mineral crystals — is equally important for fracture resistance. A person with Marshall syndrome may have a normal DEXA score yet still have increased fracture susceptibility because the collagen scaffold is structurally compromised.

DEXA Interpretation in Skeletal Dysplasia

Critical Measurement Issue
Standard DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) measures areal bone mineral density — grams of mineral per square centimeter (g/cm²). This measurement is inherently size-dependent. Smaller bones and compressed vertebrae will produce falsely low readings even if volumetric bone density is normal. In Marshall syndrome, platyspondyly (flattened vertebral bodies) directly causes this artifact at the lumbar spine, potentially leading to unnecessary treatment.

Site-Specific Considerations

DEXA Site Reliability in Marshall Syndrome Notes
Lumbar spine (L1–L4) Potentially unreliable Platyspondyly reduces vertebral height, concentrating the same bone mineral into a smaller projected area and producing falsely low areal BMD. Scoliosis can further distort the measurement
Proximal femur (hip) More reliable Less affected by the vertebral geometry issues; generally a better reflection of true bone density in patients with spinal abnormalities
Whole body (head excluded) Preferred in children Pediatric standard; provides total-body bone mineral content. Head is excluded because skull thickness varies independently of systemic bone health
Distal radius Variable Can be useful as an alternative cortical bone site but is not routinely used in pediatric assessment

Size-Adjusted BMD (BMAD) & DEXA Interpretation in Short Stature

For individuals with short stature — common in Marshall syndrome — bone mineral apparent density (BMAD) provides a more accurate assessment than standard areal BMD. BMAD estimates volumetric bone density by mathematically adjusting for bone size, reducing the artifact that makes smaller bones appear less dense than they actually are.

Standard aBMD Underestimates Density in Short Stature
Standard areal bone mineral density (aBMD) measured by DEXA divides bone mineral content by the projected bone area. Because smaller bones have a lower projected area, they produce artificially low aBMD values even when true volumetric density is normal. In Marshall syndrome, where short stature is a defining feature, this size-dependent artifact can make a child’s bones appear osteopenic when they are not. Failure to correct for body size leads to overdiagnosis and potentially unnecessary treatment.

BMAD Calculation

  • Formula: BMAD = BMC / BA1.5, where BMC is bone mineral content (grams) and BA is bone area (cm²). This partially corrects for bone size by estimating volumetric density from the two-dimensional DEXA projection
  • Some DEXA software includes automatic BMAD calculations; if not, your endocrinologist or radiologist can calculate it manually from the BMC and BA values reported on the standard DEXA printout
  • In children with short stature, BMAD may reclassify a “low” standard aBMD as normal, potentially avoiding unnecessary bisphosphonate treatment

ISCD Guidelines for Short Stature

International Society for Clinical Densitometry (ISCD) Position
The ISCD mandates that in children with short stature, bone density results MUST be adjusted using either BMAD or height-for-age Z-score correction. Reporting unadjusted Z-scores in a child with short stature is considered an interpretive error. When reviewing your child’s DEXA report, confirm that this adjustment has been applied.

Additional Interpretation Challenges in Marshall Syndrome

  • Platyspondyly complicates lumbar spine DEXA: Flattened vertebral bodies concentrate the same amount of bone mineral into a reduced projected area, producing falsely low aBMD at the lumbar spine. This artifact compounds the short-stature artifact, making lumbar spine measurements particularly unreliable in Marshall syndrome. Hip and whole-body (head excluded) sites should be prioritized
  • Use bone age, not chronological age, if skeletal maturity is delayed: If skeletal maturation lags behind chronological age (common in skeletal dysplasia), Z-scores calculated against chronological age will underestimate bone density relative to skeletal development. Request that Z-scores be recalculated using bone age when there is known skeletal delay — this requires a bone age assessment (typically a left hand and wrist X-ray)
  • Serial monitoring is more informative than single measurements: A single DEXA scan in Marshall syndrome is difficult to interpret definitively because of the overlapping artifacts from short stature, platyspondyly, and altered bone geometry. Tracking the trend over sequential measurements (performed on the same machine with the same protocol) provides far more clinically useful information than any individual value. An improving or stable trend, even at a nominally low absolute value, may not require intervention
What to Ask Your Doctor
When reviewing DEXA results, ask: “Has the BMD been adjusted for body size using BMAD or height Z-score?” and “Were bone-age-adjusted Z-scores used if skeletal maturity is delayed?” and “Is the lumbar spine result reliable given the vertebral shape abnormalities?” If the answer to any of these questions is uncertain, request BMAD calculation, bone age assessment, and prioritize the hip or whole-body results over the lumbar spine.

Peak Bone Mass: The Critical Window

Approximately 90% of peak bone mass is achieved by age 18, with the remainder accruing through the mid-20s. Peak bone mass is one of the strongest predictors of fracture risk later in life — every 10% increase in peak bone mass is estimated to reduce osteoporotic fracture risk by 50% in adulthood.

In Marshall syndrome, this critical bone-building window coincides with several challenges that can compromise bone accrual:

  • Growth plate abnormalities: The COL11A1 mutation directly impairs the endochondral ossification process through which new bone is formed during growth, potentially limiting the rate and quality of bone deposition
  • Deconditioning from pain and activity restriction: Chronic joint and back pain can reduce physical activity levels. Bone responds to mechanical loading — less weight-bearing activity means less stimulus for bone formation
  • Vitamin D deficiency risk: Reduced outdoor activity (due to pain, photosensitivity from thick glasses, or social withdrawal) can limit sun exposure. Combined with potential dietary insufficiency, this creates risk for suboptimal vitamin D levels during the years when bone accrual is most active
  • Medication effects: Some medications used to manage Marshall syndrome symptoms (particularly corticosteroids for inflammation and certain anticonvulsants for pain) can directly reduce bone density
The Central Paradox
The period when bone building matters most (childhood and adolescence) is precisely when Marshall syndrome symptoms may most limit weight-bearing activity. Recognizing this paradox early allows proactive intervention — targeted exercise programs, nutritional optimization, and vitamin D supplementation — during the years when they will have the greatest lifetime impact on skeletal health.

Optimizing Bone Density

Bone density optimization in Marshall syndrome rests on an evidence-based triad: vitamin D, calcium, and weight-bearing exercise. Each component has specific considerations in the context of a collagen XI disorder.

Vitamin D

Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption, bone mineralization, and muscle function. Deficiency is common in the general population and likely more prevalent in Marshall syndrome due to reduced outdoor activity.

Age Group Daily Recommended Intake Target 25-OH-D Level
Infants (0–12 months) 400 IU ≥20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L)
Children (1–18 years) 600–1000 IU (higher if deficient) 30–50 ng/mL (75–125 nmol/L)
Adults (>18 years) 600–2000 IU 30–50 ng/mL (75–125 nmol/L)
  • Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is preferred over D2 (ergocalciferol) for supplementation due to superior bioavailability
  • In deficiency (25-OH-D <20 ng/mL), loading doses of 2000–5000 IU daily for 6–8 weeks may be needed before transitioning to maintenance
  • Monitor levels every 6–12 months; see Endocrine & Bone Health Monitoring for the complete vitamin D monitoring protocol
  • Take vitamin D with a fat-containing meal to improve absorption
Research Update (R522): Vitamin D Optimization — Target ≥30 ng/mL, Check Annually

Updated evidence strengthens the case for proactive vitamin D management in connective tissue disorders affecting bone:

  • Target serum 25-OH-D ≥30 ng/mL (75 nmol/L): While the general population target of ≥20 ng/mL may be adequate for preventing rickets, the bone-building, immune-modulatory, and collagen-supportive functions of vitamin D operate optimally at levels ≥30 ng/mL. For children with Marshall syndrome during the critical peak bone mass accrual window (ages 8–18), maintaining levels in the 30–50 ng/mL range provides the best support for bone formation on a structurally compromised collagen scaffold
  • Annual testing is the minimum: Check 25-OH-D at least once annually, ideally at the end of winter (when levels are lowest). If the child is on supplementation, recheck 3 months after initiating or changing the dose to confirm adequacy. If levels remain below 30 ng/mL despite standard dosing, evaluate for malabsorption (common with GI involvement) or medication interactions
  • Collagen peptide supplementation may be synergistic: Preliminary evidence suggests that hydrolyzed collagen peptides (5–10 g/day in adolescents) combined with adequate vitamin D and calcium may improve bone mineral density more than vitamin D and calcium alone, possibly by providing bioavailable amino acids for collagen synthesis. While this is not yet established in Marshall syndrome specifically, the biological rationale is compelling in a collagen-deficient condition. Discuss with the treating endocrinologist or nutritionist
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7) co-supplementation: Vitamin K2 activates osteocalcin (directing calcium into bone) and matrix Gla protein (preventing arterial calcification). Co-supplementing K2 (45–180 μg/day) with vitamin D ensures that mobilized calcium is deposited in bone rather than soft tissue. K2 deficiency is common and under-tested

Calcium

  • Supplementation: If dietary intake is insufficient, calcium citrate is better absorbed than calcium carbonate, especially on an empty stomach. Split doses (≤500 mg at a time) improve absorption
  • Absorption considerations: Excessive caffeine, high sodium intake, and phosphoric acid (found in cola beverages) can increase calcium excretion. Iron supplements and calcium should be taken at different times as they compete for absorption

Weight-Bearing Exercise

Swimming Builds Muscle, Not Bone
Swimming and aquatic therapy are excellent for cardiovascular fitness, muscle strengthening, and pain management in Marshall syndrome. However, because the body is buoyant in water, swimming provides minimal mechanical loading on bone and does not stimulate bone formation the way weight-bearing activities do. A comprehensive program must include both aquatic and land-based activities.

Bone responds to mechanical loading — the mechanostat theory describes how impact and muscle forces stimulate osteoblast activity and bone formation. For individuals with Marshall syndrome, the challenge is finding activities that load bone without excessively stressing vulnerable joints and connective tissue.

  • Walking: The most accessible weight-bearing activity; daily walking (even 20–30 minutes) provides meaningful bone stimulus
  • Cycling uphill: Provides lower-limb loading with less joint impact than running; inclines increase the bone-building stimulus
  • Vibration therapy: Whole-body vibration platforms provide mechanical loading without impact; see the Vibration Therapy section below and dedicated page for details
  • Resistance training: Age-appropriate strength training with proper supervision creates muscle-mediated bone loading; focus on major muscle groups
  • Avoid: High-impact activities (jumping, running on hard surfaces) that could stress compromised joints; collision sports

Additional Factors

  • Protein: Adequate protein intake supports both collagen synthesis and bone matrix formation. Target 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day for children; 0.8–1.0 g/kg/day for adults
  • Vitamin K2 (menaquinone): Activates osteocalcin (a bone-building protein) and matrix Gla protein (prevents arterial calcification). Found in fermented foods, egg yolks, and some cheeses; supplementation at 45–180 μg/day is generally safe
  • Minimize bone-depleting habits: Excessive caffeine (>3 cups coffee/day), cola beverages (phosphoric acid), excessive alcohol, and smoking all reduce bone density through various mechanisms

Vibration Therapy for Bone

Low-magnitude, high-frequency whole-body vibration (WBV) has emerged as a promising intervention for individuals with low bone density who cannot tolerate high-impact exercise — a common scenario in Marshall syndrome.

Evidence

  • Randomized controlled trials in children with disabling conditions (cerebral palsy, muscular dystrophy) have shown improvements in trabecular bone density of 6–12% over 6–12 months of daily WBV
  • Studies in postmenopausal women show preservation of hip and spine bone density with regular WBV use
  • The mechanism is thought to involve stimulation of mesenchymal stem cells toward osteoblast differentiation (bone formation) rather than adipocyte differentiation (fat storage)

Protocol

  • Duration: 10–20 minutes daily, standing on a vibration platform
  • Frequency: 30–40 Hz (vibrations per second) at low magnitude (0.3–0.4g acceleration)
  • Positioning: Standing with slightly bent knees; can hold a railing for balance
  • Consistency: Benefits require sustained daily use; improvements reverse when therapy is discontinued
Cervical Spine Clearance Required
Before initiating whole-body vibration therapy, cervical spine stability must be confirmed through appropriate imaging. Marshall syndrome can involve cervical spine abnormalities, and transmitting vibration through an unstable cervical spine could cause injury. Obtain flexion-extension cervical X-rays or MRI clearance from the treating orthopedist or spine specialist before starting WBV.

See the dedicated Vibration Therapy page for complete protocols, equipment recommendations, and contraindications.

When to Consider Bisphosphonates

Bisphosphonates (medications that inhibit bone resorption) are the primary pharmacological treatment for pediatric osteoporosis. However, their use in connective tissue disorders requires careful consideration — particularly because a low DEXA score alone is not an indication for treatment.

Indications in Pediatric Connective Tissue Disorders

  • Recurrent low-trauma fractures: Two or more long bone fractures by age 10, or three or more by any age, occurring with minimal or no trauma (a fall from standing height or less)
  • Vertebral compression fractures: Confirmed on imaging and distinguished from baseline platyspondyly (see Fracture Risk Assessment below)
  • Progressive bone density decline: Documented worsening of Z-scores over sequential DEXA scans despite optimization of vitamin D, calcium, and weight-bearing activity
  • Significant bone pain attributed to microfractures: Particularly vertebral pain with confirmed osteoporosis
NOT Indicated for DEXA Score Alone
A low DEXA Z-score in the absence of fractures or progressive decline does not automatically warrant bisphosphonate treatment, especially in skeletal dysplasia where the DEXA measurement itself may be artifactually low due to bone geometry. Treatment decisions must integrate the complete clinical picture: fracture history, DEXA trends, size-adjusted BMD, bone quality indicators, and clinical symptoms.

Treatment Options

Medication Route Typical Protocol Considerations
Pamidronate IV infusion 1 mg/kg/day for 3 days, every 3–4 months Preferred in pediatric practice; most evidence in children with osteogenesis imperfecta; requires IV access and monitoring during infusion
Zoledronic acid IV infusion 0.05 mg/kg (max 4 mg), every 6 months Less frequent dosing; single short infusion; increasingly used as alternative to pamidronate
Alendronate Oral 5–10 mg daily or 35–70 mg weekly Less evidence in children; strict administration requirements (empty stomach, upright for 30 minutes); GI side effects can be limiting

Monitoring During Treatment

  • DEXA scan at baseline and every 12 months during treatment
  • Bone turnover markers (P1NP, CTX) to confirm medication effect (should decrease substantially)
  • Serum calcium, phosphate, and 25-OH-D at each infusion cycle or every 3–6 months
  • Dental assessment before initiating treatment (bisphosphonates can rarely cause osteonecrosis of the jaw, particularly with dental procedures)

Duration and Drug Holidays

There is no consensus on optimal treatment duration in pediatric connective tissue disorders. General principles include:

  • Treat until fracture rate normalizes and bone density stabilizes (typically 2–4 years)
  • Drug holidays (planned treatment pauses) are considered when bone density has improved and fracture rate has decreased, allowing assessment of whether gains are maintained
  • Bisphosphonates incorporate into bone and continue to exert effect for months to years after discontinuation
  • Resumption of treatment if fractures recur or bone density declines significantly during the drug holiday

Emerging Bone Therapies: Beyond Bisphosphonates

While bisphosphonates remain the primary pharmacological treatment for pediatric bone fragility, newer agents targeting bone biology through different mechanisms are entering the clinical pipeline — with particular relevance to collagen-based bone disorders.

Setrusumab (Anti-Sclerostin Antibody)

Setrusumab is a monoclonal antibody targeting sclerostin, a protein produced by osteocytes that naturally inhibits bone formation. By blocking sclerostin, setrusumab stimulates new bone formation rather than simply preventing bone resorption (which is what bisphosphonates do). This bone-building mechanism is mechanistically distinct and potentially complementary to anti-resorptive therapy.

  • FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designation (2024): Setrusumab received Breakthrough Therapy Designation from the FDA in 2024 for the treatment of osteogenesis imperfecta (OI), based on Phase 2 trial data demonstrating significant increases in bone mineral density and reductions in fracture incidence
  • 67% fracture reduction: In OI clinical trials, setrusumab demonstrated a 67% reduction in fracture incidence compared to placebo. While OI and Marshall syndrome are different conditions (different collagen genes, different inheritance patterns), both involve a defective collagen scaffold in bone. The principle that bone formation can be pharmacologically stimulated even when the collagen matrix is abnormal is directly relevant
  • Bone-building vs. anti-resorptive: Bisphosphonates work by slowing bone breakdown. Setrusumab works by accelerating bone building. In a collagen disorder where the bone matrix is qualitatively abnormal, adding new bone (even with the collagen defect) may provide mechanical benefit by increasing total bone mass and cortical thickness. Whether the quality of new bone formed under setrusumab treatment is adequate in COL11A1 disorders is unknown and would need to be studied
Relevance to Marshall Syndrome
No clinical trials of setrusumab in Marshall syndrome or COL11A1-related bone disease exist. The Breakthrough Therapy Designation is specific to OI. However, the biological rationale for bone-building therapy applies across collagen disorders affecting bone, and the OI precedent may eventually open pathways for studying anti-sclerostin therapy in other skeletal collagen conditions. Families should monitor this space and discuss with their endocrinologist or orthopedist whether emerging bone-building agents may become options as the evidence base expands.

Bisphosphonate Evidence from Osteogenesis Imperfecta: What Transfers

The strongest evidence for bisphosphonate use in pediatric collagen-based bone disorders comes from osteogenesis imperfecta (OI), where decades of clinical experience have established several principles that inform treatment decisions in Marshall syndrome:

  • Fracture reduction is consistent: Across OI studies, IV bisphosphonates (pamidronate, zoledronic acid) reduce fracture incidence by 30–50% and improve bone density by 15–25% over 2–4 years. These gains occur despite the underlying collagen defect, suggesting that increasing mineral density on a defective scaffold still provides meaningful mechanical benefit
  • Vertebral compression fracture prevention: Bisphosphonates are particularly effective at reducing vertebral compression fractures in OI — directly relevant to Marshall syndrome, where platyspondyly and vertebral fragility coexist. Even a modest increase in vertebral bone density may prevent the superimposition of compression fractures on already-flattened vertebral bodies
  • Timing matters: Earlier initiation (during active growth, when bone is being most rapidly deposited) produces better outcomes than later initiation. The window for maximum benefit aligns with peak bone mass accrual (ages 8–18)

DEXA Z-Score Treatment Thresholds in Collagen Disorders

The Z-score threshold for treatment in collagen disorders is not the same as in the general population.
In the general pediatric population, a Z-score ≤ −2.0 plus a clinically significant fracture history defines “osteoporosis.” In collagen disorders where bone quality is compromised independently of bone quantity:
  • A Z-score of −1.5 with a fracture history may already warrant treatment consideration, because the bone quality deficit means fracture risk is higher than the Z-score alone suggests
  • A Z-score of −2.0 without fractures requires careful interpretation — the low score may reflect the DEXA artifact from short stature and platyspondyly rather than true osteoporosis (see Size-Adjusted BMD above)
  • Serial DEXA trend is more informative than any single threshold: a declining trajectory, even above −2.0, warrants intervention if the decline is progressive over 2+ measurements
  • The treatment decision should integrate: Z-score trend, size-adjusted BMAD, fracture history (number, location, trauma mechanism), bone turnover markers (P1NP, CTX), and the overall clinical context of the collagen disorder

Fracture Risk Assessment

FRAX Tool Limitations

The WHO FRAX fracture risk calculator was developed for postmenopausal women and older men and has significant limitations in rare skeletal disorders:

  • Does not account for bone quality defects from collagen disorders
  • Not validated for use in children or young adults
  • Cannot incorporate skeletal dysplasia as a risk factor
  • May underestimate fracture risk by relying solely on DEXA-based BMD, which may be artifactually low or normal despite compromised bone quality

Clinical Fracture Risk Assessment

In Marshall syndrome, clinical assessment is more informative than algorithmic tools. The key question is: are fractures occurring with low-trauma mechanisms?

  • Low-trauma fracture: A fracture resulting from a fall from standing height or less, or from normal daily activities. These suggest intrinsic bone fragility
  • High-trauma fracture: A fracture from significant force (vehicular accident, fall from height, contact sports collision). These are expected in anyone and do not necessarily indicate bone fragility
  • Fracture pattern: Multiple fractures at different sites, recurrent fractures at the same site, or fractures in unusual locations (vertebrae, ribs, pelvis) in the absence of significant trauma are red flags for pathological bone fragility

Vertebral Compression Fractures vs. Platyspondyly

A Diagnostic Challenge
Marshall syndrome involves platyspondyly (generalized flattening of vertebral bodies) as a baseline feature of the skeletal dysplasia. This creates a significant diagnostic challenge: how do you distinguish a new vertebral compression fracture from the pre-existing vertebral shape abnormality? The answer requires serial imaging comparison. A baseline spinal X-ray or MRI establishes each vertebra’s starting shape. Any new height loss, endplate irregularity, or focal wedging compared to baseline suggests a compression fracture superimposed on the underlying platyspondyly.
  • Obtain baseline thoracolumbar spine imaging when bone density concerns first arise
  • Compare any new imaging to baseline to identify interval changes
  • MRI with STIR sequences can detect acute or subacute fractures (bone marrow edema) even when plain X-rays are equivocal
  • Vertebral fracture assessment (VFA) on DEXA can screen for vertebral fractures during routine bone density scans, though dedicated imaging is more sensitive
Fracture Healing in Collagen XI Disorders
When fractures do occur in Marshall syndrome, healing follows a different trajectory than in individuals with normal collagen. Understanding these differences allows proactive support of the repair process.
  • Collagen XI contributes to fracture callus organization. The callus (the initial bridge of new tissue at a fracture site) requires organized collagen fibrils to mineralize properly. Mouse models with heterozygous Col11a1 mutations (Col11a1+/-) show 23% slower callus mineralization compared to wild-type animals
  • Nutritional support during healing: Vitamin D 2000 IU/day, calcium 1200 mg/day, protein 1.5 g/kg/day, and vitamin C 500 mg/day. These targets are higher than maintenance levels because fracture repair is a metabolically demanding process that requires abundant raw materials for collagen synthesis and mineralization
  • Avoid NSAIDs during acute healing (first 2 weeks): NSAIDs inhibit COX-2, an enzyme that is specifically required for early fracture callus formation. Short-term NSAID use during the acute healing phase can delay union and reduce callus strength. Use acetaminophen for pain control during this window; reintroduce NSAIDs only after the initial callus has formed
  • Early range of motion after immobilization: Once the fracture is stable, begin gentle range-of-motion exercises promptly. In Marshall syndrome, where joint hypermobility is common, immobilized joints develop contractures faster than in the general population. Supervised early ROM prevents stiffness without compromising fracture healing
  • Healing duration is typically normal (R532): Despite the collagen defect, fracture healing duration in type XI collagenopathies is generally within normal range (6–8 weeks for most long bone fractures). The callus mineralizes more slowly but ultimately achieves union. Delayed union (failure to heal by 12 weeks) or non-union is uncommon and, if present, should prompt investigation for vitamin D deficiency, inadequate immobilization, or concurrent bisphosphonate use. Families should be reassured that fractures in Marshall syndrome heal — they may simply require more careful nutritional and activity support during the process
  • Early mobilization protocol: Begin supervised mobilization as soon as fracture stability allows — typically 2–4 weeks for upper extremity fractures and 4–6 weeks for lower extremity fractures. Physical therapy should emphasize isometric strengthening within the cast or splint when possible, progressing to active ROM immediately after immobilization removal. The goal is to minimize the deconditioning and muscle atrophy that compound hypermobility-related instability

Practical Tip
Keep a personal record of all DEXA results, including the raw BMD values (not just T- or Z-scores), the sites measured, and whether BMAD was calculated. This allows accurate comparison across different facilities and machines, and ensures that any trend — improving or worsening — is based on consistent measurements rather than differences in technique or reporting.
