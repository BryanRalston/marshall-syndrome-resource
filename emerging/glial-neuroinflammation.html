The Self-Sustaining Fire Supported

Most injuries heal. A cut closes, a bruise fades, inflammation resolves. Neuroinflammation is different. Once activated, the glial cells of the central nervous system create a self-perpetuating feedback loop that maintains inflammation even after the original trigger has been removed.

In Marshall syndrome, the original triggers are structural: cord compression from cranio-cervical instability, basilar invagination, Chiari malformation, or cervical stenosis. But removing the mechanical compression through surgical decompression — while essential — does not automatically switch off the neuroinflammatory cascade that compression initiated. The fire, once lit, burns on its own fuel.

This concept is critical for families to understand because it explains a deeply frustrating clinical reality: why some children continue to have significant pain and neurological symptoms even after imaging shows “successful” decompression. The surgery addressed the trigger. The neuroinflammation is a separate, parallel problem that must be addressed in its own right.

Key insight: Neuroinflammation is not simply “brain inflammation.” It is a specific state in which resident immune cells of the nervous system (glia) shift into a sustained activation mode that amplifies pain signaling, damages myelin, and rewires neural circuits. It is the biological basis of central sensitization.[28]

Microglia: The Brain’s Immune Cells Established

Microglia are the resident macrophages of the central nervous system. They account for roughly 10–15% of all cells in the brain and spinal cord. In their resting (surveilling) state, they continuously monitor the local environment with highly motile processes, sampling the extracellular space for signs of damage, infection, or abnormal activity.

When microglia detect a threat, they shift from their resting ramified morphology into an activated amoeboid state. This activation exists on a spectrum between two broad phenotypes:

  • M1 (pro-inflammatory/classically activated): Releases TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, prostaglandins, reactive oxygen species (ROS), and nitric oxide (NO). This state drives inflammation, damages surrounding tissue, and amplifies pain signaling. M1 microglia also release BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which has a paradoxically destructive role in pain processing (see GABA reversal below).
  • M2 (reparative/alternatively activated): Releases IL-10, TGF-β, and neurotrophic factors. This state promotes tissue repair, debris clearance, and resolution of inflammation. M2 is the “cleanup crew” phenotype.

What Activates Microglia in Marshall Syndrome Hypothesis

Children with Marshall syndrome[90] face multiple concurrent microglial activation triggers:

  • Mechanical cord compression: Direct pressure on the spinal cord from craniocervical instability, stenosis, or Chiari malformation releases damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs) from stressed neurons and oligodendrocytes
  • Complement activation: The complement cascade, already dysregulated in collagen XI disorders, generates C3a and C5a — potent microglial activators. MAC (C5b-9) deposition on neural tissue amplifies this signal (see complement-ferroptosis interaction)
  • Mast cell products: Meningeal mast cells release tryptase, histamine, and prostaglandins that directly activate microglia (see mast cell–glia crosstalk section below)
  • Ferroptosis-released DAMPs: When oligodendrocytes and neurons die via ferroptosis[75], they release HMGB1, ATP, and oxidized lipids — all potent microglial activators
  • Repeated subclinical injury: Hypermobile joints and unstable cervical segments mean the cord experiences microtrauma with normal head movement, providing a constant low-grade activation signal
Safety note: Microglial activation is not inherently pathological — it is essential for normal immune surveillance and debris clearance. The problem in Marshall syndrome is sustained, unresolved activation driven by ongoing structural instability and multiple converging inflammatory pathways. Treatments should aim to modulate, not eliminate, microglial activity.

The BDNF/KCC2 GABA Reversal Established

This mechanism is arguably the single most important concept in understanding central pain amplification in Marshall syndrome. It explains how the nervous system’s own braking system gets converted into an accelerator.

Normal GABA Signaling Established

GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid) is the brain’s primary inhibitory neurotransmitter. Under normal conditions, when GABA binds to GABA-A receptors on dorsal horn neurons in the spinal cord, chloride ions (Cl−) flow into the cell. This makes the cell interior more negative (hyperpolarization), which inhibits the neuron from firing. GABA is the braking system that prevents pain signals from being amplified.

This inhibition depends on a critical condition: the intracellular chloride concentration must be low. The protein responsible for keeping intracellular chloride low is the KCC2 (potassium-chloride cotransporter 2), which continuously pumps chloride out of neurons.

The Reversal Cascade Established

  1. Activated microglia release BDNF — Brain-derived neurotrophic factor is normally a growth and repair signal, but in this context it acts as a pathological mediator
  2. BDNF binds TrkB receptors on dorsal horn neurons in the spinal cord
  3. TrkB signaling downregulates KCC2 expression — the chloride exporter is suppressed
  4. Intracellular chloride rises because KCC2 is no longer pumping it out
  5. GABA receptor activation now DEPOLARIZES the neuron (chloride flows out instead of in, making the cell interior more positive)
  6. The inhibitory signal becomes excitatory — GABA now drives neural firing instead of suppressing it
  7. Pain signals amplify and spread to adjacent dermatomes, explaining allodynia and widespread pain
The braking system becomes an accelerator. Every pain signal that would normally be dampened by GABAergic inhibition is instead amplified. This is why children with central sensitization from neuroinflammation experience pain that seems disproportionate to imaging findings, why light touch can become agonizing (allodynia), and why pain spreads beyond the original site of injury. For more on the clinical presentation and treatment of this state, see central sensitization treatment.[28]

Astrocytes: The Amplifiers Established

Astrocytes are the most abundant glial cells in the CNS. While microglia are the “first responders,” astrocytes are the amplifiers and sustainers of neuroinflammation. Microglia and astrocytes form a bidirectional activation loop that is exceptionally difficult to break once established.

The Microglia–Astrocyte Loop Established

  • Microglia → Astrocytes: Activated microglia release TNF-α, IL-1α, and C1q, which convert resting astrocytes into reactive astrocytes (A1 phenotype). This process is called reactive astrocytosis.
  • Astrocytes → Microglia: Reactive astrocytes release CCL2 (MCP-1) and CXCL1, potent chemokines that recruit and activate additional microglia. This creates a positive feedback loop: more microglia activate more astrocytes, which recruit more microglia.

Astrocyte-Specific Damage Mechanisms Established

  • S100B release: Reactive astrocytes release S100B protein, which activates RAGE (receptor for advanced glycation end products) on neurons and microglia, further amplifying inflammation
  • Glutamate transporter dysfunction: Astrocytes normally clear glutamate from synapses via EAAT1/EAAT2 transporters. Reactive astrocytes lose this function, leading to glutamate accumulation → excitotoxicity (neurons die from overstimulation)
  • Gliotransmission: Reactive astrocytes themselves release glutamate, D-serine, and ATP as “gliotransmitters,” directly modulating synaptic transmission and amplifying pain signaling at a systems level
Clinical relevance: The astrocyte amplification loop is one reason neuroinflammation can persist for months to years after the original trigger is removed. It also explains why treatments targeting only microglia (e.g., minocycline) may have limited long-term success — the astrocyte arm of the loop can sustain itself independently.

Oligodendrocyte Vulnerability Established

Oligodendrocytes are the myelinating cells of the central nervous system. Each oligodendrocyte wraps myelin sheaths around multiple axons in the spinal cord and brain, enabling the rapid saltatory conduction that normal neurological function depends on. These cells are exceptionally vulnerable to the inflammatory environment created by activated glia.

Why Oligodendrocytes Are Selectively Destroyed Supported

  • Ferroptosis susceptibility:[75] Oligodendrocytes have the highest iron content of any cell type in the CNS (iron is required for myelin synthesis). They also have extensive lipid-rich membranes (myelin itself is ~70% lipid). High iron + high polyunsaturated lipid = the perfect substrate for iron-dependent lipid peroxidation. Oligodendrocytes are primed for ferroptosis.
  • Complement attack: MAC (C5b-9) can form directly on oligodendrocyte membranes. Given the MAC-to-ferroptosis pathway, this is a direct death signal to a cell type already primed for ferroptotic death.
  • Cytokine toxicity: TNF-α and IL-1β from activated microglia directly induce oligodendrocyte apoptosis. Oligodendrocyte precursor cells (OPCs) are also impaired by inflammatory cytokines, reducing the capacity for remyelination.
  • Glutamate excitotoxicity: Oligodendrocytes express AMPA and kainate glutamate receptors. The glutamate excess caused by astrocyte dysfunction kills them via calcium overload.
The Lhermitte’s sign connection: Lhermitte’s sign — the electric shock sensation down the spine with neck flexion — is a hallmark of demyelination. In Marshall syndrome, it is commonly attributed solely to cord compression. But the neuroinflammatory destruction of oligodendrocytes provides a parallel demyelination mechanism that can persist even after decompression. This is why Lhermitte’s sign may not resolve immediately post-surgery.

Mast Cell–Glia Crosstalk Supported

The meninges surrounding the spinal cord contain a resident population of mast cells. These meningeal mast cells are in direct anatomical proximity to the glial limitans — the outermost layer of astrocyte endfeet that forms the boundary of the CNS. This proximity enables direct biochemical communication between the peripheral immune system (mast cells) and the central immune system (glia).

Mast Cell → Glia Signaling Supported

  • Tryptase activates microglia via PAR-2 receptors: Mast cell degranulation releases tryptase, a serine protease that cleaves and activates protease-activated receptor 2 (PAR-2) on microglial membranes. PAR-2 activation triggers the NF-κB pathway, driving M1 polarization and cytokine release.
  • Histamine modulates glial activation: Histamine from mast cells acts on H1 and H4 receptors on microglia, promoting pro-inflammatory cytokine release. H3 receptors on astrocytes modulate glutamate uptake, potentially worsening excitotoxicity.
  • Prostaglandin D2: A major mast cell product, PGD2 activates DP1 and DP2 receptors on glia, promoting neuroinflammatory gene transcription.

Glia → Mast Cell Signaling Supported

  • Activated microglia release complement fragments (C3a, C5a) that trigger mast cell degranulation
  • Astrocyte-derived CCL2 recruits mast cells to sites of neuroinflammation
  • TNF-α from microglia primes mast cells for enhanced degranulation
Clinical implication: This bidirectional crosstalk means that mast cell activation syndrome (MCAS), which is common in connective tissue disorders, can directly fuel CNS neuroinflammation — and vice versa. Treating MCAS with mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn, ketotifen) may therefore have CNS benefits beyond their peripheral anti-allergic effects.

Why Surgery Alone Doesn’t Turn Off Glia Hypothesis

Surgical decompression — whether posterior fossa decompression for Chiari, cervical fusion for instability, or laminectomy for stenosis — removes the mechanical trigger that initiated neuroinflammation. This is necessary and often life-changing. But it does not address the self-sustaining glial activation that developed during the period of compression.

Why Activated Glia Persist After Decompression Supported

  • The microglia–astrocyte loop is self-sustaining: Once the bidirectional activation cycle is established, it no longer requires the original external trigger to continue
  • Epigenetic changes in microglia: Sustained activation causes epigenetic modifications (histone acetylation, DNA methylation changes) that lock microglia into an activated phenotype. These changes can persist for months to years.
  • Oligodendrocyte loss is structural: Myelin destroyed by neuroinflammation must be rebuilt by oligodendrocyte precursor cells — a slow process that is itself impaired by ongoing inflammation
  • BDNF/KCC2 changes persist: KCC2 downregulation and GABA reversal do not immediately normalize when the inciting compression is removed
  • Sensitized nociceptors maintain input: Peripheral sensitization at the surgical site provides ongoing nociceptive input that continues to drive central glial activation
This does not mean surgery fails. Decompression is essential to stop ongoing mechanical damage. But families and clinicians should understand that a period of persistent symptoms after “successful” surgery is not necessarily a sign of surgical failure — it may reflect the time required for neuroinflammation to resolve. Planning for post-operative neuroinflammation management should ideally begin before surgery.

Can Neuroinflammation Be Measured? Supported

A persistent frustration for families is that neuroinflammation is largely invisible on standard imaging. MRI shows structure, not cellular activation state. However, several approaches can provide indirect evidence of central sensitization driven by neuroinflammation.

Clinical Assessment Tools Established

  • Quantitative Sensory Testing (QST): Standardized testing of pain thresholds, cold detection, heat pain, pressure pain, and vibration. Serial QST can track whether central sensitization is worsening, stable, or improving over time. Changes in cold pain threshold and mechanical detection threshold are particularly relevant to neuroinflammation.
  • Conditioned Pain Modulation (CPM): Tests the brain’s descending inhibitory control. The patient applies a painful conditioning stimulus (e.g., cold water hand immersion) while a test stimulus is measured elsewhere. Normally, the conditioning stimulus reduces the test pain (diffuse noxious inhibitory control). When CPM is impaired or absent, it suggests loss of descending inhibition — consistent with GABA reversal and central sensitization.
  • Temporal Summation: Repeated identical stimuli (e.g., pinprick) applied at the same site. In normal subjects, pain remains constant. In central sensitization, each subsequent stimulus feels more painful (wind-up). Excessive temporal summation indicates dorsal horn hyperexcitability, consistent with glial-driven central sensitization.
  • Allodynia mapping: Systematically mapping areas where normally non-painful stimuli (light touch, clothing contact) produce pain. The spatial extent and progression of allodynia provides a clinical proxy for the degree of central sensitization.

Research Markers (Not Routinely Available) Supported

  • CSF cytokine panels: Elevated TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and CCL2 in cerebrospinal fluid directly reflect CNS neuroinflammation. Requires lumbar puncture; primarily a research tool.
  • MR spectroscopy: Can detect elevated glutamate, myo-inositol (astrocyte marker), and choline (membrane turnover/demyelination) in specific CNS regions. Non-invasive but requires specialized protocols.
  • PET imaging with TSPO ligands: Translocator protein (TSPO) is upregulated on activated microglia. PET tracers such as [11C]-PK11195 or newer second-generation TSPO ligands can visualize activated microglia in vivo. This is the closest we have to directly imaging neuroinflammation, but availability is limited to research centers.
Practical approach: For most families, the clinical tools (QST, CPM, temporal summation, allodynia mapping) are the most accessible and actionable. Pain neuroscience education (PNE) may also help children understand and manage their pain experience.[25] Ask the child’s pain specialist about baseline QST testing — serial measurements before and after interventions can provide objective evidence of whether neuroinflammation is responding to treatment.
