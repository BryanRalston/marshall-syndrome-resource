Medical Disclaimer
This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Airway and sleep management in Marshall syndrome requires individualized care from qualified specialists. The information here is based on published research and clinical guidelines but should not replace the judgment of your child’s medical team. Always discuss concerns with your child’s physicians.

Breathing and sleep problems are among the most common — and most undertreated — challenges in Marshall syndrome. The same COL11A1 mutation that affects your child’s joints, eyes, and ears also shapes the bones and soft tissue of the face, jaw, and throat. This means the airway is often smaller and more collapsible than average, creating risk from birth through adulthood.

This page is a comprehensive guide for families. It covers why airway issues happen, what to watch for, how they are diagnosed and treated, critical anesthesia safety information, practical sleep strategies, and when to seek emergency help.

Key message for families. Airway and sleep problems in Marshall syndrome are treatable. Most children do well with the right combination of monitoring and intervention. But these problems are also easy to miss — snoring, poor sleep, and daytime tiredness are often attributed to other causes when the real culprit is a partially blocked airway. The most important thing you can do is know what to look for and ask for a sleep study if you have any concerns.

Why Airway Matters in Marshall Syndrome

COL11A1 — the gene mutated in Marshall syndrome — plays a critical role in craniofacial development. Type XI collagen provides structural support to cartilage throughout the body, including the cartilage and connective tissue that form the face, jaw, palate, and throat. When this collagen is defective, several anatomic consequences follow:

  • Midface hypoplasia (flat midface) — the upper jaw and cheekbones are underdeveloped, reducing the volume of the nasal airway. Present in approximately 90% of Marshall syndrome patients, this is one of the defining facial features.
  • Retrognathia / micrognathia (small, set-back jaw) — the lower jaw is smaller than average, which pushes the tongue base backward and narrows the throat behind the tongue. Present in approximately 70% of patients.
  • Cleft palate — an opening in the roof of the mouth occurs in roughly 60% of Marshall syndrome patients, sometimes as an isolated cleft and sometimes as part of Pierre Robin sequence.
  • Pharyngeal tissue laxity — the walls of the throat themselves are made partly of collagen XI. When that collagen is abnormal, the throat is more collapsible, especially during the muscle relaxation that occurs in sleep.

Together, these features create airway narrowing at multiple levels simultaneously — the nose, the area behind the palate, and the area behind the tongue. This is why standard treatments that address only one level (such as removing tonsils) may not fully resolve the problem.

Pierre Robin Sequence

Pierre Robin sequence (PRS) is a pattern of three related problems that occur together: a very small lower jaw (micrognathia), the tongue falling back into the throat (glossoptosis), and usually a cleft palate. It occurs in a significant proportion of Stickler syndrome patients — a 2025 systematic review of 1,037 patients found that Pierre Robin sequence is present in approximately 50% of Stickler/Marshall patients, making it one of the most common associated features.

Stickler syndrome is the most common underlying cause of Pierre Robin sequence, accounting for roughly 47% of syndromic PRS cases. This means that any infant born with Pierre Robin should be evaluated for Stickler/Marshall syndrome.

How Pierre Robin Affects the Airway

In Pierre Robin sequence, the small jaw cannot support the tongue in its normal forward position. The tongue falls backward (glossoptosis) and can partially or completely block the airway. In newborns, this can be life-threatening because infants are obligate nasal breathers and have very small airways to begin with.

The severity ranges widely:

  • Mild: The baby snores and has some noisy breathing but maintains adequate oxygen levels. Managed with positioning (prone or side-lying) and close monitoring.
  • Moderate: The baby has episodes of oxygen desaturation, feeding difficulty, and poor weight gain. May require a nasopharyngeal airway (a soft tube placed through the nose into the back of the throat to bypass the obstruction) or a specialized bottle (such as a Haberman feeder).
  • Severe: The baby cannot maintain a safe airway despite positioning and non-invasive measures. May require mandibular distraction osteogenesis (a surgical procedure that gradually lengthens the jawbone) or, in critical cases, a tracheostomy.

The Good News: Most Improve With Growth

The lower jaw grows significantly during the first 2–4 years of life. As the mandible elongates, the tongue is drawn forward and the airway opens. Many children who had severe Pierre Robin as infants experience substantial improvement by age 3–4. However, this is not universal — some children continue to have meaningful airway obstruction, particularly during sleep, well into childhood and beyond. Ongoing monitoring is essential even after the infant crisis period resolves.

Sleep-Disordered Breathing

Even children who never had Pierre Robin sequence — or who “outgrew” their infant airway problems — may develop obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) later in childhood. The structural risk factors (midface hypoplasia, micrognathia, pharyngeal laxity) persist even as the jaw grows. When combined with the normal childhood peak of adenoid and tonsil size around ages 3–7, the result is a common but frequently missed problem.

Published prevalence of OSA in Stickler syndrome is only 1.6%, but this figure is almost certainly a dramatic undercount. Based on the structural risk factors and comparison with similar craniofacial conditions, the true prevalence is estimated at 20–40%. The undercount occurs because polysomnography is rarely performed unless symptoms are severe, parents may normalize snoring in a child with known facial differences, and clinicians may attribute fatigue to pain or medications rather than investigating sleep breathing.

Signs Parents Should Watch For

Many of these signs are subtle. You may not notice them unless you specifically look. Consider recording your child sleeping on a few different nights — the audio and video can be invaluable for your doctor.

Practical tip: Use your phone to record 2–3 short videos of your child sleeping on different nights. Capture the sounds (snoring, pauses, gasping) and the movement (chest, positions). These recordings are often more informative than a verbal description and can help your doctor decide whether to order a sleep study.

Diagnosis: The Sleep Study

Polysomnography (PSG) — an overnight sleep study performed in a lab — is the gold standard for diagnosing sleep-disordered breathing. It monitors brain waves, eye movements, muscle activity, heart rate, breathing effort, airflow, and blood oxygen levels throughout the night.

When to Request a Sleep Study

We recommend that every child with Marshall syndrome have at least one baseline polysomnography, regardless of symptoms. Many children with significant OSA do not snore loudly or have obvious symptoms. If a baseline study is not feasible, request one urgently if your child has any of the signs listed above.

Understanding the Results: AHI Numbers

The key number from a sleep study is the Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI) — the number of breathing pauses (apneas) plus partial breathing reductions (hypopneas) per hour of sleep.

AHI (events/hour)Severity in ChildrenWhat This Means
<1 Normal No significant sleep-disordered breathing. Rescreen if new symptoms develop or at growth milestones.
1–5 Mild OSA Even mild OSA affects sleep quality, attention, and growth. Treatment is recommended — do not dismiss as “just borderline.”
5–10 Moderate OSA Significant sleep disruption. Active treatment (surgical or PAP therapy) is clearly indicated.
>10 Severe OSA Substantial health risk. Aggressive treatment is urgent. May affect heart function if untreated long-term.

Important: In children with Marshall syndrome, even “mild” OSA (AHI 1–5) can have outsized consequences because it compounds other nighttime processes already occurring — nocturnal inflammation, autonomic instability, and compromised growth hormone secretion. Treat the whole picture, not just the number.

Home Sleep Tests vs. In-Lab Studies

Home sleep apnea tests (HSATs) are convenient but have important limitations for Marshall syndrome patients:

  • Home tests measure airflow, respiratory effort, and oxygen levels but do not include EEG (brain wave monitoring). They cannot detect sleep stage information, arousals, or central apnea events.
  • In-lab polysomnography includes full EEG, which allows detection of alpha-delta sleep intrusion (a pattern of non-restorative sleep common in connective tissue disorders) and differentiation between obstructive and central apnea events.
  • For Marshall syndrome, in-lab polysomnography is strongly preferred. The ability to distinguish central from obstructive events is critical because central apnea may indicate brainstem or cervical cord compression requiring entirely different treatment.
What to ask for on the sleep study order. Request a full attended polysomnography with extended EEG montage, differentiation of obstructive vs. central events, periodic limb movement scoring, and transcutaneous CO2 monitoring. Mention that your child has Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 collagenopathy) with craniofacial features creating structural airway risk. See our Sleep-Disordered Breathing & OSA monitoring page for detailed ordering language you can share with your physician.

Treatment Options

Treatment for sleep-disordered breathing in Marshall syndrome is typically stepwise, starting with the least invasive effective option and escalating as needed. Because the airway obstruction often occurs at multiple levels, many children need a combination of approaches.

1. Adenotonsillectomy (First-Line Surgery)

Removing the adenoids and tonsils is the standard first-line surgical treatment for pediatric OSA. In otherwise healthy children, it resolves OSA in about 80% of cases. However, in children with craniofacial conditions like Marshall syndrome, up to 40% continue to have significant OSA after adenotonsillectomy. This is because the surgery removes lymphoid tissue but does not change the underlying skeletal structure (small jaw, flat midface) or the connective tissue laxity of the throat walls.

What this means for your family:

  • Adenotonsillectomy is still worth doing as a first step — even when it does not fully resolve OSA, it usually reduces severity and improves growth, behavior, and daytime function.
  • A follow-up sleep study 6–8 weeks after surgery is essential. Do not assume the surgery was curative based on symptom improvement alone. Subclinical residual OSA is common.
  • If the post-surgery sleep study shows persistent OSA, additional treatments (below) should be pursued promptly.
  • Overnight hospital observation after surgery is recommended for all Marshall syndrome patients due to the higher risk of post-operative airway complications.

2. CPAP / BiPAP (Positive Airway Pressure)

For persistent OSA after surgery, or when surgery is not appropriate, positive airway pressure therapy uses a mask worn during sleep to deliver a continuous stream of air that splints the airway open.

  • CPAP (continuous positive airway pressure) delivers a single pressure level.
  • BiPAP (bilevel positive airway pressure) delivers different pressures for breathing in and breathing out — some children find this more comfortable.
  • APAP (auto-adjusting) automatically varies pressure based on detected obstruction — may be more comfortable for children whose obstruction varies with sleep position and sleep stage.

Practical challenges:

  • Mask fit: The flat midface and small nasal bridge in Marshall syndrome often make standard masks leak. Request a fitting session with a pediatric respiratory therapist experienced with craniofacial conditions. Nasal pillow masks, pediatric-specific masks, or custom-molded interfaces may be needed.
  • Getting used to it: Start a desensitization program 2–4 weeks before full-night use. Begin with the child wearing the mask while watching a favorite show (no air pressure). Progress to mask with airflow while watching TV, then at bedtime, then full-night use.
  • Patience: Many families struggle initially but find that with consistent desensitization and positive reinforcement, children adapt within 2–6 weeks.

3. Orthodontic Approaches

Rapid maxillary expansion (RME) is particularly relevant for Marshall syndrome. A device placed across the palate gradually widens the upper jaw, increasing nasal airway volume. A 2025 literature review found that RME reduces AHI by approximately 60–70% in children with maxillary constriction and OSA. For Marshall children with narrow palates — which is very common — this is a powerful intervention that addresses the structural root cause.

Mandibular advancement devices move the lower jaw forward during sleep, increasing the space behind the tongue. These are primarily useful for adolescents who have completed or nearly completed jaw growth.

Both orthodontic approaches should be coordinated with the craniofacial team.

4. Myofunctional Therapy

Exercises that strengthen the tongue, lip, and throat muscles can improve tongue posture and promote nasal breathing. Evidence supports approximately 50% reduction in AHI for mild-to-moderate OSA. This is particularly relevant for Marshall patients because stronger pharyngeal muscles can partially compensate for the connective tissue laxity caused by the COL11A1 mutation. Requires a trained myofunctional therapist and daily practice.

5. Positional Therapy

Sleeping on the back (supine) worsens OSA because gravity pulls the tongue backward. Sleeping on the side or stomach often significantly reduces apneic events. Positioning devices, wedge pillows, or specialized sleep shirts can help maintain a non-supine position. For infants with Pierre Robin, supervised prone positioning may be essential.

6. Weight Management

If a child with Marshall syndrome is overweight, weight reduction can meaningfully improve OSA by reducing fat deposition in the pharyngeal tissues. Note that many Marshall children are actually underweight due to feeding difficulties — this intervention applies only when overweight is present.

Anesthesia Precautions

SAFETY CRITICAL — Read This Entire Section Before Any Procedure
Children with Marshall syndrome are at elevated anesthesia risk due to multiple converging factors. Every surgical procedure, dental work under sedation, or medical imaging requiring sedation must be planned with these risks in mind. This information should be shared with every anesthesiologist who will care for your child — do not assume they are familiar with Marshall syndrome.

Why Marshall Children Are Higher Risk

Marshall syndrome creates a “perfect storm” of anesthesia challenges. Three major risk factors converge:

1. Difficult Airway

  • Retrognathia/micrognathia makes it harder to visualize the vocal cords during intubation because the jaw cannot be displaced forward as far as normal.
  • Limited mouth opening may be present due to TMJ involvement, reducing the space available for laryngoscope insertion.
  • Midface hypoplasia can make mask ventilation difficult because the flat face does not create a good seal with standard masks.
  • A large-scale study of 502 anesthetic events in Stickler syndrome found that while most patients (93%) were managed with a single intubation attempt, those with a history of Pierre Robin sequence or receding mandible had significantly higher rates of difficulty. Four cases required advanced airway techniques.

2. Cervical Spine Instability

  • Marshall syndrome can affect the cervical spine, including the critical C1–C2 (atlantoaxial) junction.
  • Neck extension during intubation — which is the standard technique — can stress an unstable cervical spine. In extreme cases, this could compress the spinal cord.
  • Children with Marshall syndrome should have cervical spine imaging reviewed before elective procedures. If there is any concern about instability, in-line cervical stabilization must be maintained during airway management.
  • See the Craniocervical Instability page for detailed information.

3. Eye Risks

  • Marshall syndrome patients are at elevated risk of retinal detachment.
  • Prone positioning, head-down (Trendelenburg) positioning, and any maneuver that significantly increases intraocular pressure should be avoided or minimized.
  • Inform the surgical team about the retinal detachment risk so positioning decisions account for it.

What to Tell the Anesthesiologist

Print or save this list and bring it to every pre-operative appointment.
  1. My child has Marshall syndrome (also known as Marshall/Stickler syndrome or Stickler type 2), a COL11A1 collagen disorder.
  2. Potentially difficult airway due to micrognathia and midface hypoplasia. Request that fiberoptic intubation equipment and video laryngoscopy be immediately available, even if direct laryngoscopy is planned first.
  3. Cervical spine precautions: Avoid excessive neck extension. Consider in-line stabilization if cervical imaging has not been recently reviewed or if any instability has been identified.
  4. Retinal detachment risk: Minimize prone and Trendelenburg positioning. Avoid maneuvers that significantly increase intraocular pressure.
  5. Post-operative airway monitoring: The airway may be more prone to swelling and obstruction after extubation. Plan for extended observation in recovery and consider overnight monitoring after procedures involving the airway.
  6. Please review the OrphanAnesthesia guidelines for Stickler syndrome (orphananesthesia.eu) for the most current expert recommendations.
Even for “minor” procedures. These precautions apply to every procedure requiring sedation or general anesthesia, including dental work, ear tube placement, MRI under sedation, and endoscopy. “Quick” procedures still require intubation or airway management, and the risks are the same regardless of procedure length. Never let a provider dismiss these concerns by saying “it’s just a short procedure.”

For detailed anesthesia guidance including medication considerations, see the dedicated Anesthesia Safety page and the Pre-Surgery Checklist.

Sleep Hygiene for Marshall Kids

Beyond treating apnea, sleep quality in Marshall syndrome is affected by pain, autonomic dysfunction, medication side effects, and anxiety. Optimizing sleep is one of the most impactful things families can do because restorative sleep drives tissue repair, growth hormone secretion, and pain threshold maintenance.

Sleep Positioning for Spinal Health

  • Side-lying with a pillow between the knees is optimal. This reduces lumbar disc pressure compared to supine, keeps the airway more open than back-sleeping, and the knee pillow prevents pelvic rotation that stresses the lower back and sacroiliac joints.
  • Cervical support pillow: A contoured pillow that maintains neutral spinal alignment reduces nighttime neck pain and decreases pain-related awakenings. Memory foam or water-based pillows allow customization.
  • Medium-firm mattress: Too soft increases spinal flexion; too firm creates pressure points on hypermobile joints. Medium-firm provides the best balance of support and pressure relief.

The 10-Hour Disc Rehydration Window

Intervertebral discs lose water during the day under the weight of gravity and regain it at night when lying down. Children with Marshall syndrome — whose discs are already compromised by the collagen defect — benefit from maximizing this nocturnal rehydration window. Aim for 10–11 hours in a horizontal position (including time settling and waking) to give the discs the maximum possible time to recover from the day’s compression.

Managing Pain That Disrupts Sleep

  • Schedule evening pain medication on a fixed schedule (not as-needed) so that peak analgesic effect coincides with sleep onset and early overnight hours. Waking up in pain to take medication fragments the most restorative sleep stages.
  • Gentle evening stretching — 5–10 minutes of light stretching before bed can reduce muscle tension and pain at sleep onset.
  • Heat therapy: A warm (not hot) heating pad or microwaveable wrap on the back or neck for 15–20 minutes before bed can ease muscle spasm and stiffness.

Melatonin: What the Evidence Says

  • Low-dose melatonin (0.5–1 mg) taken 30–60 minutes before target bedtime can help with delayed sleep onset. Note: the doses commonly sold in stores (3–10 mg) are much higher than what research supports. Higher doses are not more effective and can cause morning grogginess.
  • Melatonin is a circadian signal, not a sedative. It tells the brain “it is nighttime” rather than forcing sleep.
  • Discuss with your child’s physician before starting, especially if the child takes other medications.

Sleep Environment and Routine

  • Consistent bedtime routine: Same sequence every night (e.g., warm bath, stretching, reading, lights out). Same sleep and wake times within 30 minutes, including weekends.
  • Dark, cool room: Darkness supports melatonin production. A slightly cool room (65–68°F / 18–20°C) improves sleep quality.
  • No screens 1 hour before bed: Blue light from devices suppresses melatonin. If screens are unavoidable, use blue-light filtering glasses or device night mode.
  • Hearing aid considerations: Some children experience anxiety about removing hearing aids at bedtime because they cannot hear their environment. This is a legitimate safety concern. Consider a vibrating alarm clock, visual smoke detectors, or a bed-shaker alarm to address the underlying worry.

Emergency Situations

When breathing difficulty is an emergency. Call 911 (or your local emergency number) immediately if your child:
  • Stops breathing or has prolonged pauses (>20 seconds in an infant, >10 seconds in an older child) with color change (blue or pale lips, face, or fingertips)
  • Is struggling to breathe with significant chest retractions (the skin between or below the ribs pulls in with each breath)
  • Cannot be aroused or is unusually difficult to wake
  • Makes stridor (a high-pitched sound with each breath) that does not resolve with repositioning
  • Appears limp, floppy, or unresponsive

While Waiting for Help

  1. Position the child: For infants with Pierre Robin, place prone (on the stomach) or on the side with the chin slightly forward. For older children, sit them upright or lean them slightly forward.
  2. Open the airway: Gently tilt the head back (chin lift / head tilt) unless cervical spine injury is suspected. If there is any concern about cervical instability, use the jaw thrust maneuver instead (push the jaw forward without tilting the head).
  3. Clear the mouth: Remove any visible obstructions. Do not blindly sweep the mouth — you may push objects deeper.
  4. Stay calm and monitor: Note the time, count breaths per minute, and observe for color changes. This information is critical for the emergency team.
  5. Have your medical information ready. An emergency card or medical alert bracelet with Marshall syndrome, airway precautions, and cervical spine considerations speeds up care. See our Emergency Protocol Card.

Medical Alert Information

We recommend that all Marshall syndrome patients wear or carry medical alert identification that includes:

  • “Marshall Syndrome (COL11A1 collagenopathy)”
  • “Potentially difficult airway — request video laryngoscopy / fiberoptic intubation availability”
  • “Cervical spine precautions — avoid excessive neck extension”
  • “Retinal detachment risk — avoid increased intraocular pressure”
  • Emergency contact number and primary physician

Long-Term Monitoring: How Airway Issues Evolve

Airway and sleep problems in Marshall syndrome are not static. They change significantly across childhood, adolescence, and adulthood. Ongoing monitoring is essential because a child who was “fine” at age 5 may develop significant OSA at age 8, or a teenager who was well-controlled on CPAP may find their needs change during puberty.

Puberty: A Critical Transition

Puberty brings several changes that affect the airway:

  • Rapid growth changes facial proportions — the mandible may grow substantially, potentially improving the airway, but the midface remains relatively unchanged.
  • Hormonal changes affect muscle tone and tissue characteristics. Testosterone in boys promotes laryngeal growth and can change vocal cord and airway dimensions.
  • Weight changes during puberty can add pharyngeal fat deposition in some children, worsening OSA.
  • Sleep architecture changes — teenagers naturally shift toward later sleep and wake times (delayed circadian phase). This is biological, not laziness, but it can mask symptoms of OSA-related daytime sleepiness.

Transition to Adult Care

As your child moves into adulthood, the airway team should transition from pediatric to adult providers. Key considerations:

  • Establish with an adult sleep medicine specialist familiar with craniofacial conditions.
  • Update CPAP/BiPAP prescriptions and settings — adult machines may differ from pediatric ones.
  • Ensure the anesthesia precautions are documented in adult medical records. The risks do not go away with age.
  • Consider a dental sleep medicine evaluation for mandibular advancement devices if the patient prefers an alternative to CPAP.

For broader guidance on the pediatric-to-adult transition, see Transition to Adult Care.

Action Steps for Families

Here is a practical summary of what to do, organized by urgency:

Do Now

  1. Observe your child sleeping on 2–3 nights. Listen for snoring, pauses, gasping. Watch for restless sleep, mouth breathing, unusual positions. Record video.
  2. Request a sleep study if you observe any concerning signs — or request a baseline study regardless, especially if your child has never had one.
  3. Prepare anesthesia information for any upcoming procedures. Print the anesthesia checklist above. Share the OrphanAnesthesia Stickler syndrome guidelines with your surgical team.
  4. Get or update medical alert identification that includes airway and cervical spine precautions.

Do Soon

  1. Establish with a sleep medicine specialist familiar with pediatric craniofacial conditions.
  2. Optimize sleep environment — cervical pillow, medium-firm mattress, dark and cool room, consistent routine.
  3. Discuss orthodontic assessment with your craniofacial team, particularly regarding rapid maxillary expansion if your child has a narrow palate.

Do at Key Transitions

  1. Before any surgery: Review and share the anesthesia precautions above.
  2. At growth milestones: Consider repeat sleep studies at ages 4–5, during puberty, and whenever new symptoms develop.
  3. At the pediatric-to-adult transition: Establish adult sleep medicine care and ensure anesthesia precautions are transferred to adult records.

The bottom line. Airway and sleep problems are common in Marshall syndrome, often underdiagnosed, and highly treatable. The combination of midface hypoplasia, micrognathia, and COL11A1-related tissue laxity means these children are structurally predisposed to breathing difficulty — but with appropriate monitoring, early intervention, and careful surgical planning, most children do well. The anesthesia precautions in this page are safety-critical and should be shared with every medical provider who cares for your child. And remember: snoring is not normal in a child with Marshall syndrome. If your child snores, request a sleep study.
