Clinical Trials and Patient Registries

There are currently no active clinical trials specifically for Marshall syndrome treatment. However, registering with patient registries and staying connected to the research community significantly increases the chance of being identified for future trials.

Patient Registries

Registries are databases that collect clinical and genetic information from patients with specific conditions. They serve two purposes: advancing research and connecting patients with clinical trials as they emerge.

The NHS Stickler Syndrome Registry: A Model for Marshall

The single largest longitudinal dataset on COL11A1-related conditions comes from the NHS National Stickler Syndrome Diagnostic Service at Addenbrooke’s Hospital in Cambridge, UK. This registry contains 1,673 patients from 785 families — an extraordinary resource for a rare condition. While it is designated as a Stickler registry, many registered patients carry COL11A1 mutations that overlap the Marshall phenotype.

Key Findings from the Registry

  • Myopia progression rate: The registry has documented a mean myopia progression of approximately -0.08 diopters per year in COL11A1 patients. This is slower than some other hereditary myopia syndromes but still clinically significant over decades, and it provides the first longitudinal data on visual trajectory in this population.
  • Genotype-phenotype correlations: The large sample size has enabled the identification of genotype-phenotype patterns that smaller studies cannot detect — including which specific COL11A1 mutations are associated with more severe vitreoretinal involvement versus predominantly skeletal phenotypes.
  • Natural history data: By tracking hundreds of patients across years of follow-up, the registry provides the kind of natural history data that regulatory agencies (FDA, EMA) require before approving clinical trials. This data is a prerequisite for future therapy development.

The Case for a Marshall-Specific Natural History Study

No Marshall-specific natural history study exists. The NHS Stickler registry demonstrates what is possible when a condition is systematically tracked. A dedicated Marshall syndrome natural history study — even if small (20–50 patients) — would document the specific clinical trajectory of exon 54-region splice-site mutations, establish baselines for hearing, vision, and joint progression that future therapies could be measured against, and identify prognostic biomarkers. Organizations like NORD and the Rare Diseases Clinical Research Network (RDCRN) provide infrastructure and funding mechanisms for exactly this type of study.

How Families Can Contribute

  • If in the UK: Request referral to the NHS National Stickler Syndrome Diagnostic Service at Addenbrooke’s Hospital for genetic confirmation and registry enrollment.
  • If outside the UK: Contact the service to ask about international enrollment or data-sharing protocols. The registry team has published extensively and may accept external data contributions.
  • Advocate for a Marshall-specific study: When connecting with rare disease organizations (NORD, Global Genes, Genetic Alliance), explicitly request that Marshall syndrome natural history be prioritized as a research need. The Stickler registry demonstrates the feasibility; what is needed is funding and a lead investigator.

Why Registry Participation Matters

No dedicated Marshall syndrome patient registry exists. This is both a challenge and an opportunity. Because Marshall syndrome is ultra-rare, every documented patient adds disproportionately to the collective knowledge base. A single well-characterized case can shift understanding of the phenotypic spectrum, influence diagnostic criteria, or provide the tissue sample that enables a research breakthrough.
  • NORD patient registry enrollment: The National Organization for Rare Disorders (NORD) maintains a centralized patient registry platform at nrdrs.org that accepts patients with any rare disease, including those without a condition-specific registry. NORD registries are actively used by researchers and pharmaceutical companies to identify potential clinical trial participants and to establish natural history data — both of which are prerequisites for developing targeted therapies. Enrollment is strongly encouraged for all Marshall syndrome patients and families.
  • Biobanking of fibroblasts and blood: Consider banking biological specimens (skin fibroblasts from a punch biopsy, blood samples for DNA extraction, and if available, surgical tissue) at a certified biorepository. Fibroblasts are especially valuable because they can be cultured, expanded, and used indefinitely for functional studies of collagen production, splicing assays, and drug screening. Biobanks affiliated with academic medical centers or the NIH’s BioSend program can store these specimens for future research that may not yet exist today.
  • Longitudinal data collection: Even without active clinical trials, systematic documentation of the clinical course — hearing progression, visual changes, joint involvement timeline, surgical outcomes, medication responses — creates the natural history data that future researchers and regulatory agencies require. A detailed medical summary (see Transition to Adult Care) serves double duty as both a clinical tool and a research-ready dataset.
  • Connecting isolated cases: In ultra-rare conditions, researchers often cannot assemble large enough cohorts to power traditional studies. Patient registries solve this by aggregating cases across institutions, countries, and decades. When a therapy becomes viable for COL11A1, the researchers who develop it will look to registries first to identify eligible patients. Being enrolled means being findable.
Practical steps: (1) Register with at least two of the registries listed above (CoRDS and RARE-X are the most broadly useful). (2) Ask your geneticist about biobanking options at your institution. (3) Maintain a comprehensive, updated medical summary. (4) Set a calendar reminder to update registry information annually. These actions take minimal effort but create lasting research value.

Patient-Powered Research Networks: Families as Research Partners

The traditional model of clinical research — where families wait passively for researchers to invite them — is being replaced by patient-powered research networks (PPRNs) where families actively drive the research agenda. This shift is particularly important for ultra-rare conditions like Marshall syndrome where no pharmaceutical company is likely to sponsor research independently.

How Patient-Powered Research Works
  • PCORI-funded PPRNs: The Patient-Centered Outcomes Research Institute (PCORI) funds patient-powered research networks where patients and families help design studies, choose outcomes that matter to them, and recruit participants through their own communities. PPRNs for rare diseases have proven that patient-driven research can produce FDA-quality data even for ultra-rare conditions.
  • Prospective natural history through patient reporting: Validated patient-reported outcome (PRO) tools — completed by families at regular intervals — can substitute for expensive clinic-based natural history assessments. When enough families participate systematically, the aggregated PRO data becomes a natural history dataset that regulatory agencies accept for clinical trial design.
  • Stickler Syndrome model: The NHS Stickler Registry (1,673 patients from 785 families) demonstrates what happens when a rare condition is systematically tracked. A Marshall-specific PPRN, even starting with 20–30 families, could generate comparable data by using standardized PRO tools for hearing progression, vision changes, pain trajectories, and functional outcomes.
  • How to start: (1) Contact NORD about establishing a Marshall syndrome natural history working group; (2) register with RARE-X (rare-x.org), which provides infrastructure for patient-owned data collection at no cost to families; (3) connect with the International Stickler Syndrome Support Group to explore joint data-sharing protocols; (4) advocate with your geneticist to submit a proposal to the Rare Diseases Clinical Research Network (RDCRN) for a formal natural history study.

Rare Disease Clinical Trial Design: What Families Should Know

Traditional clinical trial designs — large randomized controlled trials with hundreds of participants — are impossible for conditions as rare as Marshall syndrome. However, the regulatory landscape has evolved to accommodate rare disease realities. Understanding these designs helps families evaluate future trial opportunities and advocate for appropriate study structures.

Trial DesignHow It WorksRelevance to Marshall Syndrome
N-of-1 crossover trials A single patient alternates between active treatment and placebo (or two treatments) across multiple periods, with rigorous measurement at each crossover. The patient serves as their own control. Ideal for testing interventions like doxycycline (MMP inhibition), LDN, or supplements where individual response may vary. Can generate statistically valid evidence from a single patient. See N-of-1 Trials for practical guidance.
Adaptive platform trials A single trial infrastructure tests multiple treatments simultaneously. Arms can be added or dropped based on interim results. Statistical models (Bayesian adaptive randomization) allocate more participants to treatments that appear to be working. A Marshall/Stickler platform trial could simultaneously evaluate doxycycline, metformin, chemical chaperones (4-PBA, TUDCA), and LDN — all using the same infrastructure, shared control group, and common outcome measures. More efficient than running four separate small trials.
Basket trials Group patients by shared molecular mechanism (e.g., all dominant-negative collagen mutations) rather than by specific diagnosis. Patients with Marshall, Stickler, and other COL11A1/COL2A1 conditions could be grouped together. Increases enrollment by broadening eligibility across the collagenopathy spectrum. A “dominant-negative collagen basket” trial for chemical chaperones could include Marshall, Stickler, osteogenesis imperfecta, and Bethlem myopathy patients.
External control arms (real-world evidence) Instead of randomizing half the participants to placebo, use natural history data from registries as the comparator. All trial participants receive active treatment. The FDA’s 2023 guidance on real-world evidence explicitly supports this for rare diseases. This is why registry participation and systematic natural history data collection are so important — today’s registry data becomes tomorrow’s control arm.
What families can do right now: (1) Participate in patient registries to build the natural history database that future trials require. (2) Maintain detailed, systematic medical records (the Symptom Tracker is designed for this). (3) Ask your geneticist about eligibility for any ongoing collagenopathy trials — even trials for related conditions (Stickler, OI, EDS) may accept COL11A1 patients in basket or platform designs. (4) Set up ClinicalTrials.gov alerts for “COL11A1,” “collagen XI,” “Stickler syndrome,” and “antisense oligonucleotide collagen.”

Gene Therapy Concept Validation

While no COL11A1-specific therapies are in trials, the following approved/advanced therapies validate the underlying approaches:

  • Vyjuvek (COL7A1, FDA-approved 2023) — proves collagen gene therapy is clinically viable
  • Exon-skipping ASOs (eteplirsen, golodirsen for DMD, FDA-approved) — proves splice-modulating ASOs can reach clinical use
  • N-of-1 ASO framework (milasen, Nature 2019) — proves custom therapies for individual patients are feasible
