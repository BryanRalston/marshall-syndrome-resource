Engineered Exosomes as Disc-Targeted Therapeutics

FUTURE RESEARCH — Important Disclaimer
This page describes emerging research into engineered exosome therapeutics. No exosome-based therapy is currently FDA-approved for disc degeneration or any collagen disorder. The approaches described here range from early preclinical to conceptual. This information is for educational purposes to help families and researchers understand the landscape of possibilities. Nothing here constitutes medical advice. Discuss all treatment options with qualified clinicians.

Exosomes — tiny membrane-bound vesicles (30–150 nm) naturally released by cells — have emerged as one of the most versatile drug delivery platforms in modern medicine. They carry molecular cargo (proteins, RNA, lipids) between cells, and researchers have learned to engineer them: loading specific therapeutic molecules, decorating their surfaces with targeting peptides, and embedding them in hydrogels for sustained release. For a condition like Marshall syndrome, where the fundamental challenge is delivering therapy to the avascular intervertebral disc, engineered exosomes offer a uniquely promising approach.

This page synthesizes the latest research (2024–2026) across nine domains, connecting each to the specific biology of COL11A1 dominant-negative disc degeneration.

Why Exosomes for Marshall Syndrome?

The intervertebral disc is the largest avascular tissue in the human body. Nutrients reach disc cells by diffusion through the cartilage endplates, and this same barrier blocks most systemically delivered drugs. This is the central delivery problem for any disc therapy: how do you get therapeutic molecules to cells that sit behind a diffusion barrier, in a low-oxygen, low-nutrient, acidic microenvironment?

Exosomes solve this problem through several unique properties:

  • Size advantage: At 30–150 nm, exosomes are small enough to diffuse through the dense extracellular matrix of the disc, reaching nucleus pulposus (NP) cells that larger drug carriers cannot access.
  • Natural uptake: Cells naturally internalize exosomes via endocytosis, receptor-mediated uptake, or direct membrane fusion — no transfection agents needed.
  • Multi-cargo capacity: A single exosome can carry miRNAs, mRNAs, proteins, and small molecules simultaneously. This means one injection could address multiple pathological cascades at once.
  • Low immunogenicity: Exosomes derived from certain sources (especially red blood cells and mesenchymal stem cells) trigger minimal immune response, critical for repeat dosing.
  • Stability in hostile environments: The lipid bilayer membrane protects cargo from degradation in the acidic, enzyme-rich disc environment.
  • Surface engineerable: Targeting peptides can be added to direct exosomes specifically to disc cells or cartilage endplate cells.
The Marshall-specific opportunity: In COL11A1 dominant-negative disease, the mutant allele produces a shortened alpha1(XI) chain that poisons collagen XI heterotrimers. Null carriers (people with one working copy and one completely silenced copy) are unaffected. This means that if you could selectively silence the mutant allele in disc cells, you would convert a Marshall patient to an asymptomatic carrier. Exosomes could deliver allele-specific antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) directly to nucleus pulposus cells to accomplish exactly this.

MSC-Derived Exosomes: The Current Standard

Mesenchymal stem cell (MSC)-derived exosomes represent the most extensively studied exosome platform for disc degeneration, with dozens of preclinical studies published in 2024–2025 alone [1,2,3].

Mechanisms of Action

MSC exosomes work through multiple synergistic pathways:

  • Anti-inflammatory: Suppress pro-inflammatory cytokines (IL-1β, TNF-α) while promoting M2 macrophage polarization
  • ECM restoration: Promote synthesis of type II collagen and aggrecan; inhibit matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs)
  • Anti-apoptotic: miRNAs like miR-21 suppress cell death pathways in NP cells
  • Autophagy enhancement: Stimulate the Akt-mTOR pathway to maintain NP cell homeostasis
  • Anti-senescence: Reverse cellular senescence markers (P53, P21) in aged NP cells
  • Antioxidant: Activate the Keap1/Nrf2 axis, reducing reactive oxygen species (ROS) production [4]

Sources and Optimization

MSC exosomes can be derived from multiple tissue sources, each with distinct properties:

Preconditioning strategies can enhance MSC exosome potency: hypoxic preconditioning (mimicking the disc's low-oxygen environment), inflammatory preconditioning with IL-1β or TNF-α, and chemical preconditioning [2,3]. These primed exosomes show enhanced anti-inflammatory and regenerative cargo compared to naïve MSC exosomes.

Marshall-specific caution: Unmodified MSC exosomes stimulate extracellular matrix production in disc cells. In a COL11A1-mutated patient, this could drive production of defective collagen XI via the dominant-negative mechanism — the same concern as with PRP therapy. MSC exosomes are more beneficial as a delivery vehicle for specific cargo (ASOs, miRNAs) than as an unmodified therapeutic for Marshall syndrome.

Clinical Milestone: EVast Bio (2025)

In March 2025, EVast Bio achieved the world’s first human application of exosome therapy for knee osteoarthritis. Their product EVA-100, using umbilical cord MSC-derived small extracellular vesicles, was administered to a 56-year-old patient with osteoarthritis. Results showed significant pain score reduction within six months, validated cartilage regeneration, and no adverse effects during 12 months of follow-up. EVA-100 is now in Phase I dose-escalation trials (NCT06431152) [5].

Surface Engineering for Disc Targeting

Unmodified exosomes injected into the disc will diffuse outward over time. Surface engineering adds targeting ligands that direct exosomes specifically to disc cells and anchor them where they are needed [6,7].

For Marshall syndrome, the ideal surface engineering would combine a collagen-binding peptide (to anchor exosomes in the disc matrix for sustained exposure) with a NP cell uptake enhancer (to ensure cargo delivery to the cells that need it). A dual-targeting approach is particularly important because the disc’s diffusion barrier means you cannot simply inject more exosomes to compensate for off-target losses.

Exosome-Delivered ASOs for COL11A1

Antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) can selectively silence a single mutant allele while leaving the healthy copy intact. This approach has been validated in four sister collagen disorders [10,11,12]:

  • COL6A1 (G293R): Optimized allele-specific siRNA silencing of dominant-negative collagen VI mutation (2024) [10]
  • COL6A3: Gapmer ASOs restored functional collagen VI protein in Ullrich muscular dystrophy cells [11]
  • COL3A1: Allele-specific siRNA knockdown in vascular Ehlers-Danlos syndrome fibroblasts [12]
  • COL1A2: ASOs selectively suppressed mutant collagen I in osteogenesis imperfecta [13]

The delivery challenge has always been: how do you get ASOs into the right cells? For COL11A1, the target cells are nucleus pulposus cells deep inside an avascular disc. Exosomes are the answer.

Loading ASOs into Exosomes

Several techniques have been validated for loading oligonucleotides into exosomes:

  • Electroporation: Electrical pulses create transient pores in the exosome membrane. Capricor Therapeutics demonstrated a scalable framework for loading siRNA and phosphorodiamidate morpholino oligomers (PMOs) into exosomes at the 2025 AAEV meeting. Both scale-up and scale-out electroporation strategies produced comparable loading efficiencies to small-volume protocols [14].
  • Extrusion: Mechanical forcing of cargo through exosome membranes, achieving higher loading but with potential membrane disruption.
  • Incubation with cholesterol-conjugated ASOs: The cholesterol moiety spontaneously inserts into the exosome lipid bilayer, carrying the ASO along. Simpler but lower efficiency.
  • Endogenous loading: Engineering the parent cell to produce the ASO or siRNA, which is naturally packaged into exosomes during biogenesis.
Locked nucleic acid (LNA) ASOs in exosomes: A key early study demonstrated that antisense locked nucleic acid (LNA)-loaded exosomes showed no cytotoxicity to chondrocytes and successfully inhibited IL-1β-mediated type II collagen degradation in inflammation-induced chondrocytes [15]. LNA modifications increase ASO binding affinity and nuclease resistance, making them ideal for the harsh disc environment.

The COL11A1-Specific Opportunity

The ASO sequences for COL11A1 exon 50 have already been computationally designed (see ASO Sequence Design). The next step is packaging these gapmer ASOs into disc-homing exosomes. The envisioned therapeutic would be:

  1. COL11A1 exon 50 gapmer ASO, designed to discriminate the mutant allele by a single nucleotide
  2. Loaded into MSC-derived or RBC-derived exosomes via electroporation
  3. Surface-decorated with cartilage affinity peptide for disc retention
  4. Injected intradiscally, possibly embedded in a slow-release hydrogel

This would achieve non-viral, allele-specific gene silencing in the one tissue that matters most — converting the patient’s NP cells from collagen XI “poisoners” to functional haploinsufficient cells.

Anti-Ferroptosis Exosome Cargo

Ferroptosis — iron-dependent cell death driven by lipid peroxidation — is one of the 8 self-amplifying cascades that drive disc degeneration in Marshall syndrome. A landmark 2025 study in Nature Communications demonstrated a direct exosome-based solution [16].

The NPPC Exosome / miR-221-3p Discovery

Nucleus pulposus progenitor cells (NPPCs) naturally protect nucleus pulposus cells from ferroptosis through exosome-dependent delivery of miR-221-3p. Researchers found that:

  • NPPC-derived exosomes with enhanced miR-221-3p expression increased COL2 (type II collagen) and GPX4 (glutathione peroxidase 4) protein levels in NP cells
  • GPX4 is the master regulator of the anti-ferroptotic defense — it converts lipid hydroperoxides to non-toxic lipid alcohols
  • Synthetically tailored NPPC exosomes with enhanced miR-221-3p expression and NP cell uptake capacity were developed
  • These exosomes were integrated into an injectable ECM-mimetic hydrogel (HACS) for sustained release
  • The combined system abolished ferroptosis-induced post-discectomy herniation recurrence in vivo

Multi-Cargo Anti-Ferroptosis Payload

Building on this discovery, a multi-cargo exosome payload could address the ferroptosis cascade comprehensively:

GPX4 mRNA GPX4 protein Direct delivery of GPX4-encoding mRNA for immediate translation into protective enzyme Conceptual
Selenium nanoparticles SelK-GPX4 axis The selenium-SelK-GPX4 axis protects NP cells against mechanical overloading-induced ferroptosis and attenuates disc senescence [17] Preclinical

The multi-cargo approach is uniquely suited to exosome delivery because it addresses the interconnected nature of the ferroptosis cascade. GPX4 upregulation alone may not suffice if the labile iron pool remains elevated and MMP-driven matrix degradation continues releasing iron from damaged matrix. Exosomes can deliver all three interventions simultaneously.

The Counter-Exosome Strategy

One of the most insidious aspects of disc degeneration is that pathological exosomes actively spread the disease. Degenerated disc cells release exosomes carrying destructive cargo that converts healthy neighboring cells into degenerated ones. This is the “exosomal amplification” problem [18,19,20]:

Pathological ExosomeCargoEffectReference
Degenerated NP cell exosomes miR-27a-3p Induces M1 macrophage polarization, amplifying inflammation in the disc [18]
Degenerated NP stem cell exosomes let-7b-5p Exacerbates annulus fibrosus cell degradation by inhibiting PI3K/AKT signaling [19]
M1 macrophage exosomes LCN2 (lipocalin-2) Activates NF-κB pathway, accelerates NP cell senescence, increases MMP13, decreases type II collagen [20]

In Marshall syndrome, this problem is amplified: the COL11A1 mutation causes earlier and more severe disc degeneration, which means pathological exosome release begins earlier and at higher volume. The cascade is self-reinforcing — degenerated cells release exosomes that degenerate neighboring cells, which release more pathological exosomes.

Therapeutic Counter-Strategies

Several approaches could neutralize pathological exosome signaling:

  1. Competitive inhibitor exosomes: Therapeutic exosomes loaded with anti-miR-27a-3p (antagomir), anti-let-7b-5p, and anti-LCN2 siRNA. These would directly neutralize the pathological cargo upon uptake by the same target cells. Conceptual
  2. Decoy exosomes: Engineered exosomes displaying surface receptors that bind and sequester pathological miRNAs before they can reach target cells — functioning as “sponges” in the extracellular space. Conceptual
  3. Exosome biogenesis inhibition: GW4869, a neutral sphingomyelinase inhibitor, blocks exosome production. In disc degeneration studies, GW4869 reversed M1 macrophage exosome-driven senescence and degeneration both in vitro and in vivo [20]. However, this is a blunt tool — it blocks all exosome production, including beneficial signaling. Preclinical
  4. Selective uptake blocking: Antibodies or peptides that block the surface receptors used by pathological exosomes to enter NP cells, while leaving therapeutic exosome uptake intact (via different surface markers). Conceptual
The dual-function exosome concept: The most elegant counter-exosome strategy for Marshall would combine offensive and defensive cargo in a single exosome: allele-specific ASOs to silence the mutant COL11A1 (offensive, addressing the root cause) plus anti-miR-27a-3p and anti-LCN2 siRNA (defensive, neutralizing the spreading degeneration signal). One injection, two functions.

Best Platform for Marshall Syndrome

For a Marshall-specific therapeutic, the most promising platforms are:

  • Near-term: MSC-derived exosomes (most clinical data for disc applications, already in OA trials)
  • Medium-term: RBC-derived exosomes loaded with COL11A1 ASOs (best scalability for chronic repeat dosing)
  • Long-term: Fully engineered exosomes with surface targeting + multi-cargo payloads (ultimate precision medicine)

Exosome-Delivered CRISPR

The ultimate version of allele-specific gene silencing is permanent gene editing. CRISPR-Cas9 can disable the mutant COL11A1 allele once and for all — no repeat dosing needed. The challenge is delivery: how do you get the bulky Cas9 protein and guide RNA into NP cells without using viral vectors? Exosomes may provide the answer [21,22,23].

Recent Advances

  • ExosomeRNP (2022–2023): Cas9 ribonucleoprotein (RNP) complexes were loaded into exosomes isolated from hepatic stellate cells via electroporation, achieving tissue-specific gene editing in liver disease models [22].
  • Modular EV-CRISPR system (2025): A Nature Communications study described a versatile approach using MS2 coat proteins fused to EV-enriched proteins, combined with UV-activated photocleavable linker domains (PhoCl), enabling controlled loading and release of Cas9 RNP within target cells [23].
  • Cell-specific CRISPR sEVs (2024): Small extracellular vesicles were engineered for cell-specific delivery of CRISPR-Cas9, demonstrating precise gene editing in cancer cells while sparing normal tissue [24].

Application to COL11A1

For Marshall syndrome, CRISPR-loaded exosomes could achieve:

  • Allele-specific knockout: Guide RNA designed to target the mutant COL11A1 allele (exploiting the exon 50 splice site mutation as a unique sequence distinguisher), permanently disabling mutant protein production
  • One-time treatment potential: Unlike ASOs (which require repeat dosing), CRISPR editing is permanent. Once the mutant allele is disabled in a NP cell, all daughter cells inherit the edit
  • No viral vector risks: Exosome delivery avoids the immunogenicity, insertional mutagenesis, and manufacturing complexity of AAV or lentiviral vectors
Important limitations: Exosome-delivered CRISPR for disc cells remains conceptual. The Cas9 RNP is large (~160 kDa protein + guide RNA), pushing the limits of exosome cargo capacity. Loading efficiency is low (typically <10%), and off-target editing risks must be carefully characterized. NP cells have low turnover, which is actually an advantage here — you need to edit relatively few cells, and those edits persist. But this approach is likely 10–15 years from clinical application.

Evidence level: Conceptual for disc (proven in liver and cancer models)

Hydrogel-Exosome Combinations for Sustained Release

A single intradiscal injection of exosomes provides a burst of therapeutic activity, but the disc’s hostile environment (acidic pH, protease-rich, low nutrients) degrades exosomes within days. For sustained benefit, exosomes need a carrier that releases them gradually over weeks to months. Injectable hydrogels solve this problem [25,26,27].

A 2025 systematic review and meta-analysis of preclinical studies confirmed that exosome-loaded hydrogels consistently increased type II collagen expression and decreased MMP13 levels across multiple animal models, demonstrating the broad validity of this combination approach [27].

For Marshall syndrome: The ideal hydrogel-exosome system would combine the HACS-type ECM-mimetic carrier with the CaCO3 acid-neutralizing approach. Marshall discs likely develop acidosis early due to accelerated degeneration. A single intradiscal injection that simultaneously (1) neutralizes acid, (2) releases anti-ferroptosis exosomes over weeks, and (3) delivers COL11A1-specific ASOs would address three pathological layers at once.

Red Blood Cell-Derived Exosomes: The Scalability Solution

The biggest practical challenge for exosome therapeutics is manufacturing at scale. MSC-derived exosomes require expensive cell expansion and produce limited yields. Red blood cell-derived extracellular vesicles (RBCEVs) solve this fundamentally [29,30].

Why RBCEVs Are Transformative

  • Unlimited supply: Type O red blood cells are available from blood banks worldwide. A single unit of blood can produce billions of exosomes.
  • No DNA contamination: Mature RBCs have no nucleus, eliminating the risk of horizontal gene transfer.
  • No MHC molecules: RBCEVs lack the surface markers that trigger immune rejection, enabling universal donor compatibility.
  • Homogeneous size: RBCEVs are consistently ~150 nm, simplifying quality control and dose standardization.
  • Proven loading: RBCEVs can be loaded with nucleic acids up to 10 kilobases in size and deliver cargo efficiently to both cultured cells and tissues in vivo [29].

Carmine Therapeutics: REGENT™ Platform

Carmine Therapeutics (Cambridge, MA) is the leading company developing RBC-derived exosome therapies. Their proprietary REGENT™ (Red blood cell Extracellular vesicle GENe Therapy) platform isolates, engineers, and manufactures RBCEVs for gene therapy applications. Their pipeline focuses on hematology, oncology, and immunology, but the platform is cargo-agnostic — it could be loaded with COL11A1-targeting ASOs or CRISPR components [30].

For a child with Marshall syndrome who may need repeat exosome injections over years, the RBCEV platform’s scalability and low immunogenicity make it the most practical long-term delivery vehicle. The key missing piece is surface engineering for disc targeting, which would need to be added to the base RBCEV platform.

Clinical Trials and Commercial Landscape

As of January 2025, 292 EV-related clinical trials were registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, including 122 observational and 170 interventional studies. MSCs are the most widely used exosome source (61% of therapeutic EV drugs) [31]. However, no exosome product has yet received full FDA approval. The regulatory pathway classifies exosomes as both drugs and biological products.

The Codiak BioSciences Lesson

Codiak BioSciences, once a leader in engineered exosome therapeutics (exoSTING for cancer), filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in March 2023. Their assets were acquired by Evox Therapeutics for $2.1 million. This cautionary tale highlights the financial risks in exosome development: the science is promising, but the path to commercialization is long and expensive. However, the technology survived — Evox integrated Codiak’s engEx-AAV™ technology into their platform [34].

No pharmaceutical drug — not even semaglutide at 8/8 cascades — can carry the specificity of engineered exosomes. Drugs work through receptor binding and systemic signaling. Exosomes deliver instructions — RNA, proteins, editing machinery — directly into the target cell’s cytoplasm. The difference is between shouting at a building from outside versus walking in and rewiring the circuits.

Development Timeline

For a child diagnosed with Marshall syndrome today, the following timeline maps when each exosome approach might become clinically accessible:

2026–2029

Near-Term: Unmodified MSC Exosomes

MSC-derived exosome therapies for osteoarthritis and disc degeneration are in Phase I trials now (EVast Bio EVA-100). If Phase II/III results are positive, compassionate use or off-label access for genetic disc disease becomes a clinical conversation. These would provide general anti-inflammatory and regenerative benefit but would NOT address the COL11A1 root cause.

Relevance to Marshall: Supportive therapy to slow degeneration while targeted approaches develop. Same dominant-negative caution applies as with PRP.

2029–2033

Medium-Term: Engineered Cargo Exosomes

COL11A1-specific ASOs loaded into disc-targeting exosomes. Requires: (1) validated ASO sequences from in vitro testing, (2) established exosome loading protocols (Capricor’s scalable electroporation, published 2025), (3) disc-targeting surface engineering. The individual components exist; integration and safety testing will take 3–7 years.

Relevance to Marshall: First disease-modifying therapy — allele-specific silencing converts patient to asymptomatic carrier phenotype in treated discs. Requires repeat dosing (every 3–6 months).

2030–2035

Medium-Term: Multi-Cargo Anti-Cascade Exosomes

Exosomes carrying anti-ferroptosis (miR-221-3p + GPX4 mRNA), anti-senescence (anti-LCN2 siRNA), and anti-MMP (siMMP13) cargo simultaneously, embedded in acid-neutralizing hydrogel. Each component is validated individually; the challenge is combinatorial safety and manufacturing.

Relevance to Marshall: Addresses 6–7 of 8 cascades in a single injection. Combined with ASO cargo, could be a comprehensive disease management platform.

2035–2040

Long-Term: CRISPR-Loaded Exosomes

Exosome-delivered Cas9 RNP targeting the mutant COL11A1 allele for permanent gene correction. The 2025 modular EV-CRISPR system (aptamer loading + UV-activated release) provides the technical foundation. Disc-specific delivery and allele-specific guide RNA design are solved problems in principle; safety and off-target characterization will require extended development.

Relevance to Marshall: One-time curative treatment for individual discs. Each treated disc permanently stops producing mutant collagen XI. Could be applied prophylactically to discs before degeneration begins.

2035+

Long-Term: Counter-Exosome + Regenerative Combination

Dual-function exosomes that simultaneously (1) neutralize pathological exosome signaling and (2) deliver allele-specific gene therapy, combined with allogeneic disc cell therapy (e.g., DiscGenics IDCT) for structural regeneration. This represents the convergence of all the approaches on this page.

Relevance to Marshall: Comprehensive cure — stop the mutation, block the cascades, replace the damaged cells, rebuild the matrix.

The pediatric advantage: Because Marshall syndrome is typically diagnosed in childhood, there is a strategic window. If degeneration-slowing therapies (metformin, semaglutide, MSC exosomes) can preserve disc health through adolescence, the child reaches adulthood as engineered exosome therapies and CRISPR approaches mature. The 10–15 year development timeline aligns with the 10–15 year growth timeline. This is why the bridge strategy matters so much.

Current Challenges and Open Questions

Manufacturing

  • Yield: MSC exosome production yields are low (typically 109–1010 particles per batch). RBCEVs offer orders of magnitude higher yields but require exogenous cargo loading.
  • Standardization: No consensus on isolation, purification, or quality control methods. The International Society for Extracellular Vesicles (ISEV) has published guidelines, but practices vary widely between labs.
  • Storage: Exosomes degrade during freeze-thaw cycles. Lyophilization and trehalose cryoprotection show promise but are not yet standardized.

Delivery to Disc

  • Intradiscal injection risks: Needle puncture of the annulus fibrosus can itself accelerate degeneration (the “needle stick” problem). Smaller gauge needles (25G+) and hydrogel sealants mitigate this.
  • Retention: Injected exosomes may leak from the disc through the needle tract. Hydrogel embedding and surface-targeting peptides address this, but retention quantification is still limited.
  • Dosing frequency: Unknown for disc applications. ASO-loaded exosomes likely require re-dosing every 3–6 months based on ASO half-life in tissues.

Safety

  • Off-target effects: Exosomes may be taken up by non-target cells. Surface engineering reduces but does not eliminate this risk.
  • Long-term effects: No long-term safety data exists for repeat exosome injections in the disc. Animal studies are encouraging but limited to months, not years.
  • CRISPR off-target editing: The most consequential concern for gene-editing exosomes. Whole-genome off-target analysis is essential before clinical application.

Regulatory

  • Classification: The FDA classifies exosome therapeutics as both drugs and biological products, requiring full IND/BLA approval pathway.
  • No approved products: As of early 2026, no exosome product has received FDA approval for any indication. The first approvals are anticipated in the 2027–2030 timeframe.
  • Unregulated market: Clinics already market “exosome injections” without FDA approval. Families should be aware that these unregulated products are not the engineered, quality-controlled exosomes described in this research.

What Families Can Do Now

  1. Preserve disc health: Use the bridge strategy (cascade-interrupting drugs) to slow degeneration while exosome technologies mature. Every year of preserved disc structure expands the therapeutic window.
  2. Monitor clinical trials: Search ClinicalTrials.gov for “exosome disc degeneration” and “extracellular vesicle osteoarthritis” regularly. The EVast Bio OA trial (NCT06431152) is the closest proxy for disc applications.
  3. Connect with researchers: Share the ASO sequence designs with exosome delivery researchers. The COL11A1-specific sequences are ready for loading experiments — what is missing is a lab willing to combine them with exosome technology.
  4. Avoid unregulated exosome injections: No currently marketed exosome product has undergone the safety and efficacy testing needed for disc applications. Wait for properly conducted clinical trials.
  5. Consider biobanking: If pursuing cell banking, bank samples that could be used for future autologous exosome production or as a reference for allele-specific ASO design.
