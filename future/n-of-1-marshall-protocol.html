Why N-of-1 Trials Matter for Marshall Syndrome

Marshall syndrome has a prevalence estimated below 1 in 1,000,000. Traditional randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are statistically impossible at that scale — you cannot recruit 200 patients for a placebo-controlled study when fewer than a few hundred people worldwide carry the diagnosis.

But that does not mean families have to wait. N-of-1 trials are rigorous single-patient studies in which each patient serves as their own control. By systematically introducing interventions one at a time and tracking objective biomarkers, a single patient can generate meaningful evidence about what works for them specifically.

  • Each patient serves as their own control — eliminating inter-patient variability entirely
  • The FDA recognizes N-of-1 evidence for rare diseases, particularly when combined with mechanistic rationale
  • Multiple N-of-1 trials across different patients can be combined into meta-analyses, building a collective evidence base
  • The approach is practical, affordable, and can be conducted by any willing physician with standard lab equipment

For methodology background and general guidance on N-of-1 trial design, see N-of-1 Trials & Building Evidence.

The Protocol Design

This protocol is structured in four sequential phases, spanning approximately 30 weeks minimum. Each phase builds on the one before it. The entire protocol is designed so that only one variable changes at a time, allowing clear attribution of any observed effect.

Phase 1: Baseline (4 weeks)

No interventions beyond current standard of care. The goal is to establish a stable baseline for all biomarkers and symptoms before any experimental intervention begins.

  • Continue all current medications and therapies without change
  • Establish baseline measurements for the full biomarker panel (see below)
  • Daily symptom diary: pain (0–10), pain location, pain quality, morning stiffness duration (minutes), fatigue (1–5), activity level (1–5)
  • Weekly questionnaires: PedsQL (quality of life), PROMIS Pain Interference, functional assessment
  • Draw baseline labs at week 0 and week 4 to establish variability range
Why 4 weeks? A single blood draw is a snapshot. Two draws 4 weeks apart establish a patient’s natural variability — the range their biomarkers fluctuate within without intervention. Any future change must exceed this natural variability to be meaningful.

Phase 2: Supplement Tier (12 weeks, staggered introduction)

Each supplement is introduced one at a time in 3-week blocks to isolate its individual effect. If a supplement causes problems, it can be stopped without confounding the assessment of others.

WeeksInterventionDoseRationale
1–3 NAC (N-acetylcysteine) 600 mg twice daily (adjust by weight with physician) Glutathione precursor; supports GPX4 defense against ferroptosis; ER stress reduction
4–6 Add Selenium 100 mcg daily (confirm dose with physician based on age/weight) Essential cofactor for GPX4 enzyme; directly supports the primary ferroptosis defense
7–9 Add Vitamin K2 (MK-7) 100 mcg daily Activates matrix Gla protein (MGP); opposes endplate calcification cascade
10–12 Add Omega-3 (EPA+DHA) 1–2 g daily (physician-guided dose) Resolvin precursors; MMP inhibition; anti-inflammatory across all cascades

Measure biomarkers at the end of each 3-week block. This creates a time series showing what changed (if anything) after each addition.

Phase 3: Assessment & Adjustment (2 weeks)

  • Compare all biomarker trends to baseline values
  • Physician review: Which supplements showed measurable effect? Which biomarkers moved?
  • Continue supplements that showed benefit; discontinue those that did not
  • Allow 2 weeks of stabilization on the finalized supplement regimen
  • Prepare for the prescription tier if physician agrees that the evidence warrants escalation

Phase 4: Prescription Tier (physician-supervised, 12+ weeks)

Each prescription drug is added sequentially with sufficient time to assess effect. This phase requires close physician supervision. All dosing decisions are the physician’s.

BlockDurationInterventionNotes
Block 1 4 weeks Metformin Start low, titrate up per physician. AMPK activator; senescence modifier; anti-inflammatory.
Block 2 4 weeks Add low-dose doxycycline (20 mg daily) If age-appropriate per physician. Sub-antimicrobial dose targets MMP activity directly.
Block 3 4 weeks Add losartan Start at low dose, titrate per physician. TGF-beta modulation; anti-fibrotic; connective tissue protection.

Each block: measure biomarkers, continue symptom diary, physician assessment at end of block.

Symptom Tracking

Daily tracking should be simple enough to sustain for 30+ weeks. A paper diary, spreadsheet, or symptom-tracking app are all acceptable. Consistency matters more than precision.

  • Morning pain (0–10 at wake-up, before any medication)
  • Evening pain (0–10 at bedtime)
  • Worst pain (0–10, peak of the day)
  • Pain location map (mark affected areas — back, hips, knees, hands, etc.)
  • Morning stiffness duration (minutes from waking until stiffness resolves)
  • Activity level (1–5 scale: 1 = bedbound, 5 = fully active)
  • Sleep quality (1–5 scale)
  • Fatigue (1–5 scale)
  • Any new symptoms (free text)
  • Medication/supplement compliance check (did you take everything today? yes/no + notes)

Safety Monitoring

Safety is the first priority. This protocol includes stop rules, interaction warnings, and mandatory physician review intervals. No intervention should continue if safety labs deteriorate.

Physician Review Schedule

  • Review all labs every 3–6 weeks (matching the biomarker draw schedule)
  • In-person or telehealth assessment at the end of each intervention block
  • Formal assessment at Phase 3 before proceeding to prescription tier

Stop Rules

  • If any safety lab (CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel) moves significantly outside normal range, stop the most recently added intervention
  • Recheck in 1–2 weeks; if values normalize, the intervention was likely the cause
  • If values do not normalize, physician should investigate further before resuming the protocol

Drug Interactions to Watch

  • Metformin + contrast dye: hold metformin 48 hours before and after any contrast imaging (CT with contrast, cardiac catheterization)
  • Doxycycline + dairy/antacids: separate by at least 2 hours; calcium, iron, and antacids reduce absorption
  • Losartan + potassium: monitor serum potassium; avoid potassium supplements or salt substitutes without physician guidance

Report Immediately

  • New muscle weakness
  • Vision changes (especially in a patient at risk of retinal detachment)
  • Severe or unusual headache
  • GI bleeding (blood in stool, dark tarry stools)
  • Allergic reaction (rash, swelling, difficulty breathing)
  • Any symptom the patient or family considers alarming — do not wait for the next scheduled visit

Statistical Analysis

Rigorous analysis transforms a personal experiment into publishable evidence. Even if a family is not planning to publish, structured analysis makes it possible to share findings with other Marshall syndrome families and physicians.

  • Trend analysis: For each biomarker, plot values over time and fit a linear regression. Is the trajectory improving, stable, or worsening?
  • Block comparison: For each intervention block, compare the pre-block mean to the during-block mean. A paired comparison within the same patient.
  • Clinically meaningful change thresholds:
    • hsCRP reduction >30%
    • MMP-3 reduction >20%
    • Pain score improvement ≥2 points on 0–10 scale
    • PedsQL improvement ≥4.5 points (established minimal clinically important difference)
    • Morning stiffness reduction ≥30%
  • Visual display: Graph each biomarker over time with vertical markers showing when each intervention was introduced. This makes the signal (or lack of signal) immediately visible.
  • Crossover consideration: For the prescription tier, consider a crossover design (4 weeks on, 4 weeks off, 4 weeks on) to strengthen evidence. If a biomarker improves on the drug, worsens off it, and improves again on rechallenge, the evidence is substantially stronger than a single on-period alone.

What to Bring to Your Doctor

A checklist for the appointment where you propose this protocol:

  1. This protocol page (printed or on your phone)
  2. The drug repurposing screen — published evidence for each proposed drug (Drug Repurposing for Marshall Syndrome)
  3. The doctor letter — a one-page summary confirming diagnosis and key clinical features (Doctor Letter)
  4. Your child’s genetic test results — the specific COL11A1 variant, with lab report
  5. Current medication list — every medication, supplement, and therapy currently in use
  6. Your opening request: “I’d like to systematically try some of these interventions and track biomarkers. Can we design a plan together?”
Framing matters. You are not telling your doctor what to prescribe. You are presenting evidence and a structured framework, and asking them to partner with you. The best N-of-1 trials are collaborative — the physician brings clinical expertise, and the family brings detailed knowledge of a condition most doctors have never seen.

Template: Letter Requesting N-of-1 Trial

A brief letter a parent can give to their physician. Adapt as needed for your specific situation.

Dear Dr. [Name], Our child has Marshall syndrome (OMIM #154780), a rare autosomal dominant collagen disorder caused by a COL11A1 mutation. With a prevalence below 1 in 1,000,000, traditional clinical trials for this condition are impractical. Recent research has identified 8 self-amplifying destructive cascades that accelerate disease progression beyond the structural collagen defect alone. A systematic drug repurposing screen has identified several existing, approved drugs and supplements that target these cascades, with published evidence for relevant mechanisms. We would like to conduct a structured N-of-1 trial under your supervision, systematically introducing one intervention at a time while monitoring a panel of biomarkers. This approach is recognized by the FDA as valid evidence for rare diseases and allows us to identify which interventions benefit our child specifically. We have prepared a detailed protocol with biomarker panels, dosing schedules, safety monitoring, and stop rules. We would welcome the opportunity to review this with you and adapt it to our child’s specific situation. Attached: Drug Repurposing Screen, Biomarker Panel, Protocol Timeline

Disclaimer

This protocol is a framework for discussion with your physician. It is not medical advice. Dosing, timing, and drug selection must be individualized by a qualified medical professional who knows your child’s complete medical history. Some interventions may not be appropriate for all patients. Never start, stop, or change medications without physician supervision.
