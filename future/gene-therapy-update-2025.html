Overview: From Theory to Reality

Gene therapy for collagen disorders has crossed a threshold. As of 2025, it is no longer a theoretical concept — it is a clinical reality for one collagen disorder, with an expanding pipeline of tools directly applicable to others. The FDA-approved gene therapy Vyjuvek (beremagene geperpavec) for COL7A1 dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa proved that collagen gene delivery works in patients. Two major 2025 reviews in the journal Gene Therapy have mapped the full landscape of gene editing approaches for collagen disorders, from CRISPR-Cas9 to base editing, prime editing, and antisense strategies.

For families affected by Marshall syndrome (caused by dominant-negative mutations in COL11A1), this matters because every tool being validated in more common collagen disorders — COL7A1, COL1A1, COL6A1 — is, in principle, transferable to COL11A1. The question is no longer whether gene therapy can treat collagen disorders, but when the delivery and precision challenges specific to Marshall syndrome's target tissues will be solved.

The central biological fact. People who carry a null mutation in COL11A1 (one copy simply does not produce protein) are clinically unaffected. Haploinsufficiency is benign. Marshall syndrome is caused by the mutant protein actively poisoning normal collagen XI trimers (dominant-negative mechanism). Therefore, any therapy capable of silencing the mutant allele while leaving the normal allele intact would convert a patient to an asymptomatic carrier. This makes the therapeutic goal simpler than for many genetic diseases: silence the bad copy, rather than deliver a replacement.

The 2025 Landscape: Proof That Collagen Gene Therapy Works

Vyjuvek: First Approved Collagen Gene Therapy

Vyjuvek (beremagene geperpavec-svdt), developed by Krystal Biotech, is the first and currently only FDA-approved gene therapy for any collagen disorder. It treats dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (DEB), a devastating skin-blistering disease caused by mutations in COL7A1 (encoding type VII collagen). Its regulatory journey and expanding approvals establish the precedent for all collagen gene therapies that follow:

MilestoneDateDetails
FDA approvalMay 2023First-ever redosable gene therapy approved by the FDA. Indicated for patients 6 months and older with DEB (both recessive and dominant forms).
EMA approvalApril 2025European Medicines Agency recommended marketing authorization for Vyjuvek as a suspension and gel formulation for treatment of DEB wounds in patients of all ages.
Label expansionSeptember 2025FDA approved updated label expanding eligibility to DEB patients from birth and allowing at-home administration — demonstrating the therapy's strong safety profile and practical usability.

How it works: Vyjuvek, developed on the Krystal Biotech platform, uses a genetically modified herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1) to deliver functional COL7A1 genes directly to wound sites. The HSV-1 vector is non-integrating (does not insert into the patient's DNA, reducing insertional mutagenesis risk) and has a large cargo capacity (~152 kb), making it capable of carrying even the largest collagen transgenes. It is applied topically to individual wounds once a week as small droplets. The HSV-1 vector delivers the normal collagen gene to skin cells, which then produce functional type VII collagen, restoring the dermal-epidermal adhesion that DEB patients lack.

With both FDA approval (2023) and EMA approval (2025), Vyjuvek is now the first-ever in vivo gene therapy for a collagen disorder approved by both major regulatory bodies. This dual approval validates the regulatory pathway for future collagen gene therapies and establishes the precedent that collagen gene delivery via viral vector can meet the safety and efficacy standards of both the FDA and EMA.

Why Vyjuvek matters for Marshall syndrome. Though COL7A1 (skin) is different from COL11A1 (cartilage, vitreous, disc, inner ear), Vyjuvek establishes five critical precedents: (1) collagen gene delivery via viral vector works in patients; (2) redosable gene therapy is feasible and FDA-approved; (3) the manufacturing pipeline for clinical-grade collagen gene therapy vectors exists; (4) the regulatory pathway for rare collagen disorders is navigable; (5) insurance coverage frameworks are being established. Each of these precedents de-risks the development of COL11A1-targeted therapies.

Two Major 2025 Review Papers

The journal Gene Therapy (Nature) published two comprehensive reviews in 2025 that together map the entire landscape of gene therapy and gene editing for collagen disorders:

  • "Gene editing for collagen disorders: current advances and future perspectives" (Modarai et al., 2025) — covers CRISPR-Cas9, base editing, prime editing, and allele-specific inactivation strategies across collagen disorders including osteogenesis imperfecta (COL1A1/COL1A2), Alport syndrome (COL4A3/COL4A4/COL4A5), and dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (COL7A1). Concludes that gene editing technologies are "safe and capable tools" with "the potential to bring forth an era of transformative genetic therapies."
  • "How far have we come? From understanding collagen disorders to advancing treatment strategies" (2025) — provides a broader treatment landscape review covering gene therapy, protein replacement, and emerging pharmacological approaches. Highlights the persistent challenge of delivery to hard-to-reach tissues (cartilage, disc, vitreous).

Key findings from these reviews with direct relevance to COL11A1:

  • AAV-delivered CRISPR for COL1A1: CRISPR-Cas9 editing has been demonstrated for COL1A1 mutations in osteogenesis imperfecta using AAV delivery to skeletal tissue — proving that CRISPR can reach collagen-producing cells in hard tissues, not just skin fibroblasts.
  • siRNA allele-specific silencing: Allele-specific silencing of COL1A1 and COL1A2 has been achieved using siRNAs designed to exploit polymorphisms (natural sequence differences between the two alleles) to selectively degrade only the mutant transcript. This demonstrates an alternative to CRISPR-based silencing that works at the RNA level and does not require permanent genome modification.

Together, these reviews confirm that collagen gene therapy research has moved decisively from proof-of-concept to optimization of delivery and precision — the exact barriers that remain for COL11A1.

Allele-Specific CRISPR: Silence the Poison

For dominant-negative collagen disorders like Marshall syndrome, allele-specific CRISPR is the most conceptually elegant approach: use a guide RNA that recognizes only the mutant allele and introduce a frameshifting edit that silences it, while the normal allele remains untouched. The patient's cells then produce only normal collagen XI from the surviving good copy — converting a dominant-negative disease to benign haploinsufficiency.

The proof-of-concept came from work on collagen VI muscular dystrophies (Bethlem myopathy and Ullrich congenital muscular dystrophy), which share the same dominant-negative pathology as Marshall syndrome:

  • In 2024, researchers used allele-targeted CRISPR-Cas9 guide RNAs to introduce inactivating frameshifting indels at a dominant-negative COL6A1 variant (c.868G>A; G290R) in patient fibroblasts. More than 40% of reads showed silencing of the mutant allele, with a recovery of the collagen VI extracellular matrix in treated cells.
  • A second 2024 study achieved allele-specific silencing of COL6A1 G293R, another dominant-negative collagen VI mutation, in patient fibroblasts using CRISPR-Cas9. The approach identified a mutation-specific sequence to distinguish the mutant allele from the wildtype, designed a guide RNA targeting only the mutant allele, and silenced it — restoring the normal collagen VI network. This is the closest existing analogue to what would be needed for COL11A1: convert a dominant-negative state to haploinsufficiency (clinically benign for haplosufficient genes like COL11A1).
  • A separate optimized study achieved selective silencing with minimal activity at the wild-type allele, confirming that allele-specific discrimination is achievable.
  • Because COL6A1, like COL11A1, is haplosufficient (losing one copy does not cause disease), the silencing approach converts a severe disease to a clinically silent carrier state.
Direct relevance to Marshall syndrome. Marshall syndrome is caused by dominant-negative COL11A1 splice-site mutations. These mutations create distinctive sequences at the DNA level (e.g., a mutant splice donor or acceptor site) that can serve as CRISPR guide RNA targets. The mutation itself provides the discriminating sequence between the mutant and normal alleles — the exact same principle used in the COL6A1 work. The strategy is directly transferable: design a guide RNA specific to the Marshall patient's COL11A1 mutation, introduce a frameshifting indel to silence that allele, and let the normal copy work alone.

Base Editing: Precision Without Breaks

Base editors chemically convert one DNA letter to another at a precisely targeted position without creating double-strand breaks. This eliminates the risk of unintended insertions, deletions, or chromosomal rearrangements — a critical safety advantage for any therapy administered to children.

  • Cytosine base editors (CBEs) convert C→T, and adenine base editors (ABEs) convert A→G, at a targeted position.
  • For many COL11A1 splice-site mutations, a single base change at the splice donor (GT) or acceptor (AG) site is the causative lesion. If an ABE or CBE can convert the mutant base back to the normal sequence, the correction is precise, permanent, and avoids the risks of standard CRISPR cuts.
  • In 2025, researchers demonstrated that porous silicon nanoneedles can efficiently deliver adenine base editor to correct a recurrent pathogenic COL7A1 variant in recessive dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa fibroblasts — showing that delivery innovations are advancing in parallel with editing precision.
  • Base editing has achieved 18% efficiency in fibroblasts for COL1A1 promoter modification, providing a benchmark for collagen gene targets.

Limitation: Base editing requires a PAM site (the short DNA sequence that Cas9 uses to dock onto DNA) near the target mutation. Not all COL11A1 mutations have a conveniently located PAM site. Newer Cas9 variants with relaxed PAM requirements (SpRY, xCas9) are expanding the targetable window.

Prime Editing: The Most Versatile Tool

Prime editing is a "search and replace" system that can make insertions, deletions, and all 12 types of point mutations without double-strand breaks. It is the most flexible editing tool available, capable of correcting virtually any mutation type — including the complex splice-site disruptions common in Marshall syndrome.

  • In 2025, a prime editing strategy corrected pathogenic COL17A1 variants that cause junctional epidermolysis bullosa (JEB), achieving up to 60% editing efficiency in patient keratinocytes and successfully restoring functional type XVII collagen protein.
  • Prime editing can correct the specific base changes, small deletions, or splice-site disruptions found across different Marshall families — each patient's mutation could theoretically receive a personalized prime edit.
  • The trade-off is lower efficiency compared to base editing in most cell types tested so far, and the prime editing machinery (the pegRNA and the prime editor protein) is larger, making delivery via AAV more challenging.
The PASTE platform. A newer technology called PASTE (Programmable Addition via Site-specific Targeting Elements) combines prime editing with serine integrases to allow insertion of large collagen genes. PASTE has been demonstrated for COL4A3 and COL4A4 (Alport syndrome genes) while eliminating off-target effects. Since the COL11A1 coding sequence (~5.4 kb) approaches the packaging limit of standard AAV vectors, PASTE may offer an alternative route to full-gene correction that avoids the dual-vector problem.

Antisense Oligonucleotides (ASOs): Splicing Correction at the RNA Level

Antisense oligonucleotides represent a fundamentally different approach from gene editing: they work at the mRNA level rather than the DNA level, correcting or bypassing the consequences of mutations without permanently altering the genome. For Marshall syndrome's characteristic splice-site mutations, ASOs have particular appeal.

  • Exon skipping: ASOs can bind to pre-mRNA near a mutant exon and force the splicing machinery to skip it entirely. If the skipped exon maintains the reading frame, the result is a shorter but potentially functional collagen XI protein. This approach has restored collagen production in multiple collagen disorders:
    • In COL7A1 (DEB), AONs targeting exon 105 induced exon skipping at the RNA level and restored type VII collagen production both in vitro and in vivo in mouse skin grafts
    • In COL6A1 (Ullrich CMD), exon-skipping ASOs restored functional collagen VI
    • In COL17A1 (JEB), personalized ASOs induced skipping of exon 7 and restored type XVII collagen expression
  • Splice-switching: ASOs can also redirect splicing to correct aberrant splicing caused by mutations — rather than skipping an entire exon, they can restore normal exon inclusion or exclude a cryptic exon activated by the mutation.
  • Clinical precedent: ASO therapies are already FDA-approved for other genetic diseases — nusinersen (Spinraza) for spinal muscular atrophy and eteplirsen for Duchenne muscular dystrophy — establishing the regulatory pathway and safety track record.
  • Advantages: reversible (effects wear off when dosing stops), no permanent DNA changes, established manufacturing pipelines, and potential for personalized design for each family's specific mutation.
  • Trade-off: requires repeated dosing (typically every 1–6 months) rather than a one-time correction.

Relevance to Marshall syndrome: Most Marshall syndrome mutations are splice-site mutations that cause exon skipping, leading to dominant-negative protein. ASOs could potentially be designed to (1) suppress the aberrantly spliced mutant transcript, or (2) redirect splicing to skip the mutant exon in a way that maintains reading frame. The splice-site mutation pattern is well-characterized and creates predictable target sequences for ASO design.

Delivery Challenges Specific to Marshall Syndrome

The largest unsolved problem in applying gene therapy to Marshall syndrome is not the editing technology — it is getting the therapeutic payload to the right cells. COL11A1 is expressed in multiple tissues, several of which are among the hardest in the body to reach with viral vectors or other delivery vehicles.

The Avascular Problem

Cartilage, intervertebral discs, and the vitreous humor are all avascular — they have no blood supply. This means systemically administered therapies (intravenous injection) cannot reliably reach these tissues. Each target requires a specialized local delivery approach, and a single gene therapy administration route cannot treat all Marshall-affected tissues simultaneously.

This multi-tissue challenge means that Marshall syndrome gene therapy will likely require a staged, tissue-specific approach: intravitreal for the eye first (most accessible), followed by inner ear delivery, and eventually intradiscal/intra-articular for cartilage and spine.

Local vs. Systemic Delivery

  • Local delivery (intravitreal, intratympanic, intradiscal, intra-articular): higher concentration at the target tissue, lower systemic exposure, reduced immune response risk. But requires specialized procedures and cannot treat all tissues at once.
  • Systemic delivery (IV, subcutaneous): could theoretically reach all COL11A1-expressing tissues simultaneously, but current vectors do not reliably transduce avascular tissues after systemic administration. Lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) and engineered AAV capsids are being developed to improve tissue targeting.
  • Immune concerns: AAV vectors trigger neutralizing antibodies after first exposure, potentially preventing re-dosing with the same serotype. This makes getting the first dose right critically important, and favors redosable platforms (ASOs, siRNA, mRNA) for tissues requiring ongoing treatment.
2025 advance in cartilage-targeted AAV. Researchers have developed rationally engineered novel AAV capsids specifically designed for intra-articular gene delivery. These modified capsids show improved cartilage penetration and chondrocyte transduction compared to natural serotypes. Clinical trials evaluating AAV-mediated gene therapy for osteoarthritis (ICM-203, encoding the transcription factor Nkx3.2) are underway, providing safety and efficacy data for intra-articular AAV delivery that will directly inform future collagen disorder applications.

The Ex Vivo Alternative: Sidestep Delivery Entirely

The in vivo delivery challenges above can be entirely avoided through ex vivo gene correction: rather than delivering editing tools into the patient's body, the patient's own cells are corrected in the laboratory and then transplanted back. This approach is particularly attractive for Marshall syndrome because every step has been independently demonstrated:

  1. Bank the patient's cells (fibroblasts from skin biopsy, PBMCs from blood, dental pulp stem cells from baby teeth) — see Cell Banking
  2. Reprogram to iPSCs (induced pluripotent stem cells) — established technology
  3. CRISPR-correct the COL11A1 mutation in vitro — where efficiency can be optimized and off-target effects fully characterized before any cells go back into the patient
  4. Differentiate corrected iPSCs into nucleus pulposus cells, chondrocytes, or other target cell types
  5. Transplant corrected cells into degenerated discs or damaged cartilage

The ex vivo approach eliminates the need for in vivo viral vector delivery, allows complete quality control of the gene correction, and produces autologous (self-derived) cells that avoid immune rejection. The timeline for this pipeline overlaps with the regenerative medicine approaches described on the Regenerative Medicine Roadmap page.

The Minigene Assay Breakthrough (2025)

A 2025 publication in Frontiers in Genetics represents a critical advance in the infrastructure needed for personalized gene therapy: the use of minigene splicing assays to verify the pathogenicity of novel COL11A1 splice variants.

What happened: Researchers identified a novel de novo COL11A1 splicing variant (c.4069-1G>T) in a patient with Stickler syndrome (Frontiers in Genetics, 2025). To confirm that this variant actually caused disease (rather than being a benign polymorphism), they constructed a minigene vector containing COL11A1 exons 53–55 and performed an in vitro splicing assay. The assay confirmed that the variant resulted in abnormal splicing — specifically, exon 54 skipping and 21-bp intron retention from intron 53 — definitively establishing pathogenicity.

Why this matters for gene therapy:

  • Variant classification: Many COL11A1 variants are classified as "variants of uncertain significance" (VUS). Minigene assays provide a functional readout that can reclassify VUS as pathogenic or benign — essential for determining which patients are candidates for allele-specific therapies.
  • Therapy design: The minigene assay reveals the exact splicing consequence of each mutation (which exon is skipped, what aberrant product is produced). This information is required to design allele-specific CRISPR guides, ASOs, or base editing strategies tailored to each family's specific mutation.
  • Personalized medicine infrastructure: As gene therapy moves toward clinical application, every patient will need their specific mutation functionally characterized. The minigene assay methodology demonstrated in 2025 provides a scalable, reproducible tool for this purpose.
  • Research acceleration: By enabling functional confirmation of novel variants, minigene assays expand the pool of confirmed pathogenic COL11A1 mutations available for gene therapy development, potentially enabling researchers to design therapies that cover clusters of similar splice-site mutations rather than requiring entirely unique approaches for every family.
  • ASO design enablement: This type of functional splicing characterization is essential for designing splice-correcting antisense oligonucleotides. Knowing the exact exon skipping pattern and intron retention product tells ASO designers precisely where to target the pre-mRNA to redirect splicing. The 2025 c.4069-1G>T study demonstrates that the tools for validating COL11A1 splice mutations are mature and reproducible.

Strategic Insight: The Dominant-Negative Advantage

Why Marshall syndrome is SIMPLER to treat than many genetic diseases. Marshall syndrome is caused by a shortened-but-translated COL11A1 protein that poisons heterotrimers (dominant-negative mechanism). Crucially, null carriers — people who simply do not produce protein from one COL11A1 allele — are clinically unaffected. Haploinsufficiency is proven to be benign. Therefore, silencing the mutant allele alone converts the disease to an asymptomatic carrier state. This is fundamentally simpler than gene replacement, because there is no need to deliver a full-length COL11A1 transgene (~5.4 kb coding sequence). You only need to shut down the bad copy.

This dominant-negative-to-haploinsufficiency conversion strategy has now been proven in four sister collagen disorders:

The bottleneck is exclusively delivery. The science of allele-specific silencing for dominant-negative collagen mutations is proven. What remains unsolved is getting the silencing payload to the avascular tissues where COL11A1 is expressed: cartilage, intervertebral disc, vitreous humor, and inner ear. The delivery challenge, not the gene editing science, is the rate-limiting step for Marshall syndrome gene therapy.

What Families Can Do Today

Gene therapy for COL11A1 is years away, but the actions families take now directly determine how quickly they can access it when it arrives. Every item on this list is actionable today.

1. Bank Cells Now

Cell banking is the single most impactful preparatory action. Banked cells become the starting material for ex vivo gene correction, iPSC generation, and personalized therapy testing. Younger cells have longer telomeres, fewer somatic mutations, a younger epigenetic profile, and higher iPSC reprogramming efficiency. A 6-year-old's fibroblasts will outperform the same person's cells banked at 20.

See the Cell Banking & Biobanking page for detailed guidance on what to bank, how, and where.

2. Get Exact Genetic Characterization

Allele-specific therapies require knowing the exact COL11A1 variant — the precise genomic position, the specific base change, and the resulting splice consequence. Without this, no personalized guide RNA, ASO, or base editor can be designed. Ensure you have the complete clinical genetic testing report, not just a summary. Request the variant in HGVS nomenclature (e.g., NM_080629.3:c.4069-1G>T).

If your variant is classified as VUS, the minigene assay approach described above may be relevant — discuss with your geneticist whether functional characterization is warranted.

3. Contribute to Registries and Natural History Studies

Gene therapy development requires genotype-phenotype data: understanding which mutations cause which symptoms, how the disease progresses, and what biomarkers track severity. This data comes from patient registries and natural history studies. The more families participate, the stronger the case for developing COL11A1-specific therapies.

  • ClinicalTrials.gov — register for alerts on collagen gene therapy, disc regeneration, and COL11A1 studies
  • NORD Patient Registry — National Organization for Rare Disorders
  • GeneMatcher — connects patients with researchers studying the same gene
  • Stickler Involved People — patient advocacy and registry for the COL11A1/Stickler spectrum

See the Clinical Trials & Registries page for more details.

4. Consider iPSC Banking

Beyond simple cell cryopreservation, some families may want to consider having fibroblasts reprogrammed into iPSCs now. iPSC lines are a more versatile starting material for future therapies because they can be differentiated into any cell type (chondrocytes, disc cells, retinal cells, inner ear cells). The cost is higher than simple cryopreservation, but the flexibility is substantially greater.

5. Stay Connected with Research

  • Follow publications from groups working on collagen gene therapy (the labs publishing on COL6A1, COL7A1, and COL1A1 editing are the most likely to extend their work to COL11A1)
  • Connect with rare disease advocacy organizations that lobby for research funding and regulatory reform — see Rare Disease Advocacy
  • Participate in any natural history studies for Marshall or Stickler spectrum disorders — this data is required for clinical trial design

6. Protect Existing Tissues

Every year of preserved disc integrity, every degree of retained hearing, every year without retinal detachment is a year closer to gene therapy becoming available. The interventions described throughout this site — exercise, nutrition, monitoring, pain management — are not merely symptomatic treatment. They are time-buying strategies that preserve the tissues gene therapy will eventually target. Gene therapy can only work if there are viable cells remaining to treat.

The bottom line. The gene therapy tools applicable to COL11A1 are advancing rapidly — but they are arriving through work on related collagen disorders, not through Marshall-specific programs. The 2025 landscape shows a clear trajectory: allele-specific CRISPR silencing, base editing, prime editing, and ASO exon-skipping all have demonstrated proof-of-concept for collagen disorders. Delivery to the hardest Marshall-affected tissues (disc, cartilage) remains the major barrier, but inner ear and retinal delivery routes are being actively developed. What families can control is their own readiness: cells banked, mutations characterized, registries joined, and tissues preserved. The race is between disease progression and therapeutic development — and every action that slows the first or accelerates the second matters.
