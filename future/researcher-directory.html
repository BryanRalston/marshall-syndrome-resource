Researcher & Lab Directory

This directory maps the researchers, laboratories, and organizations whose work is most relevant to Marshall syndrome — whether they study COL11A1 directly, work on allele-specific silencing of dominant collagen mutations, develop gene therapy delivery platforms, or pursue regenerative approaches for cartilage and disc tissue. Not every group listed here knows Marshall syndrome exists. That is part of the opportunity: connecting families with scientists whose tools could change the trajectory of this disease.

How to Use This Directory

This is a starting point, not a comprehensive database. The researchers and labs listed here were identified through published literature, institutional websites, and public clinical trial registries. Inclusion does not imply endorsement, and contact information should always be obtained through official institutional channels.
  • Not all researchers listed are working on Marshall syndrome specifically. Many work on related conditions — collagen VI muscular dystrophies, osteogenesis imperfecta, Stickler syndrome, inherited hearing loss — whose science is directly transferable to COL11A1 dominant-negative mutations.
  • Contact via published email, institutional websites, or at rare disease conferences. See the How to Reach Out to a Researcher section below for practical guidance.
  • This directory will evolve. If you know of a researcher or lab that should be listed here, please let us know so we can keep this resource current.

Dominant-Negative Collagen Silencing Labs

The therapeutic strategy for Marshall syndrome is not to fix the mutant gene, but to silence the mutant allele so only the normal copy produces protein. Haploinsufficiency of COL11A1 is benign — the disease is caused entirely by the dominant-negative protein poisoning collagen XI trimers. The following labs have demonstrated allele-specific silencing for related dominant collagen disorders, and their approaches are directly transferable in principle to COL11A1.

Lab / Lead Researchers Institution Approach Key Publication(s)
Haiyan Zhou, Francesco Muntoni Dubowitz Neuromuscular Centre, Great Ormond Street Institute of Child Health, University College London, UK Allele-specific siRNA and gapmer ASOs for COL6A1 and COL6A3 COL6A1 G293R siRNA (2024): Designed 19 siRNAs tiling across the mutant COL6A1 transcript; achieved >80% allele-specific silencing of the dominant-negative G293R variant while preserving normal allele expression. Published in Molecular Therapy — Nucleic Acids.

COL6A3 gapmer ASOs (2017): Selectively suppressed mutant COL6A3 transcripts using gapmer antisense oligonucleotides, restoring functional collagen VI protein deposition. Published in Molecular Therapy — Nucleic Acids.

Gapmer design optimization (2024): Published strategies to improve gapmer ASO design for allele-specific silencing of single-nucleotide variants.

Why relevant: The UCL group is the world leader in allele-specific silencing of dominant collagen mutations. Their siRNA and ASO platforms could be adapted for COL11A1 splice-site mutations, which create distinctive junction sequences in the mutant mRNA that serve as targeting sites.
Cecilia Jiménez-Mallebrera Laboratory of Translational Research in Children’s Neuromuscular Diseases, Institut de Recerca Sant Joan de Déu, Hospital Sant Joan de Déu, Barcelona, Spain CRISPR/Cas9 allele-specific disruption of dominant COL6A1 mutations COL6A1 CRISPR editing (2022): Used CRISPR/Cas9 to specifically disrupt the mutant COL6A1 c.877G>A allele in patient fibroblasts. Reduced mutant allele presence from 50% to ~10% while leaving the wild-type allele intact. Improved collagen VI secretion and extracellular matrix organization. Published in International Journal of Molecular Sciences.

Col6a1 knock-in mouse model (2025): Generated the first preclinical mouse model for collagen VI-related dystrophies using a knock-in of the equivalent human mutation.

Why relevant: Demonstrates that CRISPR can achieve allele-specific disruption of a dominant collagen mutation at the DNA level — a one-time correction rather than ongoing RNA silencing. The same principle applies to COL11A1.
COL1A1/COL1A2 ASO Pioneers Multiple institutions (osteogenesis imperfecta research community) Antisense oligodeoxyribonucleotides (ODNs) for dominant-negative collagen I mutations Early work demonstrated selective 37–43% suppression of mutant COL1A2 transcripts with only 20% reduction of normal mRNA, establishing the proof-of-concept that antisense technology can convert a dominant-negative collagen mutation into a functionally null (and clinically mild) genotype.

Why relevant: This was the original demonstration that allele-specific silencing works for dominant collagen disorders — the conceptual foundation on which all subsequent COL6A and potentially COL11A1 work builds.

Gene Therapy Delivery Labs

Even the best silencing strategy is useless without a way to deliver it to the right tissue. The following groups are developing delivery platforms that could carry COL11A1-targeted therapies to the eye, ear, cartilage, or disc.

Organization / Lab Platform Status (as of early 2026) Relevance to Marshall Syndrome
Krystal Biotech (Pittsburgh, PA, USA) Non-replicating HSV-1 viral vector; topical/local delivery VYJUVEK (beremagene geperpavec): FDA-approved gene therapy for dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (COL7A1). First topically applied, redosable gene therapy.

KB801: Investigational redosable eyedrop gene therapy for neurotrophic keratitis. Delivering nerve growth factor (NGF) to corneal epithelial cells. Phase 1/2 EMERALD-1 trial ongoing. FDA granted Platform Technology Designation to the HSV-1 vector in October 2025. Registrational study data expected before end of 2026.

Pipeline also includes KB803 (ocular DEB complications), KB407 (cystic fibrosis), KB111 (Hailey-Hailey disease). 		Krystal’s HSV-1 platform is redosable (unlike AAV, which triggers immune responses on repeat dosing) and can deliver genes topically to the eye. For Marshall syndrome, this platform could theoretically deliver COL11A1-silencing constructs to the vitreous or retina. The eyedrop format (KB801) is particularly intriguing for COL11A1 eye pathology. Krystal has not announced COL11A1 work, but their platform is the most plausible delivery vehicle for ocular gene therapy in collagen disorders.
Regeneron Pharmaceuticals / Decibel Therapeutics Dual AAV vector; intracochlear delivery DB-OTO: Gene therapy for profound hearing loss caused by OTOF mutations. Phase 1/2 CHORD trial results published in New England Journal of Medicine: 11 of 12 children showed clinically meaningful hearing improvement, including 3 who achieved normal hearing levels. Regeneron planning FDA approval submission by end of 2025.

Regeneron acquired Decibel Therapeutics in 2023 for $109 million. 		DB-OTO demonstrates that gene therapy can reach the inner ear and restore hearing in children. While it targets OTOF (not COL11A1), the surgical delivery approach (intracochlear infusion under general anesthesia, similar to cochlear implantation) establishes the route by which a COL11A1-silencing therapy could one day reach the tectorial membrane and cochlear structures affected in Marshall syndrome.
Intra-Articular AAV Research Groups AAV vectors optimized for joint delivery Multiple labs developing intra-articular gene therapy for osteoarthritis, including AAV-mediated delivery of IL-1 receptor antagonist (IL-1Ra) and PRG4 (lubricin). Novel engineered capsids (e.g., AAV62) with improved joint transduction efficiency. Phase I/II clinical trials completed or underway.

Key challenges: transduction efficiency in avascular cartilage, pre-existing AAV immunity, durability of expression. 		Marshall syndrome causes early-onset osteoarthritis from defective collagen XI in cartilage. Intra-articular delivery is the most likely route for getting a COL11A1-silencing therapy into joint cartilage. Progress in AAV capsid engineering for cartilage penetration directly benefits Marshall patients.

Senolytic & Anti-Aging Research

Senescent cells accumulate in Marshall syndrome tissues — discs, cartilage, and vitreous — releasing inflammatory factors (the SASP) that accelerate degeneration. Senolytics are drugs that selectively eliminate senescent cells, and they represent one of the nearest-term therapeutic opportunities for slowing Marshall syndrome progression.

Researcher / Lab Institution Focus Key Work
James L. Kirkland, MD, PhD Robert and Arlene Kogod Center on Aging, Mayo Clinic, Rochester, MN, USA Pioneer of senolytic therapy; Dasatinib + Quercetin (D+Q) Published the first paper on senolytic drugs. Demonstrated that intermittent oral D+Q clears senescent cells in humans and improves physical function. Multiple clinical trials underway including for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis. Active research projects running through 2025–2026. Kirkland’s work is the foundation of the entire senolytic field.
D+Q Disc Degeneration Group Multiple institutions Senolytics for intervertebral disc degeneration Published in Nature Communications (2021): Long-term D+Q treatment in mice reduced disc senescence burden (lower p19ARF and p21), improved disc cell viability and phenotype retention, and retarded nucleus pulposus fibrosis. This is the single most relevant preclinical study for Marshall syndrome disc disease.
D+Q Osteoarthritis Chondrocyte Group Multiple institutions Senolytics for osteoarthritis cartilage Published in Aging Cell (2025): D+Q selectively eliminated senescent cells in human cartilage explants and isolated chondrocytes, promoted chondroanabolism (increased COL2A1, ACAN, SOX9 expression), and elevated collagen type II and glycosaminoglycan biosynthesis. Directly relevant to Marshall syndrome early-onset OA.
Fisetin Clinical Trials Multiple sites Fisetin (natural flavonoid senolytic) in osteoarthritis Clinical trial NCT04210986 is investigating fisetin in OA patients. Fisetin is available over-the-counter as a supplement but is being formally studied for senolytic efficacy and safety in joint disease.

Cartilage & Disc Regeneration

Even if the underlying genetic defect is corrected, decades of damage to discs and cartilage may need regenerative repair. These groups are developing approaches to restore disc and cartilage tissue.

Research Area Approach Status Relevance
Mesenchymal Stem Cell (MSC) Disc Repair Intradiscal injection of MSCs to repopulate the nucleus pulposus 13 clinical studies published (2011–2025) enrolling 1,299 patients total. 5 randomized controlled trials. The IDCT trial’s high-dose group showed statistically significant pain relief and structural regeneration sustained over 2 years. Marshall syndrome causes accelerated disc degeneration. MSC injection could restore disc cell populations, though the hostile extracellular environment (due to defective collagen XI) may limit engraftment unless combined with gene correction.
Hydrogel + Stem Cell Combination Biocompatible hydrogels as scaffolds for cell and exosome delivery to the disc Late preclinical / early clinical. Hydrogels can deliver stem cells, growth factors, and anti-inflammatory exosomes while providing mechanical support. Preliminary clinical studies confirmed safety. The vision: patient-derived iPSCs, genetically corrected with CRISPR, delivered into the disc space within a custom hydrogel scaffold loaded with nutrient-releasing microspheres and anti-inflammatory exosomes. This is the long-term regenerative endgame for Marshall disc disease.
Tissue-Engineered Cartilage 3D-bioprinted scaffolds seeded with chondrocytes or iPSC-derived cartilage progenitors Preclinical. Multiple groups developing cartilage constructs for joint resurfacing. Key challenge: replicating the complex zonal architecture of native articular cartilage. For Marshall syndrome patients facing joint replacement in their 30s–40s, bioengineered cartilage could provide a biological alternative to metal/plastic prostheses with limited revision lifespans.
Exosome-Based Disc Therapy MSC-derived exosomes for anti-inflammatory and regenerative signaling in discs Late preclinical. 2025 studies demonstrated that exosomes can deliver siRNAs to chondrocytes embedded in dense cartilage matrix, overcoming penetration barriers. First human trials expected within 3–5 years for general degenerative disc disease. Opens the possibility of exosome-delivered allele-specific siRNA for mutant COL11A1 silencing — combining the specificity of RNA interference with the tissue-penetrating ability of exosomes.

Patient Registries

Patient registries are among the most important tools for advancing rare disease research. They create the datasets that reveal natural history patterns, attract researchers, enable clinical trials, and ultimately lead to treatments. Registering is one of the most impactful things a Marshall syndrome family can do right now.

Registry Scope How to Enroll
Stickler Involved People (SIP) Database Stickler syndrome and related conditions including Marshall syndrome Contact SIP through their website. They maintain a patient database and networking service.
GenomeConnect (ClinGen) NIH-funded patient registry for anyone with genetic test results. Participants securely share genetic and health information, which is curated and shared with ClinVar to improve variant classification. Free online enrollment at GenomeConnect.org. Upload your genetic test report and complete health surveys. Your data helps improve COL11A1 variant interpretation for all future patients.
CoRDS (Coordination of Rare Diseases at Sanford) Centralized international registry for all rare diseases. Free for participants and researchers. Coordinates research across 10,000+ rare diseases. Free online enrollment. CoRDS connects your data with researchers who may be studying your condition or related conditions.
Why registries matter: Natural history data from registries reveals patterns that individual clinicians cannot see. When a researcher wants to study COL11A1 disc degeneration, the first question is “Where are the patients?” If 50 Marshall/Stickler families are enrolled in CoRDS or GenomeConnect, that researcher can find them. If zero are enrolled, the study never happens. Registry enrollment is free, takes 15–30 minutes, and creates permanent research value.

How to Reach Out to a Researcher

Researchers are busy, but most are genuinely interested in hearing from patients — especially for rare diseases where patient contact is unusual. A well-crafted email can open a door that leads to collaboration, samples being studied, or your family being connected to an emerging trial.

Practical Tips

  • Email is usually the best first contact. Find the researcher’s institutional email through their university or hospital profile page. Do not use personal social media.
  • Lead with your diagnosis and specific variant. Example: “My child has Marshall syndrome caused by a heterozygous splice-site mutation in COL11A1 (c.XXX, exon XX).” This immediately tells the researcher you are a real patient family with confirmed molecular diagnosis.
  • Mention that you’ve banked cells (if you have). Researchers studying dominant collagen mutations need patient-derived cells (fibroblasts, iPSCs) for in vitro work. If you’ve followed the cell banking protocol, mention this — it can make you an invaluable research partner.
  • Ask if they’re interested in COL11A1 patient samples. Even labs focused on COL6A1 may be interested in testing their silencing approach on a related collagen gene.
  • Be concise. 3–5 paragraphs maximum. Researchers receive hundreds of emails. Get to the point: who you are, what your diagnosis is, what you’re hoping to discuss.
  • Offer to be a resource, not just ask for help. “We would be happy to provide samples, participate in studies, or connect you with other Marshall syndrome families.”
  • Mention this site as a research compilation. It demonstrates that there is an organized knowledge base and an engaged patient community — both things that make a researcher more likely to invest attention in the disease.

Sample Email Template

Subject: Marshall Syndrome (COL11A1) Family — Interest in [Researcher’s Specific Work]

Dear Dr. [Name],

My name is [Name] and my [child/family member] has been diagnosed with Marshall syndrome caused by a heterozygous splice-site mutation in COL11A1 ([specific variant if known]). I am writing because your work on [specific topic, e.g., allele-specific silencing of dominant collagen mutations] is directly relevant to our condition.

Marshall syndrome is caused by dominant-negative COL11A1 mutations, mechanistically similar to the dominant COL6A1 mutations your lab has studied. Like COL6A1, COL11A1 is haplosufficient — silencing the mutant allele would be therapeutically sufficient.

We have [banked fibroblasts / genetic test results / clinical data] and would welcome the opportunity to discuss whether COL11A1 patient samples could be useful for your research. We are also connected with other Marshall syndrome families who may be interested in participating in studies.

Thank you for your time and your important work.
