Understanding Pain in Marshall Syndrome

Pain in Marshall syndrome is not one thing. It is a convergence of multiple pain generators, each operating through a different mechanism, often amplifying each other. Understanding why your child hurts — really understanding it, at the level that lets you advocate effectively — is the first step toward managing it.

It Is Not “Growing Pains”

The term “growing pains” is the most common misdiagnosis families encounter, and it is deeply unhelpful. Growing pains are benign, self-limiting, and affect the legs of otherwise healthy children. What Marshall syndrome produces is structurally different:

  • Joint hypermobility pain: Collagen XI is a critical structural protein in cartilage, ligaments, and joint capsules. When it is defective, joints move beyond their normal range, causing microtrauma, subluxations, and chronic mechanical stress on surrounding tissues
  • Disc degeneration: COL11A1 mutations cause premature degeneration of intervertebral discs — the shock absorbers of the spine. This process begins in childhood and produces back pain that is real, structural, and progressive. See Why the Spine is Affected
  • Growth plate stress: During growth spurts, the combination of rapid skeletal growth and defective collagen creates enormous stress on growth plates, endplates, and the developing spine. The vertebral endplates — already compromised by abnormal collagen — can fail, producing Schmorl’s nodes
  • Inflammatory cascades: The immune system responds to collagen breakdown products as if they were foreign invaders, generating chronic low-grade inflammation that further sensitizes pain receptors. See The Four-Mechanism Immune Cascade
  • Central sensitization: When structural pain persists untreated, the nervous system itself changes — amplifying pain signals far beyond what the tissue damage would explain. This is the transition from acute to chronic pain, and in children it can happen in as little as 3–6 months

The Seven-Mechanism Pain Model describes this in clinical detail. What matters for parents is this: your child’s pain likely involves multiple overlapping mechanisms, and treating only one (say, giving an anti-inflammatory for what is partly neuropathic pain) will produce incomplete relief.

When Structural Pain Becomes Neuroplastic Pain

This is the concept that changes everything. Structural pain — from disc degeneration, joint instability, endplate failure — is the starting point. But if that pain is inadequately treated for months or years, the nervous system physically rewires itself to amplify pain signals. The pain becomes nociplastic: real, measurable, neurological — but no longer proportional to the tissue damage.

This is not “in their head.” This is measurable with Quantitative Sensory Testing. It is visible in functional brain imaging. The IASP (International Association for the Study of Pain) created an entirely new pain category — nociplastic pain — specifically because this phenomenon does not fit the traditional categories of tissue damage or nerve injury.

The urgency. Every month of inadequately managed pain in a developing nervous system allows further rewiring. The same neuroplasticity that enables a child to learn a language in two years also enables their spinal cord to rewire around chronic pain in months. Early, aggressive, multimodal pain management is neuroprotective. It is not about comfort alone — it is about preventing permanent changes to neural architecture. See Urgency: Early Pain Management.

Central Sensitization: When the Volume Knob Gets Stuck

Central sensitization is the process by which the central nervous system “turns up the volume” on pain. The spinal cord and brain begin interpreting normal sensory signals as painful, and painful signals as catastrophic. In Marshall syndrome, the risk factors for developing central sensitization are unusually concentrated.

Why It Seems “Too Much”

If a doctor examines your child’s imaging and says “I don’t see enough damage to explain this much pain,” central sensitization is likely the reason. The pain your child reports is real. The disconnect is between what the imaging shows and what the nervous system is doing with those signals.

  • Wind-up: Persistent pain signals from the body bombard the spinal cord’s dorsal horn, causing neurons there to become progressively more excitable. Each signal produces a bigger response than the last
  • Glial activation: Immune cells in the spinal cord (microglia and astrocytes) shift into an activated state, releasing inflammatory molecules that further sensitize pain-processing neurons. Once activated, these cells maintain pain independently of the original tissue damage
  • GABA reversal: In the most alarming development, the brain’s primary inhibitory neurotransmitter (GABA) can become excitatory in children with chronic pain — the braking system literally starts accelerating. This is driven by BDNF-mediated KCC2 downregulation
  • Allodynia: Touch, pressure, or movement that should not be painful starts being interpreted as pain. Clothing seams, gentle hugs, sitting in a car seat — things that should be neutral become aversive

How to Explain This to a Skeptical Doctor

When your child’s pain seems disproportionate to their imaging findings, try framing it this way:

“We understand that central sensitization — nociplastic pain, the IASP’s third mechanism — can develop when a structural pain source persists in a developing nervous system. Our child has multiple concurrent structural pain generators (disc degeneration, joint hypermobility, endplate changes) that have been present since early childhood. Could we assess for central sensitization using Quantitative Sensory Testing? We’d also like to discuss whether the pain management approach should target both the structural source and the central amplification.”

For the full clinical picture, share the Central Sensitization page with your medical team.

Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE): Understanding Pain Reduces It

This is one of the most counterintuitive findings in pain research: teaching people how pain works actually reduces their pain. This is not a placebo effect. It is a measurable neurological change. When the brain understands that pain signals are being amplified rather than accurately reporting tissue damage, it can begin to modulate those signals differently.

The HOPE Program Adaptation

The Hypermobile Online Pain Management (HOPE) program, developed specifically for hypermobility conditions, provides a structured framework for pain neuroscience education that is directly applicable to Marshall syndrome families. A 2025 pilot randomized controlled trial confirmed its feasibility and acceptability. Key principles:

  • Pain is a protector, not a damage meter. Pain is the brain’s alarm system. An alarm that works correctly goes off when there’s a fire. An alarm that has been sensitized goes off when someone makes toast. Your child’s alarm system is stuck on high sensitivity — it is doing its job too well, not incorrectly
  • Pain does not equal harm. In Marshall syndrome, some activities will produce pain but not cause tissue damage. Learning to distinguish between “hurt” and “harm” is critical. Pain during gentle swimming is not harming the joints — it is the sensitized alarm responding to safe activity
  • The brain has a volume knob. Sleep, stress, beliefs about pain, and attention all affect how loud the alarm rings. These are not minor factors — they are physiologically real modulators of pain intensity

What the Evidence Says

A 2025 systematic review of pain neuroscience education in children and adolescents with chronic pain found:

  • Pain knowledge improves with moderate certainty (7 studies) — children who understand pain neuroscience demonstrate measurably better understanding of their condition
  • Function and self-efficacy improve — children feel more capable of managing their pain and participate more in daily activities
  • Emotional variables shift: Anxiety, catastrophizing, and kinesiophobia (fear of movement) all show reduction, though effects are sometimes modest and require reinforcement
  • Most effective when combined with exercise and when parents are included in the education program. A child’s understanding of pain only goes so far if the parents around them are communicating fear and catastrophe
  • School-based delivery is feasible: A 2025 randomized controlled trial demonstrated that pain science education can be effectively delivered in school settings

For the neuroscience details, see Pain Neuroscience in Children and Pain Neuroscience Education.

Medication Approaches

Medication is one tool in the pain management toolkit — not the only tool, and often not the most important one. But when used appropriately, it can provide the relief that makes everything else (physical therapy, psychological support, school participation) possible.

First-Line: Over-the-Counter Options

  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol/paracetamol): Effective for mild to moderate pain. Does not address inflammation. Liver-safe at recommended doses; hepatotoxic in overdose. Useful as a baseline analgesic
  • NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen): Address both pain and inflammation. Particularly useful when pain has an inflammatory component (joint swelling, flares). Naproxen offers longer duration (twice daily vs. every 6 hours for ibuprofen). Long-term use requires monitoring for GI, kidney, and cardiovascular effects
  • Topical NSAIDs (diclofenac gel): Applied directly over painful joints. Provides local anti-inflammatory effect with minimal systemic absorption. Good option for reducing oral NSAID burden while maintaining targeted pain relief

Second-Line: Prescription Options

  • Gabapentin: First-line for neuropathic or central sensitization-driven pain. Binds alpha-2-delta calcium channel subunits in the dorsal horn, reducing excitatory neurotransmitter release. Pediatric dosing: 5–35 mg/kg/day divided into three doses, titrated slowly over 2–3 weeks. May cause sedation (which can be therapeutic if sleep is disrupted)
  • Low-dose amitriptyline: 10–25 mg at bedtime. Dual mechanism: enhances descending inhibitory pathways (serotonin and norepinephrine) while improving sleep quality by suppressing alpha-delta intrusion. One of the most useful agents for the sleep-pain cycle
  • Low-dose naltrexone (LDN): 1.5–4.5 mg at bedtime. Blocks TLR-4 receptors on microglia, reducing the neuroinflammatory cascade that maintains central sensitization. 65% benefit rate in a 14-year retrospective study. Off-label; requires compounding pharmacy. See Low-Dose Naltrexone

Why Opioids Are Generally Avoided

Opioids are described in the Stickler/Marshall literature as “often unhelpful for bone and muscle pain.” The reasons go beyond addiction risk:

  • Opioid-induced hyperalgesia: Chronic opioid use can paradoxically increase pain sensitivity over time, worsening the very central sensitization you are trying to treat
  • Immune effects: Opioids suppress immune function, which may worsen the inflammatory cascades already active in Marshall syndrome
  • Developing brains: The adolescent brain is uniquely vulnerable to the reward-pathway effects of opioids. Codeine is contraindicated in children under 12
  • Does not address the mechanism: Opioids mask pain signals without addressing the structural, inflammatory, or central sensitization components that drive pain in Marshall syndrome

Doxycycline: Dual Role

Doxycycline deserves special mention because it serves double duty in Marshall syndrome. Beyond its antibiotic properties, it is a potent MMP (matrix metalloproteinase) inhibitor. MMPs are the enzymes that actively degrade cartilage and disc tissue in Marshall syndrome. By inhibiting MMP-3 and MMP-13, doxycycline may slow the structural destruction that generates pain in the first place — while also providing anti-inflammatory benefit. See Drug Repurposing and the Drug Repurposing Screen for the full mechanistic rationale.

Low-Level Laser Therapy (LLLT) / Photobiomodulation

Low-level laser therapy (LLLT), also called photobiomodulation (PBM), uses specific wavelengths of red and near-infrared light to reduce pain and inflammation at the cellular level. For Marshall syndrome, this modality is particularly attractive because it is non-invasive, has no systemic side effects, and can be used at home.

How It Works

Near-infrared light (810–850 nm wavelength) is absorbed by cytochrome c oxidase in the mitochondria, triggering a cascade that:

  • Increases ATP (cellular energy) production
  • Activates NRF2 — the master antioxidant transcription factor — via controlled oxidative eustress
  • Reduces NF-kB-driven inflammation
  • Modulates pain signaling at the dorsal root ganglia (DRG), the sensory nerve clusters that relay pain from the body to the spinal cord

Evidence Base

  • Meta-analyses confirm LLLT reduces pain in musculoskeletal conditions with moderate effect sizes
  • Demonstrated anti-inflammatory effects through reduced TNF-alpha, IL-1-beta, and IL-6 at the treatment site
  • Promotes fibroblast activity and collagen synthesis — directly relevant to a collagen disorder
  • Non-thermal mechanism means no tissue damage risk at therapeutic doses

Psychological Approaches: The Mind-Body Connection Is Real

Let’s be clear about what “psychological approaches” means here. It does not mean the pain is psychological. It means the brain is the organ that processes pain, and how the brain processes pain can be changed through specific, evidence-based techniques. This is neurology applied through psychology.

Pain Catastrophizing: The Single Strongest Modifiable Predictor

Research consistently identifies pain catastrophizing — the tendency to ruminate on pain, magnify its threat, and feel helpless to control it — as the strongest psychological predictor of disability in pediatric chronic pain. Stronger than pain intensity itself. The child’s relationship to their pain predicts their functional outcome more than how much pain they actually have.

  • Catastrophizing is modifiable — it responds to treatment
  • Parent catastrophizing matters too: A parent’s catastrophizing about their child’s pain independently predicts the child’s disability. Programs that include parent education produce better outcomes
  • Measurement: The Pain Catastrophizing Scale for Children (PCS-C) can track changes. A reduction of 10 or more points is clinically meaningful

CBT: The Strongest Evidence Base

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) has the most robust evidence base for pediatric chronic pain. It specifically addresses:

  • Catastrophizing: Identifying and restructuring pain-related thought patterns (“this will never get better” → “I’ve had bad days before and they passed”)
  • Fear-avoidance: Breaking the cycle where fear of pain leads to activity avoidance, which leads to deconditioning, which leads to more pain
  • Coping skills: Building an active toolkit (relaxation, distraction, pacing) rather than passive suffering
  • Cochrane evidence: Effective for reducing pain post-treatment and reducing pain-related disability with lasting effects at follow-up

ACT: When CBT Isn’t Enough

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT) takes a different philosophical approach. Rather than trying to change pain-related thoughts, ACT teaches the child to observe them without being controlled by them, and to pursue meaningful activities (values) even in the presence of pain.

  • ACT was more effective than multidisciplinary treatment including amitriptyline in one randomized controlled trial with pediatric longstanding pain
  • Particularly valuable for Marshall syndrome because the structural pain source cannot be eliminated. ACT helps children build a meaningful life alongside pain, rather than waiting for pain to resolve before living
  • May be more relevant when a child has significant difficulty identifying or pursuing values-based activities, or when cognitive restructuring techniques feel invalidating
  • Results show improvements in both adolescent functioning and parent psychological flexibility
Which one? CBT and ACT are not competitors — they are complementary tools. Many therapists use elements of both. Start with whichever is available from a therapist experienced in pediatric chronic pain. The specific approach matters less than the therapist’s experience with chronic pain in children. Ask: “Have you worked with children whose pain is structural and ongoing, not expected to resolve?”

Physical Approaches

Physical strategies for pain management work best when they target the specific mechanisms driving pain. For Marshall syndrome, the goal is loading without overloading — providing enough mechanical stimulus to maintain tissue health while respecting the structural limitations of defective collagen.

Aquatic Therapy

Water is the single best exercise environment for a child with Marshall syndrome. Buoyancy reduces joint loading by up to 90%, while hydrostatic pressure reduces edema and warm water activates descending pain inhibitory pathways. See Aquatic Therapy for the full protocol.

TENS (Transcutaneous Electrical Nerve Stimulation)

TENS units deliver mild electrical impulses through skin electrodes, activating A-beta nerve fibers that inhibit pain transmission at the spinal cord (gate control mechanism). They are non-invasive, drug-free, cost $30–100, and can be self-administered at home. See the TENS Practical Guide for electrode placement and protocols.

Heat and Cold

  • Heat: Improves blood flow, reduces muscle spasm, and provides comfort during stiffness. Particularly useful in the morning when Marshall syndrome stiffness peaks. Moist heat (warm bath, heated rice sock) penetrates deeper than dry heat
  • Cold: Reduces acute inflammation and provides numbing analgesia during flares. Ice packs wrapped in cloth, 15–20 minutes at a time, with skin checks for children who may not report discomfort accurately
  • Contrast therapy: Alternating warm and cold can be effective for joint pain, but should be introduced gradually — some children with central sensitization find temperature changes painful initially

Massage

Gentle, therapeutic massage (not deep tissue) can reduce muscle guarding, improve circulation, and provide comfort. Caution: in hypermobile joints, vigorous massage can destabilize joint alignment. Communicate the hypermobility diagnosis to any massage therapist. See Complementary Therapies for safety guidelines.

The Disc Loading Cycle

Intervertebral discs are avascular — they receive nutrients through a pumping action driven by alternating loading and unloading. Prolonged sitting (constant load) or prolonged bed rest (no load) both starve the disc of nutrients. The healthiest strategy is intermittent loading: regular movement breaks, alternating positions, and gentle exercise that creates the pump cycle without excessive axial force.

Sleep Ergonomics

A child with Marshall syndrome spends 8–12 hours in bed. The sleeping environment matters:

  • Mattress: Medium-firm. Too soft allows the spine to sag; too firm creates pressure points on hypermobile joints
  • Pillow: Must maintain neutral cervical alignment. This is critical in Marshall syndrome where cervical spine pathology may be present. The pillow should fill the gap between the ear and the shoulder in side-sleeping position
  • Position: Side-sleeping with a pillow between the knees reduces spinal torsion. Avoid prone sleeping, which hyperextends the cervical spine
  • Temperature: Cool room (65–68°F / 18–20°C). Autonomic dysregulation in Marshall syndrome can make temperature regulation difficult — layers that can be adjusted are better than a single heavy blanket

Gentle Exercise: Movement Is Medicine

Exercise produces analgesic effects through multiple pathways: endorphin release, descending inhibitory pathway activation, NRF2 upregulation, anti-inflammatory myokine secretion, and improved sleep. The key is finding the right type and dose. See Exercise as Molecular Medicine for the full protocol, and the 10 Molecular Pathways for the science behind why exercise reduces pain.

Critical sequencing. If central sensitization has developed, the normal analgesic response to exercise may be impaired or reversed. A centrally sensitized child may experience more pain after exercise, not less. In this case, central sensitization must be addressed first (pharmacologically, with sleep optimization, and with PNE) before exercise programs can succeed. See The Exercise Paradox.

School and Daily Life

Pain does not stop at the school door. Managing pain at school requires planning, communication, and a willingness to modify — not eliminate — activities.

School Accommodations

  • 504 Plan: A legal document requiring the school to provide accommodations. This is not optional kindness — it is a legal right. See School 504 Plan for a template
  • Standing desk or sit-stand option: Allows the child to alternate positions, maintaining the disc loading cycle
  • Movement breaks: Every 30–45 minutes. Standing up, walking to the water fountain, gentle stretching. This is not a privilege — it is a medical necessity for disc nutrition
  • Modified PE: Not excused from PE entirely (deconditioning is dangerous), but modified to avoid high-impact activities. Swimming, cycling, yoga are excellent alternatives to running, jumping, and contact sports
  • Extra time for transitions: Moving between classes with a heavy backpack through crowded hallways is genuinely painful. Allow early dismissal from class, use of a rolling backpack, or a second set of textbooks to avoid carrying
  • Access to medication: NSAID or acetaminophen available from the nurse without having to wait in line while in pain
  • Quiet space: A designated place the child can go when pain escalates — not as a reward for complaining, but as a regulated de-escalation strategy

Activity Modification, Not Avoidance

The single biggest mistake families make is eliminating all physical activity to avoid pain. This creates the deconditioning spiral: less activity → weaker muscles → less joint stability → more pain → less activity. The goal is modification, not avoidance.

  • Green light activities: Swimming, cycling, gentle yoga, walking on flat surfaces, elliptical machines
  • Yellow light activities: Light jogging (if tolerated), modified dance, recreational sports without contact
  • Red light activities: Trampolines, contact sports, gymnastics, heavy lifting, high-impact jumping

See Sports Clearance & Activity Guide for the complete traffic light system.

Pacing: The Energy Battery

Pacing means distributing activity evenly throughout the day rather than doing too much on good days (boom) and crashing on bad days (bust). Teach your child to think of their energy as a battery:

  • Start the day at whatever charge level they wake with
  • Activities drain the battery; rest, comfort strategies, and sleep recharge it
  • The goal is to end each day with some charge remaining — not at zero
  • Over time, as fitness improves and central sensitization resolves, the battery capacity grows — but only if they avoid repeatedly draining it to zero

For the detailed framework, see the Pain Flare Protocol: Traffic Light System.

The Pain Flare Protocol

Flares — temporary, significant worsening of pain beyond the child’s usual baseline — are a predictable feature of Marshall syndrome. They are not a sign of disease progression. They have a beginning, a peak, and a resolution. The key is having a plan before the flare happens. See the full Flare Management & Pain Crisis Protocol.

Quick Reference: Traffic Light Zones

ZonePain LevelResponse
GREEN Baseline — manageable, can participate in usual activities Maintain regular routine. Continue exercise program. Use comfort strategies preventively. Monitor for triggers
YELLOW Above baseline — functioning is reduced but not stopped Activate flare plan. Reduce (don’t eliminate) activity. Increase scheduled medication if pre-authorized. Apply heat/cold. Prioritize sleep. Contact school to activate 504 modifications
RED Severe — unable to participate in daily activities Execute rescue medication plan (pre-authorized by physician). Rest without guilt. Contact medical team if red zone persists beyond 48 hours or if new neurological symptoms appear. Do NOT push through — this is the body requesting genuine rest
Pre-authorize the plan. Work with your pain specialist before a flare to create a written action plan that specifies exactly what medications to use at each zone, when to contact the office vs. go to the ER, and what activities to modify vs. stop. Making decisions about medication during a crisis — when both the child and the parent are stressed — leads to worse outcomes than executing a pre-made plan.

When to Seek Urgent Help

Most Marshall syndrome pain, while distressing, is managed at home with the strategies above. But some pain presentations are red flags that require urgent medical evaluation. These are situations where “normal” Marshall pain is not the explanation.

Go to the Emergency Department if your child has:
  • New leg weakness, numbness, or bowel/bladder changes: May indicate cervical myelopathy or cauda equina syndrome. This is a surgical emergency. See Cervical Myelopathy
  • Sudden severe headache with neck stiffness: May indicate CSF leak, meningitis, or Chiari-related obstruction. See CSF Leak
  • Sudden vision changes (flashes, floaters, curtain across vision): May indicate retinal detachment. This requires ophthalmologic emergency evaluation within hours. See Eye Management
  • Electric shock sensation down the spine with neck flexion (Lhermitte’s sign) that is new or worsening: Indicates cervical cord irritation. See Lhermitte’s Sign
  • Pain so severe that the child cannot be consoled or positioned comfortably: Especially if accompanied by fever, which may indicate discitis (disc infection) or other acute pathology
  • New gait abnormality or inability to bear weight: May indicate fracture through osteoporotic bone, joint dislocation, or neurological compromise

Bring the Emergency Protocol Card to the ER. It contains critical information about airway management, cervical spine precautions, and medication sensitivities that ER doctors need to know about Marshall syndrome.

Intensive Programs: When Outpatient Isn’t Enough

Intensive Interdisciplinary Pain Treatment (IIPT)

When standard outpatient approaches fail to restore function, IIPT programs offer the most powerful evidence-based escalation. These are 3–4 week intensive day programs (6–8 hours daily) combining physical therapy, occupational therapy, psychology, and pain medicine. Outcomes are striking:

  • 50–70% reduction in functional disability
  • School attendance normalized in 70% of participants
  • 2025 data confirms IIPT is safe and effective for hypermobile patients specifically
  • Cost-effective: approximately $27,000 savings per family per year compared to ongoing fragmented outpatient care

Programs with hypermobility experience: Boston Children’s, Cincinnati Children’s, Cleveland Clinic, and CHOP. When inquiring, ask specifically about experience with connective tissue disorders. See the Treatment: Pain Management page for the full IIPT section.

Putting It Together: A Framework for Families

Pain management in Marshall syndrome is not one intervention. It is a system — an integrated approach that addresses multiple pain mechanisms simultaneously. Here is a framework for building that system with your medical team:

  1. Understand the pain. Which mechanisms are active? Structural? Inflammatory? Neuropathic? Central sensitization? The answer determines the treatment mix
  2. Assess for central sensitization. Request Quantitative Sensory Testing. If central sensitization is present, it must be addressed before exercise programs can succeed
  3. Build the medication foundation. First-line analgesics for breakthrough pain, plus mechanism-specific agents (gabapentin for neuropathic, LDN for neuroinflammation, amitriptyline for sleep-pain cycle) as needed
  4. Start pain neuroscience education. For the child AND the family. Understanding pain changes the brain’s processing of pain. This is not optional — it is foundational
  5. Add physical approaches. Aquatic therapy as the cornerstone. TENS, PBM, heat/cold for daily management. Gentle exercise progressed as tolerance allows
  6. Engage psychological support. CBT or ACT with a therapist experienced in pediatric chronic pain. Address catastrophizing in both the child and the parents
  7. Optimize sleep. Sleep disruption maintains and amplifies every other pain mechanism. See Sleep Architecture and the Morning Protocol
  8. Create the flare plan. Written, pre-authorized, traffic-light coded. Execute the plan during flares rather than making decisions under stress
  9. Accommodate school. 504 plan with movement breaks, modified PE, and access to medication and a quiet space
  10. Track outcomes. Use functional measures (school attendance, activity participation, sleep quality), not just pain scores. A child whose pain score is unchanged but who is back at school full-time is a treatment success
You are your child’s best advocate. Doctors have 15 minutes per appointment. You have 24 hours a day with your child. You will learn their pain patterns better than any clinician. Trust what you see. Bring data (pain diaries, functional tracking, sleep logs) to appointments. Ask questions. Push back when something doesn’t make sense. Your instinct that “something is wrong” brought you to this page — that same instinct will guide you through the management plan. You are not alone in this, and it does get better.
