This is not a general overview of clinical research. This is a concrete, dated guide to the trials, programs, and registries that Marshall syndrome families should know about right now — with specific actions you can take today. The landscape is moving fast. Several developments in 2025–2026 have opened doors that were closed even a year ago.

Actions You Can Take Now Established

These are not aspirational suggestions. These are concrete steps you can initiate this week.

  1. Get whole genome sequencing if not already done. This is the prerequisite for n-Lorem Foundation ASO therapy and future gene therapy eligibility. Ask your geneticist to order it. Many insurance plans now cover whole genome sequencing for diagnosed genetic conditions — especially when a known pathogenic variant has been identified in the family. Without your exact mutation characterized at the genomic level, you cannot be considered for any allele-specific therapy.
    Action: Call your geneticist’s office and ask whether whole genome sequencing has been completed or only targeted panel testing.
  2. Discuss prophylactic retinal treatment with a retinal specialist familiar with the Cambridge protocol. 360-degree cryotherapy or laser retinopexy reduces retinal detachment risk from approximately 47% to approximately 7% in Stickler type 1. Ask whether your child’s COL11A1 variant warrants prophylaxis. This is one of the most impactful preventive interventions available for Marshall/Stickler patients today.
    Action: Request a referral to a vitreoretinal surgeon experienced with inherited vitreoretinopathies. Mention the Cambridge protocol by name.
  3. Join patient registries — your participation directly accelerates research and ensures you hear about trials when they open:
    • Stickler Involved People (SIP) — primary patient organization with a physician database and family network
    • Marfan Foundation Stickler program — virtual support groups and advocacy resources
    • NEI Stickler Syndrome Natural History Study — free comprehensive testing at NIH Clinical Center, travel costs covered for participants
    • Cincinnati Children’s Stickler Registry — one of the largest phenotypic databases for Stickler-spectrum patients
    • NORD and GARD databases — ensure your condition is counted in prevalence statistics that drive funding
    Action: Register with at least two of these organizations this month. Each registration takes 10–20 minutes.
  4. Establish baseline monitoring so changes over time can be tracked quantitatively, not anecdotally:
    • Audiograms (pure tone and speech discrimination) — annually
    • Comprehensive ophthalmologic exam including OCT and visual field testing — annually
    • Spinal imaging (MRI of cervical and lumbar spine) — baseline now, then as clinically indicated
    • DEXA scan for bone density if over age 10
    Action: Ask your care coordinator to schedule baseline studies for any that have not been completed in the past 12 months.
  5. Bank cells now. A simple skin biopsy or blood draw can provide cells for future iPSC generation and gene correction. The younger the cells, the better — every year adds approximately 40 somatic mutations. Cryopreservation costs $500–$2,000 for initial banking plus $100–$300 per year for storage.
    Action: Ask your geneticist or dermatologist about fibroblast banking. See our Cell Banking guide for detailed instructions.

The n-Lorem Foundation Supported

This may be the single most important near-term opportunity for some Marshall families. If your child has a COL11A1 mutation shared by fewer than 30 people worldwide, you should know about n-Lorem.

The n-Lorem Foundation is a non-profit that provides free, lifelong experimental antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) medicines for “nano-rare” patients — individuals whose specific genetic mutation is found in approximately 1–30 people worldwide. Each ASO is custom-designed for the individual patient’s mutation.

Why this matters for Marshall families. Many COL11A1 mutations ARE nano-rare. Specific splice-site variants may be unique to a single family. If your mutation is private or shared by only a handful of people globally, n-Lorem may consider your case.

Requirements:

  • U.S. resident (or willing to travel to a U.S. treatment center)
  • Whole genome sequencing completed (not just a gene panel)
  • Research physician at a tertiary care center willing to serve as the treating clinician
  • Mutation confirmed to be nano-rare (found in 1–30 people)

The process:

  1. Your physician submits an application to n-Lorem describing the mutation and clinical phenotype
  2. n-Lorem’s scientific team reviews feasibility of ASO design for the specific mutation
  3. The Antisense Technology and Therapeutics Committee (ATTC) evaluates and recommends
  4. If accepted, n-Lorem designs, manufactures, and provides the ASO at no cost — for life

Track record: More than 30 patients treated across 5+ years, primarily neurological conditions. n-Lorem has demonstrated the ability to design, manufacture, and deliver patient-specific ASOs on a timeline of months, not years.

The challenge for Marshall: n-Lorem has focused primarily on CNS-deliverable ASOs (intrathecal injection). Delivery to cartilage, vitreous humor, and the cochlea is biologically harder — these are avascular or poorly vascularized tissues. However, the conversation is worth having. ASO technology is advancing rapidly, and n-Lorem’s scope is expanding.

Action: Discuss with your geneticist whether to submit an application. Even if n-Lorem determines that delivery to your child’s target tissues is not yet feasible, the application puts your mutation on their radar for future capability expansion. Visit nlorem.org for application details.

FDA Plausible Mechanism Framework Established

On February 23, 2026, the FDA published draft guidance on a Plausible Mechanism Pathway for ultra-rare diseases. This is potentially the most important regulatory development in a decade for families like yours.

What it does: This framework enables approval of gene-editing and RNA therapies for ultra-rare diseases based on mechanistic evidence — demonstrating that a therapy corrects the known molecular defect — rather than requiring large randomized controlled trials that are impossible to run when only a few hundred patients exist worldwide.

Why Marshall syndrome is an ideal candidate:

  • Well-defined genetic cause — COL11A1 mutations with a clear dominant-negative mechanism
  • Documentable natural history — progressive hearing loss, disc degeneration, and vitreous abnormalities that can be measured over time
  • Measurable target engagement — collagen XI protein levels, cartilage biomarkers, audiometric testing, and imaging can objectively demonstrate whether a therapy is working
  • Small population — traditional large trials are not feasible, which is exactly the scenario this pathway addresses

Key dates:

  • Draft guidance published: February 23, 2026
  • Comment period closes: April 27, 2026
  • Final guidance expected: late 2026 or early 2027

This is the regulatory pathway that could enable a future COL11A1 therapy to reach patients without requiring a 500-person Phase III trial.

Action: Patient advocacy organizations should submit comments during the open comment period supporting the inclusion of connective tissue disorders. Individual families can also submit comments through regulations.gov. If you are connected to SIP or the Marfan Foundation, ask them whether they are preparing a comment — and offer your family’s story as supporting evidence.

Hearing Protection Pipeline Supported

Hearing loss in Marshall syndrome is progressive and currently irreversible. The pipeline for hearing protection agents is the most immediately relevant therapeutic area.

Ebselen (SPI-1005)

Ebselen is an NRF2 activator and glutathione peroxidase mimetic that has completed Phase 3 trials for Meniere’s disease with positive results. It has received FDA Breakthrough Therapy Designation. Likely FDA approval: 2027–2028.

Relevance to Marshall: Ebselen activates the NRF2 antioxidant defense pathway, which protects against oxidative damage to cochlear structures. Once approved for Meniere’s disease, it could be discussed with your ENT specialist for off-label hearing preservation in connective tissue disorders with progressive sensorineural hearing loss.

Current hearing gene therapy trials

Active hearing gene therapy programs (DB-OTO, SENS-501) target OTOF mutations only — these are not applicable to COL11A1-related hearing loss. The mechanism is fundamentally different: OTOF therapies restore a missing protein in inner hair cells, while Marshall hearing loss involves structural collagen defects in the tectorial membrane.

The gap

No therapy currently in clinical trials targets tectorial membrane collagen defects specifically. The NRF2/ferroptosis defense pathway (sulforaphane, ebselen) represents the most promising near-term hearing protection strategy — not by fixing the collagen defect, but by protecting the cochlear cells from the downstream oxidative damage it causes.

Action: Ask your ENT about monitoring for ebselen approval. In the meantime, discuss whether dietary NRF2 activators (sulforaphane from broccoli sprout extract) are appropriate as a low-risk protective measure. See our hearing protection strategies page for evidence-based approaches available today.

Disc Regeneration Pipeline Supported

Premature disc degeneration is one of the most disabling features of Marshall syndrome. Several programs are advancing toward clinical availability.

DiscGenics IDCT (rebonuputemcel)

DiscGenics’ injectable discogenic cell therapy (IDCT) is in Phase 3 trials, with the first patient treated in January 2026. The therapy uses allogeneic progenitor disc cells injected directly into degenerating discs. It holds both RMAT (Regenerative Medicine Advanced Therapy) and Fast Track designations from the FDA. Results are expected approximately 2028.

Limitation: Currently enrolling adults only. If approved, pediatric studies would follow. The therapy addresses disc degeneration generally, not COL11A1-specific degeneration — but the end result (disc height loss, pain, instability) is the same regardless of cause.

Senolytics for disc degeneration

The senolytic combination dasatinib + quercetin (D+Q) prevented disc degeneration in mouse models (Nature Communications, 2021). Fisetin has completed Phase I/II trials for knee osteoarthritis with an acceptable safety profile. No human trial for disc-specific senolytic therapy exists yet, but the preclinical rationale is strong — senescent cells accumulate in degenerating discs and secrete inflammatory factors (the SASP) that accelerate destruction.

Gap: No gene therapy trials for disc degeneration exist in any condition. Disc regeneration remains a cell therapy and small molecule frontier.

Action: Ask your spine specialist whether your child would be a candidate for DiscGenics IDCT if it receives FDA approval. Establish the baseline spinal MRI now so that disc degeneration can be documented quantitatively over time — this documentation will be required for any future trial enrollment or therapy access.

Gene Therapy Horizon Hypothesis

There are no active clinical trials for COL11A1 gene therapy in any tissue. But the path is being paved by programs in related collagen disorders.

Closest pathfinders

  • COL6A1 ASO program: Approaching clinical trial readiness based on 2024 preprint data. If this enters human trials, it validates the entire approach of allele-specific silencing for dominant-negative collagen mutations — the same mechanism that causes Marshall syndrome.
  • Krystal Biotech’s HSV-1 platform: Already FDA-approved as Vyjuvek for COL7A1 delivery in epidermolysis bullosa. Proves that collagen gene delivery via viral vector works in humans, can achieve regulatory approval, and can be manufactured at clinical scale.
  • PASTE technology: Demonstrated for large collagen gene insertion (COL4A3, COL4A4 in Alport syndrome models). Relevant because COL11A1 is a large gene that exceeds standard AAV packaging capacity — PASTE offers a potential route for full-length gene delivery.

Realistic timeline: 5–10 years before a COL11A1-specific therapy reaches clinical trials. This is not pessimism — it reflects the time required for preclinical development, IND filing, and trial design. The Plausible Mechanism Pathway could shorten the approval timeline once trials begin.

Action: Bank cells now (see Cell Banking guide). Connect with research labs working on COL6A1 correction (see our Researcher Directory). Your family’s cells and clinical data could help accelerate the jump from COL6A1 to COL11A1.
