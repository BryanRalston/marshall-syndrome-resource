Why Reach Out to Researchers?

Every breakthrough in rare disease treatment started with a connection between a patient family and a scientist. Vyjuvek — the first FDA-approved collagen gene therapy — exists because epidermolysis bullosa families sought out researchers, offered cells, organized registries, and made the case that their disease was solvable. The science that could reverse Marshall syndrome already exists in adjacent fields. The researchers working on dominant-negative collagen silencing for COL6A1, gene therapy delivery for COL7A1, and clinical management of Stickler syndrome do not necessarily know that their work applies to COL11A1. A single well-crafted letter from a family can put Marshall syndrome on a lab’s radar.

The letters below target three specific research groups whose existing platforms, expertise, and published work make them the most plausible near-term collaborators for Marshall syndrome families. Each letter is tailored to that lab’s specific publications and capabilities. They are written from a parent’s perspective — professional but personal, specific but concise.

How to Use These Templates

  • Keep it concise. Researchers receive hundreds of emails. These templates are already at the upper limit of appropriate length. Do not add more than a sentence or two of personal detail.

Letter 1: Krystal Biotech — Gene Therapy Delivery Platform

Who They Are

Krystal Biotech (Pittsburgh, PA) developed Vyjuvek (beremagene geperpavec), the first FDA-approved redosable gene therapy, for dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa caused by COL7A1 mutations. Their proprietary non-replicating HSV-1 viral vector platform has several properties that make it uniquely suited for collagen gene delivery:

  • Redosable — unlike AAV vectors, which trigger immune responses on repeat dosing, the HSV-1 vector can be administered multiple times
  • Large cargo capacity (~30 kb) — COL11A1 cDNA is approximately 5.4 kb, well within the vector’s payload limit
  • Topical/local delivery — Vyjuvek is applied topically to skin; KB801 is an investigational eyedrop delivering nerve growth factor to corneal epithelial cells
  • FDA Platform Technology Designation granted October 2025, recognizing the vector’s broad applicability
  • KB801 is in Phase 1/2 (EMERALD-1 trial) for neurotrophic keratitis — an eyedrop gene therapy, directly relevant to ocular COL11A1 pathology

Why They Matter for Marshall Syndrome

The eye is the most accessible target tissue for COL11A1 gene therapy. Marshall syndrome causes vitreous degeneration, severe myopia, cataracts, and retinal detachment — all driven by defective collagen XI in the vitreous and retina. Krystal’s KB801 eyedrop platform demonstrates that their HSV-1 vector can deliver genes to ocular tissues via a non-invasive route. The vector could theoretically deliver COL11A1-silencing constructs (antisense sequences, shRNA expression cassettes, or dominant-negative-resistant replacement constructs) to the vitreous or retinal pigment epithelium. This would be the most plausible first-tissue gene therapy for Marshall syndrome.

Letter 2: UCL — Haiyan Zhou & Francesco Muntoni

Who They Are

Dr. Haiyan Zhou and Professor Francesco Muntoni lead the Dubowitz Neuromuscular Centre at the Great Ormond Street Institute of Child Health, University College London. They are the world leaders in allele-specific silencing of dominant collagen mutations, with published success in two collagen genes using two different technologies:

  • COL6A1 G293R siRNA (2024, Molecular Therapy — Nucleic Acids): Designed 19 siRNAs tiling across the mutant COL6A1 transcript and achieved >80% allele-specific silencing of the dominant-negative G293R variant while preserving normal allele expression.
  • COL6A3 gapmer ASOs (2017, Molecular Therapy — Nucleic Acids): Selectively suppressed mutant COL6A3 transcripts using gapmer antisense oligonucleotides, restoring functional collagen VI protein deposition in patient fibroblasts.
  • Gapmer design optimization (2024): Published improved strategies for designing gapmer ASOs that discriminate between wild-type and mutant transcripts differing by a single nucleotide.

Why They Matter for Marshall Syndrome

The dominant-negative mechanism in COL11A1 is mechanistically identical to that in COL6A1 and COL6A3: a mutant chain is incorporated into the collagen trimer, distorting the entire complex and preventing normal extracellular matrix assembly. The UCL group has already solved the hardest part of the problem — designing allele-specific silencing molecules for dominant collagen mutations. Their siRNA tiling approach (for missense mutations) and gapmer ASO approach (for splice-site mutations) are directly transferable to COL11A1. Marshall syndrome splice-site mutations create aberrant exon-exon junctions in the mutant mRNA that serve as unique targeting sequences for gapmer ASOs — the exact scenario their COL6A3 work addressed.

Letter 3: Cambridge — Martin P. Snead

Who They Are

Mr. Martin P. Snead leads the Vitreoretinal Service at Addenbrooke’s Hospital, University of Cambridge, and directs the NHS England Highly Specialised Stickler Syndrome Service — the only dedicated national clinical service for Stickler syndrome in the world. His contributions include:

  • Cambridge Prophylactic Cryotherapy Protocol: Demonstrated in 778 patients that prophylactic cryotherapy significantly reduces retinal detachment rates in Stickler syndrome type 1. This is the standard of care for retinal prophylaxis.
  • Vitreous phenotype classification: Developed the vitreous phenotyping system that distinguishes Stickler types (membranous type 1 vs. beaded type 2 vs. hypoplastic), enabling clinical diagnosis before genetic testing.
  • COL11A1 mutation characterization: Co-authored key publications establishing genotype-phenotype correlations for COL11A1 mutations, including the beaded vitreous phenotype associated with dominant-negative variants.
  • Co-authored “Therapeutic and diagnostic advances in Stickler syndrome” (2020), the most comprehensive recent review of the clinical landscape.

Why They Matter for Marshall Syndrome

Mr. Snead has more clinical experience with COL11A1 patients than any other clinician worldwide. His service sees the full spectrum of COL11A1 disease and has longitudinal follow-up data spanning decades. This clinical expertise, combined with the systematic cascade analysis and drug repurposing screen compiled on this site, creates a uniquely powerful collaboration opportunity. His patients could be among the first to benefit from cascade-breaking interventions, and his clinical network could validate the N-of-1 trial protocol framework. He is also the most credible clinical voice to advocate for research funding and drug development in COL11A1 disorders.

Before You Send: Checklist

Before sending any letter, review this checklist to ensure your outreach is professional, complete, and maximally effective.

Pre-Send Checklist
  • All [bracketed fields] replaced with your family’s specific information — no template placeholders remaining.
  • Variant notation verified. Your COL11A1 variant is in HGVS format (e.g., NM_080629.3:c.4069-1G>T) from your official genetic testing report. If you are unsure of the exact notation, contact your genetic counselor before sending.
  • Clinical features listed are accurate and current. Include confirmed diagnoses only — do not speculate about features that have not been clinically evaluated.
  • Contact information correct. You have included a valid email address and, optionally, a phone number.
  • Researcher’s contact obtained from institutional sources. Use the university, hospital, or company website — not personal social media or third-party directories.
  • Subject line is specific. It mentions COL11A1/Marshall syndrome and the specific aspect of their work you are referencing. A specific subject line is far more likely to be opened than a generic “Rare disease inquiry.”
  • Tone is professional but personal. You are a parent, not a corporation. Be warm but not emotional. Be specific but not overwhelming. Be clear about what you are offering, not just what you are asking.
  • Letter is concise. Under 500 words if possible. Researchers will not read a multi-page letter from an unknown sender. Get to the point, make the connection to their work, state what you can offer, and propose a next step.
  • Site URL included. The Marshall Syndrome Resource demonstrates that an organized, scientifically literate patient community exists — this is a significant credibility signal.
  • Proofread. Spelling and grammar errors undermine credibility. Have someone else read the letter before sending.
  • Follow-up plan. If you do not receive a response within 2–3 weeks, one polite follow-up is appropriate. After that, do not persist — the researcher may not be in a position to engage. Consider alternative contacts at the same institution.
  • Expectations calibrated. Most researchers will respond if the letter is well-written and relevant to their work. But a response does not mean a commitment. The goal of the first letter is to open a conversation, not to secure a collaboration. Be patient and grateful for any engagement.

General Guidance for Research Outreach

Beyond these three specific letters, the Researcher & Lab Directory provides general guidance on reaching out to researchers, including a generic email template. The key principles:

  • Lead with your diagnosis and variant. This immediately establishes you as a real patient family with confirmed molecular diagnosis — not a speculative inquiry.
  • Connect their work to your disease. Show that you have read their publications and understand why their science is relevant. Researchers respond to people who understand what they do.
  • Offer to be a resource. Patient cells, clinical data, family recruitment, registry participation — these are things researchers need and have difficulty obtaining for ultra-rare diseases. You are not just asking for help; you are offering a partnership.
  • Mention banked cells. If you have followed the Cell Banking & Biobanking protocol, say so. Banked patient fibroblasts are immediately useful for in vitro experiments and dramatically increase the likelihood of engagement.
  • Be concise and specific. A 4-paragraph email with a clear ask will always outperform a 3-page emotional plea. Scientists are trained to evaluate evidence and proposals, not narrative.
  • Reference this site. The existence of a comprehensive, citation-backed research resource signals that the patient community is organized, scientifically literate, and ready to collaborate.
