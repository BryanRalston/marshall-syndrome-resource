Medical Disclaimer
This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Hearing loss management should be guided by a qualified audiologist and otolaryngologist (ENT) familiar with connective tissue disorders. Every child’s hearing profile is different — the strategies described here are a framework for informed conversations with your medical team, not a treatment plan.

Hearing loss is one of the most common features of Marshall syndrome, affecting over 82% of individuals with COL11A1 mutations. It is also one of the most manageable. Unlike spinal or joint involvement, where treatment options are limited, hearing loss has a well-established toolkit of monitoring, amplification, and support strategies that can dramatically improve quality of life when applied early and consistently.

This page brings together the science, the practical audiology, and the day-to-day strategies that families need — from why hearing loss happens at the molecular level, through the types of loss your child may experience, to the devices, school accommodations, and emerging therapies that can help. For deeper dives on specific topics, see the companion pages on Hearing Management, Hearing Loss Mechanisms, and Tinnitus Management.

Why Hearing Loss Happens in Marshall Syndrome

To understand hearing loss in Marshall syndrome, you need to understand one structure: the tectorial membrane (TM). This tiny, ribbon-like sheet sits inside the cochlea — the spiral-shaped organ in the inner ear that converts sound into nerve signals. The TM’s job is to transmit mechanical vibrations from incoming sound waves to the outer hair cells (OHCs), which amplify the signal before it reaches the brain.

COL11A1: A Core Structural Protein of the Inner Ear

The tectorial membrane is composed primarily of three collagen types: type II (COL2A1), type IX, and type XI (COL11A1 and COL11A2). Together, these proteins form a precisely organized scaffold of radial collagen fibrils approximately 20 nanometers in diameter. These fibrils are not random — they are arranged in meticulous radial arrays that create the directional stiffness (mechanical anisotropy) the TM needs to do its job.

A comprehensive 2025 review in Frontiers in Neurology (“Tectorial membrane: structure, function, and its implications for hearing loss”) confirmed that COL11A1 is one of the TM’s essential structural proteins. It provides the molecular scaffolding that:

  • Anchors the TM to outer hair cell stereocilia — the physical connection that transmits sound energy to the cells that generate nerve signals
  • Controls fibril diameter and spacing — acting as a molecular spacer that prevents collagen fibrils from fusing into thick, disordered bundles
  • Creates graded stiffness along the cochlea — stiffer at the base (high frequencies) and more compliant at the apex (low frequencies), enabling frequency-specific sound processing
The key insight. When COL11A1 is mutated, the radial collagen fibrils become disorganized and more widely spaced. The TM cannot properly couple sound energy to the outer hair cells. The cochlear amplifier — which normally provides 40–60 dB of gain — is weakened. The result is elevated hearing thresholds across frequencies, typically worse at higher frequencies. This is a structural engineering problem, not a primary sensory cell defect. The hair cells themselves may be initially functional, but they cannot do their job because the defective tectorial membrane fails to deliver the mechanical signal.

Why It Gets Worse Over Time

The tectorial membrane is malformed from birth due to the COL11A1 mutation. But hearing loss in Marshall syndrome is typically progressive — it worsens over years, often with a noticeable decline during adolescence and again in middle adulthood. The 2025 Frontiers review explains why: a structurally compromised TM is vulnerable to ongoing micro-damage from normal acoustic stress. Every day of sound exposure applies mechanical forces that a healthy TM can absorb but a COL11A1-defective TM cannot fully withstand. The collagen continues to degrade, and the coupling between TM and hair cells weakens further.

This progressive deterioration is why noise protection is not optional — it is a medical necessity for anyone with Marshall syndrome. Every decibel of unnecessary noise exposure accelerates structural degradation of an already vulnerable tectorial membrane.

Types of Hearing Loss in Marshall Syndrome

Hearing loss in Marshall syndrome is not one thing. Most individuals have more than one type operating simultaneously, and understanding which types are present changes the monitoring, treatment, and prognosis.

1. Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SNHL) — Most Common

This is the dominant form, arising from the tectorial membrane pathology described above. It affects approximately 82.5% of individuals with COL11A1 mutations. Characteristics include:

  • Progressive: Typically worsens over time, with acceleration during growth spurts and adolescence
  • All frequencies affected: Unlike COL2A1 Stickler syndrome (which primarily affects high frequencies), COL11A1 mutations cause hearing loss across all frequencies, though high frequencies are usually hit hardest
  • Typical audiogram: Mild-to-moderate loss in low and mid frequencies, moderate-to-severe loss in high frequencies, producing a characteristic sloping pattern
  • Cannot be surgically corrected: The damage is in the inner ear (cochlea), not the middle ear

2. Conductive Hearing Loss

Common in children with Marshall syndrome, particularly those with palatal involvement (cleft palate, submucous cleft, or Pierre Robin sequence). The middle ear cannot efficiently transmit sound to the inner ear due to:

  • Chronic otitis media with effusion (OME): Fluid buildup behind the eardrum, extremely common in children with palatal abnormalities because the Eustachian tubes do not function properly
  • Eustachian tube dysfunction: The midface hypoplasia characteristic of Marshall syndrome shortens and narrows the Eustachian tubes
  • Middle ear structural differences: Collagen XI is present in the ossicular chain and middle ear structures
Practical distinction: Conductive hearing loss from middle ear fluid is often treatable with ear tubes (tympanostomy tubes/grommets) and typically improves. Sensorineural loss from the tectorial membrane is permanent and progressive. Many children with Marshall syndrome have both at the same time, which is called mixed hearing loss. Treating the conductive component (getting tubes placed, draining fluid) can unmask the underlying sensorineural loss and clarify the true baseline hearing level.

3. Mixed Hearing Loss

A combination of sensorineural and conductive loss, which is common in Marshall syndrome. On an audiogram, this shows as a gap between bone conduction thresholds (which measure inner ear function) and air conduction thresholds (which measure the entire pathway). The “air-bone gap” represents the conductive component, and the bone conduction level represents the sensorineural floor.

Understanding the mix matters because the conductive component may be treatable (potentially improving hearing by 10–30 dB) while the sensorineural component requires amplification or implant strategies.

Hearing loss in Marshall syndrome is progressive and can change rapidly, especially during growth periods. Regular monitoring is the foundation of good management — you cannot treat what you do not detect.

Understanding the Audiogram: A Parent’s Guide

The audiogram is the primary hearing test. It plots the quietest sounds your child can hear at different pitches (frequencies). Here is what the numbers mean:

Important for Marshall syndrome: The typical COL11A1 audiogram shows a sloping pattern — mild-to-moderate loss in the low frequencies (250–1000 Hz) with moderate-to-severe loss in the high frequencies (2000–8000 Hz). This means your child may seem to hear “fine” in quiet settings (they can hear the low-pitched sounds of speech) but struggle significantly in noisy environments (the high-frequency consonant sounds — s, f, th, sh — that distinguish between similar words are lost). If your child seems to hear you at home but struggles at school, this is the classic COL11A1 pattern.

Beyond the Audiogram: Tests That Matter

  • Tympanometry: Measures middle ear pressure and eardrum mobility. Essential for detecting fluid (effusion) behind the eardrum, which is extremely common in Marshall children with palatal issues
  • Otoacoustic Emissions (OAE): Measures sounds produced by healthy outer hair cells. In Marshall syndrome, OAEs may be absent or reduced even when the audiogram shows only mild loss, because the defective tectorial membrane prevents normal OHC function
  • Auditory Brainstem Response (ABR): Measures the auditory nerve’s electrical response to sound. Critical for infants too young for behavioral testing. Also helps distinguish cochlear from neural hearing loss
  • Speech-in-noise testing: Measures ability to understand speech in background noise. Often the most sensitive functional test — it reveals difficulties that the pure-tone audiogram underestimates

Hearing Aids & Amplification Devices

Hearing aids are the first-line treatment for sensorineural hearing loss in Marshall syndrome. When fitted early and worn consistently, they protect the auditory cortex during the critical language development window and maintain the brain’s ability to process sound.

Types of Hearing Aids

Receiver-in-canal (RIC)Mild to moderately severe loss; older teens/adultsMore discreet, natural sound quality, open-fit option for sloping loss. May be suitable for teens concerned about cosmetics, but less durable for active children

Fitting Challenges Specific to Marshall Syndrome

Several features of Marshall syndrome create unique challenges for hearing aid fitting:

  • Midface hypoplasia: The flat midface and reduced nasal bridge common in Marshall syndrome can affect the fit of glasses-style hearing aids and behind-the-ear devices. Work with an audiologist experienced in craniofacial differences
  • Small or unusually shaped ear canals: Custom earmolds may need more frequent remaking. Deep-insertion molds may not be appropriate
  • Progressive loss: Choose hearing aids with a wide fitting range so they can accommodate worsening thresholds without needing to be replaced prematurely. A hearing aid fitted for mild loss today should have the power reserve to handle moderate loss in 2–3 years
  • Sloping audiogram: The typical COL11A1 pattern (good low frequencies, poor high frequencies) requires frequency-specific amplification. Modern digital hearing aids with open-fit or frequency-lowering technology can preserve the natural low-frequency hearing while boosting the high frequencies where loss is greatest
  • Ear tube compatibility: If your child has tympanostomy tubes, water protection during bathing and swimming is essential. Waterproof earplugs that fit over or around the hearing aid mold may be needed

When to Start

As soon as hearing loss is documented. The 1-3-6 guideline (screen by 1 month, diagnose by 3 months, intervene by 6 months) applies to every infant with a COL11A1 diagnosis. Early amplification during the first months of life protects the auditory cortex during its period of maximum plasticity. Infants who receive hearing aids before 6 months of age develop speech and language skills that parallel their normal-hearing peers. Delays beyond this window produce measurably worse outcomes that persist into school age and beyond.

Frequency-lowering technology. A relatively new hearing aid feature called frequency lowering (also called frequency compression or frequency transposition) takes high-frequency sounds that your child cannot hear and shifts them down into the frequency range where hearing is better. This is especially useful for the sloping audiogram typical of COL11A1, because it makes high-frequency consonant sounds (“s,” “f,” “th”) audible without over-amplifying the low frequencies where hearing is still relatively good. Ask your audiologist about this feature.

FM Systems & Remote Microphone Technology

Even the best hearing aids struggle in noisy environments — and a typical classroom is one of the noisiest environments your child will face. FM systems and remote microphone technology are essential supplements to hearing aids for school-age children.

How They Work

The teacher wears a small wireless microphone. The microphone transmits the teacher’s voice directly to the child’s hearing aids, bypassing the distance, background noise, and reverberation that normally degrade the speech signal. The result: the teacher’s voice arrives at the child’s ears at a consistent volume, as if they were standing right next to them.

Types of Systems

  • Traditional FM: Uses radio frequency transmission. Reliable, well-established, widely available through school districts
  • Digital remote microphone (Roger): Newer technology with better signal-to-noise ratio than traditional FM. Automatically adjusts to ambient noise levels. Compatible with most modern hearing aids and cochlear implants
  • Bluetooth LE Audio: Emerging standard that may eventually replace dedicated FM systems. Some newer hearing aids support direct streaming from Bluetooth-enabled devices
  • Soundfield systems: Speaker systems that amplify the teacher’s voice for the entire classroom. Benefits all students, not just those with hearing loss. Can be used alongside personal FM/Roger systems
Getting FM systems through school. Under IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act) and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, children with documented hearing loss are entitled to assistive listening technology in the classroom at no cost to the family. The FM system should be written into your child’s IEP or 504 plan. If the school pushes back, know that federal law is clear on this point. Your audiologist can provide a letter documenting the medical necessity.

Cochlear Implants

Cochlear implants (CIs) are surgically implanted devices that bypass the damaged cochlea entirely and directly stimulate the auditory nerve. They are the most effective intervention for severe-to-profound sensorineural hearing loss and become a consideration when hearing aids no longer provide sufficient benefit.

When Cochlear Implants Are Considered

  • Severe-to-profound SNHL (hearing thresholds >70–90 dB) where hearing aids provide limited speech understanding
  • Progressive loss reaching severe levels — a common trajectory in Marshall syndrome
  • Poor speech discrimination scores even with optimally fitted hearing aids
  • Age considerations: For children, implantation before age 3.5 produces significantly better speech perception and production outcomes than later implantation. Because Marshall syndrome hearing loss is progressive, monitoring must be frequent enough to detect the threshold for CI candidacy before this critical window closes

Why Cochlear Implants Make Particular Sense for COL11A1

The hearing loss mechanism in Marshall syndrome is fundamentally a tectorial membrane defect — the problem is in the mechanical coupling between the TM and the hair cells, not necessarily in the auditory nerve itself. Cochlear implants bypass the tectorial membrane entirely by electrically stimulating the auditory nerve directly. This means the CI sidesteps the exact structure that is defective, making it a particularly logical intervention when the TM has degraded beyond useful function.

Surgical Considerations Specific to Marshall Syndrome

Vestibular risks in COL11A1. Collagen XI is present not only in the cochlea but also in the vestibular system (the balance organs of the inner ear). Many individuals with Marshall syndrome already have some degree of vestibular dysfunction. Cochlear implant surgery carries a known risk of damaging vestibular function, and this risk may be elevated in individuals with pre-existing vestibular vulnerability from collagen XI deficiency.
  • Pre-operative vestibular assessment is essential: Before CI surgery, a full vestibular evaluation (caloric testing, vHIT, VEMP, and posturography) should establish baseline function. If vestibular function is already compromised in the ear being considered for implantation, this influences the risk-benefit calculation and may affect which ear is implanted
  • Connective tissue fragility: The dural and perilymphatic membranes may be more fragile due to collagen deficiency. Surgeons should be prepared for potential perilymph gusher and use minimally invasive insertion techniques to preserve residual function
  • Anesthesia considerations: All surgical anesthesia considerations for Marshall syndrome apply — see the Anesthesia Safety page. Key concerns include difficult airway (midface hypoplasia, micrognathia) and cervical spine instability
  • Round window vs. cochleostomy: Round window insertion is generally preferred as it is less traumatic, but cochlear anatomy may be affected by the COL11A1 mutation. Preoperative high-resolution CT can identify anatomical variations
  • Bilateral consideration: If vestibular function is already impaired bilaterally, simultaneous bilateral CI may be considered to avoid two separate surgeries, but this must be weighed against the risk of bilateral vestibular damage

Hybrid Cochlear Implants

For individuals with the classic COL11A1 sloping audiogram — useful low-frequency hearing but severe high-frequency loss — hybrid (electroacoustic) cochlear implants may be an option. These devices combine a short electrode array in the cochlea (for electrical stimulation of high frequencies) with acoustic amplification (a hearing aid component for the preserved low frequencies). This preserves the natural sound quality of the low frequencies while providing access to the high-frequency consonant sounds that are critical for speech understanding.

Tinnitus Management

Tinnitus — the perception of ringing, buzzing, or hissing when no external sound is present — is common in Marshall syndrome. In individuals with sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus prevalence rises to 70–85%. The same tectorial membrane defect that causes hearing loss also creates the conditions for chronic tinnitus.

Why Tinnitus Happens

When the defective tectorial membrane reduces the cochlear signal reaching the brain, the central auditory system responds by increasing its sensitivity — essentially “turning up the volume” to compensate for the weaker peripheral input. This compensatory hyperactivity is perceived as tinnitus. The brain is amplifying its own internal noise because the external signal has become too quiet.

What Helps

Sound Therapy

The most effective first-line approach for tinnitus, particularly in children. The principle is simple: provide background sound that reduces the contrast between tinnitus and silence. Options include:

  • White noise machines or apps at bedtime and during quiet study
  • Nature sounds (rain, ocean, running water) played at a level just below the tinnitus
  • Hearing aid masking features: Most modern hearing aids include tinnitus masking programs that generate low-level broadband sound. Because many Marshall patients already wear hearing aids, this is often the most convenient approach
  • Notched sound therapy: A newer technique where music or sound is filtered to remove the specific frequency of the tinnitus. Research suggests this can retrain the auditory cortex to reduce tinnitus perception over time

Cognitive and Behavioral Approaches

  • Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT): Combines counseling with sound therapy to promote habituation — the process of learning to ignore the tinnitus signal. In children, TRT achieves positive results in approximately 90% of cases after 18 months of treatment
  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Addresses the emotional distress and sleep disruption that tinnitus can cause. Particularly important for adolescents, who may be more distressed by tinnitus than younger children
  • Counseling and reassurance: For many children, simply understanding why they hear the sound — and that it is not dangerous — significantly reduces distress. Children often do not report tinnitus unless directly asked, because they assume everyone hears it

What Does NOT Help

  • Silence: Quiet environments make tinnitus more noticeable. Avoid silence at bedtime — use background sound
  • Anxiety about the sound: The more attention and worry directed at tinnitus, the louder it seems. Breaking the attention cycle is the core of TRT and CBT approaches
  • Ototoxic medications: Some medications (aminoglycoside antibiotics, cisplatin, high-dose aspirin, loop diuretics) can worsen both hearing loss and tinnitus. Review all medications with your audiologist and otolaryngologist — the Emergency Protocol Card includes a list of ototoxic drugs to avoid
Ask your child about tinnitus. Many children with Marshall syndrome do not spontaneously report tinnitus because they have experienced it their entire lives and do not realize it is abnormal. During hearing evaluations, always ask directly: “Do you ever hear sounds like ringing, buzzing, or humming when it’s quiet?” Early identification allows early management.

School & Communication

Hearing loss affects every aspect of a child’s school experience — from hearing the teacher, to socializing with peers, to participating in fire drills. Proactive planning makes the difference between a child who struggles silently and one who thrives.

IEP and 504 Accommodations

Children with documented hearing loss qualify for accommodations under federal law. Key accommodations to request:

  • Preferential seating: Front and center, with the better-hearing ear toward the teacher. Away from noise sources (air conditioners, hallways, windows)
  • FM/remote microphone system: For all classes, assemblies, and field trips. The system follows the child, not the classroom
  • Visual aids: Written instructions to supplement verbal directions. Captions on all videos and media
  • Note-taking support: A classmate note-taker or teacher-provided notes, because the child cannot simultaneously watch the teacher’s face (for lipreading cues) and write notes
  • Reduced background noise: Tennis balls on chair legs, carpet or rugs in the classroom, acoustic panels
  • Testing accommodations: Extended time, quiet testing room, oral administration of test questions if needed
  • Speech-language therapy: School-based SLP services to support speech clarity, vocabulary development, and auditory comprehension
  • Check-in protocol: Teachers confirm understanding by asking the child to repeat instructions, not just “Did you hear that?” (children with hearing loss almost always say yes)
  • Emergency procedures: Visual fire alarms, buddy system for announcements

Speech Therapy

Hearing loss during the language-development years can affect speech clarity, vocabulary, and reading skills. Key areas of focus:

  • Articulation: High-frequency consonant sounds (s, f, th, sh) are the first affected by sloping hearing loss. Targeted articulation therapy can maintain clear speech even as hearing changes
  • Auditory training: Structured practice in listening to and discriminating speech sounds, particularly in background noise
  • Language enrichment: Children with hearing loss often develop vocabulary at a slower pace than hearing peers. Deliberate vocabulary instruction closes the gap
  • Self-advocacy skills: Teaching the child to recognize when they have not heard something and to ask for repetition — a critical life skill

Sign Language Considerations

For most children with Marshall syndrome, hearing loss is mild to moderate and well-managed with hearing aids and FM systems — sign language is typically not needed as a primary communication method. However, there are situations where sign language exposure is valuable:

  • Baby sign language (birth to age 2) provides communication before spoken language develops and reduces frustration during the early amplification period
  • Progressive loss to severe/profound: If hearing deteriorates to the point where spoken language comprehension is significantly impaired, sign language provides a communication backup while cochlear implant evaluation proceeds
  • Social connection: Knowing some sign language connects your child to the Deaf/Hard-of-Hearing community, which can provide peer support and identity resources

Gene Therapy Horizon: The Cochlea as a Target

The cochlea has emerged as one of the most promising targets for gene therapy in all of medicine. Its small, enclosed anatomy, limited immune surveillance, and accessible delivery routes make it uniquely suited to genetic intervention. While no gene therapy is currently available for COL11A1 hearing loss, the field is advancing rapidly.

Current State of Cochlear Gene Therapy

Proof of concept is established. A 2024 Frontiers in Audiology and Otology review confirmed that gene therapy for hereditary hearing loss has moved from concept to clinical reality. Gene therapy for OTOF mutations (otoferlin deficiency) has demonstrated rapid and stable restoration of hearing in clinical trials, with data from 21 patients showing improvement from complete hearing loss (>100 dB) to moderate levels (~50 dB) on average. This is the first gene therapy to restore hearing in humans.

Key developments relevant to Marshall syndrome:

  • Delivery routes are proven: Round window injection and intracochlear delivery of viral vectors (AAV) can reach the target cells in the inner ear
  • Hair cell transduction is possible: AAV vectors can transfect both inner and outer hair cells in the cochlea
  • The structural challenge: OTOF gene therapy works by replacing a missing protein in hair cells. COL11A1 hearing loss involves a structural defect in the tectorial membrane — a fundamentally different problem. Delivering functional COL11A1 to restore tectorial membrane architecture would require transducing the cells that produce and maintain the TM, and the structural collagen would need to integrate into the existing (malformed) matrix
  • Allele-specific silencing may be more feasible: Rather than replacing the gene, silencing the mutant allele (as described on the Gene Therapy: 2026 Update page) could prevent the dominant-negative protein from disrupting normal collagen XI. Whether this can arrest or reverse TM degradation in the cochlea is an open question
  • Timeline: Cochlear gene therapy for monogenic (single-gene) hearing loss conditions is in clinical trials now. Extension to structural collagen disorders like COL11A1 is likely 7–15 years away, pending proof-of-concept in tectorial membrane collagen delivery

Practical Tips: Protecting Residual Hearing

Every decibel of residual hearing matters. Protecting what your child has is as important as treating what has been lost.

Noise Exposure Limits

Because the tectorial membrane is already structurally compromised, the standard noise exposure limits that apply to healthy ears are not conservative enough for Marshall syndrome. Recommended limits:

110+ dBSirens, firecrackers, sporting events2 minutesAvoid entirely
  • Headphone/earbud rule: Volume-limited headphones (maximum 75–80 dB) for any audio device. Many children’s headphones have built-in volume limiters. For teens, noise-cancelling headphones allow lower volume use by eliminating the need to turn up volume to overcome background noise
  • Concerts and events: Custom musician’s earplugs (flat-attenuation, made from ear impressions) reduce volume evenly across frequencies without distorting sound quality. Far superior to foam plugs for someone who already has hearing loss
  • Sports and recreation: Waterproof ear protection for swimming (especially with ear tubes). Helmets with ear protection for cycling, skateboarding, and similar activities

Water Precautions

If your child has ear tubes (tympanostomy tubes), water entering the middle ear through the tubes can cause infection:

  • Use custom-fitted waterproof earplugs for swimming, bathing, and water play
  • A neoprene headband over the earplugs provides an additional barrier
  • Avoid diving and jumping into water feet-first (water pressure can force water through the tubes)
  • Surface swimming with plugs and headband is generally safe — confirm with your ENT

Medication Awareness: Ototoxic Drugs

Several categories of medication can damage hearing, and individuals with pre-existing hearing loss are more vulnerable:

  • Aminoglycoside antibiotics (gentamicin, tobramycin, amikacin): Use only when no alternative exists. Request serum level monitoring. The Emergency Protocol Card flags these
  • Loop diuretics (furosemide/Lasix): Can cause temporary or permanent hearing loss, especially with IV administration or in combination with aminoglycosides
  • Cisplatin and carboplatin: Chemotherapy agents with known ototoxicity. If ever needed, baseline and serial audiometry are mandatory
  • High-dose aspirin or NSAIDs: Can cause temporary tinnitus and hearing threshold shifts at high doses

When to Seek Urgent Evaluation

See an audiologist or ENT promptly (within days, not weeks) if your child experiences:

  • Sudden hearing change — any noticeable drop in hearing over hours to days. Sudden sensorineural hearing loss (SSNHL) is a medical emergency that may respond to steroid treatment if caught within 72 hours
  • New or worsening tinnitus — especially if accompanied by hearing change, dizziness, or ear fullness
  • Vertigo or dizziness — may indicate vestibular involvement (see the Vestibular Dysfunction page)
  • Ear pain or drainage — possible infection, especially in children with ear tubes
  • Regression in speech clarity — a child whose articulation was clear but is becoming less distinct may be losing high-frequency hearing

Building Your Hearing Team

Hearing management in Marshall syndrome requires a coordinated team. The core members:

  • Pediatric audiologist: The central figure in hearing care. Performs all testing, fits and programs hearing aids, recommends FM systems, and monitors progression. Choose an audiologist with experience in pediatric sensorineural hearing loss and syndromic conditions
  • Otolaryngologist (ENT): Manages ear tubes, evaluates for middle ear disease, and refers for cochlear implant evaluation when indicated. An ENT familiar with connective tissue disorders understands the surgical nuances
  • Speech-language pathologist (SLP): Provides speech therapy, auditory training, and language support. School-based SLP services should supplement private therapy
  • Teacher of the Deaf/Hard of Hearing: Many school districts have specialists who support children with hearing loss in mainstream classrooms, including FM system management and accommodation planning
  • Clinical geneticist: Coordinates the overall Marshall syndrome care plan and ensures hearing management is integrated with eye, spine, and joint monitoring
The dual sensory concern. Many individuals with Marshall syndrome have both hearing loss and vision impairment (high myopia, retinal detachment risk). This dual sensory vulnerability makes hearing preservation and optimization even more critical — if vision is compromised, the remaining hearing becomes even more important for safety, communication, and quality of life. Regular auditory follow-up is strongly advised precisely because of this dual vulnerability. Ensure your audiologist and ophthalmologist communicate with each other.

Key Takeaways

  • Hearing loss affects 82%+ of individuals with COL11A1 mutations. It is one of the most common and most manageable features of Marshall syndrome
  • The cause is structural: Defective type XI collagen in the tectorial membrane disrupts the coupling between sound vibrations and sensory hair cells
  • Loss is progressive: Expect worsening over time. Monitor every 6–12 months. Accelerate monitoring during puberty and growth spurts
  • Intervene early: Follow the 1-3-6 guideline. Hearing aids by 6 months of age if loss is detected at birth. Early amplification protects language development
  • FM systems are essential at school, not optional. Federal law requires schools to provide them at no cost
  • Protect residual hearing: Noise limits, volume-limited headphones, ototoxic drug avoidance, and water precautions for ear tubes
  • Ask about tinnitus: Many children do not report it spontaneously
  • Cochlear implants bypass the defective TM and are a logical option when hearing aids are no longer sufficient. Pre-operative vestibular assessment is essential due to COL11A1 involvement in the vestibular system
  • Gene therapy is emerging for hereditary hearing loss. Clinical trials for other forms of genetic hearing loss are underway. COL11A1-specific cochlear gene therapy is likely 7–15 years away
