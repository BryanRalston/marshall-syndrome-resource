Gene Therapy: 2026 Update

Medical Disclaimer
This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Gene therapy for collagen disorders is in various stages of research and clinical development. None of the approaches described here are currently approved for Marshall syndrome. Discuss emerging therapies with a clinical geneticist or connective tissue specialist.

The gene therapy landscape has shifted dramatically in the past 18 months. Multiple proof-of-concept successes in related collagen disorders, combined with a transformative FDA regulatory pathway change in February 2026, have moved targeted collagen gene correction from “someday” to “plausible within a decade.” This page synthesizes the current state as of early 2026.

Proven Technologies: What Works Now

Vyjuvek (beremagene geperpavec) — FDA Approved

Vyjuvek is the first FDA-approved gene therapy for any collagen disorder. It delivers functional COL7A1 via a topical herpes simplex virus vector for dystrophic epidermolysis bullosa (DEB). While COL7A1 is not the gene affected in Marshall syndrome, Vyjuvek establishes critical precedents:

  • Regulatory proof that collagen gene delivery can achieve FDA approval
  • Demonstrated safety of viral vector-mediated collagen gene delivery in humans
  • Manufacturing pipeline for clinical-grade collagen gene therapy vectors
  • Insurance coverage pathways for rare collagen disorders

Allele-Specific CRISPR for COL6A1

Researchers have demonstrated allele-specific CRISPR editing for COL6A1 dominant-negative mutations, achieving >80% mutant allele knockdown while preserving the normal allele. This is directly relevant to Marshall syndrome because:

  • COL11A1 (the Marshall gene) has the same dominant-negative pathology as COL6A1 — the mutant protein poisons the normal protein’s function
  • The allele-specific CRISPR strategy (target a mutation-specific PAM site or SNP) is transferable across collagen genes
  • Selective knockdown of the mutant allele converts a dominant-negative disorder into haploinsufficiency — which is substantially milder

siRNA Proven for Multiple Collagen Genes

Small interfering RNA (siRNA) has demonstrated successful knockdown of mutant alleles for COL1A1, COL1A2, COL3A1, and COL6A1. siRNA advantages include:

  • No permanent genomic modification — effects are reversible (safety advantage)
  • Can be re-dosed and dose-adjusted
  • Lipid nanoparticle delivery technology is mature (mRNA vaccine infrastructure)
  • Allele-specificity achievable through single-nucleotide discrimination

Emerging Approaches

Ex Vivo iPSC Correction Pipeline

The iPSC (induced pluripotent stem cell) correction approach represents the most comprehensive potential cure:

  1. Harvest patient cells (skin biopsy or blood draw)
  2. Reprogram to iPSCs
  3. Correct the COL11A1 mutation via CRISPR in vitro (where efficiency and off-target effects can be fully characterized)
  4. Differentiate corrected iPSCs into nucleus pulposus (disc) cells or chondrocytes
  5. Implant corrected cells into target tissues

Each step in this pipeline has been independently demonstrated. The integration into a single clinical workflow is the remaining challenge.

mRNA Therapy

Transient delivery of functional COL11A1 mRNA could provide temporary but meaningful collagen XI production in target tissues:

  • No genomic modification required
  • COVID vaccine infrastructure provides manufacturing scale
  • Challenge: achieving sustained delivery to avascular disc tissue
  • Potential role as a “bridge therapy” while definitive approaches mature

CRISPRa “Silence-and-Boost” Combined Strategy

A novel two-pronged approach combining:

  • Silence: Allele-specific CRISPR or siRNA to knock down the dominant-negative mutant allele
  • Boost: CRISPRa (CRISPR activation) to upregulate expression of the remaining normal allele, compensating for haploinsufficiency

This combined strategy addresses both aspects of the dominant-negative pathology: eliminating the toxic protein and increasing normal protein production. Early data in COL6A1 models show promise.

FDA Plausible Mechanism Pathway (February 2026)

Regulatory game-changer. In February 2026, the FDA finalized guidance on the Plausible Mechanism Pathway for ultra-rare diseases (<2,000 patients). This pathway allows accelerated approval based on mechanistic plausibility rather than large randomized controlled trials — which are impossible for diseases as rare as Marshall syndrome.

Key features of the Plausible Mechanism Pathway:

  • Requires demonstration of biological plausibility (molecular mechanism), not statistical significance in large trials
  • Accepts biomarker endpoints rather than clinical endpoints for initial approval
  • N-of-1 or small cohort trial designs are sufficient
  • Post-marketing surveillance replaces traditional Phase III trials
  • Specifically designed for genetic diseases with known causal mutations

Marshall syndrome — with its known COL11A1 mutation, clear molecular mechanism, and population of approximately 1,500–2,000 worldwide — is an ideal candidate for this pathway.

BANK CELLS NOW

Critical action item — do this regardless of timeline. Bank your child’s cells (skin fibroblasts or PBMCs from blood) as soon as possible. Younger cells have fewer accumulated mutations, shorter telomeres, and a younger epigenetic profile. The cells banked today will be the starting material for any future iPSC correction, gene therapy vector testing, or personalized medicine approach. Cryopreservation costs are typically $500–2,000 for initial banking plus $100–300/year for storage. Several biobanking services accept samples from children.

Why banking now matters:

  • Youngest epigenetic state: Every year of aging adds epigenetic modifications that reduce iPSC reprogramming efficiency
  • Fewest somatic mutations: Cells accumulate approximately 40 new mutations per year. Banking at age 8 means ~320 fewer background mutations than banking at age 16.
  • Future-proofing: Technologies that do not exist yet will require patient cells. Having banked cells ensures access to any future therapy regardless of the technology used.
  • Research participation: Banked cells can be provided to researchers working on COL11A1 therapies, accelerating development for the entire Marshall community.

What This Means Today

Gene therapy for Marshall syndrome is not imminent, but it is no longer speculative. The practical implications for families today are:

  1. Bank cells now — the single most impactful action you can take for future treatment access
  2. Protect the discs — every year of preserved disc health is a year closer to definitive therapy
  3. Register in clinical databases — ClinicalTrials.gov, NORD Patient Registry, GeneMatcher
  4. Connect with research groups — labs working on collagen gene therapy need Marshall patients and families
  5. The Plausible Mechanism Pathway changes everything — ultra-rare diseases are no longer regulatory orphans
