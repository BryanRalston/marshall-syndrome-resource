Rare Disease Advocacy

Families affected by Marshall syndrome face the challenge of advocating for a condition most doctors have never encountered.

FDA Plausible Mechanism Framework (February 2026)

New Opportunity: The FDA's updated Plausible Mechanism Framework allows approval based on mechanistic evidence rather than large-scale trials, which are impossible for ultra-rare conditions.
  • Drugs addressing the specific molecular mechanism could be approved without traditional RCTs
  • Existing FDA-approved drugs (like 4-PBA) could be repurposed with strong mechanistic rationale
  • N-of-1 trial data combined with mechanism may suffice for expanded access

N=1 Collaborative (Boston Children's Hospital)

  • Designs patient-specific therapies based on exact mutation
  • Successfully developed ASO therapies for single patients
  • Accepts referrals for patients with identified genetic mutations
  • Website: n1collaborative.org

NORD (National Organization for Rare Disorders)

  • Patient Assistance Programs for medication costs
  • Research grants and advocacy training
  • Website: rarediseases.org

Stickler Syndrome Support Group

  • Most relevant patient community (both are COL11A1 disorders)
  • Annual conference, family networking, specialist referral list
  • Website: stickler.org.uk

NIH Undiagnosed Diseases Program (UDP)

  • Free evaluation at NIH Clinical Center
  • Multidisciplinary team + genomic sequencing
  • Can identify additional genetic modifiers affecting severity
  • Referral by physician required
  • Website: NIH UDP

Research Funding Pathways

Rare disease research is chronically underfunded relative to disease burden. For ultra-rare conditions like Marshall syndrome, where pharmaceutical industry interest is minimal due to small patient populations, the path to research funding typically starts with patient organizations and public funding mechanisms. Understanding these pathways empowers families to actively contribute to the research ecosystem rather than waiting passively for progress.

Key Organizations and Funding Sources

NORD (National Organization for Rare Disorders) Patient assistance programs, research grants (up to $100K), advocacy training, policy lobbying NORD research grants can fund pilot studies — the small-scale investigations that generate preliminary data needed to apply for larger NIH grants. NORD also provides advocacy training for families who want to become effective voices for their condition.

Families can advocate for rare disease research funding, FDA pathway reforms, and insurance coverage for genetic therapies.
RDCRN (Rare Diseases Clinical Research Network) NIH-funded network of research consortia studying rare diseases; provides infrastructure for multi-site clinical studies RDCRN consortia include connective tissue disorder research groups. Patient registries within RDCRN provide the natural history data essential for designing future clinical trials. Enrolling in an RDCRN registry contributes directly to the evidence base.
Global Genes Patient advocacy toolkit, RARE Champions of Hope program, community building, advocacy resources Global Genes provides structured programs to train patient advocates and connect them with the rare disease research and policy community. Their toolkit is practical and actionable for families starting from zero advocacy experience.
NCATS (National Center for Advancing Translational Sciences) Translational research funding, drug repurposing programs, Therapeutics for Rare and Neglected Diseases (TRND) NCATS specifically funds the translation of basic science into treatments for rare diseases. Their drug repurposing program identifies existing approved drugs that may have therapeutic potential for rare conditions — directly relevant to the Drug Repurposing approaches being explored for Marshall syndrome.

SIP (Stickler Involved People)

Stickler Involved People is the primary family support and advocacy organization for Stickler syndrome, which shares the COL11A1 genetic basis with Marshall syndrome. SIP provides:

  • Annual family conference with specialist presentations and networking
  • Connection to researchers studying COL11A1-related disorders
  • A community of families navigating overlapping medical challenges (retinal detachment, hearing loss, joint problems, midface hypoplasia)
  • Patient registry participation that contributes to natural history studies

Because Marshall syndrome and Stickler syndrome are closely related COL11A1 disorders, research advances in one directly benefit the other. SIP is the most immediately relevant patient organization for Marshall syndrome families.

How Patient Organizations Drive Research

For ultra-rare conditions, the sequence from no research to funded clinical trials typically follows a specific pathway that patient organizations catalyze:

  1. Patient organizations fund pilot studies ($25K–$100K) — these small grants from organizations like NORD generate the preliminary data that researchers need to write competitive grant applications for larger funding sources.
  2. Pilot data enables NIH R21/R01 applications — NIH requires preliminary data for most grant mechanisms. The pilot study results become the foundation for multi-year, multi-hundred-thousand-dollar federal grants.
  3. Patient registries provide natural history data — the FDA and clinical trial designers need to know the natural course of a disease to design meaningful endpoints. Every family that participates in a registry builds this essential evidence base.
  4. Every participating family builds the evidence base — in ultra-rare conditions, each patient’s documented clinical course, genetic data, and treatment response is a meaningful data point. The difference between a treatable condition and an untreatable one often comes down to whether enough families have contributed their data to make research feasible.
Rare Disease Day is observed annually on the last day of February (February 28, or February 29 in leap years — the rarest day for the rarest diseases). Participation ranges from social media awareness campaigns to organized events at medical centers and legislative offices. It is a concrete opportunity to make Marshall syndrome visible to the broader medical and research community.

Building Your Advocacy Toolkit

  1. Create a one-page medical summary — mutation, key findings, treatments, emergency considerations
  2. Register in rare disease databases — GeneMatcher, NORD registry, ClinVar
  3. Connect with researchers — authors of relevant publications are often eager to hear from families
  4. Document everything — photos, imaging, labs, symptom diaries become future data points
  5. Consider genetic counseling
  6. Attend Rare Disease Day (last day of February)
  7. Attend legislative conferences — the EveryLife Foundation RDLA conference and similar events provide direct access to policy makers. Even a single family telling their story to a legislator can influence funding decisions.
  8. Join or start a patient organization — SIP (Stickler Involved People) is the most relevant existing organization. If Marshall syndrome-specific advocacy is needed, NORD provides guidance on starting a new patient organization.
