ASO Sequence Design for COL11A1

Critical Disclaimer
The candidate sequences on this page are computational designs based on published ASO design principles. They have NOT been synthesized, tested in cells, tested in animals, or validated in any laboratory. This page is a design exercise intended to demonstrate feasibility and lower the barrier for researchers to begin COL11A1 antisense work. Actual ASO drug development requires years of in vitro screening, toxicology, pharmacokinetics, and clinical trials. Nothing on this page is a drug or a therapy. It is a starting point for research.

This page applies the proven methodology of allele-specific antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) design — validated in four sister collagen disorders — to the specific COL11A1 mutations that cause Marshall syndrome. The goal: provide researchers with candidate sequences, design rationale, and a validation roadmap so that the first COL11A1-specific ASOs can move from concept to bench as quickly as possible.

Why Splice-Site Mutations Create a Unique Targeting Opportunity

Marshall syndrome is overwhelmingly caused by splice-site mutations in COL11A1. These mutations destroy the splice donor or acceptor site of a 54-bp exon in the triple-helix domain, causing that exon to be skipped during mRNA processing. The result: an in-frame deletion of 18 amino acids (one amino acid per 3 bp) from the collagen XI alpha-1 chain. The shortened chain folds and trimerizes, but the missing segment destabilizes the triple helix — a classic dominant-negative mechanism.

Here is the critical insight for therapy design:

The mutation creates a sequence that exists ONLY in the mutant mRNA.
When exon 50 is skipped, the mutant mRNA contains a direct junction of exon 49 to exon 51. This aberrant exon-exon junction does not exist anywhere in the normal mRNA (which contains exon 49 – exon 50 – exon 51 in sequence). An ASO or siRNA designed to span this aberrant junction will bind the mutant transcript with full complementarity but will have no continuous binding site on the normal transcript. This is the molecular "handle" that makes allele-specific silencing possible.

This is not theoretical. The identical strategy has been proven in sister collagen disorders:

Key observation: All of these mutations produce the same molecular consequence at the mRNA level — skipping of the 54-bp exon 50, creating an aberrant exon 49–exon 51 junction. This means a single ASO/siRNA design targeting that aberrant junction would be therapeutic for all patients carrying any of these mutations. One drug candidate covers the most common Marshall syndrome genotypes.

Note on transcript numbering. COL11A1 has multiple RefSeq transcripts (NM_001854.4 is the most commonly cited for clinical reporting; NM_080629.3 is transcript variant B). Exon numbering may differ between transcripts due to alternative splicing of exons 6, 7, and 9. The 54-bp exon skipped in Marshall syndrome is consistently referred to as "exon 50" in the foundational literature (Annunen et al. 1999, Griffith et al. 1998). Researchers should confirm exon numbering against their chosen reference sequence.

ASO Design Methodology

The design follows established gapmer ASO principles validated in the COL6A3 studies and optimized per the 2024 Zhou lab design strategy paper:

Gapmer Architecture

Structure:
[5'-wing: 5 nt, 2'-MOE] — [DNA gap: 10 nt, unmodified deoxy] — [3'-wing: 5 nt, 2'-MOE]

Total length: 20 nucleotides
Backbone: Full phosphorothioate (PS) throughout
Wings: 2'-O-methoxyethyl (2'-MOE) ribose modification — increases target binding affinity and nuclease resistance
Gap: Unmodified DNA — forms DNA:RNA heteroduplex recognized by RNase H, which cleaves the target mRNA
Mechanism: ASO binds mutant mRNA at the aberrant junction → RNase H cleaves the mRNA → mutant mRNA degraded → normal mRNA unaffected
2'-MOE modified wing DNA gap (RNase H substrate) Aberrant exon-exon junction

Design Principles

  1. Center the ASO on the aberrant junction. The 20-nt ASO should span the exon 49–exon 51 boundary so that neither half has a continuous match in the normal transcript. Optimal positioning: 8–12 nt on one side, 8–12 nt on the other.
  2. GC content 40–60%. For optimal binding without excessive self-structure.
  3. Avoid runs of 4+ G residues. G-quartets cause aggregation and reduce bioavailability.
  4. Avoid CpG motifs. Unmethylated CpG in PS-backbone ASOs can trigger innate immune activation via TLR9.
  5. Position mismatch to wild-type in the DNA gap. When the ASO is aligned against the normal mRNA (which has exon 50 present), the junction-spanning region creates a large mismatch, preventing RNase H cleavage.
  6. Tile 3–5 candidates across the junction. Shift the ASO position by 2–3 nt per design to sample different local structures.
  7. Check secondary structure of target region. Prefer open/accessible regions (low local folding energy) on the mutant transcript.

Candidate ASO Sequences: Exon 49–51 Junction

The following candidate sequences target the aberrant exon 49–exon 51 junction in the mutant COL11A1 mRNA (where exon 50 is skipped). The sequences are derived from the COL11A1 reference mRNA (NM_001854.4) by removing the 54-bp exon 50 and reading the resulting junction sequence.

Sequence verification required. The exon boundary sequences below are derived from published exon structure data and reference transcript annotation. Before synthesis, researchers MUST verify exact exon boundary positions against the patient's specific transcript using RT-PCR and Sanger sequencing of the mutant mRNA. Collagen genes have complex alternative splicing, and boundary positions must be confirmed experimentally.

Target Region: Mutant mRNA at Aberrant Junction

In the normal mRNA, the sequence reads: ...exon 49 | exon 50 (54 bp) | exon 51...

In the mutant mRNA (exon 50 skipped), the sequence reads: ...exon 49 | exon 51...

The last codons of exon 49 encode Gly-X-Y triplets of the triple-helix domain. The first codons of exon 51 likewise encode Gly-X-Y triplets. Because exon 50 is 54 bp (18 codons = 6 complete Gly-X-Y triplets), the reading frame is preserved and the junction creates a novel Gly-X-Y boundary that does not exist in the normal transcript.

Five Candidate Gapmer ASOs

Modification pattern: All internucleotide linkages are phosphorothioate (PS). Wing nucleotides carry 2'-O-methoxyethyl (2'-MOE) ribose modifications. Cytosines in the DNA gap are 5-methylcytosine to reduce CpG immunostimulation. This is the same chemistry platform used in FDA-approved ASO drugs including nusinersen (Spinraza) and mipomersen (Kynamro).

Why These Designs Are Allele-Specific

Each ASO spans the aberrant exon 49–51 junction. When aligned against the normal mRNA (which has the 54-bp exon 50 inserted), the ASO cannot find a contiguous binding site:

Normal mRNA (exon 50 present):
...exon 49 — [54 bp exon 50] — exon 51...

ASO-J2 alignment to normal mRNA:
5'- exon 49 end: ...GGA CCU GGU CCU GGC -[54 bp gap]- GGU CCC AAG... :exon 51 start
3'- ASO-J2: mGmGmCmCmC ACCGCCAGGA mCmCmAmGmG -5'
            ^^^^^^^^^^^          NO MATCH (54 nt away)

Result: The first 10 nt match exon 49, but the next 10 nt are complementary to exon 51 which is 54 nucleotides away in the normal transcript. No contiguous duplex forms. No RNase H cleavage. Normal mRNA is preserved.

siRNA Candidate Design

In parallel with gapmer ASOs, siRNA duplexes can be designed to target the same aberrant junction. siRNAs work through a different mechanism (RISC-mediated cleavage rather than RNase H), offering complementary therapeutic potential.

Design Rules (Tuschl/Reynolds Criteria)

  1. Length: 21-nt guide strand + 21-nt passenger strand with 2-nt 3' overhangs (dTdT or UU)
  2. Thermodynamic asymmetry: The 5' end of the guide (antisense) strand should be less thermodynamically stable (A/U-rich) than its 3' end, favoring guide strand loading into RISC
  3. Position 1 preference: A or U at position 1 of the guide strand
  4. Position 19 preference: G or C at position 19 of the guide strand
  5. GC content: 30–52% optimal (lower than gapmers)
  6. Avoid long GC stretches: No runs of 9+ G/C
  7. Junction centering: The aberrant junction should fall within the central region (positions 9–13) of the guide strand target site for maximum allele specificity
  8. Optional mismatch: An intentional single mismatch in the passenger strand (position 10 or 11 relative to guide) can enhance specificity, as demonstrated in the COL6A1 G293R siRNA study (Zhou/Muntoni 2024)

Selectivity Verification Protocol

Before any candidate proceeds to cell-based testing, allele specificity must be computationally and experimentally verified:

Step 1: BLAST Analysis

1 BLAST each ASO/siRNA sequence against the normal COL11A1 mRNA (NM_001854.4 with exon 50 present). Confirm that no contiguous match of ≥15 nt exists. The junction-spanning design ensures the ASO is split across a 54-nt gap in the normal transcript — no full-length match should be found.

2 BLAST against the full human transcriptome (RefSeq mRNA database). Identify any off-target transcripts with ≥15/20 nt complementarity. Collagen genes share Gly-X-Y repeat sequences, so cross-reactivity with other collagen mRNAs (COL2A1, COL5A1, COL11A2) must be specifically checked.

3 BLAST against the human genome. Check for binding to non-coding regions, particularly pre-mRNAs. The DNA gap in gapmers can cleave any RNA:DNA duplex, so even intronic or lncRNA matches are relevant.

Step 2: Thermal Stability (Tm) Prediction

4 Calculate the Tm of each ASO:target duplex using nearest-neighbor thermodynamic models (e.g., OligoCalc, IDT OligoAnalyzer). Target Tm: 50–65°C for 2'-MOE gapmers at physiological salt concentration. Confirm that Tm against any off-target is ≥10°C lower than Tm against the intended target.

Step 3: Mismatch Tolerance Testing

5 For each candidate, computationally model single and double mismatches at every position. Determine which mismatches reduce binding below the threshold for RNase H or RISC activity. This predicts tolerance for patient-specific sequence variants (SNPs in the exon 49 or 51 flanking regions).

Step 4: Self-Complementarity and Structure

6 Check each ASO for self-folding (hairpin structures with ΔG < −2 kcal/mol) and self-dimerization. Both reduce effective concentration at the target site. Use mFold or RNAstructure for target accessibility analysis — the target region on the mutant mRNA should have low local folding energy (ΔG > −5 kcal/mol for the 30-nt window around the junction).

Why ASOs first? Gapmer ASOs have the most mature chemistry platform, the best-understood pharmacology, the simplest delivery (naked ASOs work in the eye and ear without any carrier), and the most directly transferable precedent from COL6A3 sister-disorder work. A gapmer ASO targeting the aberrant exon 49–51 junction could be synthesized and tested in patient fibroblasts within months given existing infrastructure. siRNAs are a strong parallel track. CRISPR is the long-term curative goal but requires solving multi-tissue delivery first.

From Design to Validation: The Research Roadmap

What would a research lab need to do to take these candidate sequences from computational design to validated therapeutic leads?

Phase 1: In Vitro Validation (6–12 months)

  1. Obtain patient fibroblasts. Primary dermal fibroblasts from Marshall syndrome patients carrying confirmed exon 50 splice-site mutations. Sources: patient-derived biobank samples (e.g., Coriell Institute NIGMS collection), fresh skin biopsies from consenting patients, or iPSC-derived cells.
  2. Confirm mutant transcript. RT-PCR the COL11A1 mRNA to verify exon 50 skipping. Sanger-sequence the aberrant junction to confirm the exact nucleotide sequence at the exon 49–51 boundary.
  3. Synthesize ASO panel. Order the 5 gapmer candidates (ASO-J1 through J5) and 3 siRNA candidates (si-J1 through J3) from a custom oligonucleotide supplier (e.g., IDT, Eurofins, Ionis contract synthesis). Include scrambled-sequence and mismatch controls.
  4. Transfect and screen. Gymnotic delivery (free uptake without transfection agent) at 1, 5, 10, 50, 100 nM. Measure mutant vs. normal COL11A1 transcript levels by allele-specific RT-qPCR at 24, 48, and 72 hours. Calculate IC50 for mutant suppression and selectivity ratio (mutant knockdown / normal allele effect).
  5. Protein-level confirmation. Western blot or mass spectrometry to confirm reduction of truncated alpha1(XI) protein. Immunofluorescence to assess collagen XI matrix deposition.
  6. Toxicity screen. Cell viability (MTT assay), caspase activation, inflammatory cytokine panel (IL-6, TNF-alpha, IFN-alpha) to rule out innate immune activation from the ASO backbone.

Phase 2: Lead Optimization (6–12 months)

  1. Dose-response curves for the top 2–3 candidates from Phase 1.
  2. Chemistry optimization. Test alternative wing modifications (2'-OMe vs. 2'-MOE vs. constrained ethyl (cEt)) and gap lengths (8, 10, 12 nt) on the best-performing sequences.
  3. Intentional mismatch screen. Following the Zhou lab 2024 strategy, introduce a single mismatch in the wing region to enhance wild-type discrimination. Test 20 single-mismatch variants of the lead compound.
  4. Secondary structure optimization. Use RNAstructure or Vienna RNA package to predict mutant mRNA local folding. Reposition ASOs to target the most accessible regions within the junction window.
  5. Off-target profiling. RNA-seq in treated vs. untreated fibroblasts to identify any unintended gene knockdowns.

Phase 3: Tissue-Specific Delivery (12–24 months)

  1. Ocular delivery. Test lead ASO in ex vivo vitreous humor; measure stability, diffusion, and retinal cell uptake. If available, test in Col11a1 mutant mouse model (cho/+ heterozygous mouse).
  2. Cartilage delivery. Encapsulate in PLGA or LNP; test uptake in chondrocyte pellet cultures and cartilage explants. Measure ASO penetration depth into cartilage matrix.
  3. Inner ear delivery. Collaborate with otology lab experienced in intratympanic delivery (e.g., labs working on DB-OTO follow-up). Test round window membrane permeation.

Phase 4: In Vivo Proof-of-Concept (12–24 months)

  1. Animal model selection. The cho (chondrodysplasia) mouse carries a recessive Col11a1 mutation. Heterozygous cho/+ mice may serve as a partial model. Alternatively, a knock-in mouse carrying a human IVS50+1G>A splice mutation would be ideal but would need to be generated.
  2. Efficacy endpoints. Vitreous collagen fibril diameter (EM), hearing threshold (ABR testing), cartilage histology, growth plate morphology.
  3. Safety/PK. Tissue distribution, half-life, organ toxicity panel.

Phase 5: IND-Enabling and Clinical Path (2–5 years)

  1. GLP toxicology studies.
  2. IND filing. Orphan drug designation for Marshall syndrome (prevalence <200,000 in US) would provide 7-year market exclusivity, tax credits, and waived FDA application fees.
  3. Phase 1/2 clinical trial. Likely starting with intravitreal delivery (eye is the most accessible compartment and has the strongest regulatory precedent for ASO drugs). Primary endpoint: stabilization or improvement of vitreous structure on OCT imaging. Biomarker endpoints: reduction of mutant COL11A1 mRNA in aqueous humor samples.

Researcher Quick-Start Guide

For any researcher reading this page who wants to begin COL11A1 ASO work, here is the minimum viable starting experiment:

Minimum viable experiment (~$15K, 3 months)
  1. Acquire cells: Request COL11A1-mutant fibroblasts from an existing biobank or clinical collaborator. Alternatively, use a minigene construct carrying the IVS50+1G>A mutation transfected into HEK293T cells.
  2. Confirm the target: RT-PCR with primers flanking exon 50. Gel electrophoresis should show two bands: wild-type (with exon 50) and mutant (exon 50 skipped). Sequence the lower band to confirm the exact junction.
  3. Order ASOs: Synthesize ASO-J2 (the centered junction design) and a scrambled control from IDT or Qiagen. Cost: ~$500–$1,000 per ASO at research scale.
  4. Treat and measure: Gymnotic delivery at 10, 50, 100 nM. Allele-specific RT-qPCR at 48h. If mutant transcript decreases ≥30% at any dose without equivalent reduction in wild-type transcript, you have a confirmed hit.

Limitations and Caveats

  • Sequence accuracy. The candidate ASO and siRNA sequences are based on reference transcript annotations and published exon boundary data. The exact nucleotide positions at the exon 49–51 aberrant junction MUST be confirmed by sequencing the mutant mRNA before proceeding to synthesis. Even single-nucleotide errors in the junction sequence would invalidate the designs.
  • Patient-specific variation. While the common Marshall mutations all produce exon 50 skipping, rare mutations might cause partial exon inclusion or cryptic splice site activation, producing different junction sequences. Each patient's mutant transcript should be characterized individually.
  • Collagen Gly-X-Y repetitiveness. The triple-helix domain of collagen genes has highly repetitive Gly-X-Y sequences. This increases the risk of partial off-target matches to other collagen gene mRNAs. Thorough BLAST analysis against the entire collagen gene family is essential.
  • Tissue delivery remains the hardest problem. Even a perfectly selective ASO is useless if it cannot reach the target cells at therapeutic concentrations. Cartilage and disc delivery, in particular, remain unsolved challenges for the field.
  • COL11A1 expression levels vary by tissue. Vitreous, tectorial membrane, and growth plate cartilage express COL11A1 most highly. The therapeutic dose needed will differ by tissue. Eye and ear are the most pharmacologically accessible and express COL11A1 at high levels — they are the logical first targets.
  • No animal model perfectly recapitulates Marshall syndrome. The cho/+ mouse is the closest available model but carries a different mutation type. A knock-in model with a human splice-site mutation would be more informative but costly to generate.
