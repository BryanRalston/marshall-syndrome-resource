Cell Banking & Biobanking for Future Therapies

Evidence confidence guide

Future therapies — not currently available. Technologies described here are in various stages of research and development. Timeline projections are estimates, not commitments. None are currently available for Marshall syndrome.

Emerging therapies for connective tissue disorders — gene therapy, iPSC-derived disc and cartilage cells, tissue engineering — all have one thing in common: they require the patient's own cells as starting material. Banking cells today, while a child is young and cells are biologically optimal, may open doors to treatments that do not yet exist but are actively being developed.

Why Bank Cells Now? Established

Earlier is better — don't wait until therapies exist to bank cells. By the time a therapy reaches clinical availability, you want cells already stored and ready. Waiting until a trial opens means starting from scratch, potentially with older, less viable cells, under time pressure.
  • Gene therapy, iPSC-derived disc cells, and tissue engineering all require patient cells as starting material — banked cells become the raw ingredient for personalized treatment.
  • Younger cells are more viable, have longer telomeres, and reprogram more efficiently into induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). A 6-year-old's fibroblasts will outperform the same person's cells collected at 30.
  • A child's cells banked today may be critical for therapies available in 5–15 years, a realistic horizon given current research trajectories in collagen-related disorders.
  • Once cryopreserved, cells remain viable for decades in liquid nitrogen at −196°C. Storage does not degrade cell quality when done correctly.

What to Bank

Different cell types serve different purposes. The table below covers the most relevant options, ordered from most accessible to least.

How to Bank Dental Pulp Stem Cells

For children with Marshall syndrome, dental pulp stem cells from baby teeth represent the most accessible banking opportunity — no additional procedures, no extra appointments. Every naturally loosened baby tooth is a potential source.

Coordinate with your pediatric dentist before any baby teeth are lost. Have a collection kit on hand so you are ready when a tooth loosens. Kits shipped overnight are time-sensitive.
  1. Contact a dental pulp stem cell banking service to request a collection kit before the tooth is extracted or naturally shed.
  2. When a baby tooth loosens, the tooth must be placed in the preservation medium provided in the kit within 48 hours of extraction — ideally within a few hours. Dry teeth or teeth stored in water are not viable.
  3. Ship the tooth back to the lab via overnight courier, using the prepaid packaging in the kit.
  4. The lab extracts the pulp, culture-expands the stem cells, and cryopreserves them in multiple vials.
  5. Cells remain stored in liquid nitrogen; you receive documentation of viability and vial counts.

Typical costs: $1,000–$2,000 initial processing and storage setup, plus $100–$200 per year for ongoing storage. Prices vary by provider.

Wisdom teeth serve the same purpose in adolescents and adults. If your child will have wisdom teeth extracted, contact a banking service in advance rather than discovering the option after the fact.

How to Request a Skin Biopsy for Fibroblast Banking

Skin fibroblasts are the gold standard for iPSC reprogramming research. A punch biopsy is a quick, minimally invasive procedure that most geneticists or dermatologists can perform.

A 3–4mm punch biopsy takes less than 15 minutes, requires only local anesthetic, and leaves a very small scar — commonly done on the inner upper arm where scarring is minimal and hidden.
  • Ask your geneticist or dermatologist for a 3–4mm punch biopsy specifically for cell banking or research purposes.
  • Specify that the biopsy must go to a laboratory that will culture-expand fibroblasts — this is distinct from a standard pathology lab, which will fix the tissue and destroy cell viability.
  • Some genetic testing and biobanking labs accept samples directly; get the lab's shipping instructions before the biopsy is collected, as timing and transport medium matter.
  • Coriell Institute for Medical Research accepts rare disease fibroblast samples and may bank them at no cost as part of their publicly accessible research repository. Ask your geneticist about submitting to the NIGMS Human Genetic Cell Repository.
If your child has a planned surgical procedure — spine surgery, ear surgery, or any procedure under general anesthesia — ask the surgical team in advance whether a small skin biopsy can be added at the same time. This eliminates a separate procedure entirely.

Research Biorepositories (Potentially Free)

Banking through a commercial service is not the only option. Several research repositories actively seek cell samples from patients with rare genetic conditions, often at no cost to the family.

  • Coriell Institute for Medical Research — one of the largest rare disease biorepositories in the world; accepted samples are made available to researchers globally, which directly accelerates Marshall syndrome research.
  • NIGMS Human Genetic Cell Repository — National Institutes of General Medical Sciences repository for rare disease patient-derived cell lines.
  • International rare disease networks — EURO-RDI and similar networks coordinate cross-border sample collection for ultra-rare conditions; ask your geneticist whether any active Marshall or Stickler spectrum studies are currently enrolling.
  • Patient advocacy organizations and university research teams sometimes conduct their own biobanking as part of natural history studies — check with active researchers in the field.

Contributing to a research biorepository serves two purposes simultaneously: preserving cells for potential future personal use (depending on repository policy) and directly enabling the research that may produce those future therapies.

Questions to Ask a Cell Banking Service

Not all banking services are equivalent. Before committing, ask the following:

  • Accreditation: Is the facility AABB-accredited, CAP-certified, and FDA-registered? These are the benchmarks for clinical-grade cryopreservation.
  • Vial count: How many vials will be stored from the initial sample? Aim for at least 5 vials — more vials mean more flexibility for future research use or treatment protocols.
  • Business continuity: What happens to banked cells if the company closes? Are there arrangements for sample transfer to another facility?
  • Release policy: Can cells be shipped to a research lab or clinical trial site if needed? What is the process?
  • Long-term cost: What is the annual storage fee, and is there a cap or price-lock option?
  • Rare disease experience: Has the service processed samples from rare connective tissue disorder patients before? Do they have protocols for small-volume samples?

Insurance and Cost Considerations

Cell banking is generally not covered by health insurance. However, there are ways to reduce or offset the cost.
  • Typical commercial costs: $500–$2,500 for initial processing, plus $100–$300 per year for ongoing cryogenic storage. Dental pulp banking tends to be at the lower end; fibroblast banking varies more widely.
  • Biopsy coverage: The skin biopsy procedure itself may be covered by insurance if ordered by a physician as part of diagnostic evaluation — the banking of cells after collection is the part that typically is not covered.
  • Tax deductions: Cell banking costs may qualify as a deductible medical expense if physician-ordered. Consult a tax professional regarding IRS rules for medical expense deductions in your situation.
  • Research pathways: Biorepository submissions (Coriell, NIGMS) involve no cost to the family and remove the ongoing storage burden entirely.
  • Grant and foundation funding: Some rare disease foundations offer grants for biobanking costs; check with Marshall/Stickler patient advocacy organizations for current programs.

Practical Timing: Opportunities Not to Miss

Every loose baby tooth is a limited-time opportunity. Once a tooth falls out unprepared and dries out, the stem cells are gone. The window to act is measured in hours, not days.
  • Baby teeth: Talk to your pediatric dentist now, before any teeth loosen. Order a collection kit in advance so it is already in your home when the time comes.
  • Planned surgeries: Any surgery — including myringotomy (ear tubes), sinus surgery, or spinal procedures common in Marshall syndrome — is an opportunity to request a simultaneous skin biopsy or tissue collection. Raise it with the surgical team at the pre-operative appointment, not the day of surgery.
  • Routine blood draws: If your child has routine audiology, ophthalmology, or genetics monitoring that includes bloodwork, ask whether PBMC collection can be added to the same draw.
  • Adolescent wisdom teeth: If wisdom teeth are anticipated, contact a banking service 6–12 months before the expected extraction date.
  • Future siblings: If another child is planned, cord blood banking at birth requires pre-arrangement with the delivery team — the window closes at delivery.
