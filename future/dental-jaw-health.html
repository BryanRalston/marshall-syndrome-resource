Medical Disclaimer
This information is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical advice. Dental and orthodontic treatment plans should be developed in partnership with providers who understand connective tissue disorders. Every child is different — what works for one Marshall child may not be right for another. Discuss all treatment decisions with your child’s dental and medical team.

When people think of Marshall syndrome, they usually think of eyes, ears, and spine. But COL11A1 — the gene mutated in Marshall syndrome — profoundly affects the jaw, palate, teeth, and temporomandibular joint (TMJ). Collagen XI is a critical structural protein in cartilage, and the jaw joint is cartilage. The palate, the roots of the teeth, and the connective tissues that anchor teeth in place all depend on normal collagen function. This means dental and jaw health in Marshall syndrome is not a side issue — it is a core part of the condition.

This page brings together what we know from the latest research, including a 2025 systematic review of 1,037 patients, mouse model studies on COL11A1 and TMJ degeneration, and practical clinical experience — all translated into guidance that families can use.

The key biological fact. COL11A1 encodes the alpha-1 chain of type XI collagen, which is a critical regulator of cartilage fibril diameter. The TMJ condyle, the mandibular growth plate, the palatal shelf, and the periodontal ligament all contain cartilage or collagen-rich connective tissue. When collagen XI is defective, every one of these structures is affected — from before birth through adulthood.

Common Oral & Maxillofacial Features

A landmark 2025 systematic review published in the Journal of Stomatology, Oral and Maxillofacial Surgery analyzed data from 15 studies encompassing 1,037 patients with Stickler syndrome (which includes Marshall syndrome as a COL11A1-related type). The prevalence of oral and maxillofacial features was striking:

What families should take from this: More than half of children with COL11A1-related conditions are born with Pierre Robin sequence, and over 40% have cleft palate. These are not rare complications — they are central features of the syndrome. Early involvement of a craniofacial team is essential, not optional.

Submucous cleft palate. Some children have a submucous cleft palate — the surface mucosa looks intact, but the underlying muscle and bone are split. This can be missed on casual examination. Signs include a bifid (notched) uvula, a palpable notch at the back of the hard palate, and nasal-sounding speech. If your child has nasal speech or fluid coming from the nose when drinking, ask for a specific submucous cleft evaluation.

TMJ Vulnerability: The Jaw Joint at Risk

The temporomandibular joint is unlike any other joint in the body. It is covered with fibrocartilage rather than hyaline cartilage, and it contains a disc that slides during opening and closing. Both the cartilage and the disc depend on collagen for their structural integrity. When COL11A1 is mutated, the TMJ is vulnerable from the start.

What the Research Shows

Mouse models have provided direct evidence of the COL11A1-TMJ connection:

  • Col11a1+/- mice (which have only one working copy of the gene, similar to Marshall patients) develop progressive condylar cartilage degeneration in the TMJ. Increased proteoglycan staining — one of the earliest signs of cartilage breakdown — appears throughout the condylar and fossa cartilage. (Lam et al., 2014)
  • The receptor DDR2 (discoidin domain receptor 2), which preferentially binds type I and III collagen found in the TMJ, acts as a key driver of this degeneration. Reducing DDR2 expression in Col11a1+/- mice delayed TMJ degeneration by approximately 3 months — a significant window in mouse years. (Lam et al., 2018)
  • DDR2’s destructive role is specific to TMJ fibrocartilage and is not seen in hyaline cartilage of the knee, highlighting that the jaw joint has unique vulnerability mechanisms.

This means TMJ problems in Marshall syndrome are not just bad luck or teeth grinding — they are a direct molecular consequence of the COL11A1 mutation. The cartilage in the jaw joint is structurally compromised from the genetic level.

Symptoms to Watch For

TMJ degeneration can be insidious. Children may not complain until it is advanced. Watch for:

  • Jaw clicking or popping when opening or chewing — especially if it changes over time (a click that disappears may mean the disc has permanently displaced)
  • Pain in front of the ear, especially with chewing, yawning, or first thing in the morning
  • Limited jaw opening — difficulty fitting three stacked fingers between the teeth
  • Jaw locking — getting stuck open or closed
  • Headaches localized to the temples or behind the eyes
  • Ear pain without ear infection — the TMJ is immediately anterior to the ear canal
  • Changes in bite alignment — an anterior open bite developing in an adolescent may indicate condylar resorption
  • Facial asymmetry — if one condyle degenerates faster, the chin can shift to that side

Protective Strategies

  • Soft diet during flares. Avoid hard, chewy, or crunchy foods (bagels, hard candy, raw carrots, chewing gum) when the jaw is symptomatic.
  • Anti-inflammatory support. Omega-3 fatty acids and an anti-inflammatory diet may help reduce MMP-driven cartilage breakdown in the TMJ, just as they do in spinal discs.
  • Avoid extreme jaw opening. Teach your child to support the jaw (hand under chin) during yawning. Dental procedures requiring prolonged wide opening should include rest breaks.
  • Custom nightguard. A properly fitted nightguard (fabricated by a dentist, not a store-bought boil-and-bite) protects the TMJ from nocturnal clenching and grinding forces. This is especially important during the stress of adolescence.
  • Heat therapy. Moist heat to the TMJ area (warm washcloth for 15–20 minutes) before bed can reduce muscle tension and pain.
  • Posture awareness. Forward head posture shifts the mandible and increases TMJ loading. A physical therapist can assess and address cervicothoracic posture.

Orthodontic Timing & Strategy

Orthodontic care for a Marshall child is fundamentally different from orthodontics for a typically developing child. The jaw grows differently, the connective tissue is different, and the teeth move differently through compromised bone and ligament. Getting the timing and approach right matters enormously.

The Growth Pattern

Marshall children typically show characteristic skeletal features on cephalometric (head X-ray) analysis:

  • Small SNA and SNB angles — both the upper and lower jaw are set back relative to the skull base
  • Steep mandibular plane — the lower jaw grows more downward than forward, creating a “long face” pattern
  • Enlarged gonial angle — the corner of the jaw is more open than typical
  • Retroclined incisors — the front teeth tilt backward in both arches
  • Class III tendency — the lower jaw can sit forward relative to the upper jaw despite being small, because the midface (upper jaw) is even more underdeveloped

These are not standard orthodontic problems. A skilled orthodontist will recognize that the underlying collagen disorder means that the usual assumptions about growth direction, bone remodeling, and retention stability may not hold.

What to Discuss With Your Orthodontist

  • Growth-guided approaches first. Functional appliances that work with the jaw’s growth (twin-block, Herbst, headgear) are preferred over extracting teeth to make room. The goal is to guide the jaw, not just line up the teeth.
  • Gentle force. Collagen-compromised periodontal ligaments may respond differently to orthodontic force. Lower forces over longer treatment times may be safer and more stable than aggressive, rapid tooth movement.
  • Root resorption risk. All orthodontic treatment carries some root resorption risk, but connective tissue disorders may increase this risk. Periodic panoramic X-rays during treatment should monitor root length.
  • Permanent retention. In the general population, many patients can stop wearing retainers after a few years. For Marshall patients, lifelong retention (bonded lingual retainers or nightly removable retainers) should be the default plan. The ligament holding teeth in place is collagen-based, and in a collagen disorder, teeth can shift for life.
  • Avoid unnecessary extractions. Premolar extraction to resolve crowding is common in standard orthodontics but may complicate jaw growth management in a child who already has a small jaw.
  • TMJ baseline before treatment. Get a TMJ assessment (clinical exam, panoramic X-ray, possibly CBCT or MRI if symptomatic) before starting orthodontic treatment. Treatment can change joint loading, and you need a baseline to detect any degeneration during treatment.
  • Coordinate with the craniofacial team. If cleft palate repair, bone grafting, or jaw surgery (orthognathic surgery) is being considered, orthodontics must be coordinated with the surgical timeline.
Catch-up growth. Some children with Pierre Robin sequence experience spontaneous mandibular “catch-up growth” in the first few years of life, particularly when the Pierre Robin was caused by fetal positioning (deformational) rather than the underlying collagen defect. However, in Marshall syndrome, where the small jaw is driven by a genetic collagen deficiency, catch-up growth is typically incomplete. Plan accordingly.

Dental Protection Protocol

Routine dental care matters more in Marshall syndrome because the teeth, gums, and supporting structures are all affected by the underlying collagen disorder. Prevention is far easier than repair in a child whose tissues heal differently.

Enamel Considerations

While Marshall syndrome is not primarily an enamel disorder like amelogenesis imperfecta, some children show:

  • Enamel hypoplasia — thin or patchy enamel, particularly on permanent teeth. This increases cavity risk.
  • Tooth morphology differences — teeth may be smaller or have unusual root shapes, discovered incidentally on dental X-rays.
  • Periodontal ligament laxity — the ligament anchoring teeth into bone is collagen-rich. In connective tissue disorders, teeth may be slightly more mobile than expected, and extraction sites may heal more slowly.

Cavity Prevention (Non-Negotiable)

  • Fluoride varnish at every dental visit (typically every 6 months, or every 3–4 months if enamel is compromised)
  • Pit-and-fissure sealants on all permanent molars as soon as they erupt — these create a physical barrier over the chewing surfaces
  • Xylitol — chewing xylitol gum or mints (if old enough) reduces Streptococcus mutans counts and cavity risk
  • Prescription-strength fluoride toothpaste (1.1% sodium fluoride) if your dentist identifies high cavity risk
  • Limit sugar exposure — especially sticky, prolonged sugar contact (fruit snacks, juice sipped over hours). Frequency of sugar exposure matters more than total amount.
  • Electric toothbrush with a 2-minute timer — more effective than manual brushing, especially for children with joint hypermobility in the hands

Anesthesia Precautions During Dental Work

Dental procedures requiring local anesthesia or sedation carry specific risks in Marshall syndrome. Print this section and give it to your dentist before any procedure.

Airway alert. Children with Marshall syndrome may have difficult airways due to micrognathia (small jaw), midface hypoplasia, and cervical spine abnormalities. A 502-patient case series confirmed that while airway complication rates are low overall, patients with a receding jaw or cleft palate history represent a higher risk group and should be managed by experienced providers. Any procedure requiring sedation beyond local anesthesia should be performed in a facility with advanced airway management capability.
  • Local anesthetic resistance. Some connective tissue disorder patients report that local anesthetic wears off faster or is less effective. Use articaine (more lipophilic, better tissue penetration) rather than lidocaine when possible. Discuss with the dentist if previous numbing has been inadequate.
  • Jaw instability during procedures. Prolonged wide mouth opening can stress the TMJ. Request rest breaks every 15–20 minutes during long procedures. A bite block can help stabilize the jaw.
  • Cervical spine precautions. This is critical. Marshall syndrome can involve cervical spine instability, including craniocervical instability (CCI) and atlantoaxial instability. During dental procedures:
    • Avoid hyperextending the neck in the dental chair
    • Use a small pillow or rolled towel to maintain neutral cervical alignment
    • If your child has known or suspected cervical instability, inform the dentist before the appointment
    • If sedation or general anesthesia is required, the anesthesia team must be aware of cervical precautions during intubation
  • Wound healing. Collagen disorders affect wound healing. After extractions or surgical procedures, expect longer healing times. Stitches may need to stay in longer, and the extraction site should be monitored more closely for dry socket or delayed closure.
  • Mitral valve prolapse (MVP) alert. Some Marshall patients have MVP. While current AHA guidelines no longer recommend routine antibiotic prophylaxis for MVP before dental procedures unless there is a history of endocarditis, discuss this with your cardiologist to confirm your child’s specific recommendation.

Dental Emergencies

  • Tooth avulsion (knocked-out tooth). Place the tooth in milk or a Save-A-Tooth kit and get to a dentist within 30 minutes. Reimplantation may be complicated by periodontal ligament fragility — the dentist should be aware of the collagen disorder.
  • Tooth fracture. Even minor falls can fracture teeth in a child with enamel concerns. Save any fragments and see the dentist same-day.
  • Jaw dislocation. Hypermobile joints can mean the jaw dislocates (locks open) during wide yawning or dental procedures. This is usually reducible by a trained provider but can be frightening. If it has happened once, discuss a prevention plan with the dental team.
Mouthguards for sports. A custom-fabricated mouthguard (from a dental impression, not a store-bought boil-and-bite) is essential for any sport or physical activity with contact or fall risk. This protects both the teeth and the TMJ. Custom guards fit better, stay in place, and are more comfortable — which means your child will actually wear it.

What to Tell Your Dentist

At the first visit with any new dental provider, share the following:

  1. “My child has Marshall syndrome (COL11A1 mutation), a connective tissue disorder.” Many dentists will not have heard of Marshall syndrome. That is okay. The key phrase is connective tissue disorder — they will understand the implications for wound healing, anesthesia, and tissue fragility.
  2. “Please check the jaw joint (TMJ) at every visit.” A quick palpation and range-of-motion check should be part of routine exams.
  3. “Local anesthetic may wear off faster or require more.” This allows them to plan dosing and timing.
  4. “Cervical spine precautions are needed.” No neck hyperextension in the dental chair.
  5. “Wound healing may be slower.” Important for any surgical or extraction procedures.
  6. “Please avoid fluoroquinolone antibiotics if antibiotics are needed.” Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) carry an FDA black box warning for tendon damage and are particularly concerning in connective tissue disorders.

Consider bringing a copy of your child’s Emergency Protocol Card or Doctor Letter to the dental office.

Craniofacial Centers

For children with cleft palate, Pierre Robin sequence, or significant jaw discrepancies, care should be coordinated through a multidisciplinary craniofacial team. These teams typically include a plastic surgeon or oral surgeon, orthodontist, speech-language pathologist, ENT, audiologist, and geneticist — all in one place. Most children’s hospitals have a craniofacial program. Ask your geneticist or pediatrician for a referral.

Practical Tips for Families

Daily Home Care

  • Brush twice daily with fluoride toothpaste (use a pea-sized amount for children under 6, a full strip for older children)
  • Floss daily — floss picks or a water flosser (Waterpik) can be easier for children with hand hypermobility or limited jaw opening
  • Rinse with fluoride mouthwash (for children old enough not to swallow it, typically 6+)
  • Inspect the mouth monthly — look for white spots on teeth (early cavities), gum recession, or new clicking/popping in the jaw
  • Teach jaw relaxation. The resting position of the jaw should be: lips together, teeth apart, tongue on the roof of the mouth. Many children (and adults) unconsciously clench, especially during screen time or homework.

Diet for Jaw & Dental Health

  • Calcium and vitamin D — essential for tooth and jawbone mineralization. Dairy, fortified alternatives, leafy greens, and supplementation as guided by your doctor.
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7) — directs calcium into bones and teeth rather than soft tissue. Found in natto, hard cheeses, and supplements.
  • Limit acidic beverages — soda, sports drinks, citrus juice, and sparkling water can erode already-vulnerable enamel. If consumed, use a straw and rinse with plain water afterward.
  • Crunchy vegetables (when TMJ allows) — raw vegetables like celery and cucumber stimulate healthy bone loading in the jaw. Cut into small pieces rather than biting into large, hard items.
  • Omega-3 fatty acids — anti-inflammatory support for the TMJ. Fish, walnuts, flaxseed, or supplements.

Nightguards for TMJ Protection

  • A custom hard-soft nightguard (hard exterior, soft interior) is generally recommended for TMJ protection in connective tissue disorders
  • Have it made by a dentist from a dental impression — store-bought versions do not fit properly and can worsen TMJ symptoms
  • Replace every 1–2 years or sooner if worn through — check regularly for cracks or fit changes
  • If your child also has bruxism (teeth grinding), the nightguard serves double duty: protecting both the teeth and the joint
  • Some children resist wearing nightguards. Start with short daytime wear to build tolerance, then transition to nighttime use.

When to Seek Urgent Dental Care

Go to the dentist or emergency room promptly if your child experiences:

  • A knocked-out permanent tooth (within 30 minutes if possible)
  • Jaw locked open or closed and unable to self-reduce
  • Severe facial swelling with fever (possible dental abscess — infection can spread rapidly)
  • Sudden change in bite alignment (may indicate condylar fracture or resorption)
  • Uncontrolled bleeding after dental surgery lasting more than 4 hours despite gauze pressure
  • Numbness or tingling in the lower lip persisting more than 8 hours after dental work (possible nerve injury)

Research Directions

Several active areas of research may lead to better treatments for jaw and dental issues in Marshall syndrome:

  • DDR2 inhibition. Since reducing DDR2 expression delays TMJ cartilage degeneration in Col11a1+/- mice, DDR2 inhibitors could potentially slow TMJ damage in patients. No human trials yet, but the target is identified and validated. (Lam et al., 2014)
  • MMP-13 inhibition. MMP-13 (collagenase-3) is a key enzyme in both TMJ and disc cartilage destruction. Doxycycline, already discussed as a potential drug repurposing candidate for Marshall syndrome, inhibits MMP-13 at sub-antimicrobial doses and could protect the TMJ.
  • Tissue engineering for TMJ disc. Research into bioengineered TMJ discs using scaffolds and stem cells is advancing, though clinical application is still years away.
  • Dental pulp stem cells. Baby teeth contain dental pulp stem cells that can be banked. These cells are relevant for future regenerative medicine approaches and are a potential source of iPSCs. If your child is losing baby teeth, consider dental pulp banking through a service like StemSave or Provia Labs. (See the Cell Banking page for details.)
Baby teeth as a resource. When your Marshall child loses baby teeth naturally, the dental pulp inside contains mesenchymal stem cells. These can be cryopreserved and may be useful for future regenerative therapies. Banking is straightforward: the tooth must be placed in a preservation kit within 30 minutes of falling out and shipped to the banking facility. Talk to your dentist about this before teeth start falling out (around age 6).

Key Takeaways

  1. Dental and jaw health is a core part of Marshall syndrome, not a secondary concern. Over 50% of patients have Pierre Robin sequence, and over 40% have cleft palate.
  2. The TMJ is molecularly vulnerable due to COL11A1 mutation. TMJ degeneration is a direct consequence of the collagen defect, not just wear and tear.
  3. Orthodontic care requires a modified approach — growth-guided strategies, gentle forces, lifelong retention, and coordination with the craniofacial team.
  4. Prevention is paramount. Aggressive cavity prevention (fluoride, sealants, diet) prevents the need for complex restorative procedures that carry higher risk in connective tissue disorders.
  5. Every dental visit should include cervical spine precautions, TMJ assessment, and awareness of anesthetic and wound healing differences.
  6. Build your team early. A pediatric dentist, orthodontist, and TMJ specialist who understand connective tissue disorders will serve your child for decades.
  7. Bank those baby teeth. Dental pulp stem cells may be valuable for future therapies.
